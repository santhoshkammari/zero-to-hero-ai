Normalizing Flows

Chapter 15: Generative Models 5 subtopics
TL;DR

Normalizing flows transform a simple distribution (like a Gaussian) into a complex one through a chain of invertible, differentiable functions — and they give you exact log-likelihoods by tracking how volume changes at each step via the Jacobian determinant. NICE introduced additive coupling layers with trivial Jacobians. RealNVP upgraded to affine coupling (scale + shift) with multi-scale architecture. Glow replaced fixed channel permutations with learned invertible 1×1 convolutions. Modern extensions include continuous normalizing flows via Neural ODEs, autoregressive flows (MAF vs IAF), and flow matching — which connects flows to diffusion models and is becoming the dominant training paradigm.

Foundations: Why Exact Likelihoods Matter

Let's start with a frustration. You've trained a VAE and it gives you blurry samples. You know why — the ELBO is a lower bound on the true log-likelihood, and the gap between the bound and reality means you're optimizing an approximation of an approximation. You switch to a GAN and get sharp images, but now you have no likelihood at all. You can't score how probable a given data point is. You can't do anomaly detection. You can't compare models on a proper statistical footing.

What if you could have both — sharp samples AND exact likelihoods? That's the promise of normalizing flows.

The idea is this: start with a distribution you understand perfectly, like a standard Gaussian. Then warp it through a series of invertible transformations until it matches your data distribution. Because every step is invertible and differentiable, you can trace any data point backward to the Gaussian and compute its exact probability. No approximations. No adversarial training. No ELBO gap.

Change of Variables in 1D: The Rubber Band

Before we touch matrices or Jacobians, think about a rubber band. Stretch a rubber band and the marks on it spread out. Compress it and they bunch up. If you had a uniform distribution of ink dots along the band and you stretched one section, the density of dots in that section decreases — same number of dots, more space.

That's exactly what the change of variables formula captures. Say you have a random variable z drawn from a known distribution p(z), and you transform it with an invertible function x = f(z). What's the density of x?

In 1D, the answer is elegant:

p(x) = p(z) · |dz/dx|
     = p(f⁻¹(x)) · |d(f⁻¹(x))/dx|

The |dz/dx| term is the "stretching factor." If f spreads things out (derivative > 1), density goes down. If f compresses (derivative < 1), density goes up. Probability mass is conserved — it gets redistributed.

A concrete example: let z ~ N(0, 1) and x = 2z + 3. Then z = (x - 3) / 2, and dz/dx = 1/2. So p(x) = p((x-3)/2) · |1/2| — a Gaussian with mean 3 and standard deviation 2. The factor of 1/2 accounts for the stretching.

The Full Multivariate Formula

In higher dimensions, the "stretching factor" becomes the absolute value of the determinant of the Jacobian matrix. The Jacobian is the matrix of all partial derivatives — entry (i, j) tells you how much output dimension i changes when you wiggle input dimension j.

For a transformation x = f(z) where both x and z are vectors in ℝᵈ:

log p(x) = log p(z) + log |det(dz/dx)|
         = log p(f⁻¹(x)) + log |det(J_{f⁻¹}(x))|

That log |det(J)| term is the log-absolute-determinant of the Jacobian. It measures how the transformation changes volume at a particular point. When the determinant is large, the inverse mapping compresses space — packing more probability into less volume. When it's small, volume expands and density thins out.

Why invertibility? Two reasons. First, to compute p(x) you need to find the corresponding z = f⁻¹(x) — you literally need to run the transformation backward. Second, to generate samples you go forward: draw z ~ p(z) then compute x = f(z). Invertibility gives you both directions.

Chaining Transformations

One invertible function can only warp the base distribution so much. But chain K of them together — f = f_K ∘ f_{K-1} ∘ ... ∘ f_1 — and you get a composition that's also invertible (invert each step in reverse order). The Jacobian determinants multiply, so in log space they add:

log p(x) = log p(z₀) + Σᵢ log |det(J_{fᵢ}(zᵢ₋₁))|

where z₀ ~ p(z₀),  zᵢ = fᵢ(zᵢ₋₁),  and x = z_K

This is why it's called a "flow" — data flows through a chain of transformations. "Normalizing" refers to the fact that we're mapping from a normalized (known) distribution to a complex one.

Here's the core formula in PyTorch:

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
from torch.distributions import MultivariateNormal

class NormalizingFlow(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, transforms):
        super().__init__()
        self.transforms = nn.ModuleList(transforms)
        # Base distribution: standard Gaussian
        self.base_dist = MultivariateNormal(
            torch.zeros(2), torch.eye(2)
        )

    def log_prob(self, x):
        """Compute exact log p(x) via change of variables."""
        log_det_sum = 0
        z = x
        # Walk backward through transforms
        for t in reversed(self.transforms):
            z, log_det = t.inverse(z)  # z_{i-1}, log|det J|
            log_det_sum += log_det
        # z is now in base space
        log_pz = self.base_dist.log_prob(z)
        return log_pz + log_det_sum

    def sample(self, num_samples):
        """Generate samples by going forward."""
        z = self.base_dist.sample((num_samples,))
        for t in self.transforms:
            z = t.forward(z)
        return z

Training is maximum likelihood: push data through the inverse, compute log-probabilities, and maximize them. No reconstruction loss, no adversary, no ELBO. Clean optimization of the exact log-likelihood.

The computational bottleneck: Computing det(J) for a general d×d matrix costs O(d³). For images with thousands of dimensions, that's a dealbreaker. The entire history of normalizing flow architectures is the story of designing transformations where the Jacobian determinant is cheap to compute — ideally O(d) instead of O(d³).

NICE: Non-linear Independent Components Estimation

The first practical normalizing flow architecture, published by Dinh et al. in 2014, made an observation that changed the field: if you design your transformation so the Jacobian is triangular, the determinant is the product of the diagonal entries. That's O(d) instead of O(d³). Huge win.

How do you get a triangular Jacobian? With additive coupling layers. Here's the trick: split your input z into two halves, z₁ and z₂. Leave one half completely unchanged, and transform the other half using a function conditioned on the unchanged half:

  Input z = [z₁, z₂]        (split in half)

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  z₁  ─────────────────────────────► y₁  │   (identity — unchanged)
  │   │                                      │
  │   └──► Neural Net m() ──► m(z₁)         │
  │                              │           │
  │  z₂  ──────────── + ───────►  y₂        │   y₂ = z₂ + m(z₁)
  └─────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Output y = [y₁, y₂]

Look at the Jacobian of this transformation:

  J = ∂y/∂z = ┌  I        0    ┐
              │                 │
              └ ∂m/∂z₁    I    ┘

That's a lower triangular matrix. Its determinant is the product of the diagonal elements, which are all 1 (since the diagonal blocks are identity matrices). So det(J) = 1 for every additive coupling layer, always, no matter what the neural network m does.

This is beautiful and terrible at the same time. Beautiful because computing the Jacobian determinant is free — it's always zero in log space. Terrible because det = 1 means these transformations are volume-preserving. They can shift probability mass around but they cannot concentrate or spread it. Every unit cube of input space maps to a region of exactly the same volume in output space.

The inverse is also trivial: z₁ = y₁, z₂ = y₂ - m(y₁). Notice that m can be any neural network — arbitrarily deep, with ReLUs, whatever you want. The invertibility comes from the coupling structure, not from m itself. You never need to invert m.

One coupling layer only transforms half the dimensions. The fix: alternate which half gets transformed. Layer 1 keeps z₁ fixed and transforms z₂. Layer 2 keeps z₂ fixed and transforms z₁. Stack enough layers and every dimension gets to interact with every other dimension.

NICE's limitation: Volume-preserving flows are inherently less expressive. If your data distribution has regions of high density and regions of low density (which real data always does), you need transformations that can compress and expand volumes. NICE compensates by learning a diagonal scaling matrix at the end, but this is a band-aid. RealNVP fixes this properly.

RealNVP: Real-valued Non-Volume Preserving

Dinh et al. came back in 2016 with the obvious upgrade: instead of only adding, also multiply. An affine coupling layer applies both a scale and a shift to the transformed half:

  y₁ = z₁                         (unchanged)
  y₂ = z₂ ⊙ exp(s(z₁)) + t(z₁)   (scale and shift)

Here s and t are neural networks that take z₁ as input and produce scale and translation parameters. The exp() ensures the scale factor is always positive (you can't flip the sign — that would mess up invertibility). The ⊙ is element-wise multiplication.

The Jacobian is still triangular:

  J = ┌  I              0           ┐
      │                              │
      └ ∂(...)/∂z₁    diag(exp(s))  ┘

The determinant is now the product of exp(s(z₁)), which in log space is the sum of s(z₁):

  log |det(J)| = sum(s(z₁))

Still O(d) — you just sum up the scale outputs. But now the determinant is not locked to 1. The model can learn to expand and compress volumes. This is what "Non-Volume Preserving" in the name means.

The inverse is equally easy: z₁ = y₁, z₂ = (y₂ - t(y₁)) ⊙ exp(-s(y₁)). Division by the scale factor, undo the shift. Done.

Here's a complete PyTorch implementation:

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class AffineCouplingLayer(nn.Module):
    """RealNVP affine coupling layer."""
    def __init__(self, dim, hidden_dim=256, mask_even=True):
        super().__init__()
        self.mask_even = mask_even
        half = dim // 2
        # s and t networks share a backbone, split at output
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(half, hidden_dim),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim),
            nn.ReLU(),
        )
        self.scale_out = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, dim - half)
        self.shift_out = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, dim - half)

    def _split(self, z):
        d = z.shape[-1] // 2
        if self.mask_even:
            return z[..., :d], z[..., d:]
        else:
            return z[..., d:], z[..., :d]

    def _merge(self, z1, z2):
        if self.mask_even:
            return torch.cat([z1, z2], dim=-1)
        else:
            return torch.cat([z2, z1], dim=-1)

    def forward(self, z):
        """Forward pass: base → data direction."""
        z1, z2 = self._split(z)
        h = self.net(z1)
        s = self.scale_out(h)
        t = self.shift_out(h)
        y2 = z2 * torch.exp(s) + t
        log_det = s.sum(dim=-1)  # sum of log-scale factors
        return self._merge(z1, y2), log_det

    def inverse(self, y):
        """Inverse pass: data → base direction."""
        y1, y2 = self._split(y)
        h = self.net(y1)
        s = self.scale_out(h)
        t = self.shift_out(h)
        z2 = (y2 - t) * torch.exp(-s)
        log_det = -s.sum(dim=-1)  # negative because inverse
        return self._merge(y1, z2), log_det


class RealNVP(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, dim, num_layers=6, hidden_dim=256):
        super().__init__()
        self.layers = nn.ModuleList([
            AffineCouplingLayer(dim, hidden_dim, mask_even=(i % 2 == 0))
            for i in range(num_layers)
        ])
        self.base_dist = torch.distributions.MultivariateNormal(
            torch.zeros(dim), torch.eye(dim)
        )

    def log_prob(self, x):
        log_det_total = 0
        z = x
        for layer in reversed(self.layers):
            z, log_det = layer.inverse(z)
            log_det_total += log_det
        return self.base_dist.log_prob(z) + log_det_total

    def sample(self, n):
        z = self.base_dist.sample((n,))
        for layer in self.layers:
            z, _ = layer.forward(z)
        return z

Multi-Scale Architecture

For images, RealNVP introduced a clever trick to keep things tractable: a multi-scale architecture that progressively reduces the spatial dimensions while factoring out latent variables at each scale.

  Input image: 32×32×3
       │
       ▼
  ┌──────────────┐
  │ Squeeze 2×2  │  → 16×16×12  (trade spatial for channels)
  │              │
  │ Coupling ×3  │  → 16×16×12
  │              │
  │ Factor out   │  → keep 16×16×6, output z₁ (16×16×6)
  └──────────────┘
       │
       ▼
  ┌──────────────┐
  │ Squeeze 2×2  │  → 8×8×24
  │              │
  │ Coupling ×3  │  → 8×8×24
  │              │
  │ Factor out   │  → keep 8×8×12, output z₂ (8×8×12)
  └──────────────┘
       │
       ▼
  ┌──────────────┐
  │ Squeeze 2×2  │  → 4×4×48
  │              │
  │ Coupling ×4  │  → 4×4×48  → output z₃
  └──────────────┘

  Final latent: z = [z₁, z₂, z₃]   (all Gaussian)

The squeeze operation takes a 2×2 block of spatial pixels and stacks them into the channel dimension. A 32×32×3 image becomes 16×16×12. You lose spatial resolution but gain channel depth. This lets subsequent coupling layers operate on a spatially smaller but channel-richer representation.

The factoring out step is even cleverer. After a few coupling layers at each scale, half the channels are split off and directly modeled as Gaussian. They don't pass through any more transformations. This dramatically reduces computation — later layers only process half as many channels. It also gives the model a natural coarse-to-fine hierarchy: early-factored-out dimensions capture large-scale structure, late dimensions capture fine details.

RealNVP also introduced batch normalization in flows. Ordinary batch norm is invertible (it's an affine transformation with running statistics), and its Jacobian determinant is cheap — it's just the product of the scale parameters. This helps training stability enormously.

Glow: Invertible 1×1 Convolutions

RealNVP has a nagging problem. Coupling layers only transform half the channels, and the two halves are swapped in alternating layers. This is a fixed, hand-designed permutation. With 3 channels (RGB), the same channel always gets paired with the same partner. Information mixing across channels is limited.

Kingma and Dhariwal (2018) asked: what if we learned the permutation? Their solution was the invertible 1×1 convolution — a pointwise linear transformation that mixes channels at every spatial location.

A 1×1 convolution with c input channels and c output channels is parameterized by a c × c weight matrix W. At each spatial position, it multiplies the channel vector by W. If W is invertible (non-zero determinant), the whole operation is invertible.

The log-determinant contribution is straightforward. Since the same W is applied at every spatial position, and there are h × w positions:

  log |det| = h × w × log |det(W)|

Computing det(W) for a c × c matrix costs O(c³), but c is typically 48-512 (number of channels), not the full image dimension. That's very manageable.

Glow further optimized this with an LU decomposition: parameterize W = P · L · U where P is a fixed permutation, L is lower triangular with ones on the diagonal, and U is upper triangular. The determinant is then the product of the diagonal of U — O(c) instead of O(c³).

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

class Invertible1x1Conv(nn.Module):
    """Glow's invertible 1x1 convolution with LU decomposition."""
    def __init__(self, num_channels):
        super().__init__()
        c = num_channels
        # Initialize with a random orthogonal matrix
        W_init = torch.linalg.qr(torch.randn(c, c))[0]
        # LU decompose for efficient det computation
        P, L, U = torch.linalg.lu(W_init)
        # Fix the permutation (not learned)
        self.register_buffer('P', P)
        # Learn L and U
        self.L = nn.Parameter(L)
        self.log_diag_U = nn.Parameter(torch.log(torch.abs(torch.diag(U))))
        self.U_upper = nn.Parameter(
            U.triu(diagonal=1)  # strict upper triangle
        )
        self.register_buffer('L_mask', torch.tril(torch.ones(c, c), -1))
        self.register_buffer('I', torch.eye(c))

    def _assemble_W(self):
        c = self.P.shape[0]
        L = self.L * self.L_mask + self.I     # lower tri with 1s on diag
        U_diag = torch.diag(torch.exp(self.log_diag_U))
        U = self.U_upper.triu(diagonal=1) + U_diag
        W = self.P @ L @ U
        return W

    def forward(self, z):
        # z shape: (batch, channels, height, width)
        W = self._assemble_W()
        h, w = z.shape[2], z.shape[3]
        # log-det: h * w * sum(log|diag(U)|)
        log_det = h * w * self.log_diag_U.sum()
        # Apply as 1x1 conv
        W_conv = W.unsqueeze(-1).unsqueeze(-1)  # (c, c, 1, 1)
        y = F.conv2d(z, W_conv)
        return y, log_det

    def inverse(self, y):
        W = self._assemble_W()
        W_inv = torch.inverse(W)
        h, w = y.shape[2], y.shape[3]
        log_det = -h * w * self.log_diag_U.sum()
        W_inv_conv = W_inv.unsqueeze(-1).unsqueeze(-1)
        z = F.conv2d(y, W_inv_conv)
        return z, log_det

Actnorm: Data-Dependent Initialization

Glow replaced batch normalization with actnorm (activation normalization). The idea: an affine transformation (per-channel scale and bias) whose parameters are initialized so that the output of each channel has zero mean and unit variance on the first mini-batch of data. After initialization, these parameters are learned normally via gradient descent.

Why not batch norm? Batch norm's behavior changes between training and inference (running statistics vs mini-batch statistics), and it introduces dependencies between samples in a mini-batch. Both are annoying for generative models where you want consistent likelihoods. Actnorm sidesteps these issues — it's a proper invertible affine transform with a trivial Jacobian (product of scale parameters), initialized cleverly.

The Complete Glow Block

Each step of the Glow model consists of three operations in sequence:

  ┌───────────────────────────┐
  │                           │
  │  1. Actnorm               │   (channel-wise scale + bias)
  │         │                 │
  │         ▼                 │
  │  2. Invertible 1×1 Conv   │   (learned channel permutation)
  │         │                 │
  │         ▼                 │
  │  3. Affine Coupling       │   (the RealNVP-style layer)
  │                           │
  └───────────────────────────┘
        × K times per scale

Stack K of these blocks (typically K=32), add squeeze operations and factoring-out between scales, and you get the full Glow architecture. The model famously generated high-quality 256×256 face images and enabled latent space manipulation — interpolating between faces, adding and removing attributes like "smiling" or "wearing glasses" by moving along learned directions in the latent Gaussian space.

The latent manipulation works because the base distribution is a well-behaved Gaussian. Directions in Gaussian space correspond to meaningful semantic directions in image space, and the invertible mapping preserves the smoothness. This is a significant advantage over GANs, where the latent space geometry is harder to reason about.

Glow's scale: The original Glow paper used models with ~200 million parameters trained on 40 GPUs. Each forward pass computes exact log-likelihoods for 256×256 images. The cost of this exactness is architectural constraint — every operation must be invertible with a tractable Jacobian.

Advanced Flow Models

Coupling-layer flows (NICE, RealNVP, Glow) are the workhorses, but the field kept pushing in several directions. Let's walk through the most important ones.

Continuous Normalizing Flows (Neural ODEs)

Here's a conceptual leap. Discrete flows chain a finite number of layers: z₁ = f₁(z₀), z₂ = f₂(z₁), ..., z_K = f_K(z_{K-1}). What if you took K to infinity? Instead of discrete steps, you'd have a continuous transformation governed by an ordinary differential equation:

  dz/dt = v(z(t), t; θ)      t ∈ [0, 1]

The neural network v defines a velocity field — at every point in space and time, it tells you which direction to push the particle. Start at z(0) ~ p₀(z) (your base Gaussian), integrate the ODE forward to t=1, and you arrive at your data distribution. This is a continuous normalizing flow, or CNF.

The change of variables formula has a continuous analog called the instantaneous change of variables:

  d(log p(z(t)))/dt = -tr(∂v/∂z)

Instead of computing a determinant, you compute a trace of the Jacobian. Computing the exact trace is still O(d²), but Grathwohl et al. showed you can get an unbiased estimate using the Hutchinson trace estimator: tr(A) ≈ εᵀAε where ε is a random vector. This reduces the cost to a single Jacobian-vector product, which auto-diff gives you efficiently.

The beauty: your velocity field v can be any neural network. No coupling structure, no triangular Jacobians, no architectural constraints. The ODE solver handles invertibility for you — integrate forward to sample, integrate backward to compute likelihoods. The downside: ODE solving is sequential and slow. Each likelihood evaluation requires running an adaptive ODE solver, which can take dozens to hundreds of neural network evaluations.

Autoregressive Flows: MAF and IAF

There's another way to get triangular Jacobians: make each output dimension depend only on previous input dimensions. This is the autoregressive structure.

Masked Autoregressive Flow (MAF) defines the transformation as:

  x₁ = z₁ · σ₁ + μ₁
  x₂ = z₂ · σ₂(x₁) + μ₂(x₁)
  x₃ = z₃ · σ₃(x₁, x₂) + μ₃(x₁, x₂)
  ...
  xₐ = zₐ · σₐ(x₁...xₐ₋₁) + μₐ(x₁...xₐ₋₁)

Each output depends on all previous outputs plus the corresponding base variable. The Jacobian is triangular by construction — ∂xᵢ/∂zⱼ = 0 for j > i. The determinant is the product of the scale factors σᵢ.

But notice the asymmetry. Computing p(x) (density evaluation) requires inverting this — solving for each zᵢ given x. That can be done in parallel because each zᵢ = (xᵢ - μᵢ(x₁...xᵢ₋₁)) / σᵢ(x₁...xᵢ₋₁) depends on x values, which are all known. One forward pass through the masked network gives you all parameters at once. Fast likelihood, great for training.

Sampling, however, is sequential. To generate x₁, you need z₁. To generate x₂, you need x₁ and z₂. Each dimension requires a separate forward pass. Slow for generation.

Inverse Autoregressive Flow (IAF) flips this. Each output depends on previous inputs (base variables) instead of previous outputs:

  x₁ = z₁ · σ₁ + μ₁
  x₂ = z₂ · σ₂(z₁) + μ₂(z₁)
  x₃ = z₃ · σ₃(z₁, z₂) + μ₃(z₁, z₂)
  ...

Now sampling is fast — all z values are known upfront (you sampled them from the base distribution), so you can compute all x values in one parallel pass. But density evaluation is slow because inverting requires sequential computation. IAF is great for VAE decoders (where you need fast sampling) but bad for standalone density estimation.

This tradeoff is fundamental — it's the same tradeoff between autoregressive models (fast likelihood, slow sampling) and latent variable models (fast sampling, slower likelihood). MAF and IAF live on opposite sides of this fence.

Residual Flows

Residual networks compute y = x + f(x). This is almost a valid flow — the only issue is invertibility. A residual block is invertible if the Lipschitz constant of f is less than 1, meaning f can't change its output faster than its input changes. In practice, you enforce this through spectral normalization on the weight matrices of f.

The Jacobian determinant of I + J_f doesn't have a cheap closed form, but you can estimate log det(I + J_f) using a power series expansion:

  log det(I + J_f) = tr(J_f) - tr(J_f²)/2 + tr(J_f³)/3 - ...

Each trace term can be estimated with the Hutchinson estimator. Truncate the series and you get a biased but practical estimator. Residual flows use an unbiased estimator based on Russian roulette truncation — randomly decide how many terms to include, and weight them to keep the expectation correct.

The advantage: residual flows are free-form. No coupling structure, no masking. Any Lipschitz-constrained network works. This gives more expressive transformations per layer, potentially requiring fewer layers overall.

Flow Matching: The Modern Approach

Flow matching, introduced by Lipman et al. (2022), is arguably the most important recent development. It connects normalizing flows to diffusion models and dramatically simplifies training.

The core idea: instead of parameterizing the flow through discrete coupling layers, define a continuous-time velocity field that transports the base distribution to the data distribution. But instead of training via the change-of-variables formula (which requires the expensive trace computation of Neural ODEs), you directly regress the velocity field against a target.

The target comes from conditional flow matching. For each training example x₁, define a simple conditional path from the base distribution to that example. The easiest choice is a straight line:

  z_t = (1 - t) · z₀ + t · x₁      where z₀ ~ N(0, I), t ∈ [0, 1]

The velocity along this path is constant: v_target = x₁ - z₀. Train a neural network v_θ(z_t, t) to predict this target velocity:

  L = E_{t, z₀, x₁} || v_θ(z_t, t) - (x₁ - z₀) ||²

That's it. A regression loss. No Jacobians, no determinants, no trace estimators, no ODE solvers during training. Sample t uniformly from [0, 1], sample z₀ from the base distribution, pick a training example x₁, interpolate to get z_t, predict the velocity, compute MSE. Standard deep learning training loop.

At inference time, you do need an ODE solver to integrate the learned velocity field from t=0 to t=1. But the paths are much straighter than those learned by score-based diffusion models, meaning you need fewer integration steps. In practice, flow matching models generate high-quality samples with 20-50 ODE steps, compared to hundreds or thousands for diffusion models.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class VelocityNet(nn.Module):
    """Predicts velocity field v(z_t, t)."""
    def __init__(self, dim, hidden_dim=512):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(dim + 1, hidden_dim),  # +1 for time
            nn.SiLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim),
            nn.SiLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim),
            nn.SiLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, dim),
        )

    def forward(self, z_t, t):
        # t: (batch,) → (batch, 1)
        t_embed = t.unsqueeze(-1)
        inp = torch.cat([z_t, t_embed], dim=-1)
        return self.net(inp)


def flow_matching_loss(model, x1, sigma_min=1e-4):
    """One training step of conditional flow matching."""
    batch_size = x1.shape[0]
    dim = x1.shape[1]

    # Sample time uniformly
    t = torch.rand(batch_size, device=x1.device)

    # Sample base noise
    z0 = torch.randn_like(x1)

    # Interpolate: straight path from z0 to x1
    t_expand = t.unsqueeze(-1)  # (batch, 1)
    z_t = (1 - t_expand) * z0 + t_expand * x1

    # Target velocity: direction along the straight path
    v_target = x1 - z0

    # Predict velocity
    v_pred = model(z_t, t)

    # MSE loss
    loss = ((v_pred - v_target) ** 2).mean()
    return loss


@torch.no_grad()
def sample_flow_matching(model, num_samples, dim, num_steps=50):
    """Generate samples using Euler integration."""
    dt = 1.0 / num_steps
    z = torch.randn(num_samples, dim)

    for i in range(num_steps):
        t = torch.full((num_samples,), i * dt)
        v = model(z, t)
        z = z + v * dt  # Euler step

    return z

Flow matching has become the backbone of state-of-the-art generative models. Stable Diffusion 3 uses it. Meta's Voicebox uses it for speech synthesis. It's replaced traditional normalizing flows in most practical settings because the training is more stable, the paths are straighter (faster sampling), and there are no architectural constraints on the neural network.

The landscape in 2024: Traditional coupling-layer flows (NICE, RealNVP, Glow) are still used for exact density estimation tasks where you need the actual likelihood numbers — anomaly detection, scientific applications, probabilistic modeling. For sample quality at scale (image generation, audio, video), flow matching has won. It offers the best of both worlds: the theoretical elegance of continuous flows with the practical simplicity of regression training.

A Taxonomy of Flow Models

  Normalizing Flows
  │
  ├── Discrete (finite chain of layers)
  │   ├── Coupling Flows
  │   │   ├── NICE (2014)         — additive coupling, det=1
  │   │   ├── RealNVP (2016)      — affine coupling, multi-scale
  │   │   └── Glow (2018)         — + invertible 1×1 conv, actnorm
  │   │
  │   ├── Autoregressive Flows
  │   │   ├── MAF (2017)          — fast density, slow sampling
  │   │   └── IAF (2016)          — fast sampling, slow density
  │   │
  │   └── Residual Flows (2019)   — free-form, Lipschitz-constrained
  │
  └── Continuous (ODE-based)
      ├── Neural ODE / FFJORD (2018-19)  — instantaneous change of vars
      └── Flow Matching (2022+)          — regression on velocity field
                                            (Stable Diffusion 3, etc.)

The thread connecting all of these is the same: transform a simple distribution into a complex one through invertible mappings, and use the change of variables formula to keep track of probabilities. The differences are in how they parameterize those mappings, how they compute (or estimate) the Jacobian determinant, and what tradeoffs they accept between expressiveness, speed, and architectural freedom.

If you're starting a new project today and you need a flow model — reach for flow matching. If you need exact likelihoods on tabular data — RealNVP or a residual flow. If you need fast density evaluation for a VAE posterior — IAF. The right tool depends on which side of the speed/exactness/expressiveness triangle matters most for your problem.
