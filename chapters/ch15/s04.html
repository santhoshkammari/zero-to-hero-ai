Modern Breakthroughs

Chapter 15: Generative Models 7 subtopics
TL;DR

The diffusion world didn't stand still. Consistency models collapse 50+ denoising steps into 1–2. Flow matching straightens the noise-to-data path for faster, simpler training. Diffusion Transformers (DiT) replace U-Net with a transformer backbone that scales predictably — powering DALL·E 3, Stable Diffusion 3, and Sora. Video generation extends the framework to temporal sequences (with enormous compute cost). ControlNet and IP-Adapter give fine-grained spatial and stylistic control without retraining. And Score Distillation Sampling lets 2D diffusion models supervise 3D asset generation. These advances turned diffusion from a research curiosity into the production backbone of generative AI.

Consistency Models — Generation in One Step

Standard diffusion is slow. You start from noise and walk backward through 20, 50, sometimes 1000 denoising steps to get a clean image. Each step is a full neural network forward pass. That's fine for research; it's a problem for production.

Yang Song et al. (2023) asked a sharp question: what if we could skip the walk entirely?

The Core Insight

Think of the diffusion process as drawing a trajectory through space — from clean data x₀, through progressively noisier versions x₁, x₂, …, xT, all the way to pure Gaussian noise. A trained diffusion model can map from any point on that trajectory back to x₀, but it does so incrementally — small steps.

A consistency model learns a function f(xt, t) that maps any point on the trajectory directly to x₀. The key constraint: all points on the same trajectory must map to the same output. That's the self-consistency property:

f(x_t, t) = f(x_t', t')    for any t, t' on the same trajectory

This means you can generate an image in a single forward pass: sample noise z ~ N(0, I), evaluate f(z, T), done. One network call. No iterative denoising.

Two Training Approaches

Consistency distillation starts from a pre-trained diffusion model. You take pairs of adjacent points (xt, xt') on a trajectory (computed using the teacher's denoiser), and train the student to produce the same output for both. The teacher provides the trajectory; the student learns to shortcut it.

Consistency training works from scratch — no pre-trained teacher needed. You enforce the self-consistency property using pairs generated by adding noise at different levels to the same clean image, plus an EMA target network for stability. It's harder to train but avoids the two-stage pipeline.

Latent Consistency Models (LCM)

Luo et al. (2023) made this practical by applying consistency distillation in latent space (on top of Stable Diffusion's VAE). The result: Latent Consistency Models generate good images in 1–4 steps. At 4 steps, quality approaches the 50-step teacher. At 1 step, you get real-time generation — fast enough for interactive applications.

The key trick: LCM uses a one-stage guided distillation that bakes classifier-free guidance directly into the consistency model, so you don't need to run the model twice per step (conditional + unconditional) like standard CFG.

# Pseudo-code: LCM inference (1-4 steps)
z = torch.randn(1, 4, 64, 64)               # noise in latent space
timesteps = [999, 749, 499, 249][:num_steps] # skip schedule

for t in timesteps:
    z = consistency_model(z, t, prompt_emb)  # single model call per step

image = vae.decode(z)                        # decode latent to pixels

LCM-LoRA took it further — a LoRA adapter that turns any fine-tuned Stable Diffusion checkpoint into a few-step generator without full distillation. Plug and play.

Rectified Flow & Flow Matching

Standard diffusion follows curved paths from noise to data. The SDE-based formulation meanders through latent space, which is why you need many steps to trace it accurately. What if the path were a straight line?

The Straight Path Idea

Flow matching (Lipman et al., 2022) and rectified flow (Liu et al., 2022) independently arrived at a beautiful simplification. Instead of learning to reverse a stochastic noise process, learn a velocity field that transports samples from a noise distribution to the data distribution along straight(ish) paths.

The interpolation is dead simple:

# Linear interpolation between noise and data
x_t = (1 - t) * x_0 + t * epsilon    # t ∈ [0, 1]

# The velocity field to learn
v(x_t, t) = epsilon - x_0            # constant along a straight path!

Train a network vθ(xt, t) to predict this velocity using a simple MSE loss. At inference, integrate the ODE dx/dt = vθ(x, t) from t=1 (noise) to t=0 (data). Straight paths mean simple integration — even Euler's method with few steps works well.

Why This Matters

Curved trajectories are harder to integrate numerically. You either need many small steps (slow) or a sophisticated solver (complicated). Straight paths let you get away with 4–8 Euler steps. In practice, flow matching models achieve comparable quality to DDPM at a fraction of the sampling cost, with simpler training code to boot.

Stable Diffusion 3 and FLUX (by Black Forest Labs) both use flow matching as their core framework. The shift from DDPM-style noise schedules to flow matching is one of the biggest practical advances in the field — simpler math, faster sampling, easier to implement.

Reflow: Straightening Further

Rectified flow adds an extra trick: reflow. After training an initial model, you generate (noise, data) pairs by running it forward. Then retrain on these pairs. The paths become even straighter because you're now matching pairs that are already approximately connected by the learned flow. Iterate this process and paths converge toward perfect straight lines — enabling near-single-step generation.

Diffusion Transformers (DiT)

For years, the U-Net was the default backbone for diffusion models. It works — skip connections preserve spatial detail, and the encoder-decoder structure handles multi-scale features naturally. But U-Nets have a ceiling: they don't scale as predictably as transformers, and they're architecturally complex with all those skip connections, attention blocks at specific resolutions, and resolution-dependent channel counts.

The DiT Architecture

Peebles & Xie (2023) proposed Diffusion Transformer (DiT): replace the U-Net entirely with a standard Vision Transformer. The design is surprisingly clean:

  1. Patchify: Split the latent (e.g., 32×32×4 from a VAE) into non-overlapping patches, project each to a token embedding.
  2. Transformer blocks: Standard self-attention + feedforward layers. No skip connections across blocks, no encoder-decoder asymmetry.
  3. Conditioning: Timestep t and class label (or text embedding) are injected via adaptive layer norm (adaLN-Zero) — scale and shift parameters of LayerNorm are predicted from the conditioning signal. This beats cross-attention for conditioning in DiT's design.
  4. Unpatchify: Project tokens back to the latent spatial layout. Predict both noise and diagonal covariance.
# DiT block (simplified)
class DiTBlock(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, dim, num_heads):
        self.norm1 = nn.LayerNorm(dim, elementwise_affine=False)
        self.attn = nn.MultiheadAttention(dim, num_heads)
        self.norm2 = nn.LayerNorm(dim, elementwise_affine=False)
        self.mlp = MLP(dim, dim * 4)
        self.adaLN = nn.Linear(dim, 6 * dim)  # scale, shift for norm1 & norm2 + gate

    def forward(self, x, cond):
        # adaLN-Zero: conditioning modulates normalization
        gamma1, beta1, alpha1, gamma2, beta2, alpha2 = self.adaLN(cond).chunk(6, dim=-1)

        # Self-attention with adaptive norm
        h = self.norm1(x) * (1 + gamma1) + beta1
        x = x + alpha1 * self.attn(h, h, h)

        # Feedforward with adaptive norm
        h = self.norm2(x) * (1 + gamma2) + beta2
        x = x + alpha2 * self.mlp(h)
        return x

Why DiT Wins at Scale

The key result from the DiT paper: compute-quality scaling follows clean power laws, just like language model scaling laws. Double the compute → predictable improvement in FID. This makes architecture and resource decisions much easier than the U-Net world, where scaling required careful per-resolution tuning.

DiT also inherits the entire transformer optimization ecosystem: FlashAttention, tensor parallelism, sequence parallelism, KV caching during iterative denoising. None of these were designed for U-Nets.

Real-world adoption is widespread:

  • DALL·E 3 — Transformer-based diffusion backbone
  • Stable Diffusion 3 / FLUX — MMDiT (multimodal DiT), jointly processes text and image tokens in the same transformer
  • Sora — Spacetime DiT for video, treating video as sequences of spacetime patches
  • Hunyuan-DiT, PixArt-α — Efficient DiT variants for text-to-image

Video Generation

If diffusion can generate a single image, can it generate a sequence of frames that form coherent video? The answer is yes — with caveats.

The Core Challenge

Image generation needs spatial coherence. Video needs spatial and temporal coherence — objects should move smoothly, lighting should stay consistent, a ball thrown in frame 1 should arc correctly through frame 30. This is dramatically harder. You're not just sampling from a distribution of images; you're sampling from a distribution of physically plausible spacetime volumes.

Architectural Approaches

Most video diffusion models extend image architectures with temporal modeling:

  • Temporal attention: Add attention layers that attend across frames at the same spatial position. Interleave with spatial attention (within-frame). This is the most common approach.
  • 3D convolutions / patches: Treat video as a 3D volume. Sora patchifies spacetime — a video becomes a sequence of spacetime tokens, processed by a DiT.
  • Autoregressive + diffusion hybrids: Generate keyframes with diffusion, interpolate between them. Reduces the temporal modeling burden.

The State of the Art (2024–2025)

Sora (OpenAI) demonstrated that scaling a spacetime DiT produces remarkably coherent videos — up to a minute long — with emergent physics understanding. It processes variable-length, variable-resolution videos natively. The approach: compress video with a visual encoder into spacetime patches, apply a large DiT, decode back.

Runway Gen-3, Kling (Kuaishou), and Pika offer production-grade text-to-video and image-to-video. Quality has reached the point where short clips (5–15 seconds) are often indistinguishable from real footage for casual viewing.

Open models like Stable Video Diffusion and CogVideo/CogVideoX have made video generation accessible to researchers, though quality still trails the best closed systems.

What's Still Hard

  • Temporal coherence over long durations: Hands gain or lose fingers. Objects teleport. Physics breaks down after ~10 seconds.
  • Compute cost: A 1-minute Sora video reportedly takes orders of magnitude more compute than a single image. Real-time video generation remains far off for high quality.
  • Controllability: Specifying precise camera motion, character actions, and scene dynamics through text alone is inherently limited.
  • Evaluation: There's no consensus metric — FVD (Fréchet Video Distance) captures statistics but misses temporal artifacts that humans notice instantly.

Image Editing & Control

Generating images from scratch is impressive. But for production workflows, you usually want control — "generate an image like this, but change the lighting," or "fill in this masked region," or "follow this edge map exactly."

ControlNet

Zhang et al. (2023) introduced ControlNet, which adds spatial conditioning to a pre-trained diffusion model without retraining the base model. The trick: clone the encoder of the U-Net (or DiT), feed your control signal (edge map, depth map, pose skeleton, segmentation mask) into the clone, and inject the clone's outputs into the original model via addition.

# ControlNet architecture (conceptual)
# Frozen: original diffusion model weights
# Trainable: cloned encoder + zero convolutions

control_features = controlnet_encoder(control_image)  # e.g., Canny edges
for block, zero_conv, ctrl_feat in zip(unet_decoder, zero_convs, control_features):
    block_input = block_input + zero_conv(ctrl_feat)   # inject control
    block_output = block(block_input)

The zero convolution layers start with all weights at zero, so ControlNet initially has no effect on the base model. As training progresses, it gradually learns to inject the control signal. This preserves the quality of the original model while adding precise spatial control.

ControlNet has spawned an ecosystem: Canny edge control, depth control (MiDaS), pose control (OpenPose), segmentation control, scribble-to-image, and more. Each requires training a separate ControlNet adapter, but the base model stays frozen.

IP-Adapter: Image Prompting

Text is powerful but imprecise. Sometimes you want to say "make it look like this" by providing a reference image. IP-Adapter (Ye et al., 2023) adds image prompt capability to text-to-image models. It encodes a reference image with a CLIP image encoder, projects it through a small adapter network, and injects the result into the cross-attention layers alongside the text embedding.

The clever part: it uses decoupled cross-attention — separate key/value projections for text tokens and image tokens. This lets the model attend to text and image prompts independently without interference.

Inpainting & Outpainting

Inpainting — filling in masked regions of an image — is a natural fit for diffusion. The approach: during denoising, keep the unmasked pixels fixed (or close to fixed) at each step, and only denoise the masked region. The model naturally generates content that blends with the surroundings.

More sophisticated approaches train dedicated inpainting models that take the mask and masked image as additional input channels. Stable Diffusion's inpainting model, for example, has a 9-channel input (4 latent channels + 4 masked latent channels + 1 mask channel).

Text-to-3D

Here's a wild idea: you have a 2D diffusion model that understands what objects look like from every angle (because it was trained on billions of images). Can you use it to create 3D objects, even though it has never seen 3D data?

Score Distillation Sampling (SDS)

DreamFusion (Poole et al., 2022) showed that yes, you can. The technique is called Score Distillation Sampling. The setup:

  1. Initialize a 3D representation (a NeRF — neural radiance field).
  2. Render the 3D object from a random camera angle to get a 2D image.
  3. Ask a pre-trained diffusion model: "Does this 2D rendering look like [text prompt]?" The diffusion model's noise prediction tells you how to improve the rendered image.
  4. Backpropagate that gradient through the renderer into the 3D representation.
  5. Repeat from many camera angles.
# SDS gradient (simplified)
rendered_image = render_nerf(nerf_params, camera_pose)
noisy_image = add_noise(rendered_image, t)
predicted_noise = diffusion_model(noisy_image, t, text_prompt)
actual_noise = noisy_image - rendered_image

# The SDS gradient: difference between predicted and actual noise
grad = predicted_noise - actual_noise
nerf_params -= lr * backprop_through_renderer(grad)

The diffusion model is never fine-tuned. It acts as a frozen critic, guiding the 3D representation toward something that looks good from every viewpoint. Brilliant — but the results tend to be over-saturated and lack fine detail (the "Janus problem" where objects grow multiple faces because the diffusion model is independently satisfied from each view).

Beyond SDS

Variational Score Distillation (VSD) / ProlificDreamer addressed the over-saturation issue with a more principled distillation objective. Magic3D added a coarse-to-fine pipeline (NeRF → mesh refinement). Zero-1-to-3 and One-2-3-45 take a different approach entirely: fine-tune a diffusion model to generate novel views given a single input image, then reconstruct 3D from the generated multi-view images. More recently, large-scale 3D generation models trained on 3D datasets (like Point-E, Shap-E, and Meshy) are reducing dependence on SDS altogether.

The field is moving fast. SDS was the proof-of-concept that 2D priors could bootstrap 3D generation. The endgame is likely large 3D-native models, but the 2D-to-3D distillation approach remains important as a technique and as a conceptual bridge.

Architecture Trends: U-Net vs. DiT

The backbone architecture for diffusion models has shifted dramatically since 2020. Here's where things stand:

The trend is clear: new systems are transformer-based. The U-Net served the field brilliantly for five years, but transformers bring predictable scaling, a mature optimization ecosystem, and natural multimodal integration. If you're starting a new generative model project in 2025, you're almost certainly using a DiT variant.
