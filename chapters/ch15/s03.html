Diffusion Models

Chapter 15: Generative Models 10 subtopics
TL;DR

Slowly destroy an image with noise, then train a neural network to reverse that process. That is the entire idea behind diffusion models — and it is how Stable Diffusion, DALL·E, Midjourney, and nearly every state-of-the-art image generator works. The forward process adds Gaussian noise step by step until the image is pure static. A U-Net learns to predict and remove that noise, one step at a time, guided by text through cross-attention and classifier-free guidance. Running diffusion in a compressed latent space (Latent Diffusion) makes the whole thing practical. The result: training is dead simple (one MSE loss, no adversarial games), quality surpasses GANs, and the framework extends to video, audio, 3D, and molecular design.

The Core Idea

Destroying information is easy. Reconstructing it is hard. Diffusion models exploit exactly this asymmetry.

Take a photograph. Sprinkle a little Gaussian noise on every pixel. The image gets slightly grainy but still recognizable. Sprinkle more. And more. After a thousand rounds, you are staring at pure static — random samples from a standard normal distribution, indistinguishable from TV snow. That progressive corruption is the forward process. It requires zero learning. It is pure math.

Now, here is the insight that launched a thousand startups: if you could learn to undo each tiny corruption step, you could start from pure noise and walk backward to a photorealistic image. A model that can reverse one step of noise addition has, implicitly, learned something deep about what real images look like — it has learned the data distribution. That reversal is the reverse process, and it is the only part that involves a neural network.

Forward (fixed — no learning):
  x₀ ──→ x₁ ──→ x₂ ──→  ...  ──→ x_T
  clean    slightly    more           pure
  image    noisy      noisy          noise

Reverse (learned neural network):
  x_T ──→ x_{T-1} ──→  ...  ──→ x₁ ──→ x₀
  pure     slightly               almost   clean
  noise    less noisy             clean    image

Compared to GANs — which need two networks in a delicate adversarial dance — diffusion is almost anticlimactic. One network. One MSE loss. Stable gradients. And the images it produces are better.

The Forward Process (q)

The forward process is a Markov chain that gradually adds Gaussian noise to the data. At each timestep t, given the previous state xt−1, the next state is:

q(xₜ | xₜ₋₁) = N(xₜ;  √(1 - βₜ) · xₜ₋₁,  βₜ · I)

Unpack this: you scale the signal down by √(1 − βt) and inject Gaussian noise with variance βt. The parameter βt is the noise schedule — a predefined, monotonically increasing sequence, typically from β₁ = 0.0001 to βT = 0.02 over T = 1000 steps. At any single step, the change is barely perceptible. Over a thousand steps, the original signal is completely obliterated.

But here is the property that makes training tractable. You do not need to run the chain sequentially. Define:

αₜ = 1 − βₜ
ᾱₜ = α₁ · α₂ · ... · αₜ  =  ∏ₛ₌₁ᵗ (1 − βₛ)

Then you can jump directly from the clean image x₀ to any arbitrary timestep t in a single operation:

q(xₜ | x₀) = N(xₜ;  √ᾱₜ · x₀,  (1 − ᾱₜ) · I)

Equivalently:   xₜ  =  √ᾱₜ · x₀  +  √(1 − ᾱₜ) · ε     where ε ~ N(0, I)

This is the reparameterization trick in action. √ᾱt controls how much of the original signal remains. √(1 − ᾱt) controls how much noise has accumulated. At t = 0, ᾱ₀ ≈ 1, so x₀ is basically the clean image. At t = T, ᾱT ≈ 0, so xT is pure noise.

Why does this matter practically? During training, you need to create noisy versions of images at random timesteps. Without the closed-form jump, you would need to run 500 sequential noise additions to get x₅₀₀. With it, you compute x₅₀₀ in a single line. This turns training from O(T) per sample into O(1).

The Reverse Process (p)

The reverse process goes from noisy to clean. The true reverse of the forward process is also Gaussian (when βt is small enough), so the model learns:

p_θ(xₜ₋₁ | xₜ) = N(xₜ₋₁;  μ_θ(xₜ, t),  σₜ² · I)

The network needs to parameterize the mean μθ. You could have it directly predict x₀, or predict the mean, or predict the noise ε that was added. The DDPM paper (Ho et al., 2020) showed that predicting the noise works best. The network εθ(xt, t) takes in the noisy image and the timestep and outputs its best guess of the noise that was added.

Given the noise prediction, the reverse step mean becomes:

μ_θ(xₜ, t)  =  (1 / √αₜ) · (xₜ  −  (βₜ / √(1 − ᾱₜ)) · ε_θ(xₜ, t))

And the loss simplifies to something almost comically simple:

L  =  E_{x₀, ε, t}[ ‖ε − ε_θ(xₜ, t)‖² ]

That is it. Mean squared error between the actual noise and the predicted noise. No adversarial objectives. No KL divergence balancing act. No mode collapse. Just a regression problem, and the model trains with the same stability as any supervised learning task.

Why predict noise instead of the clean image? You could train to predict x₀ directly — the math is equivalent. But the noise ε has zero mean and unit variance regardless of the image content, so the prediction target is well-behaved across all timesteps. Predicting x₀ would mean wildly different magnitudes at t = 1 (barely noisy, easy) vs. t = 999 (pure noise, nearly impossible). The noise parameterization keeps the loss scale uniform.

The Training Algorithm

The training loop is remarkably compact. Here it is in full:

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

# Precompute noise schedule quantities
T = 1000
betas = torch.linspace(1e-4, 0.02, T)           # linear schedule
alphas = 1.0 - betas
alpha_bar = torch.cumprod(alphas, dim=0)         # ᾱₜ

def train_step(model, x0, optimizer):
    """One training step for a diffusion model."""
    batch_size = x0.shape[0]

    # 1. Sample random timesteps
    t = torch.randint(0, T, (batch_size,), device=x0.device)

    # 2. Sample random noise
    epsilon = torch.randn_like(x0)

    # 3. Create noisy image (forward process — one-shot)
    ab_t = alpha_bar[t].view(-1, 1, 1, 1).to(x0.device)
    x_t = torch.sqrt(ab_t) * x0 + torch.sqrt(1.0 - ab_t) * epsilon

    # 4. Predict the noise
    epsilon_pred = model(x_t, t)

    # 5. MSE loss between actual and predicted noise
    loss = F.mse_loss(epsilon_pred, epsilon)

    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()

    return loss.item()

# Full training loop
model = UNet(...)  # your noise-prediction network
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=2e-4)

for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for images in dataloader:          # images normalized to [-1, 1]
        loss = train_step(model, images, optimizer)

Five lines of actual logic: sample t, sample ε, compute xt, predict ε, take gradient. Every other generative model wishes its training loop were this clean.

Sampling (Inference)

Sampling is where diffusion gets expensive. You start from pure noise xT ~ N(0, I) and iterate the reverse process for T steps. Each step runs a full forward pass through the U-Net.

@torch.no_grad()
def ddpm_sample(model, shape, device):
    """DDPM sampling: start from noise, denoise T steps."""
    x = torch.randn(shape, device=device)          # pure noise

    for t in reversed(range(T)):
        t_batch = torch.full((shape[0],), t, device=device, dtype=torch.long)
        eps_pred = model(x, t_batch)                # predict noise

        alpha_t = alphas[t]
        ab_t = alpha_bar[t]

        # Reverse step: remove predicted noise
        x = (1.0 / torch.sqrt(alpha_t)) * (
            x - (betas[t] / torch.sqrt(1.0 - ab_t)) * eps_pred
        )

        # Add stochasticity (except at t=0)
        if t > 0:
            z = torch.randn_like(x)
            x = x + torch.sqrt(betas[t]) * z

    return x   # denoised image in [-1, 1]

DDPM with T = 1000 means a thousand sequential neural network forward passes per image. On an A100, that can mean 10–30 seconds for a single 512×512 image. For a product serving millions of users, this is a non-starter. The race for faster sampling has been one of the most productive areas in diffusion research.

DDIM — Deterministic, Fewer Steps

DDIM (Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models, Song et al. 2020) reinterprets the reverse process as an ordinary differential equation (ODE) rather than a stochastic process. The key result: you can skip timesteps. Instead of denoising 1000 → 999 → 998 → ..., you jump 1000 → 980 → 960 → ..., using a subsequence of only 50–100 steps. DDIM is also deterministic — same starting noise, same output — which enables interpolation in the noise space and consistent editing.

DPM-Solver — High-Order ODE Solvers

DPM-Solver (Lu et al. 2022) applies the same insight but uses high-order ODE solvers (analogous to Runge-Kutta methods from numerical analysis). DPM-Solver++ achieves excellent results in 10–20 steps, making real-time diffusion applications genuinely feasible.

Progressive Distillation

A different approach entirely: train a student model to match the output of two teacher steps in a single step. After one round, the student takes 500 steps. Distill again: 250. Again: 125. A few rounds later, you have a 4-step or even 1-step model that approximates the original 1000-step teacher. This is how systems like SDXL Turbo achieve near-instant generation.

Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG)

This is THE technique that made text-to-image work. Without it, conditional diffusion models produce images that vaguely relate to the prompt. With it, they produce images that nail the prompt.

The earlier approach, classifier guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), trained a separate classifier on noisy images and used its gradients to steer diffusion toward a target class. It worked but required maintaining a separate model and only supported class labels, not free-form text.

Classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022) is simpler and far more powerful. The trick: during training, randomly drop the conditioning (the text prompt) some fraction of the time — typically 10–20% — replacing it with a null/empty embedding. This forces the model to learn both conditional and unconditional generation in a single network.

At inference, you run the model twice per denoising step:

# Two forward passes per step
ε_uncond = model(x_t, t, cond=∅)         # unconditional: no text
ε_cond   = model(x_t, t, cond=prompt)    # conditional: with text

# Guided prediction: amplify the difference
ε_guided = ε_uncond + w · (ε_cond − ε_uncond)

The guidance scale w controls how much the model exaggerates features that distinguish "with prompt" from "without prompt." Think of it as a confidence amplifier:

Guidance scale wBehavior
w = 1.0Standard conditional generation (no guidance)
w = 3–5Mild guidance — diverse, occasionally off-prompt
w = 7–12Sweet spot for most applications (Stable Diffusion default: 7.5)
w = 15–30Strong guidance — very faithful to prompt, reduced diversity
w > 30Oversaturated, artifacted — model tries too hard

The tradeoff is fidelity vs. diversity. High w means every image strongly matches the prompt, but they all start looking similar. Low w gives variety but weaker adherence. This is directly analogous to the temperature parameter in language models.

@torch.no_grad()
def sample_cfg(model, prompt_emb, null_emb, shape, device, w=7.5):
    """Full sampling loop with classifier-free guidance."""
    x = torch.randn(shape, device=device)

    for t in reversed(range(T)):
        t_batch = torch.full((shape[0],), t, device=device, dtype=torch.long)

        # Two forward passes
        eps_uncond = model(x, t_batch, cond=null_emb)
        eps_cond   = model(x, t_batch, cond=prompt_emb)

        # CFG formula
        eps = eps_uncond + w * (eps_cond - eps_uncond)

        # Standard reverse step
        alpha_t = alphas[t]
        ab_t = alpha_bar[t]
        x = (1 / torch.sqrt(alpha_t)) * (
            x - (betas[t] / torch.sqrt(1 - ab_t)) * eps
        )
        if t > 0:
            x += torch.sqrt(betas[t]) * torch.randn_like(x)

    return x
Negative prompts fall out of CFG for free. Instead of using the null embedding for εuncond, substitute the embedding of things you want to avoid ("blurry, low quality, deformed hands"). The guidance formula then pushes the output away from the negative prompt and toward the positive one. This is why negative prompts sharpen outputs — it is not a hack, it is a direct consequence of the math.

Latent Diffusion / Stable Diffusion

A 512×512 RGB image has 786,432 dimensions. Running a thousand U-Net forward passes in that space requires enormous compute and memory. This is the gap between "cool research paper" and "product that ships."

Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) (Rombach et al. 2022) solve this with a beautifully simple two-stage approach:

  1. Stage 1: Train an autoencoder. A VQ-VAE or KL-regularized VAE compresses images into a much smaller latent space. A 512×512×3 image becomes a 64×64×4 latent tensor — a 48× reduction in dimensionality. Train this once, then freeze it.
  2. Stage 2: Run diffusion in latent space. The entire noise/denoise pipeline operates on 64×64×4 tensors instead of 512×512×3 pixels. Same algorithm, massively cheaper compute.
Latent Diffusion Pipeline:

  "A cat astronaut on the moon, oil painting"

      ▼
  ┌──────────────────┐
  │ Text Encoder      │  (CLIP ViT-L/14, frozen)
  │ → 77 × 768 tokens │
  └────────┬─────────┘
           │ cross-attention
           ▼
  z_T ──→ ┌─────────────────────┐ ──→ z₀
  noise    │  U-Net Denoiser      │     clean latent
  64×64×4  │  (self-attn + cross- │     64×64×4
           │   attn + time emb)   │
           └─────────────────────┘
                                        │
                                        ▼
                               ┌──────────────┐
                               │ VAE Decoder   │  (frozen)
                               │ 64×64×4 → 512×512×3 │

The compute savings are dramatic. Diffusing a 64×64×4 latent is 48× fewer dimensions than a 512×512×3 image. The U-Net is smaller. Memory fits on consumer GPUs. Training time drops from "months on a cluster" to "days on 8 A100s." This is what turned diffusion from a research curiosity into a deployable technology.

The VAE is trained with a combination of pixel-level reconstruction loss, a perceptual loss (comparing VGG features of the original and reconstruction), and a KL penalty to keep the latent distribution close to a standard normal. A patch discriminator adds sharpness. The resulting latent space captures semantically meaningful features rather than raw pixel statistics — edges, textures, shapes live in this compressed representation.

Stable Diffusion is Latent Diffusion made concrete and shipped to the world. Released by CompVis, Stability AI, and Runway in August 2022, it put high-quality image generation on consumer hardware. SD v1.5 uses CLIP ViT-L/14 as the text encoder (~77 × 768 embeddings), an 860M-parameter U-Net operating at 64×64×4 latent resolution, and generates 512×512 images. SDXL (2023) scales this to dual text encoders (CLIP ViT-L + OpenCLIP ViT-bigG), a 2.6B U-Net, and 1024×1024 output resolution.

The U-Net Architecture for Diffusion

Why U-Net? The classic encoder-decoder architecture from image segmentation turns out to be almost perfectly designed for denoising. Here is what makes it work for diffusion:

Skip connections preserve spatial detail. The encoder compresses the image into a low-resolution bottleneck (good for global reasoning), while skip connections carry fine-grained details directly from the encoder to the decoder (good for textures and edges). For denoising, you need both: global understanding of what the image should look like and local precision to place every pixel.

Timestep embeddings tell the model which noise level it is dealing with. The integer timestep t is mapped to a vector using sinusoidal positional embeddings (borrowed from Transformers), then projected through an MLP. This vector is injected into every residual block via adaptive group normalization — the block scales and shifts its features based on the timestep. At t = 999, the model knows the input is nearly pure noise and should hallucinate global structure. At t = 5, it knows the input is almost clean and should focus on fine details.

Cross-attention layers inject text conditioning. At each spatial resolution in the U-Net, there are cross-attention layers where image features form the queries and text embeddings form the keys and values. This is exactly how language tells each spatial location what to generate. When the prompt says "red hat," the cross-attention at the hat's spatial location attends strongly to the "red" and "hat" tokens.

U-Net for Latent Diffusion (simplified):

  Input: z_t (64×64×4) + timestep t + text embedding c

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Encoder                    Decoder            │
  │                                                │
  │ 64×64 ── ResBlock+SA ──── skip ──→ ResBlock+SA│
  │          ↓ downsample             ↑ upsample  │
  │ 32×32 ── ResBlock+CA ──── skip ──→ ResBlock+CA│
  │          ↓ downsample             ↑ upsample  │
  │ 16×16 ── ResBlock+CA ──── skip ──→ ResBlock+CA│
  │          ↓ downsample             ↑ upsample  │
  │  8×8  ── ResBlock+CA ────────────→ ResBlock+CA│
  │               │                                │
  │         ┌─────┴──────┐                         │
  │         │  Bottleneck │                         │
  │         │  ResBlock+SA│                         │
  │         └────────────┘                         │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SA = Self-Attention    CA = Cross-Attention (to text)
  Every ResBlock receives timestep embedding via AdaGN

  Output: predicted noise ε (64×64×4)

Self-attention at the highest resolution (64×64) is expensive — that is 4096 tokens attending to each other. Production U-Nets typically use self-attention only at lower resolutions (32×32 and below), where it is computationally tractable and the model benefits most from global context. Cross-attention to the text embedding (77 tokens) is cheap at all resolutions since the key/value sequence length is small.

Noise Schedules

The noise schedule — the sequence of βt values — has an outsized impact on sample quality. Think of it as the curriculum for your denoiser.

The original DDPM used a linear schedule: β increases linearly from 0.0001 to 0.02 across 1000 steps. The problem? It destroys too much information too quickly in the early-to-middle steps. By step 400–500, the image is already nearly indistinguishable from pure noise. The model wastes half its capacity on the boring task of denoising near-identical noise distributions.

The cosine schedule (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021) fixes this by defining ᾱt as a cosine curve, ensuring signal-to-noise ratio drops smoothly rather than falling off a cliff:

import math

def cosine_alpha_bar(t, T, s=0.008):
    """Cosine schedule: ᾱₜ follows a shifted cosine."""
    f_t = math.cos(((t / T) + s) / (1 + s) * math.pi / 2) ** 2
    f_0 = math.cos((s / (1 + s)) * math.pi / 2) ** 2
    return f_t / f_0

The small offset s = 0.008 prevents βt from being too small near t = 0. With this schedule, the model spends roughly equal capacity learning to denoise at every noise level, from nearly-clean to nearly-noise.

A good way to reason about schedules is through the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR): SNR(t) = ᾱt / (1 − ᾱt). A well-designed schedule has log-SNR that decreases roughly linearly from +∞ (clean) to −∞ (noise). Some recent work learns the schedule as part of training, parameterizing it as a small monotonic neural network.

ScheduleBehaviorBest for
LinearDestroys information too fast in middle stepsHigh-res images (512+) where early destruction is less harmful
CosineSmooth, gradual signal decayLow/medium resolution (32–256), more balanced training
LearnedAdapts to data distributionResearch settings, can squeeze out extra quality

Production Considerations

Getting diffusion models from notebooks into products introduces challenges you do not face in research.

Inference Cost

Every image generation requires 20–50 forward passes through a multi-billion-parameter U-Net. At scale, this means GPU-seconds per image. Compare to GANs, which need a single forward pass. The cost is the primary reason diffusion models are expensive to serve. Mitigation strategies: faster samplers (DPM-Solver++), model distillation (SDXL Turbo, LCM), smaller architectures, quantization (INT8 U-Net), and batching across users.

Distillation for Speed

Consistency Models (Song et al. 2023) learn to map any point on the diffusion trajectory directly to x₀, enabling single-step generation. Latent Consistency Models (LCM) apply this to latent diffusion, achieving good quality in 2–4 steps. SDXL Turbo uses adversarial distillation to produce quality images in 1–4 steps. These approaches trade slight quality degradation for 50–250× speedup.

ControlNet for Spatial Conditioning

Text alone cannot specify spatial layout. ControlNet (Zhang et al. 2023) adds structural control — pose skeletons, depth maps, edge maps, segmentation masks. It clones the encoder half of the U-Net, trains the clone on paired data (e.g., edges → images), and connects it back to the frozen original via zero-initialized convolutions. The frozen model retains all generative knowledge; the trainable copy learns to incorporate the spatial signal. Because connections start at zero, training begins from a working model.

Img2Img and Inpainting

The img2img pipeline starts from an existing image instead of pure noise. Encode the input into latent space, add noise up to a chosen timestep (controlled by a "strength" parameter, 0.0–1.0), then denoise with text guidance. Low strength ≈ minor edits. High strength ≈ the model reimagines the composition. Inpainting masks a region and only denoises within the mask, keeping surrounding pixels intact.

Fine-Tuning and Personalization

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) inserts small trainable matrices (rank 4–64) alongside each attention layer. A LoRA adapter is 10–100 MB — small enough to swap at inference time. You can combine multiple LoRAs (one for style, one for character) with weighted merging. DreamBooth fine-tunes the full U-Net on 3–5 images of a specific subject with a prior preservation loss. Textual Inversion only learns a new text embedding vector — lightweight (a few KB) but limited.

Architectural Evolution: U-Net → Transformer

The latest generation of diffusion models replaces U-Net with a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) — a plain Vision Transformer operating on patchified latent tokens. DALL·E 3, Stable Diffusion 3, and Sora all use Transformer-based backbones. The benefits: Transformers scale more predictably (scaling laws apply), attention across all patches gives global coherence, and the ecosystem of Transformer optimization tricks (FlashAttention, KV caching, tensor parallelism) translates directly.
