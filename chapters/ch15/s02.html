Generative Adversarial Networks

Chapter 15: Generative Models 6 subtopics
TL;DR

GANs pit two networks against each other — a generator that creates fake data and a discriminator that tries to catch it. This adversarial game produces stunningly sharp images, but the training dynamics are notoriously unstable: mode collapse, oscillation, and delicate hyperparameter balancing plagued practitioners for years. Variants like WGAN and StyleGAN patched many issues, and GANs dominated image generation from 2014–2021. Then diffusion models solved the fundamental problems GANs couldn't, and took the crown. GANs still matter for real-time generation and data augmentation.

The Adversarial Game

Ian Goodfellow's 2014 insight was elegantly simple: train two networks simultaneously, with opposing objectives. The generator G takes random noise z and produces a fake sample G(z). The discriminator D receives both real samples from the training data and fakes from G, and outputs a probability that the input is real.

The classic analogy: G is a counterfeiter trying to produce convincing fake bills, D is a detective trying to spot them. As the detective gets better at catching fakes, the counterfeiter improves technique. As the counterfeiter improves, the detective must sharpen skills. Both improve through competition.

  Random noise z ~ N(0,1)
        │
   ┌────▼────┐
   │Generator │  G(z) → fake image
   │    G     │
   └────┬────┘
        │
        ▼
   ┌─────────┐      Real images x ~ p_data
   │Discrim- │◄─────────────┘
   │inator D │
   └────┬────┘
        │
   D(x) → P(real)     Goal: D(real)→1, D(fake)→0
                       Goal of G: make D(G(z))→1

The formal objective is a minimax game:

  min_G max_D  V(D, G) = E_x[log D(x)] + E_z[log(1 - D(G(z)))]

D wants to maximize this: push D(x) toward 1 for real data (making log D(x) large) and D(G(z)) toward 0 for fakes (making log(1 − D(G(z))) large). G wants to minimize it: make D(G(z)) close to 1 so log(1 − D(G(z))) becomes very negative.

Nash equilibrium. In theory, training converges when D can't tell real from fake — D(x) = 0.5 for everything. At this point, G has learned the true data distribution p_data. In practice, reaching this equilibrium is extremely difficult, which is the central challenge of GANs.

In practice, the generator loss is often swapped to −log D(G(z)) instead of log(1 − D(G(z))). Why? Early in training, when G produces garbage, D easily rejects it and D(G(z)) ≈ 0. The gradient of log(1 − D(G(z))) near 0 is tiny — the generator gets almost no learning signal. The −log D(G(z)) formulation provides stronger gradients early on, giving G a fighting chance to improve.

The GAN Training Loop

Here's the skeleton that every GAN implementation follows. Two optimizers, alternating updates, and the critical detail: you train D more reliably than G.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

# Generator: noise → image
G = Generator()    # z (latent_dim,) → image (C, H, W)
D = Discriminator() # image (C, H, W) → scalar (real/fake)

opt_G = torch.optim.Adam(G.parameters(), lr=2e-4, betas=(0.5, 0.999))
opt_D = torch.optim.Adam(D.parameters(), lr=2e-4, betas=(0.5, 0.999))
criterion = nn.BCEWithLogitsLoss()

for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for real_imgs, _ in dataloader:
        batch_size = real_imgs.size(0)
        real_label = torch.ones(batch_size, 1)
        fake_label = torch.zeros(batch_size, 1)

        # ---- Train Discriminator ----
        z = torch.randn(batch_size, latent_dim)
        fake_imgs = G(z).detach()  # detach: don't backprop into G

        loss_D_real = criterion(D(real_imgs), real_label)
        loss_D_fake = criterion(D(fake_imgs), fake_label)
        loss_D = (loss_D_real + loss_D_fake) / 2

        opt_D.zero_grad()
        loss_D.backward()
        opt_D.step()

        # ---- Train Generator ----
        z = torch.randn(batch_size, latent_dim)
        fake_imgs = G(z)
        # G wants D to think fakes are real
        loss_G = criterion(D(fake_imgs), real_label)

        opt_G.zero_grad()
        loss_G.backward()
        opt_G.step()

Key details: the .detach() call when training D prevents gradients from flowing back into G — we only want to update D's weights at that step. When training G, we do not detach, because G needs gradients through D to learn what "fools" the discriminator. The betas=(0.5, 0.999) in Adam is a widely-adopted GAN trick — lower momentum stabilizes training.

Training Dynamics: Why GANs Are Hard

If you've only read about GANs, you might think training is straightforward. It's not. GANs are the most finicky models to train in all of deep learning. Here's why.

Mode Collapse

This is the signature GAN failure mode. The generator discovers that producing one particular output (or a small set of outputs) reliably fools the discriminator, and just keeps producing that. Ask for 1000 face samples and you get the same face 1000 times, maybe with tiny variations.

Why does it happen? The generator is optimizing to fool D right now, not to cover the full data distribution. If one mode of the data is particularly easy to imitate, G overexploits it. D eventually catches on and G shifts to another mode — creating an oscillation where G cycles through modes without ever covering them all simultaneously.

Mode collapse in practice. You're training a GAN on CIFAR-10 (10 classes). You check generated samples and notice: great-looking trucks, decent cars, and... nothing else. Eight classes are completely missing. The generator found that trucks and cars are "enough" to keep the discriminator confused and stopped exploring.

Non-Convergence and Oscillation

The minimax game has no guarantee of convergence. Unlike a standard loss function that decreases monotonically, GAN training is a two-player game where both players are adapting. D gets better, then G responds, then D responds again. The loss curves oscillate rather than decrease, and there's no clear "we're done" signal.

Monitoring GAN training is an art. Loss values alone are nearly meaningless — D loss going down doesn't mean things are improving if G loss is going up. Practitioners typically monitor generated sample quality visually and use FID scores periodically.

The Balance Problem

If D becomes too strong too fast, it rejects everything G produces with near-100% confidence. The gradients flowing back to G become vanishingly small (because D's output is saturated), and G stops learning. If G somehow gets ahead and fools D completely, D's gradients become meaningless too. You need both networks to improve at roughly the same pace — a delicate balancing act that depends heavily on architecture, learning rates, and batch size.

Common hacks that help: train D for k steps per G step (usually k=1 or k=5), use label smoothing (replace 1.0 with 0.9 for real labels), add noise to D's inputs early in training.

Key GAN Variants

The original GAN paper spawned a zoo of variants. Most are historical curiosities, but a few solved real problems:

VariantKey InnovationWhat It Solved
DCGAN (2015)Convolutional architecture with batch norm, strided convolutions instead of poolingMade GANs work reliably on images. Established architectural best practices (no fully-connected layers, use transposed convolutions in G).
WGAN (2017)Wasserstein distance instead of JS divergence; critic instead of discriminatorTraining stability. The Wasserstein loss provides meaningful gradients even when distributions don't overlap, fixing the vanishing gradient problem. Loss actually correlates with sample quality.
WGAN-GP (2017)Gradient penalty instead of weight clippingWGAN's weight clipping caused capacity issues. Gradient penalty enforces the Lipschitz constraint more cleanly.
StyleGAN (2018)Style-based generator with mapping network, adaptive instance normalization, progressive growingHigh-resolution face synthesis. Disentangled control over coarse (pose, shape) and fine (hair texture, freckles) attributes.
Pix2Pix (2016)Paired image-to-image translation with conditional adversarial loss + L1 reconstructionSketch→photo, segmentation map→street scene, day→night. Requires paired training data.
CycleGAN (2017)Cycle-consistency loss for unpaired translationHorse→zebra, photo→painting without paired examples. Enforces that translating A→B→A recovers the original.

WGAN deserves a bit more attention because it addressed the most fundamental issue. The original GAN loss is related to the Jensen-Shannon divergence between real and generated distributions. When these distributions have non-overlapping support (which is almost always the case in high dimensions early in training), JS divergence is constant and provides zero gradient. The Wasserstein distance (earth mover's distance) is smooth even when distributions don't overlap — it measures how much "work" it takes to morph one distribution into the other. This single change made training dramatically more stable and gave practitioners a loss curve that actually means something.

Conditional GANs

Vanilla GANs give you no control over what gets generated — you sample noise and get a random output. Conditional GANs (cGANs) fix this by feeding additional information to both G and D.

The simplest version: concatenate a class label (one-hot encoded) to the noise vector for G, and to the image for D. Now G learns to produce images conditioned on class, and D learns to judge whether an image is a realistic example of that class.

  Conditional GAN objective:
  min_G max_D  E_x[log D(x|y)] + E_z[log(1 - D(G(z|y)|y))]

  where y = conditioning variable (class label, text, image, etc.)

This idea extends far beyond class labels. Pix2Pix conditions on an input image. Text-to-image GANs condition on text embeddings. Super-resolution GANs condition on a low-resolution input. The conditioning mechanism is the same — feed the extra information to both networks.

Why Diffusion Won

From 2014 to roughly 2021, GANs were the undisputed kings of image generation. Then diffusion models (DDPM, and later Stable Diffusion, DALL-E 2) took over. It wasn't close. Here's why:

Mode collapse vs. full coverage. GANs optimize a game that has no incentive to cover the entire data distribution. Diffusion models optimize a proper likelihood-based objective that naturally covers all modes. Generate 10,000 samples from a diffusion model and you'll see the full diversity of the training data.

Training stability. Diffusion models train a single denoising network with a simple MSE loss. No adversarial dynamics, no balancing act, no hyperparameter sensitivity. Training is as stable as training a classifier. GANs require babysitting.

Likelihood computation. GANs provide no way to evaluate the probability of a given sample under the model. Diffusion models (through the connection to score matching) provide approximate log-likelihoods. This matters for evaluation, comparison, and certain downstream applications.

Controllability. Classifier-free guidance in diffusion models gives fine-grained, stable control over generation. GAN conditioning works but is harder to tune and less flexible.

The core tradeoff. GANs produce sharp images in a single forward pass. Diffusion models produce diverse, high-quality images but require hundreds of denoising steps. GANs traded diversity for speed; diffusion models made the opposite trade — and the field decided diversity and stability mattered more.

Where GANs Still Matter

GANs aren't dead. They've found niches where their strengths — fast, single-pass inference — outweigh their weaknesses.

Real-time generation. A GAN generates an image in one forward pass (~10-50ms). A diffusion model needs 20-50 denoising steps. For applications like real-time video enhancement, game asset generation, or interactive editing, GANs remain practical. Distillation techniques are closing this gap, but GANs still win on raw latency.

Data augmentation. Need to expand a small medical imaging dataset? Train a GAN to generate synthetic pathology images. Mode collapse matters less here because you're supplementing real data, not replacing it. The sharp outputs are actually an advantage — blurry augmented images don't help.

Super-resolution. ESRGAN and Real-ESRGAN remain state-of-the-art for single-image super-resolution in production. The adversarial loss produces perceptually sharp upscaling that MSE-based methods can't match.

Image editing. GAN inversion — projecting a real image into the latent space of a trained GAN — enables precise semantic editing. StyleGAN's disentangled latent space lets you change a face's age, expression, or hair style by moving along specific latent directions.

Distillation targets. Ironically, one of the most effective ways to speed up diffusion models is to distill them into GAN-like single-step generators (e.g., consistency distillation, adversarial diffusion distillation). The adversarial loss is too useful to abandon entirely.

Historical perspective. GANs were the breakthrough that proved neural networks could generate photorealistic images. Every generative model since — including diffusion — builds on intuitions and techniques developed during the GAN era. Understanding GANs isn't just history; it's understanding the foundations that current methods stand on.
