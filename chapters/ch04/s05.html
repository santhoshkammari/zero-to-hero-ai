Cross-Validation

Chapter 4: ML Fundamentals & Core Concepts 3 subtopics
TL;DR

A single train/test split is like flipping a coin once and calling it fair. Cross-validation trains and tests your model multiple times on different slices of data, giving you a much more honest picture of how it will perform on unseen examples. K-Fold is the workhorse. Stratified K-Fold handles imbalanced classes. Time Series Split respects temporal order. Nested CV separates hyperparameter tuning from evaluation. Report the mean and standard deviation — mean alone hides instability.

Why Cross-Validation

The Problem with a Single Split

Imagine you have 1,000 patient records and you want to predict whether a tumor is malignant. You split the data 80/20 — train on 800, test on 200. You get 94% accuracy. Ship it?

Not so fast. What if, by sheer luck, most of the tricky borderline cases landed in the training set? Your test set got the easy ones, so 94% looks great but means little. Re-shuffle the data, split again, and you might get 88%. Or 96%. That swing is not because your model changed. The data changed.

This is the limitation of a single train/test split: your performance estimate depends heavily on which examples end up where. With small datasets — anything under a few thousand rows — this randomness can dominate. You are measuring the luck of the draw, not the quality of the model.

More Reliable Performance Estimates

Cross-validation (CV) solves this by repeating the split-train-test cycle multiple times, each time using a different chunk of data as the test set. Instead of one number, you get several. Their average is a far more stable estimate of real-world performance, and their spread (standard deviation) tells you how sensitive your model is to which data it sees.

Think of it like this: a single test score is an anecdote. Cross-validation gives you a distribution. Distributions are harder to lie with.

Better Use of Limited Data

There is a second, more practical reason. When data is scarce, locking away 20% for testing feels wasteful. In 5-fold CV, every example gets used for testing exactly once and for training four times. You evaluate on all the data without ever testing on something you trained on. That is a much better deal for small datasets.

Cross-Validation Techniques

Hold-Out Validation

Hold-out validation is the baseline: split once into training and test (and optionally a validation set for tuning). It is fast and fine when you have hundreds of thousands of examples. With big data, the randomness of a single split averages out naturally. But with small or medium datasets, hold-out is unreliable. It is the starting point, not the finish line.

K-Fold Cross-Validation

K-Fold CV divides your dataset into k equally-sized chunks called folds. In each round, one fold becomes the test set and the remaining k−1 folds become the training set. You train from scratch each round, record the test score, and at the end average all k scores. The standard choice is k=5 or k=10.

Here is how it works in scikit-learn:

from sklearn.model_selection import KFold, cross_val_score
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.datasets import load_breast_cancer
import numpy as np

X, y = load_breast_cancer(return_X_y=True)

model = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=100, random_state=42)

# 5-Fold Cross-Validation
kf = KFold(n_splits=5, shuffle=True, random_state=42)
scores = cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=kf, scoring='accuracy')

print(f"Fold scores: {scores}")
print(f"Mean accuracy: {scores.mean():.4f} ± {scores.std():.4f}")
# Fold scores: [0.9649 0.9561 0.9474 0.9649 0.9558]
# Mean accuracy: 0.9578 ± 0.0068

Notice the shuffle=True argument. Without it, the folds follow the original row order, which can be disastrous if the data was sorted by class label. Always shuffle unless your data has a temporal component (more on that below).

⚠️ Common Pitfall

Setting shuffle=True without a random_state means you get different folds every time you run the code. That makes results unreproducible. Always pin the random seed when you want comparable experiments.

Stratified K-Fold

Suppose your dataset has 90% negative examples and 10% positive. A regular K-Fold split might, by chance, stuff most positives into one or two folds. The folds that got few positives would train a model that barely knows the positive class exists.

Stratified K-Fold prevents this. It ensures each fold has approximately the same class distribution as the full dataset. If 10% of your data is positive, each fold will also be roughly 10% positive. For classification tasks, this should be your default.

from sklearn.model_selection import StratifiedKFold

# Stratified preserves class proportions in each fold
skf = StratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, shuffle=True, random_state=42)
scores = cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=skf, scoring='accuracy')

print(f"Stratified fold scores: {scores}")
print(f"Mean accuracy: {scores.mean():.4f} ± {scores.std():.4f}")
# Stratified fold scores: [0.9561 0.9737 0.9561 0.9649 0.9469]
# Mean accuracy: 0.9596 ± 0.0090

In scikit-learn, when you pass an integer to cv= for a classifier, it automatically uses Stratified K-Fold. But being explicit is better than being clever.

Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOOCV)

LOOCV is K-Fold taken to the extreme: k equals the number of samples. Each round, you train on every example except one, then test on that single held-out example. You repeat this for every example in the dataset.

The upside: maximum training data per fold, and the result is deterministic — no randomness from the split. The downside: it is brutally expensive. If you have 10,000 examples, you train 10,000 models. It also tends to have high variance because each training set differs by only a single example. For most practical work, 5-fold or 10-fold gives better tradeoffs. LOOCV makes sense mainly for very small datasets (under ~100 samples) where every data point matters.

Repeated K-Fold

Repeated K-Fold runs the entire K-Fold procedure multiple times, each time with a different random shuffle. If you do 10 repeats of 5-fold, you get 50 scores. This reduces the variance of your performance estimate further and is especially useful when you need a tight confidence interval — say, to decide if Model A is genuinely better than Model B by a slim margin.

from sklearn.model_selection import RepeatedStratifiedKFold

# 10 repeats × 5 folds = 50 evaluation rounds
rskf = RepeatedStratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, n_repeats=10, random_state=42)
scores = cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=rskf, scoring='accuracy')

print(f"50 scores, mean: {scores.mean():.4f} ± {scores.std():.4f}")

Nested Cross-Validation

Here is a subtle but important trap. Suppose you use 5-fold CV to tune your hyperparameters — trying different values of, say, the number of trees and max depth. You pick the combination with the best average CV score. Now you report that score as your model's expected performance. Is that honest?

No. You selected the hyperparameters that happened to score highest on those particular folds. Your reported score is optimistically biased — it overestimates how well the model will do on truly unseen data. This is the same problem as evaluating on your training set, one level up.

Nested cross-validation fixes this with two loops. The outer loop splits data into train and test folds for unbiased evaluation. The inner loop runs inside the outer training set to tune hyperparameters. The outer test fold is never touched during tuning, so the outer scores are honest.

from sklearn.model_selection import GridSearchCV, cross_val_score
from sklearn.model_selection import StratifiedKFold

# Inner loop: tunes hyperparameters
param_grid = {
    'n_estimators': [50, 100, 200],
    'max_depth': [3, 5, 10, None]
}
inner_cv = StratifiedKFold(n_splits=3, shuffle=True, random_state=42)
grid_search = GridSearchCV(
    RandomForestClassifier(random_state=42),
    param_grid,
    cv=inner_cv,
    scoring='accuracy',
    n_jobs=-1
)

# Outer loop: evaluates generalization performance
outer_cv = StratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, shuffle=True, random_state=42)
nested_scores = cross_val_score(grid_search, X, y, cv=outer_cv, scoring='accuracy')

print(f"Nested CV scores: {nested_scores}")
print(f"Unbiased estimate: {nested_scores.mean():.4f} ± {nested_scores.std():.4f}")
# Nested CV scores: [0.9561 0.9649 0.9561 0.9649 0.9558]
# Unbiased estimate: 0.9596 ± 0.0043
💡 When to Use Nested CV

Use nested CV whenever you are both tuning hyperparameters and reporting final performance in the same experiment. If you have a completely separate held-out test set that you never use for any decisions, a single-level CV for tuning is fine. But if your paper or report quotes the CV score as "expected performance," it must come from the outer loop of a nested procedure.

Time Series Split

All the methods above assume your data points are independent — shuffling them is fine. Time series data violates this assumption hard. If you are predicting tomorrow's stock price, you cannot train on next month's data and test on last week's. That is data leakage (using future information to predict the past), and it makes your model look spectacular in evaluation but useless in production.

Time Series Split respects temporal order. It creates expanding training windows: fold 1 trains on months 1-2 and tests on month 3, fold 2 trains on months 1-3 and tests on month 4, and so on. The training set always precedes the test set in time.

from sklearn.model_selection import TimeSeriesSplit

# 5 splits with expanding training window
tscv = TimeSeriesSplit(n_splits=5)
for i, (train_idx, test_idx) in enumerate(tscv.split(X)):
    print(f"Fold {i}: Train size={len(train_idx)}, Test size={len(test_idx)}")

Use Time Series Split for any data where rows have a natural ordering that matters: financial data, sensor readings, log events, weather forecasts.

Group K-Fold

Sometimes your data has groups that should not be split across train and test. Medical imaging is a classic example: one patient might contribute 20 scans. If some scans from Patient A are in the training set and others are in the test set, your model can "recognize" Patient A rather than learning the actual medical pattern. Performance looks great but will not generalize to new patients.

Group K-Fold ensures all data from the same group stays together in the same fold. You specify a groups array that labels which group each row belongs to.

from sklearn.model_selection import GroupKFold
import numpy as np

# Simulating 100 samples from 20 patients (5 samples each)
groups = np.repeat(np.arange(20), 5)

gkf = GroupKFold(n_splits=5)
for i, (train_idx, test_idx) in enumerate(gkf.split(X[:100], y[:100], groups)):
    train_groups = np.unique(groups[train_idx])
    test_groups = np.unique(groups[test_idx])
    print(f"Fold {i}: Train groups={train_groups}, Test groups={test_groups}")

Other examples where Group K-Fold matters: multiple essays from the same student, multiple transactions from the same user, multiple frames from the same video.

Choosing the Right Technique

With eight techniques on the table, here is how to pick:

Start with Stratified K-Fold (k=5) as your default for classification. It handles imbalanced classes correctly and runs in reasonable time. For regression, use plain K-Fold since there are no class labels to stratify over (though some libraries offer stratified regression splits by binning the target).

If your data has a time component — dates, timestamps, sequential events — switch to Time Series Split. No exceptions. Shuffling temporal data is one of the most common sources of inflated metrics in industry.

If your data has natural groups (patients, users, documents from the same author), use Group K-Fold. Ignoring group structure leads to data leakage through group memorization.

If you are tuning hyperparameters and reporting performance in the same experiment, wrap everything in Nested CV. If you need high-confidence comparisons between models, use Repeated K-Fold. If your dataset is tiny (under 100 samples), consider LOOCV. If your dataset is massive (100k+ rows), hold-out is fine — the variance from a single split becomes negligible.

Interpreting CV Results

Mean and Standard Deviation of Scores

Always report both. A model with a mean accuracy of 0.92 ± 0.01 is far more trustworthy than one with 0.93 ± 0.05. The second model performs better on average but is wildly inconsistent across folds — it might hit 0.98 on an easy fold and 0.88 on a hard one. That instability often signals the model is overfitting to certain patterns in the training data.

Fold-by-Fold Analysis

Do not just look at the summary stats. Print the individual fold scores. If one fold is dramatically worse than the others, dig into it. Maybe that fold contains a cluster of unusual examples, or a time period where the data distribution shifted. Understanding why a fold is hard teaches you more about your problem than the mean ever will.

Comparing Models with Cross-Validation

Here is a concrete example. Suppose you want to decide between a Random Forest and a Logistic Regression for the breast cancer dataset. Do not run separate CV loops — run them on the exact same folds. This way, differences in score come from the models, not from random fold assignments.

from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.model_selection import RepeatedStratifiedKFold, cross_val_score
from sklearn.datasets import load_breast_cancer
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.pipeline import make_pipeline
import numpy as np

X, y = load_breast_cancer(return_X_y=True)

# Define both models (pipeline scales features for logistic regression)
rf = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=100, random_state=42)
lr = make_pipeline(StandardScaler(), LogisticRegression(max_iter=5000, random_state=42))

# Same folds for both models — critical for fair comparison
cv = RepeatedStratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, n_repeats=10, random_state=42)

rf_scores = cross_val_score(rf, X, y, cv=cv, scoring='accuracy')
lr_scores = cross_val_score(lr, X, y, cv=cv, scoring='accuracy')

print(f"Random Forest:       {rf_scores.mean():.4f} ± {rf_scores.std():.4f}")
print(f"Logistic Regression: {lr_scores.mean():.4f} ± {lr_scores.std():.4f}")

# Per-fold differences
differences = rf_scores - lr_scores
print(f"
Per-fold difference (RF - LR): {differences.mean():.4f} ± {differences.std():.4f}")

The per-fold difference is key. If Random Forest beats Logistic Regression on 45 out of 50 folds, that is convincing even if the average gap is small. If it wins on 28 and loses on 22, the models are likely equivalent in practice.

Statistical Tests for Model Comparison

Eyeballing fold scores is a start, but for rigorous comparisons, you can use a statistical test. The paired t-test on fold-level scores is common but has a flaw: it assumes the fold scores are independent, which they are not (they share training data). A corrected version is the corrected resampled t-test, and for repeated k-fold, the Nadeau-Bengio correction adjusts for the dependence.

from scipy import stats

# Paired t-test on the 50 fold-level differences
t_stat, p_value = stats.ttest_rel(rf_scores, lr_scores)
print(f"Paired t-test: t={t_stat:.3f}, p={p_value:.4f}")

if p_value < 0.05:
    print("Statistically significant difference at α=0.05")
else:
    print("No significant difference — models are comparable")

Be cautious with p-values here. With 50 repeated folds you have a lot of statistical power, so tiny, practically meaningless differences can appear "significant." Always pair the test with the actual magnitude of the difference. A 0.2% accuracy gap might be statistically significant but operationally irrelevant.

💡 Practical Rule of Thumb

If two models are within one standard deviation of each other in cross-validation, prefer the simpler one. It will be easier to debug, faster to train, and more likely to hold up when the data distribution shifts in production. Complexity needs to earn its place.

Putting It All Together

Cross-validation is not a technique you apply once and move on from. It is a discipline that runs through every modeling decision. When you engineer a new feature, re-run CV to see if it actually helps. When you swap one algorithm for another, compare them on the same folds. When you tune hyperparameters, use nested CV so you do not fool yourself with optimistic scores.

The core lesson is this: never trust a single number from a single split. Data is noisy. Models are sensitive. The only honest way to estimate real-world performance is to test your model on multiple different slices of data and look at the full distribution of results. That is what cross-validation gives you — not certainty, but calibrated confidence.
