Evaluation Metrics

Chapter 4: ML Fundamentals & Core Concepts Section 5 of 8 12 subtopics
TL;DR

A model that predicts "not fraud" every time scores 99% accuracy on a dataset with 1% fraud — and catches nothing. Accuracy lies when classes are imbalanced. Precision asks "of everything you flagged, how much was real?" Recall asks "of all the real positives, how many did you catch?" F1 balances them. ROC-AUC measures ranking quality across all thresholds; PR-AUC is better when the positive class is rare. For regression, RMSE punishes big misses, MAE treats all errors equally. The right metric is the one that hurts when it's wrong — start with the business cost and work backward.

The Confusion Matrix: Where Everything Starts

Every classification metric you'll ever see is some arithmetic on four numbers. Before we get to any of them, you need to know where those numbers come from.

Your binary classifier looks at each example and makes a call: positive or negative. Sometimes it's right, sometimes it's wrong. That gives you four buckets:

Predicted PositivePredicted Negative
Actually PositiveTrue Positive (TP) ✓False Negative (FN) ✗
Actually NegativeFalse Positive (FP) ✗True Negative (TN) ✓

TP — you said yes, reality said yes. Correct alarm. TN — you said no, reality said no. Correct silence. FP — you said yes, reality said no. False alarm. FN — you said no, reality said yes. Missed catch.

Commit this to muscle memory. Everything below is just a ratio of these four cells.

import numpy as np
from sklearn.metrics import confusion_matrix, classification_report

# Fraud scenario: 10,000 transactions, 1% fraud
y_true = np.array([1]*100 + [0]*9900)

# A decent model: catches 85 frauds, also flags 50 legit ones
y_pred = np.zeros(10000, dtype=int)
y_pred[:85] = 1     # 85 of the 100 frauds caught
y_pred[100:150] = 1  # 50 false alarms on legit transactions

cm = confusion_matrix(y_true, y_pred)
print(cm)
# [[9850,   50],
#  [  15,   85]]
# TN=9850, FP=50, FN=15, TP=85

Classification Metrics

Accuracy — And Why It Lies

Accuracy = (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN). In our fraud example: (85 + 9850) / 10000 = 99.35%. Sounds fantastic — until you realize a model that prints return "not fraud" for every single input also scores 99.0%. That 0.35 percentage-point "improvement" hides the fact that your model actually catches 85% of fraud. Accuracy is fine when classes are roughly balanced. The moment they aren't, it's nearly worthless.

Precision — "Of Everything You Flagged, How Much Was Real?"

Precision = TP / (TP + FP). Our model: 85 / (85 + 50) = 63%. About 4 in 10 alarms are false. If a human investigator reviews every flag, low precision means they're drowning in noise.

When precision matters most: spam filters (a false positive buries a real email), content moderation (wrongly removing legitimate posts erodes trust), criminal justice (a false positive is an innocent person convicted). Anywhere the cost of a false alarm is high.

Recall (Sensitivity) — "Of All Actual Positives, How Many Did You Catch?"

Recall = TP / (TP + FN). Our model: 85 / (85 + 15) = 85%. We're missing 15% of real fraud — each one potentially thousands of dollars walking out the door.

When recall matters most: cancer screening (a false negative is a missed tumor), fraud detection, manufacturing defect inspection. Anywhere missing a positive is dangerous or expensive.

The Precision–Recall Tradeoff

Here's the mechanical reality. Most classifiers don't output a hard yes/no — they output a probability score. You pick a threshold to turn that score into a decision. Lower the threshold and you flag more things as positive. You catch more true positives (recall goes up), but you also catch more false positives (precision goes down). Raise the threshold and the opposite happens.

Think of it like a smoke detector's sensitivity dial. Crank it up: catches every fire, also goes off when you make toast. Dial it down: no false alarms, might miss a real fire. There is no free lunch. The right setting depends entirely on which mistake costs more.

F1 Score — When You Need One Number

The F1 score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall:

F1 = 2 × (precision × recall) / (precision + recall)

Why harmonic mean instead of a regular average? Because it punishes lopsided performance. If precision is 100% and recall is 1%, the arithmetic mean is a rosy 50.5%. The harmonic mean is 1.98% — which correctly screams that something is broken.

Our fraud model: F1 = 2 × (0.63 × 0.85) / (0.63 + 0.85) = 0.72.

When you don't care equally about precision and recall, use F-beta: F_β = (1 + β²) × (P × R) / (β² × P + R). β = 2 weights recall twice as much (fraud detection). β = 0.5 weights precision more (spam filtering). F1 is just F-beta with β = 1.

ROC Curve & AUC

The ROC curve plots True Positive Rate (recall) vs. False Positive Rate at every possible threshold. A perfect model hugs the top-left corner. A random coin flip traces the diagonal.

AUC (Area Under the ROC Curve) collapses the entire curve into one number between 0 and 1. The intuitive interpretation: pick a random positive and a random negative — AUC is the probability the model scores the positive higher. AUC = 0.5 is random guessing. AUC = 0.85 means "85% of the time, a random fraud gets a higher score than a random legit transaction."

from sklearn.metrics import roc_curve, roc_auc_score

# Simulated probability scores
np.random.seed(42)
y_true = np.array([1]*100 + [0]*9900)
scores_fraud = np.random.beta(5, 2, size=100)
scores_legit = np.random.beta(1.5, 5, size=9900)
y_scores = np.concatenate([scores_fraud, scores_legit])

fpr, tpr, thresholds = roc_curve(y_true, y_scores)
auc = roc_auc_score(y_true, y_scores)
print(f"AUC: {auc:.3f}")  # ~ 0.96

When to use ROC-AUC: comparing models across all thresholds, especially when classes are reasonably balanced. When NOT to use it: heavily imbalanced data. The false positive rate stays deceptively low because it's divided by a huge number of negatives — 500 false positives out of 99,000 negatives is only 0.5% FPR, but 500 false alarms might bury your ops team.

PR Curve & Average Precision

The Precision-Recall curve plots precision (y-axis) vs. recall (x-axis) at every threshold. Unlike ROC, it focuses entirely on the positive class — exactly what you care about in imbalanced problems. A random model's baseline here isn't 0.5; it's the fraction of positives in your data (1% in our fraud case). That makes differences between models much more visible.

Average Precision (AP) summarizes the PR curve as a single number — the weighted mean of precision at each threshold step, weighted by the change in recall. It's your go-to metric when positive examples are rare.

Log Loss — Punishing Confident Mistakes

Log loss (cross-entropy) evaluates predicted probabilities directly: -(1/N) × Σ[y·log(p) + (1-y)·log(1-p)]. If a true fraud case gets a predicted probability of 0.01 — you were 99% confident it was legit, and you were dead wrong — log loss hammers you. A correct but timid prediction (say, 0.6 for a true positive) gets a smaller penalty but still a penalty. Use log loss when you need calibrated probabilities, not just rankings.

MCC (Matthews Correlation Coefficient)

MCC is arguably the single best metric for binary classification on imbalanced data:

MCC = (TP×TN − FP×FN) / √((TP+FP)(TP+FN)(TN+FP)(TN+FN))

Ranges from −1 (perfect inverse) to +1 (perfect prediction), with 0 meaning "no better than random." Unlike F1, it uses all four confusion matrix cells and treats both classes symmetrically. A high MCC is hard to game — your model genuinely has to perform well on both positives and negatives. If you can only report one number on an imbalanced binary problem, make it MCC.

from sklearn.metrics import matthews_corrcoef

print(f"MCC: {matthews_corrcoef(y_true, y_pred):.3f}")  # Uses the y_pred from earlier

Multi-Class Metrics

Everything above was binary. With K classes, you get a K×K confusion matrix and per-class precision/recall/F1. The question is how to roll those into a single number. Three ways, each with different politics:

AveragingHow It WorksUse When
MacroCompute metric per class, take unweighted meanEvery class is equally important — even the tiny ones
MicroPool all TP/FP/FN globally, compute onceEvery sample is equally important (micro-F1 = accuracy for single-label)
WeightedCompute metric per class, average weighted by class sizeCompromise — big classes dominate, small classes still contribute

Quick rule of thumb: if you have 10,000 cats and 50 rare birds and the birds matter, use macro. If you just want an overall accuracy-like number, use micro. Weighted is the safe default when you're not sure.

from sklearn.metrics import f1_score

# Multi-class example
y_true_mc = [0, 0, 0, 1, 1, 2, 2, 2, 2, 2]
y_pred_mc = [0, 0, 1, 1, 0, 2, 2, 2, 1, 0]

print(f"Macro F1:    {f1_score(y_true_mc, y_pred_mc, average='macro'):.3f}")
print(f"Micro F1:    {f1_score(y_true_mc, y_pred_mc, average='micro'):.3f}")
print(f"Weighted F1: {f1_score(y_true_mc, y_pred_mc, average='weighted'):.3f}")

Regression Metrics

Classification asks "which bucket?" Regression asks "how much?" Different game, different scoreboard.

MAE — Mean Absolute Error

MAE = (1/N) × Σ|y_i − ŷ_i|

The average absolute miss. If you predict a house is worth $300K and it sells for $320K, the error is $20K. MAE treats all errors linearly — a $10K miss is exactly twice as bad as a $5K miss. Easy to explain to anyone: "on average, our predictions are off by $X." Robust to outliers because it doesn't square anything.

MSE / RMSE — When Big Misses Are Unacceptable

MSE = (1/N) × Σ(y_i − ŷ_i)²    RMSE = √MSE

Squaring the errors does two things: makes everything positive, and disproportionately punishes large errors. A $100K miss contributes 100× more to MSE than a $10K miss. RMSE takes the square root to get back to the original units (dollars, not dollars-squared), making it interpretable.

Use MSE/RMSE when a large error isn't just "proportionally bad" — it's catastrophically bad. Insurance pricing, structural engineering, energy demand forecasting. Use MAE when all errors are created equal.

R² — Proportion of Variance Explained

R² = 1 − (SS_res / SS_tot)

R² = 1.0 means your model explains all the variance. R² = 0 means you're no better than predicting the mean every time. R² < 0 means you're actually worse than the mean — yes, that's possible, and yes, it means something went very wrong.

The gotcha: R² always increases (or stays flat) when you add more features — even useless ones. The model has more knobs to turn and will always find a way to overfit slightly more. Adjusted R² penalizes for feature count: it goes down if a new feature doesn't pull its weight. Always use adjusted R² when comparing models with different numbers of features.

MAPE — The Business-Friendly Metric

MAPE = (1/N) × Σ|y_i − ŷ_i| / |y_i| × 100%

"Our predictions are off by 5% on average" is a sentence that executives actually understand. That's MAPE's superpower. Its kryptonite: when actual values are near zero, you divide by something tiny and MAPE explodes to infinity. Don't use it for data that crosses zero or hovers near it.

from sklearn.metrics import (mean_absolute_error, mean_squared_error,
                             r2_score, mean_absolute_percentage_error)
import numpy as np

y_true_reg = np.array([300, 450, 200, 520, 380])  # actual prices ($K)
y_pred_reg = np.array([310, 430, 215, 490, 395])   # predicted prices

print(f"MAE:   ${mean_absolute_error(y_true_reg, y_pred_reg):.0f}K")
print(f"RMSE:  ${np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(y_true_reg, y_pred_reg)):.0f}K")
print(f"R²:    {r2_score(y_true_reg, y_pred_reg):.3f}")
print(f"MAPE:  {mean_absolute_percentage_error(y_true_reg, y_pred_reg)*100:.1f}%")

The Decision Table

This is the table you'll actually use at work. Bookmark it.

Recall Of actual positives, % caught False negatives are costly (cancer, fraud, security) Can be trivially 100% by flagging everything as positive

F-beta Weighted harmonic mean (β controls bias) β=2 → recall matters more; β=0.5 → precision matters more Choosing β requires knowing relative costs — which you should know anyway

MCC Correlation between predicted and actual labels Imbalanced binary classification (best single metric) Less intuitive than F1 for stakeholder communication
MAE Average absolute error All errors equally bad; want robust-to-outlier metric Doesn't penalize big misses harder — if big misses matter, use RMSE
RMSE Root mean squared error Large errors are disproportionately bad Sensitive to outliers — one wildly wrong prediction can dominate

Choosing the Right Metric

Start with the Business Cost

This is the single most important question in evaluation: what happens when the model is wrong?

If false negatives are 100× more expensive than false positives (fraud, cancer), optimize for recall or F2. If false positives are more dangerous (spam filtering a legal contract, convicting an innocent person), optimize for precision or F0.5. If costs are roughly symmetric, F1 or MCC. If you can actually attach dollar amounts — "$5,000 per missed fraud, $50 per false alarm" — compute the total cost directly: cost = 5000 × FN + 50 × FP. Now you're optimizing for money, which is what the business actually cares about.

Threshold Selection

The default 0.5 threshold is arbitrary and almost never optimal. In fraud detection, you might drop to 0.2 — flag anything with a 20%+ chance. You'll catch more fraud (higher recall) at the cost of more false alarms (lower precision). The right threshold is where your business cost function is minimized.

from sklearn.metrics import precision_recall_curve

precisions, recalls, thresholds = precision_recall_curve(y_true, y_scores)

# Find threshold that gives at least 90% recall
idx = np.where(recalls >= 0.90)[0][-1]
print(f"For 90% recall, set threshold = {thresholds[idx]:.3f}")
print(f"Precision at that threshold = {precisions[idx]:.3f}")

Calibration: When Probabilities Need to Mean Something

ROC-AUC only cares about ranking — does the model score positives higher than negatives? It doesn't care if the model says "92% chance" when the true probability is 40%. But if your system uses those probabilities to make downstream decisions (pricing insurance, ordering medical tests, setting bid amounts), you need calibration. A well-calibrated model's "80% confident" predictions are actually correct about 80% of the time. If your model isn't calibrated, look into Platt scaling or isotonic regression as post-hoc fixes.

Ranking Metrics

Not everything is classification or regression. Search engines, recommendation systems, and ad platforms care about order — did the best results appear near the top?

MRR (Mean Reciprocal Rank) — where does the first correct result appear? Position 1 → score of 1. Position 3 → score of 1/3. Average across queries. Good for navigational search where there's one right answer.

MAP (Mean Average Precision) — considers all relevant results, not just the first. Computes precision at every position where a relevant result appears, averages those. Rewards putting relevant results early. The workhorse metric for information retrieval.

NDCG (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain) — handles graded relevance (not just "relevant or not" but "how relevant on a scale"). Discounts gains by log-position, normalizes by the ideal ordering. NDCG@10 is the standard for search quality. Ranges 0 to 1, where 1 means perfect ranking.

Putting It All Together: The Decision Flowchart

When you get a new problem, walk through this in order:

1. Classification or regression? Classification → start with precision, recall, F1. Regression → start with RMSE or MAE. Ranking → start with NDCG or MAP.

2. Are classes imbalanced? If yes, accuracy is dead to you. Use F1, PR-AUC, or MCC. If extremely imbalanced (1:1000+), PR-AUC beats ROC-AUC.

3. What's the cost asymmetry? False negatives worse → recall, F2. False positives worse → precision, F0.5. Symmetric → F1, MCC.

4. Do you need calibrated probabilities? If downstream decisions use the probability values (not just rankings), check calibration with a reliability diagram. Fix with Platt scaling or isotonic regression.

5. Is this going to production? Offline metrics get you to a shortlist. A/B tests give you the real answer. When offline and online metrics disagree — and they will — trust online.

⚠️ The Most Common Mistake

Optimizing for the metric that's easiest to compute instead of the one that matches your business objective. A model with 0.95 AUC and a model with 0.90 AUC can have wildly different business impact depending on where on the ROC curve your operating point falls. Always connect the metric back to dollars, lives, or user experience.
