Model Selection

Chapter 4: ML Fundamentals & Core Concepts 8 subtopics
TL;DR

There is no universally best model — a theorem literally proves it. Start with the dumbest baseline that makes sense, then earn your way up to complexity only when the data demands it. Match model complexity to dataset size. Use random search or Optuna for hyperparameter tuning, not grid search. When you need to explain predictions, reach for SHAP. For tabular data, gradient boosting is absurdly hard to beat. The model selection workflow is: baseline → simple → complex → compare on validation → check deployment constraints → ship.

No Free Lunch Theorem

If someone tells you "always use XGBoost" or "neural nets are the future, use them for everything," they're giving you bad advice. The No Free Lunch Theorem (Wolpert & Macready, 1997) proves that no single algorithm dominates all problems — averaged over every possible dataset, all learners perform identically. The algorithm that crushes your churn prediction might be mediocre on your image classifier. You have to try things on your data and measure.

This is actually liberating. It means the right answer to "which model should I use?" is always "depends — let's find out."

Start Simple, Earn Complexity

This is the single most important habit in applied ML: always start with a baseline. For regression, predict the mean. For classification, predict the most frequent class. This is your floor. If your fancy model can't beat a constant prediction, you have a data problem, not a model problem.

from sklearn.dummy import DummyClassifier, DummyRegressor
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score, mean_squared_error

# Classification: always predict the majority class
baseline_clf = DummyClassifier(strategy="most_frequent")
baseline_clf.fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"Baseline accuracy: {accuracy_score(y_test, baseline_clf.predict(X_test)):.3f}")

# Regression: always predict the training mean
baseline_reg = DummyRegressor(strategy="mean")
baseline_reg.fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"Baseline MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_test, baseline_reg.predict(X_test)):.3f}")

After the dummy baseline, try a logistic regression (classification) or linear regression (regression). Then a basic decision tree. Then maybe a random forest. At each step, ask: did the jump in performance justify the added complexity?

Why this order matters:

  • Simpler models train in seconds, not hours. You iterate faster.
  • Simpler models are easier to debug. When something goes wrong with logistic regression, you look at the coefficients. When something goes wrong with a 500-tree ensemble, good luck.
  • Simpler models are easier to explain to stakeholders, regulators, and your future self at 2 AM during an incident.
  • Simpler models overfit less. They have fewer knobs to accidentally memorize noise.

If your logistic regression already gets you to 90% of your target metric, pause and ask whether that last 10% is worth the engineering cost of a more complex model. Sometimes it is. Often it isn't.

Model Complexity vs Data Size

Here's a rough guide that saves a lot of wasted GPU hours: match your model's capacity to the amount of data you have. A model with millions of parameters trained on 500 rows will memorize the training set and generalize terribly. A linear model on a billion rows will underfit and leave performance on the table.

Dataset Size Rows (rough) Good Starting Models Why
Small < 1,000 Logistic/Linear Regression, Naive Bayes, small decision trees Few parameters → hard to overfit
Medium 1K – 100K Random Forest, Gradient Boosting (XGBoost, LightGBM) Enough data to fit interactions without memorizing
Large 100K+ Deep learning, large gradient boosting ensembles Enough data to justify millions of parameters

This isn't a law. A well-regularized neural net can work on 5K rows, and logistic regression can work beautifully on millions of rows if the relationship is actually linear. But violating this rule of thumb — throwing a transformer at 200 samples — almost always ends in tears.

💡 The Feature-to-Sample Ratio

When you have more features than samples (common in genomics, NLP bag-of-words, etc.), even "simple" models can overfit. Use aggressive regularization: Lasso, ElasticNet, or sparse SVMs. The rule of thumb is you want at least 10–20 samples per feature for unregularized models.

Interpretability vs Performance

A decision tree tells you exactly which features it checked and in what order. Logistic regression gives you a coefficient for each feature — you can literally point at a number and say "this is how much feature X pushes the prediction." These are glass-box models.

A 500-tree gradient boosting ensemble or a 10-layer neural net? Try explaining to a loan officer why a particular applicant was denied. The reasoning is distributed across thousands of parameters in ways that resist human understanding. These are black-box models.

The question isn't "which is better?" It's "what does your situation demand?"

Model Type Interpretability Typical Performance Use When
Linear / Logistic Regression ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Full transparency Good on linear problems Regulated industries, need to explain every prediction
Decision Tree (single) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ Easy to visualize Moderate, overfits easily Quick exploration, communication with non-technical stakeholders
Random Forest ⭐⭐⭐ Feature importance available Good to very good Solid default when some interpretability still needed
Gradient Boosting (XGBoost, LightGBM) ⭐⭐ Needs SHAP for explanations Excellent on tabular data Tabular data where performance is primary goal
Deep Neural Networks ⭐ Black box Excellent on images, text, audio Unstructured data (images, NLP, speech)
Explainable Boosting Machine (EBM) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ Glass-box + interactions Near gradient boosting on tabular Best of both worlds for regulated tabular tasks

In healthcare, finance, and legal contexts, regulations may require you to explain decisions. In those cases, you might accept a small accuracy hit for transparency — or use a black-box model with post-hoc explanation tools like SHAP (covered below). In other contexts — ad click prediction, image tagging, recommendation — nobody cares how the model works as long as it works. Know which world you're in before you start modeling.

Hyperparameter Tuning

Every model has knobs. A random forest has number of trees, max depth, min samples per leaf. A neural net has learning rate, layer sizes, dropout rate. These are hyperparameters — values you set before training, as opposed to parameters the model learns from data. The right values can make a surprisingly large difference.

Grid Search: The Brute-Force Approach

Define a grid of values. Try every combination. If you have 4 values for parameter A, 3 for B, and 3 for C, that's 36 experiments, each with 5-fold CV = 180 model fits. It's thorough but expensive, and the cost grows exponentially with each new hyperparameter.

Grid search has a deeper problem: it wastes trials. If only one of your four hyperparameters actually matters (which is common), grid search spends most of its budget exploring irrelevant axes.

Random Search: Surprisingly Effective

Random search (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012) samples hyperparameter combinations at random from distributions you specify. The key insight: most hyperparameters have one or two values that matter a lot, and many that don't. Random search is far more likely to stumble onto a good value for the important parameters because it doesn't waste trials covering a regular grid on the unimportant ones.

from sklearn.model_selection import RandomizedSearchCV
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingClassifier
from scipy.stats import uniform, randint

param_distributions = {
    "n_estimators": randint(50, 500),
    "max_depth": randint(2, 10),
    "learning_rate": uniform(0.001, 0.3),
    "subsample": uniform(0.6, 0.4),
    "min_samples_leaf": randint(1, 20),
}

search = RandomizedSearchCV(
    GradientBoostingClassifier(random_state=42),
    param_distributions,
    n_iter=100,
    cv=5,
    scoring="roc_auc",
    n_jobs=-1,
    random_state=42,
)
search.fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"Best AUC: {search.best_score_:.4f}")
print(f"Best params: {search.best_params_}")

In practice, 100 random trials often matches or beats a grid search with thousands of evaluations.

Bayesian Optimization with Optuna

Grid search and random search don't learn from their experiments. Bayesian optimization does. After each trial, it builds a model of how hyperparameters relate to performance, then intelligently picks the next combination to try — spending more time in promising regions and less in dead zones.

Optuna is what practitioners actually use. It's clean, Pythonic, and supports pruning (killing bad trials early). Here's the real-world pattern:

import optuna
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingClassifier

def objective(trial):
    params = {
        "n_estimators": trial.suggest_int("n_estimators", 50, 500),
        "max_depth": trial.suggest_int("max_depth", 2, 10),
        "learning_rate": trial.suggest_float("learning_rate", 1e-3, 0.3, log=True),
        "subsample": trial.suggest_float("subsample", 0.6, 1.0),
        "min_samples_leaf": trial.suggest_int("min_samples_leaf", 1, 20),
    }

    model = GradientBoostingClassifier(**params, random_state=42)
    scores = cross_val_score(model, X_train, y_train, cv=5, scoring="roc_auc")
    return scores.mean()

optuna.logging.set_verbosity(optuna.logging.WARNING)
study = optuna.create_study(direction="maximize")
study.optimize(objective, n_trials=100)

print(f"Best AUC: {study.best_trial.value:.4f}")
print(f"Best params: {study.best_trial.params}")

# Optuna also gives you free visualizations
optuna.visualization.plot_optimization_history(study)
optuna.visualization.plot_param_importances(study)

Optuna's plot_param_importances is gold — it tells you which hyperparameters actually mattered. If learning rate accounts for 80% of the variance and subsample accounts for 2%, you know where to focus future tuning effort.

💡 Which Search Method to Use

3–4 hyperparameters, small search space: Random search is fine. 5+ hyperparameters, large search space: Optuna starts to pull ahead significantly. Grid search: Mostly useful for final fine-tuning around a known-good region, not for exploration.

Practical Model Selection Workflow

Here's the workflow that works in the real world. Not in theory — in production. Memorize this sequence.

Step 1: Establish a Baseline

Fit a DummyClassifier or DummyRegressor. Record the number. This is your floor. If your dataset has 92% negatives and 8% positives, "always predict negative" gives you 92% accuracy. If your complex model also gets 92%, it has learned nothing. The baseline exposes this instantly.

Step 2: Try Simple Models

Logistic regression for classification. Linear regression for regression. Maybe a single decision tree. These train in under a second and give you interpretable results. Often, they're surprisingly competitive — especially on clean, well-engineered tabular features.

Step 3: Move to Complex Models

Random forest, gradient boosting (XGBoost or LightGBM), maybe an SVM. Compare their cross-validation scores against the simple models. If the improvement is marginal (say, 0.5% AUC), think hard about whether the added complexity is worth it.

Step 4: Compare on Validation

Run 5- or 10-fold cross-validation on your top candidates. Report mean and standard deviation. If two models are within one standard deviation of each other, prefer the simpler one. Use a paired t-test or Wilcoxon test if you need to confirm the difference is real.

Step 5: Check Deployment Constraints

Does the model need to predict in under 10ms? Does it need to fit in 500MB of RAM? Does it need to run on a phone? Does someone need to sign off on every prediction? These constraints can eliminate your best-performing model entirely. Better to find out now than after three months of productionizing.

Step 6: Decide and Document

Write down: which models you tried, their scores, why you picked the one you picked, and what tradeoffs you accepted. Future-you (or your replacement) will thank you. "Selected LightGBM over logistic regression: 4-point AUC improvement justified the added complexity. Logistic regression remains the fallback if interpretability requirements change."

⚠️ The Most Common Mistake

Skipping straight to complex models. Beginners jump to XGBoost or neural nets because they're exciting. Then they have no reference point for whether the model is actually learning anything. A baseline takes 30 seconds and can save you weeks of debugging a fundamentally broken pipeline.

Model Interpretability Tools

You shipped a gradient boosting model. Your VP asks: "Why did it flag this customer as high churn risk?" If you say "the model just knows," you've lost their trust. SHAP gives you real answers.

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)

SHAP is based on Shapley values from game theory — a method for fairly distributing a "payout" (the prediction) among "players" (the features). For every single prediction, SHAP tells you exactly how much each feature pushed the output up or down. It handles feature interactions correctly, which simpler methods like raw feature importance do not.

import shap
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingClassifier

model = GradientBoostingClassifier(n_estimators=200, random_state=42)
model.fit(X_train, y_train)

# TreeExplainer is fast for tree-based models
explainer = shap.TreeExplainer(model)
shap_values = explainer.shap_values(X_test)

# Global view: which features matter most across all predictions?
shap.summary_plot(shap_values, X_test, feature_names=feature_names)

# Local view: why THIS prediction for THIS customer?
shap.force_plot(
    explainer.expected_value,
    shap_values[0],
    X_test.iloc[0],
    feature_names=feature_names,
)

The summary plot is incredibly information-dense. Each row is a feature. Each dot is a data point. Horizontal position = SHAP value (how much the feature moved the prediction). Color = feature value (red = high, blue = low). You can immediately see that high "months since last purchase" pushes toward churn (dots are red and to the right), while high "engagement score" pushes away from churn (dots are red and to the left). One plot, the whole story.

Permutation Importance

A lighter alternative when you don't need per-prediction explanations. Shuffle one feature's values randomly, measure how much performance drops. Big drop → important feature. No drop → irrelevant feature. It's model-agnostic and takes three lines:

from sklearn.inspection import permutation_importance

result = permutation_importance(model, X_test, y_test, n_repeats=10, random_state=42)
for i in result.importances_mean.argsort()[::-1][:10]:
    print(f"{feature_names[i]}: {result.importances_mean[i]:.4f} "
          f"(± {result.importances_std[i]:.4f})")

Use permutation importance for quick sanity checks ("is the model using the features I expect?"). Use SHAP when you need to explain individual predictions or when stakeholders need convincing.

When Simpler Models Win

There's an uncomfortable truth in applied ML that doesn't get enough airtime: simpler models win more often than you'd think.

Tabular Data: XGBoost Is All You Need (Mostly)

For structured/tabular data — the kind that lives in databases and CSV files — tree-based gradient boosting (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost) dominates. This isn't controversial; it's been demonstrated in Kaggle competitions, academic benchmarks, and production systems for years. Deep learning on tabular data is an active research area, but the practical consensus as of 2024 is: gradient boosting first, neural nets only if you have a specific reason (massive scale, multimodal inputs, transfer learning).

Small Datasets

Under a few thousand rows, linear models and small ensembles are your friends. Deep learning needs data to shine — on small datasets it memorizes noise. A logistic regression with well-engineered features will frequently beat a neural net that's starving for data.

Need for Speed

If your model needs to make predictions in microseconds (real-time bidding, high-frequency trading, embedded systems), a logistic regression or a small decision tree is hard to beat. A 200-tree ensemble is fast, but a single matrix multiplication is faster. And it fits in an L1 cache.

Need for Interpretability

When a regulator says "explain this decision," a linear model can answer in one sentence: "Feature A contributed +0.3, Feature B contributed −0.2, the intercept is 0.1, total = 0.2, above the threshold of 0.15." Try that with a neural net. (Yes, SHAP exists — but explaining SHAP to a regulatory auditor adds another layer of complexity.)

When the Data Is Clean and the Signal Is Strong

If the relationship between your features and target is roughly linear, throwing a neural net at it is like using a rocket launcher to open a jar. The logistic regression will match or beat it, train in milliseconds, and never need a GPU. Check for nonlinearity before reaching for nonlinear models.

💡 The Pragmatist's Rule

If your simpler model gets you within 1–2% of the complex model's performance, ship the simpler model. The debugging time, deployment complexity, and maintenance burden of the complex model will cost you far more than that 1–2% buys you. Complexity has compound interest — it gets more expensive over time, not less.

What You Should Now Be Able To Do

  1. Explain why no single model is universally best and why empirical comparison on your data is non-negotiable.
  2. Set up a proper baseline using DummyClassifier / DummyRegressor before reaching for any real model.
  3. Match model complexity to dataset size — linear models for small data, tree ensembles for medium, deep learning for large.
  4. Choose between interpretability and performance based on your deployment context (regulated vs. unregulated, stakeholder requirements).
  5. Run random search and Optuna for hyperparameter tuning, and know when each is appropriate.
  6. Execute the 6-step model selection workflow: baseline → simple → complex → validate → deployment check → document.
  7. Use SHAP to explain model predictions — both globally (which features matter most) and locally (why this specific prediction).
  8. Articulate when simpler models are the right choice and resist the temptation to over-engineer.
