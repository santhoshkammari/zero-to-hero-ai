Nice to Know

Chapter 4: ML Fundamentals & Core Concepts 10 topics

These are concepts you'll encounter in papers, interviews, and production codebases. You don't need to deeply understand any of them right now — but when someone drops one of these terms, you should be able to nod and know roughly what they're talking about. Awareness now, depth later.

The Topics

PAC Learning & VC Dimension

PAC stands for "Probably Approximately Correct" — it's the theoretical framework for why generalization works at all. VC dimension measures a model's capacity: how many points it can perfectly shatter (classify in every possible way). A linear classifier in 2D has VC dimension 3. Higher VC dimension means more capacity, which means more potential to overfit.

When you'll need this: ML theory papers, learning theory interview questions, or whenever someone argues about sample complexity bounds.

Information-Theoretic Approaches

Mutual information measures how much knowing one variable tells you about another — great for feature selection. KL divergence measures how different two probability distributions are. Cross-entropy loss? That's information theory wearing a machine learning hat. These three concepts are deeply connected, and they show up constantly in NLP and probabilistic modeling.

When you'll need this: NLP work, probabilistic models, or any time you need to compare distributions or pick informative features.

Rademacher Complexity

A more modern measure of model capacity than VC dimension. The idea: take your training data, assign random ±1 labels, and see how well your model class can fit that noise. If it fits random noise perfectly, it has high complexity and can easily overfit real data too. It's a data-dependent measure, which makes it tighter than VC bounds in practice.

When you'll need this: Theoretical ML papers published after ~2000, or generalization bound discussions that find VC dimension too loose.

Multi-Objective Optimization

Real-world ML almost never has a single metric. You want accuracy and fairness. Speed and quality. Recall and precision (and not just their harmonic mean). Multi-objective optimization finds the Pareto frontier — the set of solutions where you can't improve one metric without hurting another. Libraries like pymoo handle this. You pick your preferred tradeoff from the frontier.

When you'll need this: Production ML where stakeholders have competing metrics and you need to show them the tradeoff space.

Conformal Prediction

Instead of "the prediction is 42," conformal prediction gives you "the prediction is between 38 and 46, and I guarantee this interval covers the true value at least 95% of the time." The coverage guarantee is distribution-free — it holds regardless of the underlying model. It's a wrapper you can put around any predictor. Increasingly popular because regulators and stakeholders want uncertainty, not just point estimates.

When you'll need this: Production ML where uncertainty quantification matters — medical, financial, safety-critical systems.

Bayesian Optimization Beyond Hyperparameters

You've probably heard of Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning. But BO works for any function that's expensive to evaluate: designing A/B tests, optimizing chemical formulations, tuning manufacturing processes. It builds a Gaussian process surrogate model that's cheap to evaluate, uses it to decide where to sample next, then updates. Minimal wasted experiments.

When you'll need this: Any expensive-to-evaluate optimization — experimental design, materials science, or when each "training run" costs real money.

AutoML

Automated machine learning pipelines — Auto-sklearn, FLAML, H2O AutoML — search over models, hyperparameters, and preprocessing steps. Point them at a dataset, come back later, and they've tried hundreds of combinations you'd never get to manually. They're excellent for baselines and prototyping. They won't replace understanding, but they'll consistently beat a lazy practitioner who just runs default XGBoost.

When you'll need this: Quick baselines, prototyping, or when a stakeholder asks "what's the best we can do on this data?" and you need an answer by Thursday.

Calibration Methods

When your model says "80% confident," is it actually right 80% of the time? Probably not — most models are poorly calibrated out of the box. Platt scaling fits a logistic regression on top of your model's outputs. Isotonic regression is more flexible but needs more data. Temperature scaling divides logits by a learned scalar — simple and effective for neural nets.

When you'll need this: Decision-making systems where the predicted probability drives actions — insurance pricing, medical triage, ad bidding.

Online Learning

Most ML is batch: collect data, train model, deploy, repeat. Online learning updates the model incrementally as each new data point arrives — no retraining from scratch. SGD is naturally online (one example at a time). Vowpal Wabbit is the go-to tool for this at scale. The challenge is catastrophic forgetting: the model can "forget" old patterns as it adapts to new ones.

When you'll need this: Streaming data, recommendation systems, or any setting where the data distribution shifts faster than you can retrain.

Structural Risk Minimization

The formal principle behind regularization. Your total risk = empirical risk (training error) + a complexity penalty that depends on the model class. Minimize the sum, not just the training error. This is the theoretical justification for L1/L2 regularization, early stopping, and model selection. It connects directly back to VC theory — the complexity term is bounded by VC dimension.

When you'll need this: Understanding why regularization works, not just that it works. Theory-heavy discussions or when justifying your regularization choices.

The Point of This Section

None of these topics require mastery right now. But each one is a door you'll eventually walk through — for a paper you're reading, a production problem you're solving, or an interview question that catches you off guard. When that happens, you'll know the door exists and roughly what's behind it. That's the entire goal.

What You Should Now Be Able To Do

  • Recognize PAC learning, VC dimension, and Rademacher complexity when they come up in theory discussions
  • Know that information theory underlies cross-entropy loss and KL divergence
  • Understand that conformal prediction gives you guaranteed prediction intervals
  • Know AutoML exists for quick baselines — and that calibration matters for real decisions
  • Explain why structural risk minimization is the "why" behind regularization
