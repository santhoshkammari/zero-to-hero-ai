ML Optimization Algorithms

Chapter 4: ML Fundamentals & Core Concepts 7 subtopics ~30 min read
TL;DR

Training a neural network means finding parameter values that minimize a loss function. Gradient descent is the workhorse: compute how the loss changes with respect to each parameter, then nudge parameters in the opposite direction. Vanilla SGD is noisy and sensitive to learning rates. Momentum smooths the path. Adam adapts the learning rate per-parameter and became the default because it works well out of the box. Learning rate schedules, gradient clipping, and mixed-precision training are the practical knobs you turn to make real training runs succeed.

Gradient Descent Fundamentals

Imagine you are blindfolded on a hilly landscape. Your goal is to reach the lowest valley. You can't see anything, but you can feel the slope under your feet. The obvious strategy: take a step in the direction where the ground slopes downward most steeply. That's gradient descent in one sentence.

More precisely, you have a loss function L(θ) — a single number that tells you how wrong your model's predictions are. θ represents all the model's parameters (weights and biases). You want to find the θ that makes L as small as possible. The gradient ∇L(θ) is a vector of partial derivatives — it points in the direction of steepest increase. So you step in the opposite direction: 

θ_new = θ_old − α · ∇L(θ_old)

Three pieces here. θ_old is where you are now. α (alpha) is the learning rate — how big a step you take. ∇L(θ_old) is the gradient evaluated at your current position. The minus sign flips the direction from "steepest ascent" to "steepest descent."

Batch Gradient Descent

Batch gradient descent computes the gradient using the entire training dataset on every step. This gives you the true gradient — no noise, no approximation. The problem? If you have a million training examples, you need to process all of them before you take a single step. That's absurdly slow for modern datasets. The update is smooth and stable, but you might wait hours between parameter updates.

Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)

The opposite extreme is stochastic gradient descent (SGD). Pick one random training example, compute the gradient from that single example, and update immediately. You get extremely fast updates — thousands per second. The catch is that each gradient estimate is noisy. One example might push you left, the next might push you right. The path toward the minimum looks drunk. But here's the surprising part: that noise often helps. It can bounce you out of shallow local minima and poor saddle points. The tradeoff is between speed and stability.

Mini-Batch Gradient Descent

In practice, almost everyone uses mini-batch gradient descent — the middle ground. You sample a batch of, say, 32 or 256 examples, compute the gradient from that batch, and update. The batch is big enough to smooth out the worst noise, small enough to fit in GPU memory and update frequently. When people say "SGD" in deep learning papers, they almost always mean mini-batch SGD. Typical batch sizes range from 32 to 4096, depending on the model and hardware.

Convergence and Learning Rate Sensitivity

The learning rate α is the single most important hyperparameter in training. Too large, and you overshoot the minimum — the loss explodes or oscillates wildly. Too small, and training takes forever, or you get stuck in a poor local minimum before making any real progress. Finding the right learning rate is often the difference between a model that works and one that doesn't.

For batch GD on a convex (bowl-shaped) loss surface, convergence is guaranteed if α is small enough and you run long enough. For SGD, you need the learning rate to decrease over time (a decaying schedule) to guarantee convergence — otherwise the noise never settles down. In practice, loss surfaces of neural networks are wildly non-convex, so theoretical guarantees are loose at best. You rely on empirical tricks and monitoring.

⚠ Common Pitfall

If your training loss oscillates wildly or increases, the learning rate is almost certainly too high. If it decreases painfully slowly, try increasing it by a factor of 3–10. Learning rate search is multiplicative, not additive — try 0.1, 0.01, 0.001, not 0.1, 0.09, 0.08.

Momentum-Based Methods

Plain SGD has a problem. Picture a long, narrow valley — steep walls on the sides, gentle slope along the bottom toward the minimum. The gradient is large in the "wall" direction and small in the "valley" direction. SGD bounces side to side off the walls while making slow progress along the bottom. This is infuriating when you can see the solution is straight ahead.

Classical Momentum

Momentum fixes this by adding a "velocity" term. Instead of stepping purely based on the current gradient, you accumulate a running average of past gradients. Think of a heavy ball rolling downhill — it builds up speed in the consistent direction and the side-to-side oscillations cancel each other out. 

v_t = β · v_(t-1) + ∇L(θ_t)
θ_(t+1) = θ_t − α · v_t

Here v is the velocity (accumulated gradient). β (beta) is the momentum coefficient, typically 0.9. At each step, 90% of the velocity comes from the previous velocity, and 10% from the new gradient. Directions where the gradient consistently points the same way build up speed. Directions where the gradient flips back and forth get dampened. The result: faster convergence along ravines.

Nesterov Accelerated Gradient (NAG)

Nesterov momentum is a clever twist. Instead of computing the gradient at your current position and then moving, you first "look ahead" to where the momentum is about to carry you, and compute the gradient there. The idea: if the momentum is about to overshoot, the look-ahead gradient will start pointing backward, applying the brakes earlier. 

v_t = β · v_(t-1) + ∇L(θ_t − α · β · v_(t-1))
θ_(t+1) = θ_t − α · v_t

In practice, Nesterov momentum gives slightly faster convergence than classical momentum, especially near the minimum. It's a small improvement, but it comes for free — same computational cost, you're evaluating the gradient at a slightly different point.

Adaptive Learning Rate Methods

Momentum addresses the oscillation problem, but there's another issue: different parameters might need different learning rates. A parameter connected to a frequently-activated feature gets large gradients constantly. A parameter connected to a rare feature gets gradients only occasionally. Using the same learning rate for both is wasteful — the rare-feature parameter needs larger steps when it finally gets a gradient signal.

AdaGrad

AdaGrad (Adaptive Gradient) was the first popular optimizer to give each parameter its own effective learning rate. It keeps a running sum of squared gradients for each parameter. Parameters that have received large gradients get their learning rate shrunk. Parameters that have received small gradients keep a larger learning rate. 

G_t = G_(t-1) + (∇L(θ_t))²        # accumulate squared gradients
θ_(t+1) = θ_t − α / (√G_t + ε) · ∇L(θ_t)

The ε (epsilon, typically 1e-8) prevents division by zero. The problem with AdaGrad: that accumulator G only grows. It never forgets. Over time, the effective learning rate shrinks to near zero, and training stalls. For convex problems and sparse data (like natural language processing), AdaGrad works well. For deep networks trained for many epochs, the never-forgetting accumulator kills it.

RMSprop

RMSprop (Root Mean Square Propagation) fixes AdaGrad's memory problem. Instead of accumulating all past squared gradients, it uses an exponential moving average. Recent gradients matter more; ancient gradients fade away. 

G_t = β · G_(t-1) + (1 − β) · (∇L(θ_t))²
θ_(t+1) = θ_t − α / (√G_t + ε) · ∇L(θ_t)

Typically β = 0.999 or 0.99. This was proposed by Geoffrey Hinton in a Coursera lecture — never published in a paper, but widely adopted because it worked. RMSprop adapts the learning rate per parameter and doesn't die over time. It was the go-to optimizer before Adam.

Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation)

Adam combines the best of momentum and RMSprop. It tracks two things: the first moment (mean of gradients, like momentum) and the second moment (mean of squared gradients, like RMSprop). 

m_t = β₁ · m_(t-1) + (1 − β₁) · ∇L(θ_t)      # first moment (momentum)
v_t = β₂ · v_(t-1) + (1 − β₂) · (∇L(θ_t))²   # second moment (RMSprop-like)
θ_(t+1) = θ_t − α · m_t / (√v_t + ε)

Default hyperparameters: β₁ = 0.9, β₂ = 0.999, ε = 1e-8. These defaults work remarkably well across a wide range of problems. That's the real reason Adam became the default — not because it's mathematically optimal, but because it requires less tuning. With SGD+momentum, you often need to carefully tune the learning rate and schedule. With Adam, the defaults frequently work on the first try.

Adam Bias Correction

There's a subtle issue. Both m and v are initialized to zero. In the first few steps, they're biased toward zero because the exponential average hasn't warmed up yet. Adam applies bias correction: 

m̂_t = m_t / (1 − β₁ᵗ)    # corrected first moment
v̂_t = v_t / (1 − β₂ᵗ)    # corrected second moment
θ_(t+1) = θ_t − α · m̂_t / (√v̂_t + ε)

At step t=1 with β₁=0.9, the correction divides by (1 − 0.9¹) = 0.1, multiplying the estimate by 10. By step 10, the correction is 1/(1 − 0.9¹⁰) ≈ 2.6. By step 100, it's essentially 1.0. The correction matters most in the early steps and gracefully fades away.

AdamW (Decoupled Weight Decay)

A tricky gotcha in the original Adam: L2 regularization (weight decay) doesn't behave the same way it does with SGD. In SGD, adding an L2 penalty to the loss is equivalent to subtracting a small fraction of each weight at every step. In Adam, the adaptive learning rate scaling messes this up — heavily-updated weights get less regularization than they should.

AdamW fixes this by decoupling the weight decay from the gradient-based update. Instead of adding the regularization term to the loss, it subtracts the weight decay directly from the parameters after the Adam step: 

θ_(t+1) = θ_t − α · m̂_t / (√v̂_t + ε) − α · λ · θ_t

Here λ is the weight decay coefficient. AdamW is the optimizer used to train most modern large language models, including GPT and BERT variants. If you're doing deep learning today, AdamW should be your starting point.

LAMB

LAMB (Layer-wise Adaptive Moments for Batch Training) extends Adam for very large batch training. When you scale up to batch sizes of 32K or 64K (to use more GPUs in parallel), vanilla Adam often diverges. LAMB normalizes the update for each layer by the ratio of the parameter norm to the update norm. This keeps the update size proportional to the parameter size, layer by layer. It was key to training BERT in 76 minutes on 1024 TPUs.

SAM (Sharpness-Aware Minimization)

SAM takes a different angle entirely. Instead of finding parameters that minimize the loss, it finds parameters where the loss is low and the loss surface is flat. Why? Because sharp minima — narrow valleys in the loss landscape — tend to generalize poorly. A tiny shift in the data, and you climb out of the valley. Flat minima are more robust.

SAM works by computing the gradient at a perturbed point (worst-case neighborhood), then taking a step to minimize that worst-case loss. It costs roughly 2x the computation of a normal step, but consistently improves generalization, especially on vision tasks.

PyTorch: Optimizers and Schedulers in Practice

Let's see how this looks in actual code. PyTorch makes it easy to swap optimizers and attach learning rate schedulers. 

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.optim as optim

# A small example model
model = nn.Sequential(
    nn.Linear(784, 256),
    nn.ReLU(),
    nn.Linear(256, 10)
)

# --- Different optimizers ---

# Vanilla SGD (rarely used alone in deep learning)
optimizer_sgd = optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.01)

# SGD with momentum (much better)
optimizer_sgd_m = optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.01, momentum=0.9)

# SGD with Nesterov momentum
optimizer_nag = optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.01, momentum=0.9, nesterov=True)

# Adam — the workhorse default
optimizer_adam = optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3)

# AdamW — Adam with proper weight decay (preferred for most tasks)
optimizer_adamw = optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3, weight_decay=0.01)

# --- Learning rate schedulers ---

# Use AdamW for this demo
optimizer = optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3, weight_decay=0.01)

# Step decay: multiply LR by 0.1 every 30 epochs
scheduler_step = optim.lr_scheduler.StepLR(optimizer, step_size=30, gamma=0.1)

# Cosine annealing: smoothly decrease LR from initial to near zero
scheduler_cosine = optim.lr_scheduler.CosineAnnealingLR(optimizer, T_max=100)

# Warmup + cosine via OneCycleLR (recommended starting point)
scheduler_onecycle = optim.lr_scheduler.OneCycleLR(
    optimizer, max_lr=1e-3, total_steps=1000
)

# --- Training loop skeleton ---
criterion = nn.CrossEntropyLoss()

for epoch in range(100):
    for batch_x, batch_y in dataloader:      # your data here
        optimizer.zero_grad()                 # clear old gradients
        output = model(batch_x)              # forward pass
        loss = criterion(output, batch_y)    # compute loss
        loss.backward()                      # compute gradients (backprop)
        torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)  # gradient clipping
        optimizer.step()                     # update parameters
        scheduler_onecycle.step()            # update LR (per-step for OneCycleLR)

Notice the order: zero gradients → forward → loss → backward → clip → step → schedule. Getting this order wrong is a classic bug. Also note that some schedulers (like OneCycleLR) step every batch, while others (like StepLR) step every epoch. Read the docs for whichever scheduler you choose.

Learning Rate Schedules

A fixed learning rate is almost never optimal. You generally want to start with a larger learning rate to make fast progress, then shrink it to fine-tune near the minimum. The art is in how you shrink it.

Step Decay

The simplest schedule: multiply the learning rate by a factor (say 0.1) every N epochs. Drop from 0.1 to 0.01 at epoch 30, then to 0.001 at epoch 60. It's crude but effective. ResNet was trained this way.

Exponential Decay

Multiply the learning rate by a constant factor γ (like 0.95) every epoch: α_t = α₀ · γᵗ. Smoother than step decay, but you need to tune γ carefully — too aggressive and training stalls early.

Cosine Annealing

Cosine annealing smoothly decreases the learning rate following a cosine curve from α_max down to α_min (often near zero). The shape is gentle at the start, accelerates in the middle, and slows again near the end. It has become one of the most popular schedules because it works well empirically and has only two parameters (start LR and total steps).

Warmup Schedules

Learning rate warmup starts training with a very small learning rate and linearly increases it over the first few hundred or thousand steps before the main schedule kicks in. Why? At initialization, the model's activations and gradients are essentially random. A large learning rate on random gradients causes destructive updates that the model never recovers from. Warmup lets the model "calibrate" before you give it full power. Nearly every transformer model uses warmup.

Cyclical Learning Rates

Cyclical learning rates (CLR) oscillate the learning rate between a minimum and maximum value repeatedly. The intuition: periodically increasing the learning rate helps escape sharp local minima and explore more of the loss landscape. Leslie Smith's 2017 paper showed this works surprisingly well and reduces the need for careful learning rate tuning.

One-Cycle Policy

The one-cycle policy, also from Leslie Smith, is a specific schedule: warm up the LR from a low value to a high value over the first ~30% of training, then cosine-anneal it back down for the remaining 70%. This was shown to enable super-convergence — reaching the same accuracy in fewer epochs (sometimes 5–10x fewer). It's built into PyTorch as OneCycleLR and is an excellent default for many tasks.

Linear Scaling Rule

When you increase the batch size by a factor k (say, going from 256 to 2048 across 8 GPUs), you should multiply the learning rate by k as well. This is the linear scaling rule. The reasoning: a larger batch gives a more accurate gradient estimate, so you can take a proportionally larger step. But this breaks down for very large batches — beyond a certain point, you need warmup and special optimizers like LAMB to keep training stable.

Optimization Challenges

Saddle Points vs. Local Minima

For decades, people worried about local minima — points where the loss is lower than the immediate neighborhood but not the global minimum. Getting "stuck" in a bad local minimum meant your model was suboptimal and couldn't escape.

It turns out that in high-dimensional spaces (a neural network with millions of parameters), local minima are not the main problem. Saddle points are. A saddle point is a point where the gradient is zero, but it's a minimum along some dimensions and a maximum along others — like the center of a horse saddle or a mountain pass. Why are saddle points so much more common than local minima in high dimensions?

Think of it this way. At a critical point (where the gradient is zero), each of the n dimensions can independently curve up or curve down. A local minimum requires all n dimensions to curve up. A saddle point only requires some to curve up and some to curve down. If each dimension has a 50-50 chance of curving either way, the probability that all n curve upward is (0.5)ⁿ. For n = 1000 parameters, the chance of a true local minimum is 2⁻¹⁰⁰⁰ — essentially zero. Almost every critical point in a high-dimensional loss landscape is a saddle point.

Saddle points are dangerous because the gradient is near zero in their neighborhood. Gradient descent slows to a crawl. The loss plateaus, and it looks like training has converged when it hasn't. Momentum helps here — the accumulated velocity can carry you through the saddle region. Adaptive methods like Adam help too because they normalize by the small gradient magnitudes, effectively amplifying the signal.

Loss Landscape Smoothness

The loss landscape is the surface defined by plotting loss as a function of all parameters. For deep networks, this landscape is extraordinarily complex — high-dimensional, non-convex, and full of ridges, valleys, and flat regions. Research has shown that wider networks tend to have smoother loss landscapes, which is one reason wider models are easier to train. Skip connections (as in ResNets) also smooth the landscape dramatically, which is why they enable training of very deep networks.

Hessian Conditioning

The Hessian is the matrix of second derivatives of the loss with respect to parameters. Its eigenvalues tell you about the curvature in every direction. If the largest eigenvalue is much bigger than the smallest (high condition number), the landscape is shaped like a narrow canyon — steep in some directions, flat in others. Gradient descent struggles with poorly conditioned problems because the optimal step size is different for each direction. Adaptive methods and second-order methods address this, but computing the full Hessian is impractical for millions of parameters.

Vanishing and Exploding Gradients

In deep networks, gradients flow backward through layers via the chain rule. Each layer multiplies the gradient by its weight matrix. If those multipliers are consistently less than 1, the gradient vanishes — by the time it reaches the early layers, it's essentially zero, and those layers stop learning. If the multipliers are consistently greater than 1, the gradient explodes — it grows exponentially and causes numerical overflow.

Vanishing gradients plagued early deep networks and were a major motivation for ReLU activations (which have gradient 1 for positive inputs, not the shrinking gradients of sigmoid), residual connections (which add a direct gradient highway), and careful weight initialization (Xavier/He initialization, which sets the initial weight scale to preserve gradient magnitude across layers).

Gradient Clipping

Gradient clipping is a brute-force defense against exploding gradients. Before applying the gradient update, you check the total gradient norm. If it exceeds a threshold, you scale the entire gradient vector down so its norm equals the threshold. The direction is preserved; only the magnitude is capped. 

# Norm clipping — the standard approach
torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)

# Value clipping — less common, clips each element independently
torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_value_(model.parameters(), clip_value=0.5)

Norm clipping is used in virtually every transformer training run. Without it, a single bad batch can produce a gradient spike that destroys hours of training progress. The max_norm value of 1.0 is a common starting point, but you should monitor gradient norms during training and adjust accordingly.

Reasons for Non-Convergence

When training fails to converge, the usual suspects are: learning rate too high (oscillating loss), learning rate too low (stuck in a flat region), bad weight initialization (vanishing or exploding from step one), incorrect loss function (mismatched to the task), data issues (mislabeled examples, preprocessing bugs), and numerical instability (NaN values in half-precision). Debugging optimization is about systematically eliminating these possibilities. Start by overfitting a single small batch — if the model can't memorize 10 examples, the bug is in your code, not your hyperparameters.

Second-Order Methods

Everything we've discussed so far uses only first-order information — the gradient, which tells you the slope. Second-order methods also use curvature information — how the slope itself is changing. This lets them take smarter steps.

Newton's Method

Newton's method uses the Hessian matrix H (second derivatives) to compute the optimal step: 

θ_(t+1) = θ_t − H⁻¹ · ∇L(θ_t)

On a quadratic surface, Newton's method converges in a single step. It automatically adjusts for different curvatures in different directions — no learning rate needed. The problem: computing and inverting the Hessian for a model with n parameters requires O(n²) storage and O(n³) computation. For a model with 100 million parameters, that's 10¹⁶ entries in the Hessian. Completely impossible.

L-BFGS

L-BFGS (Limited-memory Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno) approximates the inverse Hessian using the last m gradient evaluations (typically m = 5–20). It never stores the full Hessian — only a small buffer of vectors. L-BFGS works beautifully for small-to-medium models and convex problems. It's the default optimizer in many scientific computing applications. But it struggles with stochastic gradients (mini-batches) and doesn't scale to the massive models used in modern deep learning.

Natural Gradient and Fisher Information

The natural gradient replaces the Hessian with the Fisher information matrix, which measures how sensitive the model's output distribution is to parameter changes. The insight: in parameter space, the Euclidean distance between two parameter vectors doesn't correspond to the "distance" between the models they define. Two very different parameter vectors might produce nearly identical predictions, and vice versa. The Fisher information matrix provides the correct notion of distance in the space of probability distributions.

Natural gradient descent moves in the direction of steepest descent in distribution space rather than parameter space. It's theoretically elegant and inspired algorithms like TRPO and PPO in reinforcement learning. But computing the full Fisher matrix is as expensive as computing the Hessian, so practical implementations use approximations (like K-FAC).

Why Second-Order Methods Are Rarely Used in Deep Learning

The core issue is scale. Deep learning models have millions to billions of parameters. Any method that needs to store or invert anything approaching an n×n matrix is dead on arrival. First-order methods (SGD, Adam) need only O(n) memory — one or two extra vectors the same size as the parameters. The stochastic noise in mini-batch gradients also undermines the precise curvature estimates that second-order methods rely on. And GPU hardware is optimized for the matrix multiplications in forward/backward passes, not for the operations second-order methods need. The result: first-order methods with clever heuristics (Adam, learning rate schedules) dominate in practice.

Advanced Training Techniques

Mixed-Precision Training (FP16/BF16)

By default, neural network parameters and computations use 32-bit floating-point numbers (FP32). Mixed-precision training performs most operations in 16-bit precision instead — either FP16 (half-precision IEEE) or BF16 (bfloat16, Google's brain float format).

Why bother? Two massive practical benefits. First, memory: 16-bit parameters use half the memory, so you can train a model twice as large (or use twice the batch size) on the same GPU. Second, speed: modern GPUs (NVIDIA Ampere and later) have specialized hardware (Tensor Cores) that execute 16-bit math 2–8x faster than 32-bit. For large models, mixed precision can cut training time in half with negligible accuracy loss.

The "mixed" in mixed precision is key. You keep a master copy of the weights in FP32 for the parameter update step (because the small weight changes from the optimizer can underflow in FP16). The forward pass, backward pass, and gradient computation happen in FP16/BF16 for speed. The optimizer step uses the FP32 master weights.

BF16 has the same exponent range as FP32 (8 exponent bits) but less mantissa precision (7 bits vs 23). This means it rarely overflows or underflows, making it simpler to use than FP16. If your hardware supports BF16 (A100, H100, TPUs), it's the easier choice.

Loss Scaling

FP16 has a limited range — very small numbers get rounded to zero (underflow). Gradients in deep networks are often tiny, so many gradient values vanish in FP16. Loss scaling fixes this: before the backward pass, multiply the loss by a large factor (say 1024). This scales up all the gradients proportionally, keeping them in FP16's representable range. After computing the gradients, you divide by the same factor to get the true gradient values. 

# PyTorch automatic mixed precision
from torch.cuda.amp import autocast, GradScaler

scaler = GradScaler()   # handles loss scaling automatically

for batch_x, batch_y in dataloader:
    optimizer.zero_grad()

    with autocast(dtype=torch.float16):   # forward pass in FP16
        output = model(batch_x)
        loss = criterion(output, batch_y)

    scaler.scale(loss).backward()         # scaled backward pass
    scaler.unscale_(optimizer)            # unscale before clipping
    torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)
    scaler.step(optimizer)               # optimizer step
    scaler.update()                      # adjust scale factor

PyTorch's GradScaler handles the scaling automatically. It starts with a large scale factor and adjusts dynamically — if gradients overflow (become inf or NaN), it skips that update and reduces the scale. If several updates succeed in a row, it increases the scale to maintain maximum precision. With BF16, loss scaling is usually unnecessary because of the wider exponent range.

Batch Size and Learning Rate Interaction

Batch size and learning rate are deeply intertwined. A larger batch size gives a more accurate gradient estimate (less noise), which means you can safely use a larger learning rate. The linear scaling rule says: if you multiply the batch size by k, multiply the learning rate by k as well. This keeps the expected weight update magnitude constant.

But there's a catch. Very large batches (32K+) often lead to worse generalization — the model finds sharper minima. Some noise in the gradient is actually beneficial because it acts as implicit regularization, pushing the optimizer toward flatter minima. This is the generalization gap of large-batch training. Warmup, LAMB/LARS optimizers, and careful scheduling can close this gap partially, but it's an active area of research.

The practical sweet spot depends on your hardware. Fill your GPU memory with the largest batch that fits, then use the linear scaling rule and warmup as a starting recipe. Monitor validation performance and adjust from there.

💡 Optimizer Cheat Sheet

Starting a new project? Use AdamW with lr=1e-3, weight_decay=0.01, a cosine schedule with warmup, and gradient clipping at 1.0. This won't be optimal for every problem, but it will get you a working baseline fast. Tune from there. Switch to SGD+momentum only if you have the time budget for extensive learning rate search — SGD can find better final solutions, but Adam gets you 90% of the way with 10% of the effort.

Optimizer Key Idea When to Use
SGD + Momentum Accumulates velocity from past gradients Computer vision (with careful LR tuning)
Adam Per-parameter adaptive LR + momentum General default, quick prototyping
AdamW Adam with proper weight decay Transformers, LLMs, most modern work
LAMB Layer-wise scaling for large batches Distributed training, batch size > 8K
SAM Seeks flat minima for better generalization When final accuracy matters more than speed
