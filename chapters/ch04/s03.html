Bias-Variance & Overfitting

Chapter 4: ML Fundamentals & Core Concepts Section 3 Critical Concept
TL;DR

Every prediction error breaks into three pieces: bias (your model is too simple to capture reality), variance (your model is too jumpy and memorizes noise), and irreducible noise (randomness no model can fix). High bias is underfitting. High variance is overfitting. The bias-variance decomposition isn't abstract theory — it's the literal explanation for why models fail, and knowing which failure mode you're in determines everything about what to try next.

The Core Insight

Here's something that trips people up for months: overfitting and underfitting aren't just "things that happen." They're consequences of a precise mathematical decomposition. Every squared prediction error you'll ever see can be split into exactly three terms — and once you see the split, the entire field of model selection clicks into place.

The two ways a model can go wrong are mirror images:

  • Too rigid — the model can't capture the real pattern. A straight line through curved data. Training error is high. Test error is high. Both are bad, and no amount of data will fix it because the architecture itself is wrong. This is high bias, a.k.a. underfitting.
  • Too flexible — the model captures the pattern and the noise. It threads through every training point, memorizing accidents as if they were laws. Training error is suspiciously low. Test error is painful. The gap between them is the variance screaming at you. This is high variance, a.k.a. overfitting.

These aren't two separate topics with a vague connection. They're two ends of the same lever. Push the model toward more complexity, bias goes down and variance goes up. Push toward simplicity, the reverse. Every regularization trick, every architecture choice, every "try a bigger model" is you moving that lever.

High Bias = Underfitting

Imagine predicting house prices with a straight line when the real relationship is curved — small houses are cheap, mid-sized are moderate, mansions are exponentially expensive. No matter how you adjust slope and intercept, a line can't bend. That's bias: the error introduced by approximating a complex world with a model that's too limited.

More precisely, bias measures how far your model's average prediction is from truth, averaged over every possible training set drawn from the same distribution. A high-bias model has decided what the answer looks like before seeing data, and stubbornly ignores evidence to the contrary.

The signature: training error is high, test error is high, and the gap between them is small. Both are bad. The gap is small because the model isn't memorizing anything — it's failing to learn. A decision stump (tree with depth 1) on a complex classification boundary. Naïve Bayes on data where features are heavily correlated. Linear regression on a sine curve. Same story every time.

The critical thing to understand: more data won't help. A line fitted to a billion points from a sine curve is still a line. You need a more expressive model, better features, or less regularization.

High Variance = Overfitting

Now flip it. You fit a polynomial with 20 terms to that same house price data. With enough parameters, the curve threads through every training point. Zero training error. Looks great — until you try it on new houses.

That 20-degree polynomial didn't learn "the relationship between size and price." It learned "the exact quirks of these 50 specific houses." One was overpriced because the seller was desperate. Another was cheap because of a noisy neighbor. The model memorized those accidents as if they were patterns.

Variance measures how much your model's predictions would change if you trained it on a different random sample from the same distribution. A high-variance model gives wildly different predictions depending on which training samples it happens to see. Retrain on slightly different data, get a completely different model.

The signature: training error is low, test error is much higher, and the gap between them is the problem. That gap is the variance. Deep unpruned decision trees, high-degree polynomials, k-nearest neighbors with k=1, neural networks with millions of parameters on small datasets — all high-variance suspects.

The good news: more data helps. With more examples, it's harder to memorize your way to a good score. Regularization helps. Simpler models help. Ensembles help (we'll get to why).

The Dartboard Analogy

Picture throwing darts at a target, where the bullseye is the true correct prediction. Train many copies of the same model on different samples from the same distribution. Each model throws one dart.

Low Variance (tight cluster)High Variance (scattered)
Low Bias (cluster centered on bullseye)🎯 The dream. Accurate and consistent.Scattered but correct on average. This is why ensembles work — averaging scattered darts centered on the bullseye gives you a bullseye.
High Bias (cluster off-center)Reliably wrong. Same wrong answer every time.Wrong and inconsistent. The worst case.

The key insight hiding in this analogy: bias is about the center of your dart cluster, variance is about the spread. You need different fixes for each.

The Mathematical Decomposition

For any model f̂ predicting a target y at a point x, the expected squared error decomposes exactly:

E[(y - f̂(x))²]  =  Bias(f̂(x))²  +  Variance(f̂(x))  +  σ²

Bias² = [E[f̂(x)] − f(x)]². The squared distance between your model's average prediction (over all possible training sets) and the true function. How far off your model architecture is from truth, systematically.

Variance = E[(f̂(x) − E[f̂(x)])²]. How much your predictions bounce around their own mean when you retrain on different data. The "jitter" of your model.

Irreducible noise (σ²) = the randomness in the data itself. Even a perfect model can't predict which way a buyer's emotional state will push a house price. This term sets the floor — you can't beat it, and knowing it exists stops you from chasing impossible accuracy.

Where to Focus

When someone says "our model achieves 95% accuracy," the remaining 5% might be almost entirely irreducible noise (model is near-optimal) or almost entirely bias/variance (there's room to improve). This decomposition tells you where your effort will actually pay off.

Seeing It in Code: Polynomial Regression

Theory is nice. Let's measure bias and variance directly. We'll generate data from a known function (sine curve + noise), fit three models of increasing flexibility, and repeat 300 times with different random training sets. This lets us compute the actual bias² and variance for each model — not estimates, the real thing.

import numpy as np
from sklearn.preprocessing import PolynomialFeatures
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
from sklearn.tree import DecisionTreeRegressor
from sklearn.pipeline import make_pipeline

np.random.seed(42)

# The truth: a sine curve
def true_function(x):
    return np.sin(1.5 * np.pi * x)

def make_data(n=30, noise=0.25):
    X = np.sort(np.random.uniform(0, 1, n))
    y = true_function(X) + np.random.normal(0, noise, n)
    return X.reshape(-1, 1), y

X_test = np.linspace(0, 1, 200).reshape(-1, 1)
y_true = true_function(X_test.ravel())

models = {
    "Linear (degree=1)":     lambda: make_pipeline(
        PolynomialFeatures(1), LinearRegression()),
    "Polynomial (degree=5)": lambda: make_pipeline(
        PolynomialFeatures(5), LinearRegression()),
    "Polynomial (degree=15)": lambda: make_pipeline(
        PolynomialFeatures(15), LinearRegression()),
}

n_experiments = 300

print(f"{'Model':<25} {'Bias²':<10} {'Variance':<10} {'σ²':<10} {'Total MSE'}")
print("-" * 65)

for name, make_model in models.items():
    preds = np.zeros((n_experiments, len(X_test)))
    for i in range(n_experiments):
        X_train, y_train = make_data(n=30, noise=0.25)
        model = make_model()
        model.fit(X_train, y_train)
        preds[i] = model.predict(X_test).ravel()

    mean_pred = preds.mean(axis=0)
    bias_sq   = np.mean((mean_pred - y_true) ** 2)
    variance  = np.mean(preds.var(axis=0))
    noise_var = 0.25 ** 2  # σ² = 0.0625
    total_mse = np.mean((preds - y_true) ** 2)

    print(f"{name:<25} {bias_sq:<10.4f} {variance:<10.4f} "
          f"{noise_var:<10.4f} {total_mse:.4f}")

Typical output:

Model                     Bias²      Variance   σ²         Total MSE
-----------------------------------------------------------------
Linear (degree=1)         0.1838     0.0196     0.0625     0.2035
Polynomial (degree=5)     0.0003     0.0182     0.0625     0.0185
Polynomial (degree=15)    0.0005     0.1253     0.0625     0.1258

Read the columns and the entire tradeoff materializes:

  • Degree 1 — Massive bias (0.18). A line can't approximate a sine curve. Variance is tiny (0.02) because a line looks basically the same no matter what training data you use. Consistently wrong.
  • Degree 5 — Bias essentially zero (0.0003). Five terms is enough to capture the sine shape. Variance is modest (0.018). This is the sweet spot — total error is lowest.
  • Degree 15 — Bias is still near zero (on average, it can capture the shape). But variance explodes to 0.125 — each new training set produces a wildly different wiggly curve that threads through noise. Total error is 6× worse than degree 5.

That's the tradeoff in a table. Degree 1 fails because it can't learn. Degree 15 fails because it learns too much. Degree 5 wins because it learns the signal and ignores the noise.

How to Diagnose: Learning Curves

In practice you don't run 300 experiments to measure bias and variance. You plot learning curves — training error and validation error as training progresses (or as you add more data). The shape of these curves tells you exactly what's wrong.

High Bias (Underfitting)

Both curves plateau high. Small gap between them. The model can't even fit the training data, so there's nothing to memorize. You need more model, not more data.

High Variance (Overfitting)

Training error keeps dropping because the model is memorizing. Validation error drops at first (genuine learning), then rises — that's the moment memorization starts winning over generalization. The green dot is where you should stop.

Quick Diagnosis

High train error + high test error + small gap → High bias. Needs more model.
Low train error + high test error + large gap → High variance. Needs constraints.
Low train error + low test error + small gap → Ship it.

The Remedy Toolkit

Once you know which failure mode you're in, the fixes are specific. Don't guess — diagnose first, then apply the right tool.

ProblemRemedyWhy It Works
Overfitting
(high variance)		 More training dataHarder to memorize 100k examples than 100
Regularization (L1/L2)Penalizes large weights → simpler effective model
Dropout / early stoppingPrevents the model from fully memorizing
Ensembles (bagging)Averaging many high-variance models cancels out the jitter

(high bias)		 More complex modelGive the model capacity to capture the real pattern
More / better featuresThe model can't learn patterns in variables it doesn't have

Train longerMaybe the optimizer hasn't converged yet

Notice the symmetry. Overfitting remedies add constraints. Underfitting remedies remove them. Applying an overfitting fix to an underfitting problem makes things worse, and vice versa. This is why diagnosis comes first.

Data augmentation deserves a special mention for overfitting — it creates new training examples by applying realistic transformations (flips, rotations, synonym swaps). You're effectively increasing your dataset size without collecting new data, and forcing the model to be invariant to things that shouldn't change the answer.

Regularization Deep Dive: L1 vs L2

Regularization adds a penalty to the loss function: "not only must you fit the data, you must also keep your weights small." But how you measure "small" changes everything.

L2 (Ridge) — The Circle Constraint

L2 adds the sum of squared weights to the loss: Loss + α·Σwᵢ². Geometrically, this constrains the weights to lie within a hypersphere (circle in 2D). The optimal point is where the loss contours first touch that sphere. Because a sphere has no corners, the touching point almost never lands exactly on an axis — so L2 makes weights small but rarely exactly zero.

L1 (Lasso) — The Diamond Constraint

L1 adds the sum of absolute weights: Loss + α·Σ|wᵢ|. Geometrically, this constrains weights within a hypercube (diamond in 2D). Diamonds have corners, and those corners sit on the axes. The loss contours are much more likely to first touch the diamond at a corner — meaning some weights get pushed to exactly zero. L1 performs automatic feature selection.

This geometric difference — corners vs. smooth boundary — is the entire reason L1 produces sparse models and L2 doesn't. Not hand-waving; literal geometry.

from sklearn.linear_model import Ridge, Lasso, LinearRegression
from sklearn.datasets import make_regression
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error
import numpy as np

# 50 features, only 10 actually matter
X, y = make_regression(n_samples=100, n_features=50,
                       n_informative=10, noise=25, random_state=42)
X_train, X_val, y_train, y_val = train_test_split(
    X, y, test_size=0.3, random_state=42)

# No regularization — let it overfit
ols = LinearRegression().fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"OLS        → Train MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_train, ols.predict(X_train)):>8.0f}, "
      f"Val MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_val, ols.predict(X_val)):>8.0f}")

# L2: all weights shrink toward zero
ridge = Ridge(alpha=10.0).fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"Ridge(α=10)→ Train MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_train, ridge.predict(X_train)):>8.0f}, "
      f"Val MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_val, ridge.predict(X_val)):>8.0f}")

# L1: irrelevant weights get killed to exactly 0
lasso = Lasso(alpha=1.0).fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"Lasso(α=1) → Train MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_train, lasso.predict(X_train)):>8.0f}, "
      f"Val MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_val, lasso.predict(X_val)):>8.0f}")
print(f"Lasso zeroed out {np.sum(lasso.coef_ == 0)} of {len(lasso.coef_)} weights")

OLS overfits (low train MSE, high val MSE). Ridge closes the gap. Lasso closes the gap and zeroes out the junk features. The regularization strength α controls how aggressively you penalize — too much and you underfit, too little and overfitting persists. Tune it with cross-validation.

Elastic Net — Best of Both

In practice, Elastic Net combines L1 and L2 with a mixing parameter. When features are correlated (which is basically always in real data), pure L1 arbitrarily picks one from a correlated group and zeros the rest. Elastic Net keeps the group together while still performing selection. It's the default choice in production when you're not sure which penalty to use.

Beyond Penalties: Other Regularization

Early stopping — monitor validation error and stop training when it starts rising. No changes to the model or loss function needed. You're just stopping the optimizer before it has time to memorize.

Dropout — during each training step, randomly zero out a fraction of neurons. Forces the network to develop redundant representations, preventing any single neuron from becoming too important. It's like training an ensemble of sub-networks that share weights.

Batch normalization — originally designed to speed up training, but the per-mini-batch statistics introduce noise that acts as a mild regularizer. Sometimes adding batch norm lets you reduce or remove dropout.

from sklearn.neural_network import MLPRegressor

mlp = MLPRegressor(
    hidden_layer_sizes=(100, 50),
    max_iter=1000,
    early_stopping=True,         # stop when val error rises
    validation_fraction=0.15,
    n_iter_no_change=10,         # patience: 10 epochs
    random_state=42
)
mlp.fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"Stopped at epoch {mlp.n_iter_} "
      f"(Val MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_val, mlp.predict(X_val)):.0f})")

The Modern Reality: Double Descent

Everything above tells a clean story: more complexity → more variance → worse test error after a point. For most practical ML work, that story is true and sufficient. But if you're working with deep learning, there's a plot twist.

In 2019, Belkin et al. showed that if you keep increasing model complexity past the point where the model perfectly fits training data (the interpolation threshold), test error doesn't keep rising. It spikes at the threshold, then starts decreasing again.

Why? At the interpolation threshold, the model barely has enough capacity to fit training data, and has to use extreme weight configurations to do so — test error spikes. But once the model is heavily overparameterized, there are many ways to perfectly fit the training data, and gradient descent finds the "simplest" one (smallest weight norms). That simple interpolating solution generalizes well.

This is why modern deep neural networks with millions of parameters can generalize despite being wildly overparameterized. It's been observed in linear models, decision trees, and deep networks alike.

Don't Throw Away Regularization

Double descent doesn't mean regularization is useless — it actually smooths the curve and reduces the spike. It explains why overparameterization works, not why you should skip best practices. For tabular data, small-to-medium datasets, and classical algorithms (the vast majority of production ML), the traditional tradeoff is your primary diagnostic tool.

Learning Theory Foundations (Nice to Know)

There's rigorous math behind all of this. You don't need the formulas for day-to-day work, but knowing these exist helps you read papers and understand why the rules of thumb work.

PAC Learning (Probably Approximately Correct) — answers the question: "how many training examples do I need so that my model is probably (with high confidence) approximately (within some error tolerance) correct on new data?" The answer depends on how large your model class is. More flexibility demands more evidence. That's the entire intuition.

VC Dimension (Vapnik-Chervonenkis) — measures the "capacity" of a model class. It's the largest number of data points the model can shatter (classify correctly under every possible labeling). Linear classifiers in d dimensions have VC dimension d+1. The generalization bound roughly says: test error ≤ training error + √(VC_dim / n). Higher VC dimension or less data → looser guarantee → bigger potential gap between train and test.

Rademacher Complexity — a more modern, data-dependent alternative to VC dimension. Take your data, randomize the labels, and see how well your model class can fit pure noise. If it can fit random labels well, it's prone to overfitting. Gives tighter bounds in practice than VC dimension.

What You Should Now Be Able To Do

  • Look at training vs. validation error and immediately diagnose whether you're dealing with high bias or high variance
  • Explain the bias-variance decomposition — Bias² + Variance + σ² — and what each term means in practice
  • Predict whether "get more data" will help (it fixes variance, not bias)
  • Choose between L1 and L2 regularization based on whether you want feature selection (L1) or general shrinkage (L2)
  • Explain geometrically why L1 produces sparse models (diamond corners) and L2 doesn't (smooth sphere)
  • Draw the learning curves for overfitting and underfitting from memory
  • Apply the right remedy from the toolkit — and know that applying the wrong remedy (overfitting fix for an underfitting problem) makes things worse
  • Know that double descent exists and roughly why overparameterized models can still generalize
  • Explain VC dimension in one sentence to someone who asks
