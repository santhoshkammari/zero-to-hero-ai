Overfitting and Underfitting

Chapter 4: ML Fundamentals & Core Concepts 6 subtopics
TL;DR

Your model can fail in two opposite ways: it can memorize the training data instead of learning the pattern (overfitting), or it can be too simple to capture the pattern at all (underfitting). Learning curves — plots of training vs. validation error over time — are the best diagnostic tool. The remedies are mirror images: overfitting needs constraints (regularization, more data, simpler models), underfitting needs capacity (more features, bigger models, less regularization). Advanced theory like VC dimension and PAC learning formalize when and why generalization works, and the double descent phenomenon challenges the classical story entirely.

The Core Problem: Memorization vs. Generalization

Imagine you're studying for a history exam. One strategy is to memorize every sentence in the textbook word-for-word. You'd ace a quiz that pulls questions directly from the book — but you'd be lost if the exam asks you to apply the ideas to a scenario you've never seen. Another strategy is to skim two paragraphs the night before. You'd have a vague sense of the material but couldn't answer anything with depth. The sweet spot is understanding the underlying ideas well enough to handle new questions.

Machine learning models face this exact tension. We train them on a fixed dataset, but their whole purpose is to perform well on new, unseen data. The gap between training performance and real-world performance is the central drama of ML, and it shows up as two failure modes: overfitting and underfitting.

Overfitting

What It Is

Overfitting happens when a model learns the training data too well — it memorizes not just the real patterns but also the noise, outliers, and random quirks that are specific to that particular dataset. The model performs brilliantly on training data and poorly on anything new. It's the ML equivalent of a student who memorized the answer key instead of understanding the subject.

What Causes It

The most common cause is a model that is too complex relative to the amount of data. A polynomial of degree 20 has enough flexibility to pass through every single data point in a 20-point dataset, fitting the noise perfectly. But that wiggly curve tells you nothing useful about the underlying trend. Too little training data makes the problem worse — the fewer examples you have, the easier it is for a powerful model to memorize them. High-dimensional noisy features also contribute: if your dataset has 500 features but only 100 samples, the model has so many knobs to turn that it can always find some combination that fits the training data by coincidence.

How to Detect It

The telltale sign is a gap between training performance and validation performance. If your model gets 99% accuracy on training data but 72% on validation data, it has memorized training-specific patterns that don't generalize. This is why you always evaluate on held-out data that the model never saw during training.

Learning Curves for Overfitting

A learning curve is a plot that tracks your model's error (or accuracy) on both the training set and the validation set as you vary some quantity — typically the number of training examples or the number of training epochs. For an overfitting model, the learning curve has a characteristic shape:

The training error keeps falling because the model is memorizing more of the training set. The validation error first decreases (the model is genuinely learning) and then starts increasing — that's the moment the model starts memorizing noise instead of learning patterns. The point where validation error is lowest is the sweet spot.

Underfitting

What It Is

Underfitting is the opposite problem. The model is too simple to capture the actual patterns in the data. It's like trying to fit a straight line through data that clearly follows a curve. The model hasn't learned enough from the training data, so it performs poorly on everything — training data and validation data alike.

What Causes It

The typical cause is a model with insufficient capacity — meaning it doesn't have enough parameters or flexibility to represent the true relationship. Using linear regression when the data is quadratic is a classic example. Too few features can also cause underfitting: if the true pattern depends on variables you haven't included, no amount of training will help. Excessive regularization (which we'll cover below) can also squash a model into underfitting by constraining it too aggressively.

How to Detect It

Look for poor performance on both the training set and the validation set. If your model can't even fit the training data well, it doesn't have enough capacity. This is often the easier problem to diagnose — it's obvious when your model is doing badly everywhere.

Learning Curves for Underfitting

Both curves plateau at a high error value. They may converge close together — the model isn't doing well on training data, so there's no "memorization gap." The problem isn't generalization; it's that the model can't learn the pattern in the first place. Adding more training data won't help here — you need a more capable model.

Remedies for Overfitting

Overfitting is the more common problem in practice, especially with modern deep learning. Here's your toolkit for fighting it, roughly in order of how often you should reach for each tool.

Regularization: L1 and L2

Regularization means adding a penalty to the loss function that discourages the model from becoming too complex. Think of it as telling the model: "Not only do you need to fit the data, but you also need to keep your weights small." This prevents the model from contorting itself to memorize noise.

L2 regularization (also called Ridge) adds the sum of the squared weights to the loss. It pushes all weights toward zero but rarely makes them exactly zero. It's like a gentle tug saying "keep things small." L1 regularization (also called Lasso) adds the sum of the absolute values of the weights. It has a sharper effect: it tends to push some weights to exactly zero, effectively performing feature selection — the model learns to ignore irrelevant inputs entirely.

from sklearn.linear_model import Ridge, Lasso
from sklearn.datasets import make_regression
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error
import numpy as np

# Create a noisy dataset with some irrelevant features
X, y = make_regression(n_samples=100, n_features=50,
                       n_informative=10, noise=25, random_state=42)
X_train, X_val, y_train, y_val = train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.3,
                                                    random_state=42)

# No regularization: ordinary least squares
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
ols = LinearRegression().fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"OLS        → Train MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_train, ols.predict(X_train)):.0f}, "
      f"Val MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_val, ols.predict(X_val)):.0f}")

# L2 regularization (Ridge)
ridge = Ridge(alpha=10.0).fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"Ridge(α=10)→ Train MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_train, ridge.predict(X_train)):.0f}, "
      f"Val MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_val, ridge.predict(X_val)):.0f}")

# L1 regularization (Lasso)
lasso = Lasso(alpha=1.0).fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"Lasso(α=1) → Train MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_train, lasso.predict(X_train)):.0f}, "
      f"Val MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_val, lasso.predict(X_val)):.0f}")
print(f"Lasso zero-weights: {np.sum(lasso.coef_ == 0)} out of {len(lasso.coef_)}")

You'll typically see OLS overfit badly (low train MSE, high val MSE), Ridge close the gap, and Lasso close the gap while also zeroing out the irrelevant features. The regularization strength (α) controls how aggressively you penalize — too much and you'll underfit; too little and overfitting persists.

Reducing Model Complexity

Sometimes the most effective fix is the most obvious one. If your decision tree has depth 50, try depth 10. If your neural network has 12 layers, try 4. Fewer parameters means less opportunity to memorize.

Getting More Training Data

More data is the single most reliable remedy for overfitting. With more examples, the model has to find patterns that generalize — it can't memorize its way to a good score when there are too many examples to memorize. In practice, this is often the hardest remedy because collecting labeled data is expensive.

Data Augmentation

Data augmentation creates new training examples by applying realistic transformations to existing ones. For images, you might flip, rotate, crop, or adjust colors. For text, you might swap synonyms or translate back and forth between languages. This increases the effective size of your dataset without collecting new data and forces the model to be invariant to transformations that shouldn't change the answer.

Early Stopping

Early stopping is exactly what the learning curve suggested: you monitor validation error during training and stop when it starts increasing, even if the training error is still decreasing. This is a form of regularization that requires no changes to the model or loss function — you're just stopping the optimizer before it has time to memorize.

from sklearn.neural_network import MLPRegressor

mlp = MLPRegressor(hidden_layer_sizes=(100, 50),
                   max_iter=1000,
                   early_stopping=True,        # Enable early stopping
                   validation_fraction=0.15,    # Hold out 15% for monitoring
                   n_iter_no_change=10,         # Stop if no improvement for 10 epochs
                   random_state=42)
mlp.fit(X_train, y_train)
print(f"Stopped at epoch: {mlp.n_iter_}")
print(f"Val MSE: {mean_squared_error(y_val, mlp.predict(X_val)):.0f}")

Dropout

Dropout is a regularization technique specific to neural networks. During each training step, you randomly "turn off" a fraction of neurons — they contribute nothing to that step's forward pass or backward pass. This prevents any single neuron from becoming too important and forces the network to develop redundant representations. At inference time, all neurons are active. Dropout is like training an ensemble of sub-networks that share weights.

Ensemble Methods

Instead of relying on a single model that might overfit, you train multiple models and combine their predictions. Bagging (like Random Forests) trains many models on different random subsets of the data and averages their outputs. Boosting (like Gradient Boosting) trains models sequentially, each one focusing on the mistakes of the previous. Ensembles reduce variance — the random errors of individual models cancel out.

Feature Selection

If some of your features are irrelevant or noisy, removing them reduces the model's opportunity to memorize noise. L1 regularization does this automatically, but you can also use techniques like correlation analysis, mutual information, or recursive feature elimination.

Remedies for Underfitting

Underfitting means your model isn't powerful enough. The remedies are roughly the opposite of the overfitting fixes.

Increasing Model Complexity

Add more layers to your neural network, increase the degree of your polynomial, or allow deeper trees. More parameters give the model more flexibility to capture complex patterns.

Adding More Features

Your model might be underperforming because it's missing information. If you're predicting house prices with only square footage, adding number of bedrooms, neighborhood, and year built could dramatically improve things.

Feature Engineering

Feature engineering means creating new input features from existing ones that make the pattern easier for the model to learn. Instead of feeding raw timestamps, you might extract hour-of-day, day-of-week, and is-holiday. Instead of feeding latitude and longitude separately, you might compute distance-to-city-center. A good feature can turn an underfitting model into a well-fitting one without changing the model at all.

Reducing Regularization

If you're using L1/L2 penalties, dropout, or other constraints, try reducing them. You may have constrained the model so heavily that it can't learn the true pattern. This is a common mistake — someone applies heavy regularization as a "best practice" without checking whether the model is actually overfitting.

Training Longer

Sometimes a model appears to underfit simply because you haven't trained it long enough. If the training error is still decreasing when you stop, give it more epochs. Check the learning curve: if both errors are still dropping, you're not done yet.

Advanced Regularization Techniques

Beyond the basics, modern deep learning has discovered several nuanced ways that regularization appears (sometimes unexpectedly).

Batch Normalization as Implicit Regularization

Batch normalization was originally designed to speed up training by normalizing each layer's inputs to zero mean and unit variance. But it turns out it also has a regularization effect. Because the normalization statistics (mean and variance) are computed per mini-batch, they introduce a small amount of noise into the network — different mini-batches produce different statistics. This noise acts like a mild regularizer, and in practice you sometimes see that adding batch norm lets you reduce or remove dropout.

Weight Decay vs. L2 Regularization with Adam

With plain SGD (stochastic gradient descent), weight decay and L2 regularization are mathematically identical: both add a term that shrinks weights toward zero. But with adaptive optimizers like Adam, they're not the same. L2 regularization adds the penalty to the loss, so the gradient of that penalty gets scaled by Adam's adaptive learning rate. Weight decay directly subtracts a fraction of each weight at each step, bypassing the adaptive scaling. The paper "Decoupled Weight Decay Regularization" (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) showed that the decoupled version — called AdamW — works noticeably better. If you're using Adam, prefer AdamW with weight decay over Adam with L2 penalty in the loss.

Multi-Task Learning as Regularization

When you train a model to do two related tasks simultaneously — say, predicting both sentiment and topic of a text — the shared layers have to learn representations that are useful for both tasks. This constraint prevents the model from over-specializing on one task's noise. Multi-task learning acts as an implicit regularizer because it forces the model to learn more general features.

Parameter Norm Penalties vs. Constrained Optimization

L1 and L2 penalties are soft constraints: they discourage large weights but allow them if the data really warrants it. An alternative is hard constraints: explicitly cap the norm of each weight vector to a maximum value (this is called max-norm regularization). If during training a weight vector's norm exceeds the cap, you project it back. Both approaches work, but they behave differently near the constraint boundary and interact differently with learning rate schedules.

The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis

The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (Frankle & Carlin, 2019) says something surprising: inside a large, randomly initialized neural network, there exists a small subnetwork (the "winning ticket") that — if trained in isolation from the same initial weights — can match the full network's accuracy. In other words, most of the network's parameters are redundant. You can find these tickets by training the full network, pruning the smallest weights, and resetting the surviving weights to their original initialization. This has deep implications for regularization: the large network's initial overparameterization helps optimization find good solutions, but the actual solution is sparse.

Learning Theory Foundations

Everything we've discussed so far is practical and empirical. But there's a rigorous mathematical theory behind generalization that tells us when and why a model trained on finite data will work on new data. These ideas are worth understanding even if you never use the formulas directly — they shape how we think about ML.

PAC Learning

PAC stands for Probably Approximately Correct. It answers this question: how many training examples do I need so that my model is probably (with high confidence) approximately (within some error tolerance) correct on new data?

Here's the intuition. Suppose you're learning to classify emails as spam or not-spam, and the true pattern is some function you don't know. You have a set of candidate functions (your model class). PAC theory says: if your model class isn't too large, and you have enough examples, then whatever function from your model class fits the training data will also work on new data — with probability at least 1−δ, your error will be at most ε. The required number of training examples grows with the "size" of your model class and with how small you want ε and δ to be.

Concretely: a model class that can express a thousand different decision boundaries needs more training data than one that can only express ten. More flexibility demands more evidence.

VC Dimension

PAC theory says "if your model class isn't too large." But how do you measure the "size" of a model class? That's what VC dimension (Vapnik-Chervonenkis dimension) does. It measures the capacity of a class of models — how complex the patterns it can represent are.

The formal definition: the VC dimension is the largest number of data points that the model class can shatter, meaning classify correctly in every possible labeling. For example, consider a straight line dividing a 2D plane into two halves (a linear classifier in 2D). You can place 3 points and, for every possible assignment of +/− labels to those 3 points, find some line that separates them correctly. But with 4 points, there's always at least one labeling that no line can separate (think of an XOR pattern — diagonal opposites have the same label). So the VC dimension of linear classifiers in 2D is 3. In general, linear classifiers in d dimensions have VC dimension d+1.

Why does this matter? The VC dimension directly appears in generalization bounds. The bound roughly says:

Generalization Bound (Simplified)

Test error ≤ Training error + √(VC_dim / n), where n is the number of training examples. Higher VC dimension or less data means a looser guarantee — a bigger potential gap between training and test performance.

This formalizes the intuition that more complex models (higher VC dimension) need more data to generalize.

Structural Risk Minimization

Structural Risk Minimization (SRM) turns the VC-dimension bound into a practical strategy. Instead of picking one model class and hoping, you consider a nested sequence of model classes with increasing complexity: H₁ ⊂ H₂ ⊂ H₃ ⊂ ... (for example, polynomials of degree 1, 2, 3, ...). For each class, you compute the sum of training error plus the VC-based penalty. SRM tells you to pick the class that minimizes this total — it's the principled version of "find the sweet spot between underfitting and overfitting." This is the theoretical ancestor of practical techniques like cross-validation and regularization.

Rademacher Complexity

Rademacher complexity is a more modern alternative to VC dimension for measuring model class capacity. The idea: take your training data, randomly flip the labels (assign +1 or −1 at random), and see how well the best function in your model class can fit those random labels. If your model class can fit random noise well, it has high Rademacher complexity and is prone to overfitting. If it can't fit random noise, it's constrained and more likely to generalize.

The advantage over VC dimension is that Rademacher complexity depends on the actual data distribution, not just the model class. It gives tighter (more useful) bounds in practice.

The Double Descent Phenomenon

Classical learning theory tells a clean story: as model complexity increases, test error first decreases (less underfitting), then increases (more overfitting). You'd expect a U-shaped curve. But in 2019, Belkin et al. showed that this is incomplete. If you keep increasing complexity past the point where the model can perfectly fit the training data (the interpolation threshold), something unexpected happens: test error starts decreasing again.

This is the double descent curve. In the classical regime (fewer parameters than training points), the bias-variance tradeoff plays out as expected. At the interpolation threshold (parameters ≈ training points), the model barely has enough capacity to fit training data and has to use extreme weight configurations to do so — test error spikes. But once the model is heavily overparameterized (far more parameters than data points), there are many ways to perfectly fit the training data, and gradient descent tends to find the "simplest" one (in terms of parameter norms). That simple interpolating solution generalizes well.

This is why modern deep neural networks — which are wildly overparameterized — can generalize despite having millions more parameters than training points. Double descent has been observed in linear models, decision trees, random features, and deep networks. It challenges the classical wisdom that you should always choose the simplest model that fits, and partly explains why bigger models often work better in deep learning.

Practical Note

Double descent doesn't mean regularization is useless. Regularization smooths the curve and reduces the spike at the interpolation threshold. In practice, you should still use regularization — it makes the training process more robust. Double descent explains why overparameterization works, not why you should avoid regularization.

Regularization as Bayesian Prior

There's a beautiful connection between regularization and Bayesian statistics. When you add an L2 penalty to your loss function, you're mathematically equivalent to putting a Gaussian (normal) prior on the weights. This prior says "I believe the weights are likely close to zero, following a bell curve." The regularization strength α controls how tight that bell curve is — larger α means a stronger belief in small weights.

L1 regularization corresponds to a Laplace prior — a distribution that is sharply peaked at zero with heavy tails. This is why L1 produces exactly-zero weights: the Laplace prior concentrates probability mass right at zero, which means the posterior (the updated belief after seeing data) often lands at zero for uninformative features.

# Demonstrating: stronger L2 = tighter Gaussian prior on weights
import numpy as np
from sklearn.linear_model import Ridge

X, y = make_regression(n_samples=50, n_features=20,
                       n_informative=5, noise=30, random_state=7)

for alpha in [0.01, 1.0, 100.0]:
    model = Ridge(alpha=alpha).fit(X, y)
    weight_norm = np.linalg.norm(model.coef_)
    print(f"α={alpha:6.2f} → weight L2 norm: {weight_norm:.2f}")
# Higher alpha → smaller weight norms (tighter Gaussian prior)

This Bayesian perspective matters because it gives regularization a principled interpretation: you're not just "adding a penalty" — you're encoding a prior belief about what reasonable models look like. If you have domain knowledge about which features are important, you can even use different priors for different weights.

Putting It All Together

Diagnosing your model starts with one question: is it underfitting or overfitting? Plot the learning curves. If both errors are high, add capacity. If there's a gap between training and validation, add constraints. The remedies are specific — L1 for feature selection, L2 for general shrinkage, dropout for neural nets, ensembles for variance reduction, more data when you can get it. Under the hood, learning theory tells us this works because constraining model complexity (whether through regularization, early stopping, or architecture choices) controls the gap between training and test performance. And double descent reminds us that in the modern overparameterized regime, the story is richer than the classical U-shaped curve — but the practical advice remains: use regularization, get more data, and always evaluate on held-out sets.
