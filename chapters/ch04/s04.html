Cross-Validation

Chapter 4: ML Fundamentals & Core Concepts Section 4 Core Skill
TL;DR

A single train/test split is a coin flip dressed up as science. Cross-validation trains and evaluates your model on multiple different slices of data, giving you a distribution of performance scores instead of one lucky (or unlucky) number. K-Fold is your default workhorse. Stratified K-Fold is the actual default for classification. Time Series Split is mandatory for temporal data. Nested CV is how you honestly report performance when you're also tuning hyperparameters. Always report mean and standard deviation — the mean alone hides instability.

The Problem with a Single Split

You have 1,000 patient records. You want to predict malignant tumors. You split 80/20, train, test, get 94% accuracy. Done?

Shuffle the data, split again. Now it's 88%. Shuffle once more — 96%. Your model didn't change. The data partition changed. And that swing — eight full percentage points — is not signal. It's the luck of which examples landed where.

With small or medium datasets (anything under tens of thousands of rows), this randomness can dominate the result. You're measuring the quality of the split, not the quality of the model. That's backwards.

What Cross-Validation Actually Does

Cross-validation repeats the split→train→test cycle multiple times, each time holding out a different chunk. Instead of one score, you get several. Their average is a far more stable estimate of real-world performance, and their spread (standard deviation) tells you how sensitive your model is to the particular data it trained on.

One test score is an anecdote. A distribution of test scores is evidence.

Better Use of Limited Data

There's a practical bonus. When data is scarce, locking away 20% for testing feels wasteful. In 5-fold CV, every example gets used for testing exactly once and for training four times. You evaluate on all the data without ever testing on something you trained on. That's a much better deal when you're data-poor.

The Techniques

Hold-Out Validation

Split once. Train on the big chunk, evaluate on the small chunk. Optionally carve out a third piece (validation set) for tuning.

Hold-out is fast, simple, and perfectly fine when you have hundreds of thousands of rows — at that scale, the randomness of a single split washes out. It's the starting point, not the finish line. For anything under ~50k rows, you should do better.

K-Fold Cross-Validation

This is the workhorse. Divide the dataset into k equally-sized chunks called folds. In each round, one fold is the test set, the remaining k−1 are training data. Train from scratch each round, record the score, average them at the end.

The standard choices are k=5 or k=10. Why those? k=5 is a good bias-variance tradeoff for the estimate itself — each fold uses 80% of data for training, which is close enough to "all the data" that the per-fold models are decent. k=10 uses 90% per fold (even closer) but takes twice as long. In practice, 5-fold is the default unless you have a specific reason to want 10.

from sklearn.model_selection import KFold, cross_val_score
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.datasets import load_breast_cancer
import numpy as np

X, y = load_breast_cancer(return_X_y=True)
model = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=100, random_state=42)

kf = KFold(n_splits=5, shuffle=True, random_state=42)
scores = cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=kf, scoring='accuracy')

print(f"Fold scores: {scores}")
print(f"Mean accuracy: {scores.mean():.4f} ± {scores.std():.4f}")
# Fold scores: [0.9649 0.9561 0.9474 0.9649 0.9558]
# Mean accuracy: 0.9578 ± 0.0068

Notice shuffle=True. Without it, folds follow the original row order — disastrous if the data was sorted by class label. Always shuffle unless your data has a temporal component.

⚠️ Pin Your Random Seed

shuffle=True without random_state means different folds every run. Your results become unreproducible. Always set the seed when you want comparable experiments.

Stratified K-Fold

Suppose your dataset is 90% negative, 10% positive. A regular K-Fold split might, by chance, dump most positives into one or two folds. The model trained without those folds barely knows the minority class exists.

Stratified K-Fold ensures each fold has approximately the same class distribution as the full dataset. For classification, this should be your default. Full stop.

from sklearn.model_selection import StratifiedKFold

skf = StratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, shuffle=True, random_state=42)
scores = cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=skf, scoring='accuracy')

print(f"Stratified scores: {scores}")
print(f"Mean accuracy: {scores.mean():.4f} ± {scores.std():.4f}")
# Stratified scores: [0.9561 0.9737 0.9561 0.9649 0.9469]
# Mean accuracy: 0.9596 ± 0.0090

Fun fact: when you pass an integer to cv=5 in scikit-learn for a classifier, it silently uses Stratified K-Fold under the hood. But being explicit is better than being clever — future-you will thank present-you.

Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOOCV)

K-Fold taken to the extreme: k = n (number of samples). Each round holds out exactly one example, trains on all the rest. Repeat for every single example.

The appeal: maximum training data per fold, deterministic results (no randomness from the split). The cost: if you have 10,000 examples, you train 10,000 models. It also has a counterintuitive property — despite using nearly all data for training, LOOCV has high variance as a performance estimator, because the training sets overlap almost completely, making the fold scores highly correlated.

Use LOOCV for very small datasets (under ~100 samples) where every data point is precious. Everywhere else, 5-fold or 10-fold gives you a better tradeoff between computation and estimation quality.

Time Series Split

Everything above assumes data points are independent — shuffling is fine. Time series data violates that assumption hard.

If you're predicting tomorrow's stock price, you can't train on next month's data and test on last week's. That's temporal leakage — using future information to predict the past. Your model looks spectacular in evaluation and falls apart in production. I've seen teams waste months chasing phantom accuracy from exactly this mistake.

Time Series Split uses an expanding training window. Fold 1 trains on months 1–2, tests on month 3. Fold 2 trains on months 1–3, tests on month 4. The training set always precedes the test set in time. Always.

from sklearn.model_selection import TimeSeriesSplit
import numpy as np

# Simulate 1000 time-ordered observations
n_samples = 1000
tscv = TimeSeriesSplit(n_splits=5)

for i, (train_idx, test_idx) in enumerate(tscv.split(np.arange(n_samples))):
    print(f"Fold {i}: Train [{train_idx[0]}..{train_idx[-1]}] "
          f"({len(train_idx)} samples) → "
          f"Test [{test_idx[0]}..{test_idx[-1]}] "
          f"({len(test_idx)} samples)")
# Fold 0: Train [0..165]   (166 samples) → Test [166..332]  (167 samples)
# Fold 1: Train [0..332]   (333 samples) → Test [333..499]  (167 samples)
# Fold 2: Train [0..499]   (500 samples) → Test [500..666]  (167 samples)
# Fold 3: Train [0..666]   (667 samples) → Test [667..833]  (167 samples)
# Fold 4: Train [0..833]   (834 samples) → Test [834..999]  (166 samples)

Use this for any data with a natural time ordering: financial data, sensor streams, log events, weather forecasts. If your rows have timestamps, do not shuffle them.

⚠️ The #1 Source of Inflated Metrics in Industry

Shuffling temporal data before cross-validation is one of the most common — and most costly — mistakes in applied ML. Your offline metrics look amazing. Your production model does nothing useful. The gap is leakage, and it can persist for months before anyone notices.

Group K-Fold

Sometimes your data has groups that must not be split across train and test. Medical imaging is the classic example: one patient contributes 20 scans. If some of Patient A's scans land in training and others in testing, your model can "recognize" Patient A rather than learning the actual medical pattern. Performance looks great. Generalization to new patients is terrible.

Group K-Fold keeps all data from the same group in the same fold. You pass a groups array that labels each row.

from sklearn.model_selection import GroupKFold
import numpy as np

# 100 samples from 20 patients (5 samples each)
groups = np.repeat(np.arange(20), 5)

gkf = GroupKFold(n_splits=5)
for i, (train_idx, test_idx) in enumerate(gkf.split(X[:100], y[:100], groups)):
    train_groups = set(groups[train_idx])
    test_groups = set(groups[test_idx])
    # Verify: no group appears in both train and test
    assert train_groups.isdisjoint(test_groups)
    print(f"Fold {i}: Train patients={sorted(train_groups)}")
    print(f"         Test  patients={sorted(test_groups)}")

Other scenarios where Group K-Fold matters: multiple essays from the same student, multiple transactions from the same user, multiple frames from the same video, multiple reviews from the same product. If you can answer "could the model be recognizing the source rather than the pattern?" with yes, you need group-aware splitting.

Nested Cross-Validation

Here's a subtle trap that catches a lot of people.

You use 5-fold CV to tune hyperparameters — trying different values of tree count, max depth, regularization strength. You pick the combination with the best average CV score. Then you report that score as your model's expected performance. Is that honest?

No. You selected the hyperparameters that happened to score highest on those particular folds. That "best CV score" is optimistically biased — it overestimates real-world performance. This is the same sin as evaluating on your training set, just one level of abstraction higher.

Nested CV fixes this with two loops:

  • Outer loop — splits data into train/test folds for unbiased evaluation
  • Inner loop — runs inside each outer training set to tune hyperparameters

The outer test fold is never touched during tuning. So the outer scores are honest.

from sklearn.model_selection import GridSearchCV, cross_val_score
from sklearn.model_selection import StratifiedKFold
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.datasets import load_breast_cancer

X, y = load_breast_cancer(return_X_y=True)

# Inner loop: tune hyperparameters
param_grid = {
    'n_estimators': [50, 100, 200],
    'max_depth': [3, 5, 10, None]
}
inner_cv = StratifiedKFold(n_splits=3, shuffle=True, random_state=42)
grid_search = GridSearchCV(
    RandomForestClassifier(random_state=42),
    param_grid,
    cv=inner_cv,
    scoring='accuracy',
    n_jobs=-1
)

# Outer loop: evaluate generalization honestly
outer_cv = StratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, shuffle=True, random_state=42)
nested_scores = cross_val_score(grid_search, X, y, cv=outer_cv, scoring='accuracy')

print(f"Nested CV scores: {nested_scores}")
print(f"Unbiased estimate: {nested_scores.mean():.4f} ± {nested_scores.std():.4f}")
# Nested CV scores: [0.9561 0.9649 0.9561 0.9649 0.9558]
# Unbiased estimate: 0.9596 ± 0.0043
💡 When Do You Actually Need This?

Use nested CV whenever you're both tuning hyperparameters and reporting final performance in the same experiment. If you have a completely separate held-out test set that you never use for any decisions, single-level CV for tuning is fine. But if your paper or report quotes the CV score as "expected performance," that number must come from the outer loop of a nested procedure. Otherwise you're lying — politely, accidentally, but still lying.

Repeated K-Fold

Repeated K-Fold runs the entire K-Fold procedure multiple times, each with a different random shuffle. 10 repeats of 5-fold gives you 50 scores. The variance of your performance estimate drops, and you get tighter confidence intervals — which matters when you need to decide whether Model A's 0.3% edge over Model B is real or noise.

from sklearn.model_selection import RepeatedStratifiedKFold

# 10 repeats × 5 folds = 50 evaluation rounds
rskf = RepeatedStratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, n_repeats=10, random_state=42)
scores = cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=rskf, scoring='accuracy')

print(f"50 scores, mean: {scores.mean():.4f} ± {scores.std():.4f}")

# Bonus: 95% confidence interval
ci_low = np.percentile(scores, 2.5)
ci_high = np.percentile(scores, 97.5)
print(f"95% CI: [{ci_low:.4f}, {ci_high:.4f}]")

The cost is proportional — 10 repeats means 10× the computation. Worth it for final model comparisons. Overkill for exploratory work.

Which Technique When

This is the decision table. Bookmark it.

Technique When to Use Pros Cons
Hold-Out Large datasets (100k+ rows), quick sanity checks Fast, simple, one model to train High variance estimate on small data; wastes test data for training
K-Fold General-purpose default for regression Good bias-variance tradeoff; every sample tested once Doesn't preserve class balance; not suitable for temporal or grouped data
Stratified K-Fold Classification (especially imbalanced classes) — your actual default Preserves class distribution in each fold; stable estimates Only applies to classification; doesn't handle groups or time
LOOCV Tiny datasets (<100 samples) Maximum training data; deterministic Computationally brutal; high variance as an estimator (correlated folds)
Time Series Split Any data with temporal ordering Respects time; prevents future→past leakage Earlier folds train on less data; can't shuffle
Group K-Fold Data with natural groups (patients, users, devices) Prevents group-level leakage Fold sizes can be uneven; need a meaningful group label
Nested CV Tuning hyperparameters + reporting performance in same experiment Unbiased performance estimate even with tuning Expensive (kouter × kinner × param combos); complex to set up
Repeated K-Fold Final model comparisons; need tight confidence intervals Lower variance estimate; more statistical power n_repeats × k models to train; overkill for exploration

The decision flowchart in your head should be: Is there a time component? → Time Series Split. Are there groups? → Group K-Fold. Classification? → Stratified K-Fold. Regression? → K-Fold. Also tuning? → Wrap in nested CV. Need tighter estimates? → Repeat it.

Interpreting CV Results

Mean ± Standard Deviation

Always report both. A model at 0.92 ± 0.01 is far more trustworthy than one at 0.93 ± 0.05. The second model performs better on average, but it's wildly inconsistent — hitting 0.98 on an easy fold and 0.88 on a hard one. That kind of instability usually signals overfitting to certain patterns in the training data.

What High Variance Across Folds Tells You

If your fold scores swing widely, ask why:

  • Small dataset — each fold is a small, noisy sample. More data is the real fix; Repeated K-Fold is the band-aid.
  • Distribution shift across the data — some folds contain a cluster of unusual examples (e.g., a region, a time period, a subpopulation). This is a feature, not a bug — it tells you your model's performance varies by context.
  • Overfitting — a complex model that memorizes folds with friendly data and crashes on the rest. Try a simpler model or more regularization.
  • Data leakage — if variance is suspiciously low and the mean is suspiciously high, you might be accidentally leaking information. Check your preprocessing pipeline.

Don't just look at the summary stats. Print the individual fold scores. If one fold is dramatically worse, dig into it. Understanding why a fold is hard teaches you more about your problem than the mean ever will.

Comparing Models Statistically

When comparing two models, run them on the exact same folds. This way, differences come from the models, not from random fold assignments.

from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.model_selection import RepeatedStratifiedKFold, cross_val_score
from sklearn.datasets import load_breast_cancer
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.pipeline import make_pipeline
from scipy import stats
import numpy as np

X, y = load_breast_cancer(return_X_y=True)

rf = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=100, random_state=42)
lr = make_pipeline(StandardScaler(), LogisticRegression(max_iter=5000, random_state=42))

# Same folds for both — critical for fair comparison
cv = RepeatedStratifiedKFold(n_splits=5, n_repeats=10, random_state=42)
rf_scores = cross_val_score(rf, X, y, cv=cv, scoring='accuracy')
lr_scores = cross_val_score(lr, X, y, cv=cv, scoring='accuracy')

print(f"Random Forest:       {rf_scores.mean():.4f} ± {rf_scores.std():.4f}")
print(f"Logistic Regression: {lr_scores.mean():.4f} ± {lr_scores.std():.4f}")

# Per-fold paired difference
diff = rf_scores - lr_scores
print(f"\nPer-fold diff (RF - LR): {diff.mean():.4f} ± {diff.std():.4f}")

# Paired t-test (caveat: assumes independent folds, which they aren't exactly)
t_stat, p_value = stats.ttest_rel(rf_scores, lr_scores)
print(f"Paired t-test: t={t_stat:.3f}, p={p_value:.4f}")

The per-fold difference is the most informative number. If Random Forest beats Logistic Regression on 45 out of 50 folds, that's convincing even if the average gap is small. If it wins on 28 and loses on 22, the models are equivalent in practice — pick the simpler one.

💡 Statistical Significance ≠ Practical Significance

With 50 repeated folds, you have a lot of statistical power. A 0.2% accuracy difference can register as "significant" at p < 0.05. That doesn't mean it matters. Always pair the p-value with the actual magnitude of the difference. If two models are within one standard deviation of each other, prefer the simpler one — it'll be easier to debug, faster to train, and more robust when the data distribution shifts in production.

⚠️ On the Paired t-test

The standard paired t-test assumes fold scores are independent. They're not — folds share training data. For repeated K-Fold, the Nadeau–Bengio corrected test adjusts for this dependence. In most practical work, the paired t-test gives directionally correct answers, but if you're writing a paper, use the correction.

The Bottom Line

Cross-validation isn't a technique you apply once and move on from. It's a discipline that runs through every modeling decision. Engineer a new feature? Re-run CV. Swap algorithms? Compare on the same folds. Tune hyperparameters? Nest it. The core lesson: never trust a single number from a single split. Data is noisy. Models are sensitive. The only honest way to estimate real-world performance is to test on multiple slices and look at the full distribution.

What You Should Now Be Able To Do

  • Explain why a single train/test split produces unreliable performance estimates
  • Implement K-Fold and Stratified K-Fold CV in scikit-learn and interpret the output
  • Choose the right CV strategy based on your data structure (temporal, grouped, imbalanced, small)
  • Use TimeSeriesSplit for any dataset with a time component — and explain why shuffling temporal data is leakage
  • Set up nested cross-validation to get unbiased performance estimates when also tuning hyperparameters
  • Compare two models on the same folds and assess whether the difference is practically meaningful
  • Diagnose what high variance across fold scores is telling you about your model or data
  • Know when hold-out is sufficient and when LOOCV is worth the cost
