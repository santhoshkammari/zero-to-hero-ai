Why metrics fail in production: Goodhart's Law, distribution shift

Fraud detector scored 99% accuracy in testing. Production caught zero fraud. Model learned predict "not fraud" for every transaction, and 1% fraud rate made that look brilliant on paper. That sent me digging into what accuracy actually measured.

Evaluation metrics are scorecards for model performance. Origin: signal detection theory in 1940s — radar operators separating enemy planes from birds. Medicine, information retrieval, and machine learning kept refining them. Core question stays same: are predictions any good?

The Four Buckets

Every classification metric in career is arithmetic on four numbers. Every one. Need see where those numbers come from, because once you see four buckets, rest is deciding which ratios matter.

Scenario for whole section. You work at bank building fraud detection. Out of 10,000 daily transactions, about 100 are fraudulent — 1% fraud rate. You run trained model on test set, and it predicts "fraud" or "not fraud" for each transaction. Some predictions are right, some wrong. That gives four outcomes.

Predicted "Fraud"Predicted "Legit"
Actually FraudTrue Positive (TP) — caught real thiefFalse Negative (FN) — missed real thief
Actually LegitFalse Positive (FP) — false alarm on honest customerTrue Negative (TN) — left legit transaction alone

That is confusion matrix. Most confusion in metrics comes from not knowing which cells metric cares about and which it ignores.

Add numbers. Model catches 85 of 100 frauds, misses 15, and wrongly flags 50 legitimate transactions.

import numpy as np
from sklearn.metrics import confusion_matrix

y_true = np.array([1]*100 + [0]*9900)

y_pred = np.zeros(10000, dtype=int)
y_pred[:85] = 1      # catches 85 of 100 frauds
y_pred[100:150] = 1  # 50 false alarms on legit transactions

cm = confusion_matrix(y_true, y_pred)
# [[9850,   50],    ← TN=9850, FP=50
#  [  15,   85]]    ← FN=15,   TP=85

Four cells. Whole game. Next step: choose numerator and denominator.

The Accuracy Trap

Most natural metric is accuracy: how many predictions were right overall?

Accuracy = (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN)

In fraud scenario: (85 + 9850) / 10000 = 99.35%. Sounds fantastic. Trap snaps shut here. A model that does nothing — prints return "not fraud" for every transaction, with no fraud signal at all — scores 99.0%. Our "good" model beats total ignorance by 0.35 percentage points.

Problem is structural: accuracy gives equal credit for every correct prediction. Legit transaction right = one point. Fraud caught = one point. When 99% of transactions are legitimate, majority class drowns everything that matters.

Courtroom analogy. Judge always says "not guilty." In world where 99% of defendants are innocent, judge gets 99% accuracy. Judge is useless. Accuracy works when classes are roughly balanced — cats versus dogs at 50/50. Once one class dominates, accuracy rewards laziness. Need better metric.

Precision — The Courtroom Standard

Same courtroom. One justice philosophy says: "It is better that ten guilty persons escape than that one innocent suffer." That is precision.

Precision = TP / (TP + FP)

Of all transactions model flagged as fraud, how many really were? Here: 85 / (85 + 50) = 63%. About 4 in 10 alarms are false. If human fraud analyst investigates every flag, 63% precision means a third of day goes to ghosts. Expensive. Trust erodes. After weeks of false alarms, analyst starts ignoring flags.

Precision matters most when false alarm cost is high. Spam filter sends legal contract to junk — false positive, deal risk. Content moderation removes legit post — false positive, trust loss. Criminal justice convicts innocent person — false positive, human life cost.

Precision limitation: easy to game. Model can hit 100% precision by being absurdly cautious — flag fraud only at 99.99% certainty. You flag three transactions a year, get all three right, miss other 97 frauds. Precision alone is half story.

Recall — The Cancer Screening Standard

Opposite philosophy says: "Never let a guilty person walk free, no matter the cost." That is recall.

Recall = TP / (TP + FN)

Of all actual frauds, how many did we catch? Model: 85 / (85 + 15) = 85%. Fifteen fraudulent transactions slipped through. At $5,000 per fraud, that is $75,000 lost today alone.

Recall matters most when missing positive is dangerous or expensive. Cancer screening — false negative means tumor goes undetected. Airport security — threat gets through. Manufacturing quality control — defective part reaches customer.

Recall has opposite gaming problem. You can get 100% recall trivially: flag every transaction as fraud. You catch all 100 frauds. You also flag 9,900 legitimate transactions, and precision becomes 100/10000 = 1%. Fraud team quits. Customers cancel cards. Recall alone is other half.

The Smoke Detector Dial

What ties precision and recall together mechanically took me a while to feel, not just memorize.

Most classifiers do not output hard yes or no. They output probability — score between 0 and 1 for how suspicious transaction looks. Score 0.92 means model thinks fraud is likely. Score 0.15 means legit is likely. To turn score into prediction, pick threshold: anything above threshold becomes fraud.

Smoke detector analogy. Crank sensitivity up: catches every fire, smoldering wire, hint of smoke. Also screams for toast, hot shower, boiling water. High recall, low precision. Turn dial down: no toast alarms, no shower alarms. Also stays quiet until house is fully engulfed. High precision, low recall.

No setting gives both. Not metric flaw — fundamental property of turning continuous score into binary decision. Right setting depends on which mistake is more dangerous in your situation. Business question, not statistics question.

We will return to smoke detector dial in threshold selection. For now: can one number compress precision–recall tradeoff?

F1 and the Harmonic Mean Trick

F1 score tries to capture precision and recall in one number. It uses harmonic mean:

F1 = 2 × (precision × recall) / (precision + recall)

Why harmonic mean instead of plain average? Important "why" question. Difference between memorizing formula and understanding it.

Suppose model has precision 100% and recall 1%. Arithmetic mean is (1.0 + 0.01) / 2 = 50.5%. Sounds okay. But model catches 1% of positives — basically broken. Harmonic mean gives 2 × (1.0 × 0.01) / (1.0 + 0.01) = 0.0198, about 2%. That number screams something is wrong. Harmonic mean stays bounded by smaller value — strong side cannot hide disaster on weak side.

Our fraud model: F1 = 2 × (0.63 × 0.85) / (0.63 + 0.85) = 0.72. Decent, not great. 63% precision drags score down.

Now, F1 treats precision and recall as equally important. Real problems often do not. Cancer screening should lean toward recall — missing tumor is worse than extra biopsy. Spam filter should lean toward precision — losing real email is worse than letting some spam through. For those cases, use F-beta:

F_β = (1 + β²) × (P × R) / (β² × P + R)

β controls lean. β = 2 makes recall twice as important as precision — useful for fraud detection and medical screening. β = 0.5 makes precision twice as important — useful for spam filters and content moderation. F1 is F-beta with β = 1. Think of β² as ratio of how much you value recall relative to precision.

F1 limitation — and F-beta limitation — is simple: they ignore true negatives completely. They do not care how well model classifies majority class. For most imbalanced problems, fine — you already know model can identify legitimate transactions. But story can still be incomplete. That gap motivates next metric.

Threshold Selection — The Dial You Actually Turn

Back to smoke detector. Most classifiers output continuous score, and threshold turns score into decision. Default threshold in many libraries is 0.5. Blunt truth: 0.5 is arbitrary, and almost never optimal.

In fraud detector, model gives each transaction suspicion score between 0 and 1. At threshold 0.5, anything above 50% suspicion gets flagged. But costs are asymmetric: missed fraud costs $5,000 in chargebacks, false alarm maybe $50 in analyst time. If misses cost 100× more than false alarms, why split threshold down middle?

Drop threshold to 0.2 — flag anything with 20%+ suspicion. You catch more fraud (recall up). You also trigger more false alarms (precision down). But total dollar cost may drop hard, because each caught fraud saves $5,000 while each extra false alarm costs $50. Right threshold is where total business cost is minimized. If you can price false positives and false negatives, compute it directly: total_cost = cost_FN × FN + cost_FP × FP. Sweep thresholds. Pick smallest total_cost. That is dial setting smoke detector actually needs.

from sklearn.metrics import precision_recall_curve
import numpy as np

# Example: model outputs suspicion scores for 500 transactions
y_true = np.array([0]*475 + [1]*25)          # 5% fraud rate
y_scores = np.random.rand(500)               # replace with your model's scores

# Find the threshold that gives at least 90% recall
precisions, recalls, thresholds = precision_recall_curve(y_true, y_scores)
idx = np.where(recalls >= 0.90)[0][-1]
print(f"For 90% recall: threshold = {thresholds[idx]:.3f}, "
      f"precision = {precisions[idx]:.3f}")

This feels obvious once said aloud. Still, I have seen production systems sit on default 0.5 for years because nobody asked what mistakes actually cost.

ROC-AUC — Ranking Quality Across All Thresholds

Threshold selection gives best setting for one cost structure. But sometimes you want compare two models before choosing threshold. You want say "model A beats model B, no matter where dial sits." That is ROC curve.

ROC curve (Receiver Operating Characteristic — WWII radar relic) plots True Positive Rate (recall) on y-axis against False Positive Rate on x-axis at every threshold. Perfect model hugs top-left corner — perfect recall, zero false positives. Coin-flip model traces diagonal from bottom-left to top-right.

AUC (Area Under ROC Curve) compresses whole curve into one number between 0 and 1. Concrete reading: pick one random fraud and one random legitimate transaction. AUC is probability model gives higher suspicion score to fraud. AUC = 0.5 means model cannot separate them — coin flip. AUC = 0.85 means 85% of time random fraud scores above random legitimate transaction.

from sklearn.metrics import roc_auc_score

np.random.seed(42)
y_true = np.array([1]*100 + [0]*9900)
scores_fraud = np.random.beta(5, 2, size=100)
scores_legit = np.random.beta(1.5, 5, size=9900)
y_scores = np.concatenate([scores_fraud, scores_legit])

auc = roc_auc_score(y_true, y_scores)
print(f"AUC: {auc:.3f}")  # ~ 0.96

ROC-AUC is standard for model comparison when classes are reasonably balanced. Catch: on heavily imbalanced data, ROC-AUC can look dangerously optimistic.

False Positive Rate is FP / (FP + TN). With 99,000 legitimate transactions, even 500 false positives gives FPR of only 0.5%. Curve looks beautiful. But 500 false alarms can bury operations team. ROC-AUC misses this because denominator is stuffed with true negatives. Curve smiles while team drowns.

PR-AUC — When Positives Are Rare

Precision-Recall curve fixes that by ignoring true negatives entirely. It plots precision on y-axis against recall on x-axis at every threshold. When positive class is rare — fraud, medical diagnosis, anomaly detection — PR curve tells honest story ROC curve sugar-coats.

Key difference. Random model gets ROC baseline 0.5. PR baseline is positive-class prevalence — 1% in fraud case. So even small lifts above 1% show up and matter on PR curve, while same gains disappear into noise on ROC curve. When positives are rare, PR curve is right magnifying glass.

Average Precision (AP) summarizes PR curve as one number — weighted mean of precision at each threshold, weighted by change in recall. Use it when positive class is what you care about and it is outnumbered.

My rough heuristic: if class ratio is worse than 1:10, I check PR-AUC first and ROC-AUC second. Worse than 1:100, I often skip ROC-AUC entirely.

Log Loss — Punishing Overconfidence

Everything so far scores hard decisions — flag or not, rank right or not. But most models output probabilities, and sometimes probability quality itself matters more than resulting decision.

Log loss (cross-entropy) scores predicted probabilities directly:

log_loss = -(1/N) × Σ[y·log(p) + (1-y)·log(1-p)]

Intuition comes from information theory. Log loss measures surprise. If model says "99% chance legitimate" and transaction is fraud, model is maximally surprised, and log loss hits hard. Math: if model gives probability 0.01 to true class, penalty is -log(0.01) ≈ 4.6. If it gives 0.5, penalty is -log(0.5) ≈ 0.69. If it gives 0.99 and is correct, penalty is -log(0.99) ≈ 0.01.

That asymmetry is point. Confidently wrong is catastrophically expensive. Confidently right is almost free. Uncertain sits in middle. This makes log loss a proper scoring rule — model minimizes expected loss by reporting true beliefs. No gaming trick. Best move is honest uncertainty.

Log loss points to deeper question coming next: do model probability outputs mean what they claim to mean?

MCC — The Metric That's Hard to Game

I ignored Matthews Correlation Coefficient for years. F1 felt enough. Then I read Chicco & Jurman (2020), which makes strong case that MCC is most reliable single metric for binary classification on imbalanced data. Now I use it. Reason:

MCC = (TP×TN − FP×FN) / √((TP+FP)(TP+FN)(TN+FP)(TN+FN))

MCC ranges from −1 (every prediction wrong) through 0 (random-level) to +1 (perfect). Critical difference from F1: MCC uses all four confusion-matrix cells. F1 ignores true negatives. So model that predicts all negatives gets F1 = 0 and MCC = 0 — useless for positive class, useless overall. But consider model that over-flags positives to inflate recall. F1 may still look decent. MCC drops because FP×FN grows in numerator, pulling score down. MCC treats both classes symmetrically. High MCC is hard without doing well on positives and negatives.

If you can report only one number for imbalanced binary problem — one number for VP who will not read paragraph — report MCC.

from sklearn.metrics import matthews_corrcoef
print(f"MCC: {matthews_corrcoef(y_true, y_pred):.3f}")

Calibration — When Probabilities Need to Mean Something

ROC-AUC, PR-AUC, and even F1 measure ranking or decision quality. They ask: "Does model score positives above negatives?" Does it make good decisions at this threshold? None ask whether probability outputs are truthful. Model that says "92% chance of fraud" when real probability is 40% can still rank well, but that 92% is a lie.

When does lie matter? When downstream system uses probability value itself. Insurance pricing: premium depends on predicted risk probability. Medical testing: expensive follow-up depends on predicted disease probability. Ad bidding: bid amount is function of predicted click probability. In all those cases, you need calibration — when model says "80% likely," it should be right about 80% of time.

Weather forecaster analogy. Well-calibrated forecaster saying "70% chance of rain" should see rain about 70% of those days. If rain comes 95% of time, forecaster is underconfident. If rain comes 30% of time, forecaster is overconfident. Reliability diagram checks this visually: bucket predicted probabilities (0–10%, 10–20%, etc.), then plot average prediction against observed frequency. Perfect calibration sits on diagonal.

If model is not calibrated, two post-hoc fixes are standard. Platt scaling fits logistic regression on raw scores — good when miscalibration is roughly sigmoid-shaped, common for SVMs and neural nets. Isotonic regression fits more flexible piecewise monotonic function — handles stranger patterns but needs more data to avoid overfitting. Both need held-out calibration set. Neither changes model itself — both wrap output.

We will revisit weather forecaster in production failures. Takeaway now: if you need model probability to be actual probability, not just score, check calibration. Most models out of box are not calibrated.

Multi-Class Metrics

Everything above was binary — two classes, one confusion matrix. With K classes, you get K×K confusion matrix plus per-class precision, recall, and F1. Then question becomes: how do you roll per-class numbers into one number? Three approaches, each with different politics:

AveragingHow It WorksUse When
MacroCompute per class, then unweighted meanEvery class matters equally — even tiny ones
MicroPool TP/FP/FN globally, compute onceEvery sample matters equally (micro-F1 = accuracy for single-label)
WeightedCompute per class, weight by supportCompromise — big classes dominate, small classes still count

Politics are real. Imagine wildlife camera classifying animals: 10,000 deer, 500 coyotes, 12 mountain lions. Macro says mountain lion class matters as much as deer class — miss all 12 mountain lions and macro F1 collapses even if deer and coyotes are perfect. Micro barely notices missing mountain lions. Weighted sits in middle. Right choice depends on who gets hurt when model is wrong.

from sklearn.metrics import f1_score

y_true_mc = [0, 0, 0, 1, 1, 2, 2, 2, 2, 2]
y_pred_mc = [0, 0, 1, 1, 0, 2, 2, 2, 1, 0]

print(f"Macro F1:    {f1_score(y_true_mc, y_pred_mc, average='macro'):.3f}")
print(f"Micro F1:    {f1_score(y_true_mc, y_pred_mc, average='micro'):.3f}")
print(f"Weighted F1: {f1_score(y_true_mc, y_pred_mc, average='weighted'):.3f}")

The Regression Scoreboard

Classification asks "which bucket?" Regression asks "how far off?" Different game, different scoreboard. Extend running scenario: alongside fraud classifier, bank also predicts expected transaction amounts for anomaly detection. When predicted amount is far from actual, that itself can be investigation signal.

MAE — Mean Absolute Error

MAE = (1/N) × Σ|y_i − ŷ_i|

Simplest regression metric. Predict $300, actual $320, error is $20. MAE averages absolute errors across dataset. A $10 miss counts exactly twice a $5 miss — no more, no less. Easy to explain: "on average, predictions miss by $X." MAE is robust to outliers because it does not amplify them — one wild miss is still penalized linearly.

RMSE — When Big Misses Are Catastrophic

MSE = (1/N) × Σ(y_i − ŷ_i)²    RMSE = √MSE

Squaring errors before averaging does something important: it disproportionately punishes large errors. A $100 miss contributes 100× more to MSE than a $10 miss, because 100² / 10² = 100. RMSE takes square root so metric returns to original units — dollars, not dollars-squared — and stays interpretable.

When do you want that disproportionate punishment? When large error is catastrophically bad, not just proportionally bad. Energy demand forecasting: underestimate peak load and grid fails. Insurance pricing: underestimate risk by 10× and lose millions. Structural engineering: enough said. In those domains, one big miss is worse than many small misses, and RMSE reflects that. Use MAE when all errors are equal. Use RMSE when big misses keep you awake.

R² — Proportion of Variance Explained

R² = 1 − (SS_res / SS_tot)

R² asks different question: "How much target variance did model capture?" R² = 1.0 means perfect predictions. R² = 0 means no better than predicting mean every time — zero signal captured. R² < 0 means model is worse than mean baseline. That surprises people, but it happens, and it usually signals something fundamentally wrong — bad features, bad model, or preprocessing bug.

Gotcha: R² always rises or stays flat when you add features, even useless random noise. More knobs lets model fit noise a little. Adjusted R² penalizes feature count — it drops when new feature does not earn its keep. Comparing models with different feature counts? Use adjusted R².

MAPE — The Executive Metric

MAPE = (1/N) × Σ|y_i − ŷ_i| / |y_i| × 100%

"Our predictions are off by 5% on average." Executives understand that immediately. That is MAPE's superpower. Weak spot: when actual values are near zero, denominator is tiny and MAPE explodes. A $0.50 transaction with $2.00 prediction gives 300% error on one point. Do not use MAPE when data crosses zero or hovers near it.

from sklearn.metrics import (mean_absolute_error, mean_squared_error,
                             r2_score, mean_absolute_percentage_error)

y_true_reg = np.array([300, 450, 200, 520, 380])
y_pred_reg = np.array([310, 430, 215, 490, 395])

print(f"MAE:   ${mean_absolute_error(y_true_reg, y_pred_reg):.0f}K")
print(f"RMSE:  ${np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(y_true_reg, y_pred_reg)):.0f}K")
print(f"R²:    {r2_score(y_true_reg, y_pred_reg):.3f}")
print(f"MAPE:  {mean_absolute_percentage_error(y_true_reg, y_pred_reg)*100:.1f}%")

Ranking Metrics — When Order Is Everything

Not everything is classification or regression. Search engines, recommendation systems, and ad platforms care about order — did best results appear near top? If right answer is buried on page three, you feel exactly what ranking metrics try to measure.

Back to bank scenario. Transaction monitoring dashboard shows ranked list of suspicious transactions, most suspicious first. Analyst maybe has time to inspect 10 per day. Ranking metric asks: are real frauds concentrated near top, where analyst will actually see them?

MRR (Mean Reciprocal Rank) asks: where does first real fraud appear? Position 1 gives score 1. Position 3 gives 1/3. Position 10 gives 1/10. Average across queries for MRR. Good when there is one clear target — navigational search, question-answering. Limitation: it ignores everything after first hit. If positions 2 through 10 are also frauds, MRR does not care.

MAP (Mean Average Precision) fixes that by looking at all relevant results and their positions. Each time fraud appears, compute precision at that position, then average those values. This rewards putting all frauds near top and penalizes scattering them through list. MAP uses binary relevance — transaction is fraud or not.

NDCG (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain) goes further by handling graded relevance. Not all frauds are equally severe — $50,000 fraud matters more than $50 fraud. NDCG lets you assign relevance scores (0 = legit, 1 = suspicious, 2 = likely fraud, 3 = confirmed fraud), discounts each result by position with logarithmic discount, and normalizes by ideal ranking. NDCG@10 is industry standard for search quality. Range is 0 to 1, where 1 means perfect ranking.

One-line summary: MRR asks "did you find the answer?", MAP asks "did you find all answers and rank them first?", NDCG asks "did you rank best answers highest?"

When Metrics Go Wrong in Production

Everything above assumes offline metric you optimize is same metric that matters in real world. I wish. Usually false. Learned that hard way.

Economics has sharp line for this: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." That is Goodhart's Law. It haunts production ML. Optimize recommendation system for click-through rate, and it learns clickbait — engagement up, satisfaction down. Optimize content moderation for precision, and system becomes so conservative harmful content flows freely — precision high, harm high.

Gap between offline metrics and real outcomes appears three ways. First, distribution shift: test set is past; production is future; future changes. Second, feedback loops: fraud model changes fraudster behavior, which changes next data. Offline PR-AUC was measured on yesterday's fraudsters, not tomorrow's. Third, proxy misalignment: metric you can measure (predicted probability, F1 on history) is proxy for thing you actually care about (dollars saved, lives improved, user trust). Proxy can diverge.

Remember weather forecaster. Well-calibrated probabilities can still be useless for actual decision, because training metric is not decision-maker's metric.

Practical defense is layered. Use offline metrics as first filter — cheap and fast. Use A/B tests to measure real user outcomes. Monitor guardrail metrics beside primary metric: if click-through rate rises but session length falls, something is wrong. When offline and online metrics disagree — and they will — trust online metrics. Lab is not field.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Do not optimize easiest metric. Optimize metric tied to business objective. Model with 0.95 AUC and model with 0.90 AUC can create very different business impact depending on operating point. Metric that matters is one that hurts when wrong. Start with cost. Work backward to number.
