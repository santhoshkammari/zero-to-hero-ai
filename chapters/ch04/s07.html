Model Selection

Chapter 4: ML Fundamentals & Core Concepts 6 subtopics
TL;DR

There is no single best machine learning model — a theorem literally proves it. Model selection is the disciplined process of starting with a dumb baseline, working your way up through increasingly complex models, comparing them with statistical rigor, tuning their knobs with smart search methods, and then explaining to stakeholders (and yourself) why the model makes the predictions it does. This section walks you through every step of that workflow, with code you can run today.

Choosing Between Models

No Free Lunch Theorem

Imagine you're picking a restaurant for dinner. Someone tells you, "This place is the best restaurant in the world." You'd immediately ask: best at what? Best sushi? Best pizza? Best for a budget? There's no universal "best restaurant" because it depends on what you're optimizing for.

The same is true for machine learning algorithms, and there's a formal proof. The No Free Lunch Theorem (NFL), published by David Wolpert and William Macready in 1997, states that no single learning algorithm outperforms all others across every possible problem. Averaged over all possible datasets, every algorithm performs identically. A random forest that crushes it on your customer churn dataset might be mediocre on a medical image task. A deep neural network that dominates image classification might lose to logistic regression on a small, clean, tabular dataset.

The practical consequence is uncomfortable but liberating: you cannot skip the work of trying multiple models on your specific data. Anyone who tells you "always use XGBoost" or "neural nets are always best" is giving you bad advice. The theorem forces you to be empirical. Measure. Compare. Decide based on evidence, not dogma.

Starting Simple: Baselines

Every model selection process starts with a baseline — the dumbest possible prediction that still makes sense. For a regression problem (predicting a number), your baseline is predicting the mean of the training data for every input. For classification (predicting a category), your baseline is predicting the most frequent class every time.

Why bother? Because baselines tell you whether your fancy model is actually learning anything. If you build a neural network that gets 92% accuracy on a fraud detection dataset where 92% of transactions are legitimate, your model hasn't learned anything — it's performing no better than predicting "not fraud" every time. The baseline exposes this immediately.

from sklearn.dummy import DummyClassifier, DummyRegressor
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score, mean_squared_error

# Classification baseline: always predict the most common class
baseline_clf = DummyClassifier(strategy="most_frequent")
baseline_clf.fit(X_train, y_train)
baseline_acc = accuracy_score(y_test, baseline_clf.predict(X_test))
print(f"Baseline accuracy: {baseline_acc:.3f}")

# Regression baseline: always predict the mean
baseline_reg = DummyRegressor(strategy="mean")
baseline_reg.fit(X_train, y_train)
baseline_mse = mean_squared_error(y_test, baseline_reg.predict(X_test))
print(f"Baseline MSE: {baseline_mse:.3f}")

If your complex model can't beat these numbers, something is wrong with your features, your data, or your approach — not your model architecture.

Model Complexity vs Data Size

Here's a rule of thumb that saves a lot of wasted compute: the complexity of your model should be proportional to the amount of data you have. A model with millions of parameters trained on 500 rows of data will memorize the training set perfectly and generalize terribly. This is overfitting — the model learned the noise, not the signal.

With small datasets (hundreds to low thousands of rows), start with linear models — logistic regression, linear SVMs. They have few parameters, which means they're hard to overfit. With medium datasets (tens of thousands of rows), tree-based ensembles like random forests and gradient boosting hit a sweet spot. With large datasets (hundreds of thousands and beyond), deep learning starts to shine because it has enough data to justify its enormous parameter count.

This isn't a rigid law. It's a starting point. But violating it — throwing a deep neural net at 200 data points — almost always ends in tears.

Interpretability vs Performance

Some models are glass boxes. A decision tree tells you exactly which features it checked and in what order. Logistic regression gives you a coefficient for each feature — you can point at a number and say, "this is how much feature X contributes to the prediction." These models are interpretable.

Other models are black boxes. A gradient-boosted ensemble of 500 trees, or a neural network with 10 layers, can achieve stunning accuracy. But try explaining to a loan officer why a particular applicant was denied. The model's reasoning is distributed across thousands of parameters in ways that resist human understanding.

The tradeoff is real. In healthcare, finance, and legal contexts, regulations may require you to explain your model's decisions. In those cases, you might accept lower accuracy in exchange for transparency. In other contexts — ad click prediction, image tagging — nobody cares how the model works as long as it works. Know which world you're in before you start modeling.

Training Time vs Prediction Time

Two different clocks matter. Training time is how long it takes to build the model. Prediction time (or inference time) is how long it takes to score a single new data point.

A random forest might take 30 minutes to train but predict in microseconds. A neural network might take 12 hours to train but also predict in microseconds (on a GPU). A k-nearest neighbors model trains instantly (it stores the training data) but prediction is slow because it searches through every stored point at query time.

If your model runs in a real-time API serving thousands of requests per second, prediction latency matters more than training time. If your model runs once a week in a batch job, training time matters more. Choose accordingly.

Data Characteristics

Your data tells you which models to try first. If the relationship between features and target is roughly linear, logistic or linear regression will do well and train in seconds. If the relationships are highly nonlinear — full of interactions, thresholds, and conditional effects — tree-based models and neural nets handle that better.

Feature types matter too. Tree-based models handle a mix of numerical and categorical features natively. Linear models and neural nets require you to encode categorical variables (one-hot encoding, target encoding) and often benefit from feature scaling. If your dataset has lots of missing values, tree models handle them gracefully; most other algorithms need you to impute missing data first.

Dataset size and dimensionality are also signals. If you have more features than samples (common in genomics), you need regularization-heavy methods like Lasso, ElasticNet, or sparse SVMs. If your classes are severely imbalanced, you need models or training strategies that handle that (class weights, oversampling, or specialized loss functions).

Model Comparison Methods

Cross-Validation Comparison

You've trained three models. Their test accuracies are 0.87, 0.86, and 0.85. Is the first model genuinely better, or did it get lucky on this particular train-test split?

Cross-validation (CV) gives you a more honest answer. Instead of a single train-test split, you divide the data into k folds (typically 5 or 10). You train on k−1 folds, test on the remaining fold, and rotate. Each model gets k different scores. Now you have a distribution of performance, not a single number, and you can compare distributions rather than points.

from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier, GradientBoostingClassifier

models = {
    "Logistic Regression": LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000),
    "Random Forest": RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=100, random_state=42),
    "Gradient Boosting": GradientBoostingClassifier(n_estimators=100, random_state=42),
}

for name, model in models.items():
    scores = cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=5, scoring="accuracy")
    print(f"{name}: mean={scores.mean():.4f}, std={scores.std():.4f}")

The standard deviation matters as much as the mean. A model with mean 0.87 and standard deviation 0.05 is less trustworthy than one with mean 0.85 and standard deviation 0.01. The second model is more consistent — you can rely on its predictions across different data samples.

Statistical Significance of Differences

Cross-validation gave you mean scores, but is the difference between 0.87 and 0.85 statistically meaningful, or could it be random noise? You need a statistical test to answer that. Without one, you might pick a model based on a difference that's within the margin of error.

Paired t-Test for Model Comparison

The paired t-test compares two models fold by fold. On fold 1, model A scored 0.88 and model B scored 0.85. On fold 2, model A scored 0.86 and model B scored 0.87. And so on. You compute the difference per fold and test whether the mean difference is significantly different from zero.

from scipy import stats

scores_a = cross_val_score(model_a, X, y, cv=10, scoring="accuracy")
scores_b = cross_val_score(model_b, X, y, cv=10, scoring="accuracy")

t_stat, p_value = stats.ttest_rel(scores_a, scores_b)
print(f"t-statistic: {t_stat:.4f}, p-value: {p_value:.4f}")

if p_value < 0.05:
    print("The difference is statistically significant.")
else:
    print("No significant difference — pick the simpler model.")

A p-value below 0.05 suggests the difference is real. Above 0.05, and you cannot reject the hypothesis that both models perform the same. When there's no significant difference, pick the simpler model — it's easier to maintain, faster to train, and easier to explain.

Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test

The paired t-test assumes the differences between fold scores are normally distributed. With only 5 or 10 folds, that's a strong assumption. The Wilcoxon signed-rank test is a non-parametric alternative — it makes no assumptions about the distribution of differences. It ranks the absolute differences, considers the signs, and tests whether the positive and negative ranks are balanced.

stat, p_value = stats.wilcoxon(scores_a, scores_b)
print(f"Wilcoxon statistic: {stat:.4f}, p-value: {p_value:.4f}")

Use Wilcoxon when you have few folds or suspect the score differences aren't normally distributed. It's more conservative than the t-test, which means fewer false positives.

Multiple Model Comparison: Friedman Test

When you're comparing three or more models simultaneously, running pairwise t-tests inflates your false positive rate (this is the multiple comparisons problem). The Friedman test handles this. It ranks models within each fold, then checks whether the average ranks differ significantly across models.

from scipy.stats import friedmanchisquare

scores_a = cross_val_score(model_a, X, y, cv=10, scoring="accuracy")
scores_b = cross_val_score(model_b, X, y, cv=10, scoring="accuracy")
scores_c = cross_val_score(model_c, X, y, cv=10, scoring="accuracy")

stat, p_value = friedmanchisquare(scores_a, scores_b, scores_c)
print(f"Friedman statistic: {stat:.4f}, p-value: {p_value:.4f}")

If the Friedman test is significant, you know at least one model is different. Then use a post-hoc test (like Nemenyi) to find out which pairs differ.

Ensemble Methods Overview

Why Ensembles Work

Here's a question: if you ask one person to estimate the number of jelly beans in a jar, they'll probably be off by a lot. But if you ask 100 people and average their answers, the average is often remarkably close to the true number. This is the wisdom of crowds, and it's exactly why ensembles work in machine learning.

A single decision tree is a noisy estimator — it's sensitive to which exact data points it trains on. Train it on a slightly different subset, and you might get a very different tree. But if you train 100 trees on different subsets and average their predictions, the individual errors tend to cancel out. The key ingredient is diversity: the models must make different mistakes. If they all make the same error, averaging doesn't help.

Bagging (Bootstrap Aggregating)

Bagging creates diversity by training each model on a different random sample of the data. Each sample is drawn with replacement from the training set (this is called a bootstrap sample). Each sample is the same size as the original data, but because sampling is with replacement, about 37% of the original rows are missing from any given sample, and some rows appear multiple times.

The classic example is Random Forest: it trains many decision trees, each on a bootstrap sample, and also randomizes which features each split can consider. At prediction time, each tree votes, and the majority wins (for classification) or the average is taken (for regression). This double randomization — in data and in features — produces trees that are individually mediocre but collectively powerful.

Boosting

Boosting takes a different approach. Instead of training models independently in parallel, it trains them sequentially. Each new model focuses on the mistakes of the previous one. The first model makes predictions. The second model is trained specifically to correct the first model's errors. The third model corrects the remaining errors. And so on.

The most popular boosting algorithms today are XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost. They dominate tabular data competitions on Kaggle for a reason: boosting is extremely good at squeezing every last drop of predictive power from structured data. The downside is that boosting is more prone to overfitting than bagging, so it requires more careful tuning of hyperparameters like learning rate and number of rounds.

Stacking

Stacking (short for stacked generalization) trains multiple base models, then trains a second-level model — called the meta-learner — to combine their predictions. The base models might be a logistic regression, a random forest, and a neural net. The meta-learner takes their outputs as inputs and learns the optimal way to blend them. This is more flexible than simple averaging because the meta-learner can discover that, say, the neural net is trustworthy when its confidence is high but unreliable when it's uncertain.

Voting

The simplest ensemble: train multiple models and let them vote. In hard voting, each model predicts a class, and the majority wins. In soft voting, each model outputs a probability for each class, and you average the probabilities. Soft voting usually outperforms hard voting because it uses more information — a model that says "95% chance of class A" should count for more than one that says "51% chance of class A."

When to Use Ensembles

Ensembles add complexity. They're slower to train, harder to interpret, and more annoying to deploy. Use them when: you're close to your performance ceiling and need a small boost, you're in a competition (ensembles win Kaggle), or your individual models have complementary strengths. Don't use them when: a single model already meets your requirements, latency constraints rule out running multiple models, or interpretability is a hard requirement.

Practical Model Selection Workflow

Let's walk through a concrete example. You have a tabular dataset of 50,000 customer records with 30 features. The task is binary classification: predict which customers will churn in the next 90 days.

Step 1: Baseline

You calculate that 18% of customers churned in the training data. A "predict majority class" baseline gets 82% accuracy. This is your floor. If your model can't beat 82%, it's not learning anything useful. You also note that accuracy is misleading here because the classes are imbalanced — you'll focus on F1-score and AUC-ROC as your primary metrics.

Step 2: Try Simple Models First

You fit a logistic regression and a single decision tree. Logistic regression gets an AUC of 0.78. The decision tree gets 0.72. Logistic regression is your first serious benchmark. It trains in under a second and gives you interpretable coefficients — you can already see that "months since last purchase" and "number of support tickets" are strong predictors.

Step 3: Move to Complex Models

You fit a random forest (AUC 0.84) and a gradient boosting model (AUC 0.86). Significant improvement over logistic regression. The gradient boosting model takes two minutes to train, which is fine for your use case.

Step 4: Compare Performance Across Metrics

You run 10-fold cross-validation on the top contenders and apply a paired t-test. The gradient boosting model beats the random forest with a p-value of 0.03 — the difference is real. But the gradient boosting model also has a higher standard deviation across folds.

Step 5: Consider Deployment Constraints

Your deployment environment needs predictions in under 10ms per request. Both the random forest and gradient boosting model meet that constraint. Memory footprint matters too — the gradient boosting model is smaller because it uses fewer, shallower trees. Gradient boosting wins on both performance and deployment.

Step 6: Document the Rationale

You write down: "Selected GradientBoostingClassifier over Random Forest because it achieved a statistically significant improvement in AUC (0.86 vs 0.84, paired t-test p=0.03), meets latency requirements (<5ms), and has a smaller model size. Logistic regression was considered for interpretability but the 8-point AUC gap was too large to accept for this use case." Future-you will thank present-you.

Hyperparameter Search Methods

Every model has knobs you can turn. A random forest has the number of trees, the maximum depth, the minimum samples per leaf. These are hyperparameters — values you set before training, as opposed to parameters the model learns from data. The right hyperparameter values can make a huge difference. The question is how to find them efficiently.

Grid Search

Grid search is the brute-force approach. You define a grid of hyperparameter values and try every combination. If you have 3 values for parameter A and 4 values for parameter B, grid search runs 3 × 4 = 12 experiments.

from sklearn.model_selection import GridSearchCV
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingClassifier

param_grid = {
    "n_estimators": [100, 200, 300],
    "max_depth": [3, 5, 7],
    "learning_rate": [0.01, 0.1, 0.2],
    "subsample": [0.8, 1.0],
}

gb = GradientBoostingClassifier(random_state=42)
grid_search = GridSearchCV(
    gb, param_grid, cv=5, scoring="roc_auc", n_jobs=-1, verbose=1
)
grid_search.fit(X_train, y_train)

print(f"Best AUC: {grid_search.best_score_:.4f}")
print(f"Best params: {grid_search.best_params_}")

Grid search is thorough but expensive. That grid above has 3 × 3 × 3 × 2 = 54 combinations, each evaluated with 5-fold CV, so 270 model fits. Add a fourth hyperparameter with 4 values and you're at 1,080 fits. The cost grows exponentially with the number of hyperparameters — this is the curse of dimensionality applied to search.

Random Search

Random search is surprisingly effective. Instead of exhaustively covering a grid, it samples hyperparameter combinations at random from specified distributions. The key insight, demonstrated by Bergstra and Bengio in 2012, is that most hyperparameters have one or two values that matter a lot and many that don't. Random search is much more likely to stumble onto a good value for the important parameters than grid search, which wastes trials on unimportant combinations.

from sklearn.model_selection import RandomizedSearchCV
from scipy.stats import uniform, randint

param_distributions = {
    "n_estimators": randint(50, 500),
    "max_depth": randint(2, 10),
    "learning_rate": uniform(0.001, 0.3),
    "subsample": uniform(0.6, 0.4),
    "min_samples_leaf": randint(1, 20),
}

random_search = RandomizedSearchCV(
    GradientBoostingClassifier(random_state=42),
    param_distributions,
    n_iter=100,           # try 100 random combinations
    cv=5,
    scoring="roc_auc",
    n_jobs=-1,
    random_state=42,
    verbose=1,
)
random_search.fit(X_train, y_train)

print(f"Best AUC: {random_search.best_score_:.4f}")
print(f"Best params: {random_search.best_params_}")

In practice, random search with 100 iterations often matches or beats grid search with thousands of evaluations, at a fraction of the compute cost.

Bayesian Optimization with Optuna

Grid search doesn't learn from its experiments. Random search doesn't either. Bayesian optimization does. After each trial, it builds a probabilistic model of how hyperparameters relate to performance, then uses that model to intelligently choose the next combination to try. It spends more time exploring promising regions and less time in dead zones.

Optuna is the most popular library for this in Python. It uses a Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) — a clever algorithm that models the distribution of "good" hyperparameters separately from "bad" ones and samples from the good distribution.

import optuna
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingClassifier

def objective(trial):
    params = {
        "n_estimators": trial.suggest_int("n_estimators", 50, 500),
        "max_depth": trial.suggest_int("max_depth", 2, 10),
        "learning_rate": trial.suggest_float("learning_rate", 0.001, 0.3, log=True),
        "subsample": trial.suggest_float("subsample", 0.6, 1.0),
        "min_samples_leaf": trial.suggest_int("min_samples_leaf", 1, 20),
    }

    model = GradientBoostingClassifier(**params, random_state=42)
    scores = cross_val_score(model, X_train, y_train, cv=5, scoring="roc_auc")
    return scores.mean()

# Suppress Optuna's verbose logging
optuna.logging.set_verbosity(optuna.logging.WARNING)

study = optuna.create_study(direction="maximize")
study.optimize(objective, n_trials=100)

print(f"Best AUC: {study.best_trial.value:.4f}")
print(f"Best params: {study.best_trial.params}")

Optuna typically finds better hyperparameters than random search in fewer trials. The difference is most dramatic when you have many hyperparameters or when the search space is large. For 3-4 hyperparameters, random search is often good enough. For 8+ hyperparameters, Bayesian optimization starts to pull ahead significantly.

Halving and Successive Elimination

Successive halving takes a different approach to efficiency. Instead of fully training every candidate, it starts many candidates with a small budget (few data points or few training iterations), evaluates them, eliminates the worst half, then doubles the budget for the survivors. Repeat until one candidate remains. The idea is that terrible hyperparameters reveal themselves quickly — you don't need a full 5-fold CV to know that a learning rate of 10.0 is a disaster.

Scikit-learn implements this as HalvingRandomSearchCV and HalvingGridSearchCV. These can be 10-50x faster than their non-halving counterparts for large search spaces.

Early Stopping in HPO

Early stopping in hyperparameter optimization means killing a trial before it finishes if it's clearly going nowhere. Optuna supports this natively with its pruning feature. During training, you periodically report intermediate results. If the current trial is performing much worse than previous good trials at the same stage, Optuna prunes it — stops it early and moves on to the next trial.

def objective_with_pruning(trial):
    params = {
        "n_estimators": 500,
        "max_depth": trial.suggest_int("max_depth", 2, 10),
        "learning_rate": trial.suggest_float("learning_rate", 0.001, 0.3, log=True),
        "subsample": trial.suggest_float("subsample", 0.6, 1.0),
    }

    model = GradientBoostingClassifier(**params, random_state=42, warm_start=True)

    for n_est in range(50, 501, 50):
        model.set_params(n_estimators=n_est)
        model.fit(X_train, y_train)
        score = model.score(X_val, y_val)

        trial.report(score, step=n_est)
        if trial.should_prune():
            raise optuna.TrialPruned()

    return score

study = optuna.create_study(
    direction="maximize",
    pruner=optuna.pruners.MedianPruner(n_warmup_steps=5),
)
study.optimize(objective_with_pruning, n_trials=100)

Pruning can cut total compute time by 50-80% with minimal impact on the quality of the final hyperparameters found.

Model Interpretability Overview

You've selected a model. It performs well. Now someone asks: "Why did the model predict this customer will churn?" If you can't answer that question, you have a trust problem. Interpretability isn't a nice-to-have — it's how you debug models, build stakeholder trust, and satisfy regulatory requirements.

Feature Importance Methods

The most basic form of interpretability is feature importance — a global ranking of which features matter most. Tree-based models compute this by measuring how much each feature reduces impurity (Gini impurity for classification, variance for regression) across all splits in all trees. The feature that reduces impurity the most is ranked first.

A more reliable approach is permutation importance. You take a trained model, shuffle one feature's values randomly, and measure how much performance drops. If shuffling "account age" drops accuracy from 0.86 to 0.72, that feature is clearly important. If shuffling "favorite color" doesn't change accuracy at all, it's irrelevant. Permutation importance is model-agnostic — it works with any model.

from sklearn.inspection import permutation_importance

result = permutation_importance(model, X_test, y_test, n_repeats=10, random_state=42)

for i in result.importances_mean.argsort()[::-1][:10]:
    print(f"{feature_names[i]}: {result.importances_mean[i]:.4f} "
          f"(± {result.importances_std[i]:.4f})")

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)

SHAP is the gold standard for model interpretability. It's based on Shapley values from cooperative game theory — a method originally designed to fairly distribute payouts among players in a coalition game. In the ML context, the "players" are features, and the "payout" is the prediction. SHAP computes each feature's marginal contribution to a specific prediction, accounting for all possible feature interactions.

What makes SHAP special is that it provides both local explanations (why did the model make this prediction for this data point?) and global explanations (which features matter most across the entire dataset?).

import shap
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingClassifier

# Train the model
model = GradientBoostingClassifier(n_estimators=200, random_state=42)
model.fit(X_train, y_train)

# Create a SHAP explainer
explainer = shap.TreeExplainer(model)
shap_values = explainer.shap_values(X_test)

# Global feature importance: mean absolute SHAP value per feature
shap.summary_plot(shap_values, X_test, feature_names=feature_names)

# Local explanation: why did the model predict this for row 0?
shap.force_plot(
    explainer.expected_value,
    shap_values[0],
    X_test.iloc[0] if hasattr(X_test, "iloc") else X_test[0],
    feature_names=feature_names,
)

# Dependence plot: how does one feature affect predictions?
shap.dependence_plot("account_age", shap_values, X_test,
                     feature_names=feature_names)

The summary plot shows each feature as a row. Each dot is a data point. The horizontal position shows the SHAP value (how much that feature pushed the prediction up or down). The color shows the feature value (red = high, blue = low). You can immediately see patterns: high values of "months since last purchase" push predictions toward churn (positive SHAP), while high values of "engagement score" push away from churn (negative SHAP).

LIME (Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations)

LIME explains individual predictions by fitting a simple, interpretable model (usually a linear model) in the local neighborhood of the data point you're interested in. It generates perturbed versions of the input, gets predictions for each, and then fits a weighted linear model that approximates the complex model's behavior near that specific point.

LIME is model-agnostic — it works with any model because it only needs the model's prediction function, not its internals. The tradeoff is that LIME's explanations can be unstable: run it twice on the same point with different random seeds and you might get slightly different explanations. SHAP is more theoretically grounded and consistent, but LIME is lighter-weight and works well for quick sanity checks.

Partial Dependence Plots

A Partial Dependence Plot (PDP) shows the marginal effect of one or two features on the model's predictions, averaged over all other features. Want to know how "account age" affects churn probability? A PDP sweeps account age from its minimum to its maximum while averaging out everything else. You get a single curve showing the relationship.

from sklearn.inspection import PartialDependenceDisplay

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))
PartialDependenceDisplay.from_estimator(
    model, X_train, features=["account_age", "support_tickets"],
    ax=ax, feature_names=feature_names
)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

PDPs are useful for understanding global trends, but they assume features are independent. If "account age" and "subscription tier" are correlated, the PDP might show you unrealistic combinations (a 10-year-old account on the free tier that was launched last year). SHAP's dependence plots handle this better.

Interpretability vs Performance Tradeoffs

There's a common belief that interpretability and performance are always at odds — that you must sacrifice accuracy for explainability. This isn't entirely true. For tabular data, well-tuned interpretable models (logistic regression with good feature engineering, or explainable boosting machines) can come surprisingly close to black-box models. The gap is often 1-3%, which may be acceptable.

The real tradeoff is in effort. You can either spend time building a simpler model with careful feature engineering that achieves near-top performance, or you can train a complex model quickly and then explain it after the fact with SHAP/LIME. Both paths have their place. The point is that "I used a black box because I needed performance" is no longer a valid excuse for having no explanation at all. Post-hoc interpretability tools have closed that gap.

Key Takeaway

Model selection is not about finding the "best" model in the abstract — it's about finding the best model for your specific data, constraints, and business context. Start with a baseline. Work up in complexity. Compare with statistical rigor. Tune with smart search. Explain with SHAP. Document everything. The discipline of this process matters more than any single algorithm choice.
