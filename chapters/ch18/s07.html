Nice to Know

Chapter 18: Responsible AI and Ethics Awareness-level topics for the well-rounded ML engineer
TL;DR

Bite-sized awareness of topics you'll encounter in responsible AI discussions. None of these need deep expertise to start, but knowing they exist — and when they matter — keeps you from being blindsided.

Sustainability & Carbon Tracking

Training GPT-3 emitted an estimated 552 tonnes of CO₂. CodeCarbon wraps your training loop and reports emissions in real time — two lines of code. Chinchilla scaling laws showed most large models are over-parameterized and under-trained: matching data to parameters cuts compute (and carbon) dramatically. Carbon intensity varies 20× by cloud region — training in Quebec (hydroelectric) vs. Poland (coal) is the single biggest lever. LoRA and QLoRA reduce fine-tuning energy by 99%+ compared to full-parameter training.

When you'd need this: Any team training or fine-tuning models at scale, or any company with ESG reporting requirements.

Active Learning

Instead of labeling data at random, let the model choose which examples to label next — typically the ones it's most uncertain about. Uncertainty sampling picks examples with the highest prediction entropy. Query-by-committee trains multiple models and picks examples they disagree on most. Both can reduce labeling budgets by 50–80% compared to random selection.

When you'd need this: Labeling is expensive (medical imaging, legal documents, specialized NLP), and you have a large pool of unlabeled data.

Societal Impact — Three Failures Every ML Engineer Should Know

Amazon's hiring tool (2018): Trained on 10 years of resumes (mostly male). Learned to penalize resumes containing the word "women's" and downrank graduates of all-women's colleges. Cancelled before deployment — but only because someone checked.

COMPAS recidivism (2016): ProPublica found the system had significantly different false positive rates across racial groups — Black defendants were nearly twice as likely to be incorrectly flagged as high-risk. Sparked an ongoing debate about which fairness definition should apply when they're mathematically incompatible.

Microsoft Tay (2016): A Twitter chatbot that learned from user interactions. Within 16 hours, coordinated trolls taught it to produce offensive content. Lesson: if your model learns from users, users are an attack surface.

When you'd need this: Every design review. These are the cautionary tales that justify bias testing, adversarial robustness, and human oversight.

Deepfakes & Synthetic Media

Detection is an arms race with no finish line. Today's detectors look for artifacts (skin texture inconsistencies, eye reflection patterns), but generation quality improves faster than detection accuracy. C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity) takes a different approach: cryptographic provenance metadata embedded at capture time, so you can verify where content came from rather than analyzing whether it looks fake. It's the most promising long-term approach, but requires adoption across the content pipeline.

When you'd need this: Media platforms, identity verification systems, any system that consumes user-uploaded images or video.

AI in Healthcare Ethics

A 2019 study (Obermeyer et al.) found a widely-used healthcare algorithm systematically underestimated the health needs of Black patients because it used healthcare spending as a proxy for illness — but unequal access to healthcare meant Black patients historically spent less for the same severity. Accuracy differences across demographics aren't abstract fairness metrics — in healthcare, a false negative is a missed diagnosis.

When you'd need this: Any clinical decision support system, diagnostic AI, or patient risk stratification tool.

Intellectual Property

The US Copyright Office has ruled that purely AI-generated content cannot be copyrighted — there must be sufficient human authorship. Training data licensing is being actively litigated (NYT v. OpenAI, Getty v. Stability AI), with no settled precedent yet. For open-source models, RAIL licenses (Responsible AI Licenses) add use restrictions to otherwise permissive licenses — you can use the weights, but not for mass surveillance or to generate disinformation.

When you'd need this: Shipping AI-generated content commercially, training models on scraped data, or releasing model weights publicly.

Open Source vs. Safety

Publishing model weights enables independent safety research, reproducibility, and democratizes access. It also lets anyone fine-tune away safety guardrails or adapt the model for harmful uses. "Responsible open release" — the current compromise — means publishing weights with use restrictions (RAIL licenses), staged release with safety evaluations, or releasing smaller models while keeping frontier models closed. Neither full openness nor full closure is clearly correct.

When you'd need this: Deciding whether and how to release model weights for your organization's models.

Dual-Use Dilemma

The same protein folding model that accelerates drug discovery could theoretically design biological weapons. The same vulnerability-finding AI that secures code could exploit it. There's no technical solution — the capability is the same in both cases. Current mitigations include staged disclosure (share results with safety teams before public release), restricted API access (capability available but not weights), and structured access (tiered access based on verified use case).

When you'd need this: Working on models with clear dual-use potential — biosecurity, cybersecurity, chemistry, autonomous systems.

Economic Impact of AI

Every automation wave triggers the "this time it's different" debate. Historical precedent (ATMs didn't eliminate bank tellers, spreadsheets didn't eliminate accountants) suggests net job creation in the long run — but with brutal transition periods for displaced workers. What's genuinely different this time: AI affects cognitive work, not just manual labor, and the pace of capability improvement is faster than historical precedents. The honest answer is nobody knows how this plays out.

When you'd need this: Stakeholder communications, impact assessments, and being honest about what your automation project means for the people currently doing the work.

Responsible AI Culture

Pre-mortem exercises: Before launch, ask "it's six months from now and this system caused serious harm — what happened?" This surfaces risks that optimistic planning misses. Ethics boards only work if they have actual authority to block or modify deployments — advisory-only boards are theater. Incident disclosure policies (what gets reported, to whom, how fast) should exist before you need them, because writing a disclosure policy during a crisis guarantees a bad one.

When you'd need this: Building any team that ships ML systems. Culture is the defense layer that no amount of tooling can replace.

Asilomar AI Principles

Twenty-three principles published in 2017 by a group of AI researchers at the Asilomar conference. They cover research issues (safety, transparency), ethics and values (privacy, human control, shared benefit), and longer-term issues (capability caution, recursive self-improvement). Not legally binding, but influential — they shaped the OECD AI Principles (2019) and, through them, the EU AI Act. Worth reading once as a historical document that captures the field's consensus at a specific moment.

When you'd need this: Understanding the intellectual lineage of current AI governance frameworks.
