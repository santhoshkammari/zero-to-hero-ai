Explainability and Interpretability

Chapter 18: Responsible AI and Ethics 5 subtopics
TL;DR

When your model denies someone a loan or flags a medical scan, "the model said so" isn't an acceptable answer. Explainability is about producing human-understandable reasons for model predictions — through inherently interpretable models (linear regression, decision trees, GAMs) or post-hoc methods (SHAP, LIME, counterfactuals) applied to black-box models. Each method has tradeoffs between faithfulness, stability, and computational cost. GDPR's "right to explanation" and the EU AI Act make this a legal requirement for high-risk systems, not an optional nice-to-have.

Imagine you apply for a mortgage. The bank's AI system denies you. You ask why. The bank says "our model, which has 400 million parameters and processes your data through 96 attention layers, assigned you a score of 0.43, which is below our threshold of 0.50." Is that an explanation? Technically, yes. Is it useful? Absolutely not. You can't act on it. You can't contest it. You can't even understand it.

That gap between "technically transparent" and "actually understandable" is what explainability research tries to close. And it's not a theoretical exercise — it's a legal requirement in an increasing number of jurisdictions and a practical necessity for debugging, trust-building, and regulatory compliance.

Foundations

Explainability vs Interpretability

These terms are often used interchangeably, but there's a useful distinction. Interpretability means you can understand the model's internal mechanics — you can look at the model and see how it makes decisions. A linear regression is interpretable: each coefficient tells you how much that feature contributes. A 70-billion parameter LLM is not interpretable.

Explainability means you can produce a human-understandable reason for a specific prediction, even if the model itself is opaque. SHAP values for a neural network prediction are explanations — they tell you which features pushed the prediction up or down, without requiring you to understand the network's internals.

The Explainability-Accuracy Tradeoff

There's a commonly cited belief that more accurate models are less interpretable. This is partially true — deep neural networks outperform linear models on many tasks — but the gap is often smaller than assumed, especially with modern interpretable models like Explainable Boosting Machines (EBMs). For many tabular data problems, the accuracy difference between a tuned EBM and a gradient-boosted ensemble is negligible. Always check whether you actually need the black box before reaching for post-hoc explanations.

High-Stakes Domains

Explainability is non-negotiable in healthcare (doctors need to understand why an AI recommends a treatment), finance (regulators require explanations for credit decisions under the Equal Credit Opportunity Act), and criminal justice (defendants have a right to understand the basis for risk assessments). In these domains, deploying an unexplainable model isn't a design choice — it's a liability.

Right to Explanation (GDPR Article 22)

GDPR Article 22 gives EU citizens the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing that significantly affects them. While the exact scope of the "right to explanation" is debated by legal scholars, the practical implication is clear: if you're deploying an automated decision-making system in the EU that affects people, you need to be able to explain its decisions. The EU AI Act reinforces this with explicit transparency requirements for high-risk AI systems.

Why engineers should care about explainability beyond compliance: Explanations are the best debugging tool you have. When SHAP shows that your loan model is heavily weighting zip code, that's a signal you've got a proxy variable for race. When LIME reveals that your image classifier is looking at the background instead of the subject, that's a dataset problem. Explainability isn't a tax on model development — it's a quality assurance tool.

Inherently Interpretable Models

The most honest explanation is a model you can read directly. These models don't need post-hoc explanation methods because their decision process is the explanation.

Linear and Logistic Regression

The coefficients are the explanation. For a logistic regression predicting loan default: if the coefficient for "debt-to-income ratio" is 2.3, you can say "each unit increase in debt-to-income ratio multiplies the odds of default by e^2.3 ≈ 10x." That's a complete, faithful explanation. No approximations, no sampling, no post-hoc analysis needed.

# Interpretable model: Logistic regression with clear coefficients
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

def train_interpretable_model(X_train, y_train, feature_names):
    """Train a logistic regression and extract interpretable coefficients."""
    
    # Standardize so coefficients are comparable
    scaler = StandardScaler()
    X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X_train)
    
    model = LogisticRegression(penalty='l1', C=0.1, solver='saga',
                                max_iter=5000)
    model.fit(X_scaled, y_train)
    
    # Create coefficient table
    coef_df = pd.DataFrame({
        'feature': feature_names,
        'coefficient': model.coef_[0],
        'odds_ratio': np.exp(model.coef_[0]),
    }).sort_values('coefficient', key=abs, ascending=False)
    
    print("=== Model Explanation ===")
    print(f"Intercept (base log-odds): {model.intercept_[0]:.3f}")
    print(f"\nFeature effects (standardized):")
    for _, row in coef_df.iterrows():
        if abs(row['coefficient']) > 0.01:
            direction = "↑ increases" if row['coefficient'] > 0 else "↓ decreases"
            print(f"  {row['feature']}: {direction} risk "
                  f"(odds ratio: {row['odds_ratio']:.2f})")
    
    return model, scaler

# The output IS the explanation. No SHAP needed.

Decision Trees and Rule Lists

Decision trees produce if-then-else rules that anyone can follow. The limitation is that deep trees become unreadable. A tree with 3-5 levels of depth is genuinely interpretable; a tree with 50 levels is no more interpretable than a neural network. Rule lists (ordered sequences of if-then rules) provide similar interpretability with better control over complexity.

Generalized Additive Models (GAMs) and EBMs

Generalized Additive Models (GAMs) model the target as a sum of individual feature effects: f(x) = g(f₁(x₁) + f₂(x₂) + ... + fₙ(xₙ)). Each function fᵢ can be nonlinear (learned via splines or small trees), but the contributions are additive — no interactions. This means you can plot each feature's effect independently.

Explainable Boosting Machines (EBMs), from Microsoft Research's InterpretML library, extend GAMs by also learning pairwise interaction terms. They achieve accuracy competitive with gradient-boosted ensembles on many tabular datasets while remaining fully interpretable. If you're working on a tabular problem where explainability matters, EBMs should be your first choice.

Post-Hoc Explanation Methods

When you're stuck with a black-box model (deep learning, large ensemble), post-hoc methods provide explanations after the fact. These are approximations — they tell you what the model is roughly doing, not exactly what it's doing. Understanding their limitations is as important as knowing how to use them.

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)

SHAP values are based on Shapley values from cooperative game theory. The idea: each feature is a "player" in a coalition game, and the prediction is the "payout." The Shapley value for a feature is its average marginal contribution across all possible orderings of features. SHAP has a uniqueness theorem: it's the only method that satisfies local accuracy (contributions sum to prediction), missingness (missing features contribute zero), and consistency (increasing a feature's contribution doesn't decrease its Shapley value).

# Example: manual SHAP-like explanation for a single prediction
def explain_prediction_manually(model, x, feature_names, baseline, n_samples=1000):
    """Approximate Shapley values via sampling.
    This is a simplified version of KernelSHAP."""
    
    n_features = len(feature_names)
    shapley_values = np.zeros(n_features)
    
    for i in range(n_features):
        marginal_contributions = []
        for _ in range(n_samples):
            # Random subset of other features
            subset = np.random.binomial(1, 0.5, n_features).astype(bool)
            
            # Prediction with feature i
            x_with = baseline.copy()
            x_with[subset] = x[subset]
            x_with[i] = x[i]
            
            # Prediction without feature i
            x_without = baseline.copy()
            x_without[subset] = x[subset]
            x_without[i] = baseline[i]
            
            contribution = (model.predict([x_with])[0]
                          - model.predict([x_without])[0])
            marginal_contributions.append(contribution)
        
        shapley_values[i] = np.mean(marginal_contributions)
    
    return dict(zip(feature_names, shapley_values))

LIME (Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations)

LIME explains individual predictions by fitting a simple interpretable model (typically a sparse linear regression) in the neighborhood of the instance being explained. It perturbs the input, observes how the black-box model's output changes, and fits a local linear approximation weighted by proximity to the original instance.

# LIME: Local explanations via perturbation
import lime
import lime.lime_tabular
import numpy as np

def explain_with_lime(model, X_train, instance, feature_names,
                      class_names=None, num_features=10):
    """Generate LIME explanation for a single prediction."""
    
    explainer = lime.lime_tabular.LimeTabularExplainer(
        X_train,
        feature_names=feature_names,
        class_names=class_names or ['Negative', 'Positive'],
        mode='classification',
    )
    
    explanation = explainer.explain_instance(
        instance,
        model.predict_proba,
        num_features=num_features,
        num_samples=5000,  # More samples = more stable
    )
    
    print("=== LIME Explanation ===")
    print(f"Prediction: {model.predict([instance])[0]}")
    print(f"Prediction probability: {model.predict_proba([instance])[0]}")
    print(f"\nTop contributing features:")
    for feature, weight in explanation.as_list():
        direction = "→ positive" if weight > 0 else "→ negative"
        print(f"  {feature}: {weight:+.3f} {direction}")
    
    return explanation

SHAP vs LIME: When to Use Which

SHAP has stronger theoretical guarantees (uniqueness, consistency) and provides both local and global explanations. LIME is faster for individual explanations and more flexible (it works with any data type including text and images). In practice: use TreeSHAP for tree-based models (it's fast and exact), KernelSHAP or LIME for neural networks (both are approximate), and SHAP when you need global feature importance across many predictions.

Both SHAP and LIME can be misleading. They explain what the model does, not whether what the model does is correct. A SHAP plot might show your model correctly identifies age as the most important feature for a medical prediction — but if the model learned a spurious correlation between age and an imaging artifact, the explanation is faithful to the model but misleading about the world. Always combine explanations with domain expertise.

Integrated Gradients and DeepLIFT

Integrated Gradients is a gradient-based attribution method for neural networks. It computes the integral of gradients along a straight-line path from a baseline input (typically zeros) to the actual input. It satisfies two axioms: sensitivity (if a feature changes the output, it gets non-zero attribution) and implementation invariance (functionally equivalent networks get the same attributions).

DeepLIFT compares each neuron's activation to a reference activation and propagates "contribution scores" backward through the network. It's faster than integrated gradients (no integration needed) but makes stronger assumptions about the network architecture.

# Integrated Gradients with PyTorch (using Captum)
import torch
# from captum.attr import IntegratedGradients, DeepLift

def integrated_gradients_manual(model, input_tensor, baseline, steps=50):
    """Manual implementation of Integrated Gradients.
    
    IG_i(x) = (x_i - x'_i) * integral from 0 to 1 of
              (d F(x' + alpha*(x-x')) / d x_i) d_alpha
    """
    # Approximate the integral with Riemann sum
    scaled_inputs = [baseline + (float(i) / steps) * (input_tensor - baseline)
                     for i in range(steps + 1)]
    
    grads = []
    for scaled_input in scaled_inputs:
        scaled_input.requires_grad_(True)
        output = model(scaled_input)
        output.backward()
        grads.append(scaled_input.grad.detach().clone())
        model.zero_grad()
    
    # Average gradients (trapezoidal rule)
    avg_grads = torch.stack(grads).mean(dim=0)
    
    # Scale by (input - baseline)
    attributions = (input_tensor - baseline) * avg_grads
    
    return attributions

Concept-Based Explanations: TCAV

TCAV (Testing with Concept Activation Vectors) explains model behavior in terms of human-understandable concepts rather than individual features. Instead of "pixel 342 contributed 0.02 to the prediction," TCAV says "the concept of 'stripes' was important for classifying this image as a zebra." It works by training a linear classifier to distinguish concept examples from random examples in the model's activation space, defining a concept activation vector (CAV), and then measuring how sensitive the model's predictions are to that direction.

Counterfactual Explanations

Counterfactual explanations answer the question: "What's the smallest change to the input that would flip the prediction?" This is often the most actionable form of explanation. "Your loan was denied. If your income were $5,000 higher, it would have been approved." That tells the applicant exactly what to change.

# Counterfactual explanations with DiCE
# DiCE generates diverse counterfactual explanations
import dice_ml

def generate_counterfactuals(model, X_train, instance_df,
                             feature_names, outcome_name,
                             n_counterfactuals=3):
    """Generate diverse counterfactual explanations using DiCE."""
    
    # Create DiCE data object
    d = dice_ml.Data(
        dataframe=X_train,
        continuous_features=feature_names,
        outcome_name=outcome_name,
    )
    
    # Create DiCE model object
    m = dice_ml.Model(model=model, backend="sklearn")
    
    # Generate counterfactuals
    exp = dice_ml.Dice(d, m, method="random")
    
    counterfactuals = exp.generate_counterfactuals(
        instance_df,
        total_CFs=n_counterfactuals,
        desired_class="opposite",
    )
    
    counterfactuals.visualize_as_dataframe()
    return counterfactuals

Attention as Explanation (and Its Limits)

Attention weights in Transformers show where the model "looks." It's tempting to use them as explanations — "the model paid attention to the word 'not' when predicting negative sentiment." But Jain and Wallace (2019) showed that attention weights often don't correlate with gradient-based feature importance, and you can find alternative attention patterns that produce identical predictions. Attention rollout and attention flow are more principled approaches, but the consensus is that attention weights are at best a noisy signal, not a reliable explanation.

Evaluation and Faithfulness

Faithfulness of Explanations

An explanation is faithful if it accurately reflects what the model is actually doing. This is the most important property and the hardest to verify. If you mask the features that SHAP says are most important, the prediction should change significantly. If it doesn't, the explanation is unfaithful.

# Testing explanation faithfulness
def test_faithfulness(model, x, explanation_attributions, feature_names,
                      baseline, top_k=5):
    """Test if removing top-attributed features actually changes predictions.
    
    A faithful explanation's top features should matter most.
    """
    original_pred = model.predict_proba([x])[0][1]
    
    # Sort features by attribution magnitude
    sorted_indices = np.argsort(np.abs(explanation_attributions))[::-1]
    
    print(f"Original prediction: {original_pred:.3f}")
    print(f"\nRemoving features by attribution rank:")
    
    x_modified = x.copy()
    for i in range(top_k):
        idx = sorted_indices[i]
        x_modified[idx] = baseline[idx]  # Replace with baseline
        new_pred = model.predict_proba([x_modified])[0][1]
        change = abs(new_pred - original_pred)
        print(f"  Remove {feature_names[idx]}: "
              f"pred = {new_pred:.3f} (Δ = {change:.3f})")
    
    # Compare with random removal
    x_random = x.copy()
    random_indices = np.random.permutation(len(feature_names))
    print(f"\nRemoving features randomly:")
    for i in range(top_k):
        idx = random_indices[i]
        x_random[idx] = baseline[idx]
        new_pred = model.predict_proba([x_random])[0][1]
        change = abs(new_pred - original_pred)
        print(f"  Remove {feature_names[idx]}: "
              f"pred = {new_pred:.3f} (Δ = {change:.3f})")
    
    # Faithful explanations: attribution-ordered removal causes
    # faster prediction change than random removal.

Stability and Risks of Misleading Explanations

Explanations should be stable — small changes to the input shouldn't cause large changes in the explanation. But LIME in particular is known for instability: run it twice on the same instance with different random seeds, and you might get different top features. Slack et al. (2020) showed that adversarial models can be constructed to produce fair-looking explanations while actually discriminating — hiding bias from SHAP and LIME. This is a fundamental limitation of post-hoc explanation methods: they can be gamed.

Production Explainability

Real-Time Explanations in Serving Systems

Computing SHAP values for every prediction in a real-time serving system is often too slow. Practical approaches: pre-compute SHAP values for common input patterns, use TreeSHAP for tree models (it's polynomial, not exponential), cache explanations for similar inputs, or use simpler explanation methods (top-3 feature contributions from a linear proxy model) for real-time serving and full SHAP for audit logs.

Explaining to Non-Technical Users

A SHAP waterfall plot is great for a data scientist. For the loan applicant, you need natural language: "Your application was declined primarily because your debt-to-income ratio (42%) exceeds our typical threshold. Your strong credit history was a positive factor." Translating technical explanations into actionable, human-readable text is a design problem, not a modeling problem — and it's where most explainability efforts fail.

Explainability Toolkits

The major toolkits: SHAP (Python, the standard for feature attribution), Captum (PyTorch-native, integrated gradients, DeepLIFT, GradCAM), Alibi (counterfactual explanations, trust scores, anchors), and InterpretML (EBMs, SHAP integration, unified API). For production, Captum integrates best with PyTorch serving pipelines, and SHAP integrates best with scikit-learn and XGBoost workflows.

The pragmatist's approach: Start with an interpretable model (EBM, logistic regression). If you genuinely need a black box for accuracy, use TreeSHAP for tree models or Integrated Gradients for deep learning. Run faithfulness tests before trusting any explanation method. And always, always translate technical explanations into language your stakeholders can act on.
