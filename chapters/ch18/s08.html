AI Governance

Chapter 18: Responsible AI and Ethics 3 subtopics
TL;DR

AI governance is the system of laws, organizational policies, and accountability structures that ensure AI is developed and deployed responsibly. The EU AI Act (enforceable from 2024-2027) is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation, classifying systems by risk level. Internally, organizations need ethics boards, model documentation standards, and audit trails. And a growing body of intellectual property law is grappling with who owns AI-generated content and the training data that produced it.

You built a model. It works well in your tests. You want to ship it. But can you? In 2020 the answer was almost always "yes — nobody's stopping you." In 2025 the answer is "it depends on where you deploy, what the model does, and who it affects." AI governance has moved from academic discussion to hard law, and if you are building production ML systems, you need to understand the landscape.

This section covers three layers of governance: the external regulatory environment (what governments require), internal organizational governance (what your company should do even if no law mandates it), and intellectual property (the legal minefield around training data and generated content).

Regulatory Landscape

EU AI Act

The EU AI Act is the world's first comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence. It entered into force on August 1, 2024, with a phased rollout through 2027. If GDPR was the earthquake that reshaped data privacy, the EU AI Act is the equivalent for AI development. And like GDPR, its reach extends far beyond Europe — any company that deploys AI systems affecting EU residents must comply, regardless of where the company is headquartered.

The Act takes a risk-based approach, classifying AI systems into four tiers:

Risk Classification (Unacceptable, High, Limited, Minimal)

Unacceptable risk systems are banned outright. These include social scoring by governments (think China's social credit system), real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces (with narrow law enforcement exceptions), AI that exploits vulnerable populations (children, disabled people), and subliminal manipulation techniques that cause harm. These prohibitions took effect in February 2025.

High risk systems face strict requirements. This category covers AI used in critical infrastructure, education (automated grading, admissions), employment (resume screening, interview assessment), law enforcement (predictive policing, recidivism prediction), migration (visa processing, border control), healthcare (diagnostic systems), and credit scoring. High-risk systems must comply with rigorous requirements by August 2026.

Limited risk systems have transparency obligations. Users must be informed they are interacting with an AI system. This covers chatbots, deepfake generators, and emotion recognition systems. The idea: people have a right to know when they are talking to a machine or looking at synthetic content.

Minimal risk systems face no additional requirements. Spam filters, AI in video games, inventory management systems — these can operate freely.

# Conceptual risk classification framework aligned with EU AI Act
from enum import Enum
from dataclasses import dataclass

class RiskLevel(Enum):
    UNACCEPTABLE = "unacceptable"  # Banned
    HIGH = "high"                  # Strict requirements
    LIMITED = "limited"            # Transparency obligations
    MINIMAL = "minimal"            # No additional requirements

# Application domain -> Risk level mapping (simplified)
RISK_CLASSIFICATION = {
    "social_scoring": RiskLevel.UNACCEPTABLE,
    "realtime_biometric_public": RiskLevel.UNACCEPTABLE,
    "subliminal_manipulation": RiskLevel.UNACCEPTABLE,
    "credit_scoring": RiskLevel.HIGH,
    "resume_screening": RiskLevel.HIGH,
    "medical_diagnosis": RiskLevel.HIGH,
    "predictive_policing": RiskLevel.HIGH,
    "education_admissions": RiskLevel.HIGH,
    "critical_infrastructure": RiskLevel.HIGH,
    "chatbot": RiskLevel.LIMITED,
    "deepfake_generation": RiskLevel.LIMITED,
    "emotion_recognition": RiskLevel.LIMITED,
    "spam_filter": RiskLevel.MINIMAL,
    "game_ai": RiskLevel.MINIMAL,
    "recommendation_engine": RiskLevel.MINIMAL,
}

@dataclass
class ComplianceRequirements:
    risk_level: RiskLevel
    requirements: list
    deadline: str

def get_compliance_requirements(application: str) -> ComplianceRequirements:
    risk = RISK_CLASSIFICATION.get(application, RiskLevel.MINIMAL)

    requirements_map = {
        RiskLevel.UNACCEPTABLE: ComplianceRequirements(
            risk, ["System is PROHIBITED under EU AI Act"], "February 2025"
        ),
        RiskLevel.HIGH: ComplianceRequirements(
            risk,
            [
                "Risk management system",
                "Data governance and quality standards",
                "Technical documentation",
                "Record-keeping and logging",
                "Transparency to deployers",
                "Human oversight mechanisms",
                "Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity",
                "Registration in EU database",
                "Conformity assessment before deployment",
            ],
            "August 2026"
        ),
        RiskLevel.LIMITED: ComplianceRequirements(
            risk,
            ["Inform users they are interacting with AI",
             "Label AI-generated content (deepfakes)"],
            "August 2026"
        ),
        RiskLevel.MINIMAL: ComplianceRequirements(
            risk, ["No additional requirements (voluntary codes of conduct encouraged)"], "N/A"
        ),
    }
    return requirements_map[risk]

# Example usage
reqs = get_compliance_requirements("resume_screening")
print(f"Risk: {reqs.risk_level.value}")
print(f"Deadline: {reqs.deadline}")
for r in reqs.requirements:
    print(f"  - {r}")

Requirements for High-Risk AI Providers

Providers of high-risk AI systems must implement a comprehensive risk management system throughout the system's lifecycle. They must use high-quality training data with documented governance practices. They must maintain detailed technical documentation that allows authorities to assess compliance. They must build in logging capabilities for traceability. They must provide clear information to deployers. And they must ensure human oversight — a qualified person must be able to understand, monitor, and override the system's decisions.

Penalties for Non-Compliance

The penalties are designed to hurt. Deploying a prohibited (unacceptable risk) AI system carries fines of up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover — whichever is higher. For other violations, fines can reach €15 million or 3% of turnover. For comparison, GDPR's maximum fine is €20 million or 4% of turnover. The EU is signaling that they take AI governance at least as seriously as data protection.

General-Purpose AI Model Obligations

The Act includes specific obligations for general-purpose AI (GPAI) models like GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini. All GPAI providers must maintain technical documentation, provide information to downstream deployers, comply with copyright law, and publish a training data summary. GPAI models with "systemic risk" (defined as models trained with more than 10^25 FLOPs or equivalent) face additional requirements: adversarial testing (red-teaming), incident reporting to the EU AI Office, model evaluation for systemic risks, and cybersecurity protections. These GPAI obligations took effect in August 2025.

The Brussels Effect

Like GDPR before it, the EU AI Act will likely become the de facto global standard. Companies rarely build two versions of a product — one for EU users and one for everyone else. It is cheaper to comply globally. Brazil, Canada, and South Korea are already drafting legislation modeled on the EU approach. If you build AI products, plan for EU AI Act compliance even if you are not in Europe yet.

NIST AI Risk Management Framework

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF), released in January 2023, is the United States' answer to AI governance — but unlike the EU AI Act, it is voluntary. It provides a structured approach to identifying, assessing, and mitigating AI risks, organized around four core functions: Govern (establish accountability and culture), Map (understand the context and risks of your AI system), Measure (assess and track risks quantitatively), and Manage (prioritize and act on identified risks).

The framework is technology-agnostic and designed to be adapted across industries. While it lacks enforcement teeth, it has become the reference standard for US organizations. Many companies use NIST AI RMF compliance as evidence of due diligence — a useful defense if something goes wrong and regulators or courts come asking questions.

Industry-Specific Regulations

Beyond horizontal AI regulations, sector-specific rules add additional layers. In healthcare, the FDA regulates AI as a medical device — the SaMD (Software as a Medical Device) framework requires clinical validation before deployment. In finance, model risk management guidelines (SR 11-7 in the US) require validation, monitoring, and documentation of any model used in lending, trading, or risk assessment. In autonomous vehicles, NHTSA sets safety standards that implicitly govern the AI systems controlling the car.

The practical implication: your compliance obligations are the union of horizontal AI regulations (EU AI Act, potential US federal legislation) and your industry's existing rules. A medical AI deployed in Europe must satisfy both the EU AI Act's high-risk requirements and the EU Medical Device Regulation.

International AI Governance

The global governance landscape is fragmented. The EU has binding legislation. The US relies on voluntary frameworks and executive orders. China has enacted targeted regulations on algorithmic recommendations, deepfakes, and generative AI. The UK has taken a "pro-innovation" sector-based approach without comprehensive legislation. Japan and South Korea are developing their own frameworks.

For global companies, this patchwork creates compliance complexity. The safe strategy: build to the strictest standard (usually the EU AI Act) and add region-specific adjustments. International bodies like the OECD, G7, and the UN are working toward harmonization, but binding global standards are years away.

Organizational Governance

AI Ethics Boards

An AI ethics board is an internal body that reviews AI projects for ethical risks before deployment. Effective boards include diverse perspectives: ML engineers who understand technical capabilities and limitations, ethicists who can identify value conflicts, legal counsel who know regulatory requirements, domain experts who understand the deployment context, and representatives of affected communities.

The board should have real authority — not advisory power that can be overridden by product managers under pressure to ship. A board that can only recommend but not block is theater. The best implementations give the ethics board veto power over high-risk deployments, with an escalation path to executive leadership for disputes.

Model Risk Management

Model risk management (MRM) treats ML models as sources of operational risk that require systematic governance. Originally developed in banking (the Fed's SR 11-7 guidance), MRM principles now apply to any organization deploying ML in consequential decisions. The framework covers the full lifecycle: model development (design, training, validation), model deployment (integration, monitoring, performance tracking), and model retirement (when to take a model out of production and replace it).

Key MRM practices include independent model validation (the team that built the model should not be the team that validates it), ongoing monitoring of model performance and data drift, defined escalation procedures when models degrade, and regular model reviews on a fixed cadence (quarterly or semi-annually for high-risk models).

Documentation Standards (Model Cards, Datasheets)

Model cards (proposed by Mitchell et al., 2019 at Google) are standardized documents that describe a model's intended use, training data, evaluation metrics, performance across subgroups, and known limitations. Think of them as nutrition labels for ML models. A model card for a facial recognition system should disclose accuracy by skin tone, gender, and age group — not only the aggregate number that hides disparities.

Datasheets for datasets (proposed by Gebru et al., 2021) apply the same principle to training data. They document how data was collected, who collected it, what populations it represents, known biases, and recommended uses. Together, model cards and datasheets create an accountability chain — anyone deploying a model can trace its lineage and understand its limitations.

Audit Trails and Accountability

Audit trails record every decision made by an AI system along with the inputs, model version, confidence score, and any human overrides. When a customer asks "Why was my loan denied?", the audit trail provides the answer. When a regulator investigates potential discrimination, the audit trail provides the evidence. Without it, you have a black box producing consequential decisions with no way to explain or review them after the fact.

Implementation requires logging at multiple levels: the input data, the model version and configuration used, the raw model output (probabilities), any post-processing rules applied, the final decision, and any human review or override. Logs must be immutable (append-only), timestamped, and retained for the legally required period (which varies by industry and jurisdiction).

Responsible AI Maturity Models

A Responsible AI maturity model helps organizations assess where they stand and what to improve. Most follow a progression from ad hoc (no formal AI governance) through defined (policies exist but are inconsistently applied), managed (governance is operationalized and monitored), to optimized (continuous improvement, industry leadership). Microsoft, Google, and the OECD all publish maturity frameworks.

The value is in the self-assessment. Most organizations discover they are further behind than they assumed. Having a maturity model forces honest conversations about gaps — and creates a roadmap for closing them.

Intellectual Property

Copyright and AI-Generated Content

Copyright and AI-generated content is one of the most contested legal questions of the decade. In the US, the Copyright Office has ruled that purely AI-generated content cannot be copyrighted — copyright requires human authorship. But content created with substantial human involvement in directing, selecting, and arranging AI outputs can qualify. The lines are blurry and being tested in courts worldwide.

For practical purposes: if your product generates content for users, you need to understand whether that content is protectable. If you are using AI to create marketing copy, code, or artwork, the copyright status of the output affects your business model and legal exposure.

Training Data Licensing

Training data licensing is the other side of the IP coin. Major lawsuits (The New York Times v. OpenAI, Getty Images v. Stability AI) are testing whether using copyrighted material to train AI models constitutes fair use. The EU AI Act requires GPAI providers to publish summaries of training data and respect copyright opt-out mechanisms. Some jurisdictions (Japan) have taken a more permissive stance on training data use.

The safest approach for organizations: document your training data sources, use properly licensed datasets, respect robots.txt and opt-out signals, and maintain records that demonstrate your due diligence. Even if fair use arguments ultimately prevail, demonstrating good faith reduces legal risk.

Open Source Model Licenses

Open source AI licenses are evolving rapidly. Traditional open-source licenses (MIT, Apache 2.0) were designed for code, not models. New licenses like Meta's Llama Community License and BigScience's RAIL (Responsible AI License) add use restrictions — you can use the model freely except for specific harmful applications. These "responsible AI licenses" sit in a gray area between open source (no restrictions on use) and proprietary (all restrictions). Understand the license of any model you use, and pay special attention to commercial use clauses and acceptable use policies.

Patent Considerations

Can an AI be listed as an inventor on a patent? Courts in the US, UK, and EU have said no — patents require a human inventor. But methods, architectures, and applications of AI are routinely patented by the humans who develop them. The strategic question: if your company develops novel ML techniques, are you patenting them? If competitors patent similar approaches, could your freedom to operate be constrained? AI patent filings have increased 10x in the last decade. Ignore the IP landscape at your own risk.

Governance Is Not Optional

The transition from "AI governance is nice to have" to "AI governance is legally required" happened between 2023 and 2025. If your organization deploys AI without a governance framework — no model cards, no audit trails, no risk classification, no ethics review — you are accumulating regulatory, legal, and reputational debt. The interest rate on that debt is about to spike.
