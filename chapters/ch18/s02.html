Fairness

Chapter 18: Responsible AI and Ethics 6 subtopics
TL;DR

Fairness in ML is hard because there are many mathematically precise definitions of "fair," and they contradict each other when group base rates differ. Bias enters through data (historical, sampling, labeling), gets amplified by models (proxy variables, embedding associations), and can only be measured with fairness metrics that force you to make explicit value judgments. Debiasing techniques exist at every stage of the pipeline — pre-processing, in-processing, and post-processing — but none eliminate the fundamental tradeoffs. Tools like Fairlearn and AIF360 make measurement practical, and legal frameworks like the EU AI Act are making it mandatory.

Here's a scenario you will face. You build a loan approval model. Overall accuracy: 92%. Your manager is thrilled. Then someone slices the results by race and finds that the false rejection rate for Black applicants is three times higher than for white applicants. Is your model fair? The answer depends entirely on what you mean by "fair" — and there are at least six mathematically distinct definitions, most of which you cannot satisfy simultaneously.

Fairness isn't a checkbox. It's a series of difficult tradeoffs that require explicit value judgments. This section gives you the technical vocabulary and tools to navigate those tradeoffs — because if you don't make these choices deliberately, the defaults in your data will make them for you.

Bias in Data

Bias doesn't start with your model. It starts with your data. And understanding the different ways data can be biased is the first step toward building fairer systems.

Historical Bias

Historical bias exists when the data faithfully reflects a world that was (and is) unfair. Amazon's hiring model is the canonical example: the training data accurately captured that Amazon had historically hired mostly men for technical roles. The model learned this pattern perfectly. The data wasn't corrupted or mislabeled — it was an accurate reflection of a biased process. This is the hardest form of bias to address because the data itself is "correct."

Representation Bias

Representation bias occurs when certain groups are underrepresented in the training data. ImageNet, for years the benchmark for computer vision, was heavily skewed toward images from the US and Western Europe. Models trained on it performed significantly worse on images from other regions. Medical datasets skew toward populations that are more likely to participate in clinical trials — typically younger, whiter, and more urban than the general patient population.

Measurement Bias

Measurement bias arises when the feature you measure is a noisy or systematically distorted proxy for the concept you care about. Consider using "number of prior arrests" as a proxy for "criminal behavior." Arrest rates are influenced by policing patterns — neighborhoods with heavier policing will generate more arrests regardless of actual crime rates. The measurement itself is contaminated by the very biases you're trying to avoid.

Sampling, Label, and Aggregation Bias

Sampling bias occurs when your data collection process systematically excludes certain populations. If you train a speech recognition system on data collected from podcast listeners, you'll underrepresent elderly users, non-native speakers, and people with speech disabilities.

Label bias happens when the labels themselves reflect human prejudice. Toxicity classifiers trained on human annotations consistently flag African American English as more "toxic" than equivalent expressions in Standard American English. The annotators aren't necessarily racist — they're reflecting cultural biases embedded in language norms.

Aggregation bias is the failure to account for differences across subgroups. A diabetes prediction model trained on pooled data from multiple ethnic groups may learn patterns that are accurate on average but wrong for specific groups, because the relationship between HbA1c levels and diabetes risk varies across populations.

# Detecting representation bias in your dataset
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

def audit_representation(df, sensitive_col, label_col):
    """Check if groups are represented proportionally
    and if label distributions vary across groups."""
    
    # Group sizes
    group_counts = df[sensitive_col].value_counts()
    group_proportions = group_counts / len(df)
    print("Group representation:")
    print(group_proportions.to_string())
    print()
    
    # Label distribution per group
    print("Positive label rate per group:")
    for group in df[sensitive_col].unique():
        subset = df[df[sensitive_col] == group]
        pos_rate = subset[label_col].mean()
        print(f"  {group}: {pos_rate:.3f} (n={len(subset)})")
    
    # Check for severe imbalance
    ratio = group_counts.max() / group_counts.min()
    if ratio > 5:
        print(f"\n⚠️ WARNING: Most/least represented group ratio = {ratio:.1f}x")
        print("   Consider resampling or collecting more data for minority groups.")

Bias in Models

Algorithmic Bias and Amplification

Algorithmic bias is bias introduced or amplified by the model itself — through architecture choices, optimization objectives, or inductive biases. A key insight: models don't just reproduce bias from data, they often amplify it. If your training data shows 60% of nurses as female, the model might predict 75% of nurses as female. This happens because the model learns to exploit statistical correlations, and biased correlations in the data get reinforced through optimization.

Proxy Variables

Even if you remove protected attributes (race, gender, age) from your features, the model can reconstruct them from proxy variables. Zip code is a proxy for race in the US due to residential segregation. Name is a proxy for gender and ethnicity. Purchasing patterns can predict income. This is why "fairness through unawareness" — dropping the sensitive attribute and hoping for the best — doesn't work. The model will find proxies.

# Demonstrating proxy variable detection
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score
import numpy as np

def detect_proxy_variables(X, sensitive_attribute, feature_names):
    """Can we predict the sensitive attribute from other features?
    If yes, those features are proxies."""
    
    model = LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000)
    scores = cross_val_score(model, X, sensitive_attribute, cv=5, scoring='roc_auc')
    
    print(f"Predicting sensitive attribute from features:")
    print(f"  AUC = {scores.mean():.3f} ± {scores.std():.3f}")
    
    if scores.mean() > 0.7:
        print("  ⚠️ Features contain strong proxies for the sensitive attribute!")
        
        # Fit on full data to see which features are proxies
        model.fit(X, sensitive_attribute)
        importances = np.abs(model.coef_[0])
        top_proxies = np.argsort(importances)[-5:][::-1]
        print("  Top proxy features:")
        for idx in top_proxies:
            print(f"    {feature_names[idx]}: |coef| = {importances[idx]:.3f}")
    else:
        print("  ✓ No strong proxy relationship detected.")

Embedding Bias (Word2Vec, BERT)

Word embeddings encode human biases from the text they're trained on. The famous finding: in Word2Vec trained on Google News, the analogy "man is to computer programmer as woman is to homemaker" was a mathematical relationship in the embedding space. BERT and GPT models inherit these biases at a deeper level because they learn contextual representations from massive web corpora that reflect societal stereotypes.

Debiasing embeddings is an active research area, but it's harder than it sounds. You can project out a "gender direction" from word vectors (Bolukbasi et al., 2016), but studies have shown that apparent debiasing can mask rather than remove bias — the information migrates to other dimensions.

Fairness Metrics

This is where things get mathematically precise — and where the impossibility results live. Each metric encodes a different moral intuition about what "fair" means.

Demographic Parity

Demographic parity (also called statistical parity) requires that the selection rate be equal across groups. Formally: P(Ŷ=1 | A=0) = P(Ŷ=1 | A=1), where A is the sensitive attribute. In plain English: the fraction of people approved should be the same regardless of group membership.

The appeal is intuitive — equal rates feel fair. The problem: if the base rates actually differ between groups (for legitimate, non-discriminatory reasons), enforcing demographic parity forces you to accept different error rates. It also ignores whether the right people within each group are being selected.

Equalized Odds

Equalized odds requires that true positive rates and false positive rates be equal across groups. Formally: P(Ŷ=1 | Y=1, A=a) = P(Ŷ=1 | Y=1, A=b) AND P(Ŷ=1 | Y=0, A=a) = P(Ŷ=1 | Y=0, A=b). In plain English: the model should be equally accurate (in terms of both catching positives and avoiding false alarms) regardless of group.

Equal Opportunity

Equal opportunity is a relaxation of equalized odds — it only requires equal true positive rates across groups. This is appropriate when the cost of a false negative is much higher than a false positive. For example, in cancer screening, missing a true cancer case is far worse than an unnecessary follow-up test. Equal opportunity says: among people who truly have cancer, the detection rate should be the same regardless of demographic group.

Predictive Parity

Predictive parity requires that the positive predictive value be equal across groups: P(Y=1 | Ŷ=1, A=a) = P(Y=1 | Ŷ=1, A=b). Among those flagged as positive, the same proportion should actually be positive in each group. This was Northpointe's defense of COMPAS — among defendants scored as high-risk, similar fractions actually reoffended across racial groups.

Individual and Counterfactual Fairness

Individual fairness says that similar individuals should receive similar predictions — a Lipschitz condition on the model with respect to a task-specific similarity metric. The challenge is defining "similar" in a way that doesn't smuggle in the very biases you're trying to eliminate.

Counterfactual fairness asks: would this individual's prediction change if they had been a different race/gender/etc., but everything else about them had been the same? This requires a causal model of how the sensitive attribute affects other features — which is a strong assumption.

The Impossibility Theorem

Here's the result that makes fairness genuinely hard. Chouldechova (2017) and Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan (2016) independently proved that when base rates differ between groups, you cannot simultaneously achieve calibration (predictive parity), equal false positive rates, and equal false negative rates — except in trivial cases (perfect prediction or equal base rates).

# The impossibility theorem in action
# Using Fairlearn to measure multiple fairness metrics simultaneously

from fairlearn.metrics import (
    MetricFrame,
    selection_rate,
    demographic_parity_difference,
    equalized_odds_difference,
    false_positive_rate,
    false_negative_rate,
)
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score
import numpy as np

np.random.seed(42)

# Simulate a classifier's predictions on two groups
# Group A: 40% base rate, Group B: 25% base rate
n = 2000
groups = np.array(['A'] * n + ['B'] * n)

# True labels (different base rates)
y_true_a = np.random.binomial(1, 0.40, n)
y_true_b = np.random.binomial(1, 0.25, n)
y_true = np.concatenate([y_true_a, y_true_b])

# Model predictions (decent but imperfect classifier)
y_pred_a = np.where(np.random.random(n) < 0.7, y_true_a,
                    1 - y_true_a)
y_pred_b = np.where(np.random.random(n) < 0.7, y_true_b,
                    1 - y_true_b)
y_pred = np.concatenate([y_pred_a, y_pred_b])

# Measure everything
metrics = {
    'accuracy': accuracy_score,
    'selection_rate': selection_rate,
    'false_positive_rate': false_positive_rate,
    'false_negative_rate': false_negative_rate,
}

mf = MetricFrame(
    metrics=metrics,
    y_true=y_true,
    y_pred=y_pred,
    sensitive_features=groups,
)

print("=== Per-group metrics ===")
print(mf.by_group.to_string())
print()
print(f"Demographic parity difference: "
      f"{demographic_parity_difference(y_true, y_pred, sensitive_features=groups):.3f}")
print(f"Equalized odds difference: "
      f"{equalized_odds_difference(y_true, y_pred, sensitive_features=groups):.3f}")

# You'll see: even with the same classifier quality (70% flip probability),
# different base rates create different selection rates.
# Fixing one metric will break another.
The impossibility theorem means you must choose. There is no mathematically "correct" fairness metric. Each encodes a different moral value. Demographic parity prioritizes equal outcomes. Equalized odds prioritizes equal accuracy. Predictive parity prioritizes equal trust in predictions. Your job as an engineer is to understand these tradeoffs and make the choice explicit — not to pretend the choice doesn't exist.

Debiasing Techniques

Debiasing happens at three points in the ML pipeline, each with different tradeoffs.

Pre-Processing: Fix the Data

Pre-processing techniques modify the training data before the model sees it. Resampling adjusts group representation (oversampling minorities, undersampling majorities). Reweighting assigns different sample weights so the loss function pays more attention to underrepresented groups. These approaches are model-agnostic and easy to implement, but they can only fix representation problems — they can't address historical bias in the labels themselves.

# Pre-processing: Reweighting with Fairlearn
from fairlearn.reductions import ExponentiatedGradient, DemographicParity
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression

# Approach 1: Simple reweighting
from sklearn.utils.class_weight import compute_sample_weight

def compute_fairness_weights(y, sensitive_features):
    """Compute sample weights that equalize group influence."""
    groups = np.unique(sensitive_features)
    weights = np.ones(len(y), dtype=float)
    
    for g in groups:
        mask = sensitive_features == g
        group_size = mask.sum()
        # Upweight smaller groups, downweight larger groups
        weights[mask] = len(y) / (len(groups) * group_size)
    
    return weights

# Usage: pass weights to model.fit()
# model.fit(X_train, y_train, sample_weight=weights)

In-Processing: Constrained Optimization

In-processing techniques modify the learning algorithm itself. Adversarial debiasing adds a second network that tries to predict the sensitive attribute from the model's internal representations. The main model is trained to maximize task accuracy while minimizing the adversary's ability to recover group membership. Constrained optimization directly adds fairness constraints to the objective function — "maximize accuracy subject to demographic parity difference < 0.05."

# In-processing: Fairlearn's ExponentiatedGradient
# This reduces fair classification to a sequence of cost-sensitive problems

from fairlearn.reductions import (
    ExponentiatedGradient,
    DemographicParity,
    EqualizedOdds,
)
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score

# Train a fair classifier using exponentiated gradient reduction
constraint = DemographicParity()  # or EqualizedOdds()

mitigator = ExponentiatedGradient(
    estimator=LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000),
    constraints=constraint,
    eps=0.01,  # Fairness tolerance
)

Post-Processing: Adjust the Outputs

Post-processing techniques don't touch the model at all — they adjust prediction thresholds after the fact. Threshold adjustment uses different decision thresholds for different groups to equalize a fairness metric. This is the simplest approach and requires no model retraining, but it explicitly uses group membership in decision-making, which may be legally prohibited in some contexts.

# Post-processing: Group-specific threshold optimization
from fairlearn.postprocessing import ThresholdOptimizer

# ThresholdOptimizer finds per-group thresholds that
# optimize accuracy subject to a fairness constraint

postprocess = ThresholdOptimizer(
    estimator=base_model,         # Your trained model
    constraints="equalized_odds", # or "demographic_parity"
    objective="accuracy_score",
    prefit=True,                  # Model is already trained
)

Fairness Toolkits: AIF360 and Fairlearn

Two production-quality fairness toolkits dominate the ecosystem:

AI Fairness 360 (AIF360), from IBM Research, provides a comprehensive set of bias metrics (70+), mitigation algorithms, and dataset wrappers. It uses its own BinaryLabelDataset class, which can feel heavy but enables a consistent API across all algorithms.

Fairlearn, from Microsoft Research, integrates more naturally with scikit-learn. Its MetricFrame class is excellent for exploratory fairness analysis, and its reduction-based algorithms (ExponentiatedGradient, GridSearch) provide strong theoretical guarantees.

# AIF360: Full bias audit pipeline
from aif360.datasets import StandardDataset
from aif360.metrics import ClassificationMetric
import pandas as pd

# Assuming you have a pandas DataFrame with predictions
def aif360_audit(df, label_col, pred_col, protected_col,
                 privileged_val, unprivileged_val):
    """Run a comprehensive fairness audit using AIF360."""
    
    dataset = StandardDataset(
        df,
        label_name=label_col,
        favorable_classes=[1],
        protected_attribute_names=[protected_col],
        privileged_classes=[[privileged_val]],
    )
    
    pred_dataset = dataset.copy()
    pred_dataset.labels = df[pred_col].values.reshape(-1, 1)
    
    metric = ClassificationMetric(
        dataset, pred_dataset,
        privileged_groups=[{protected_col: privileged_val}],
        unprivileged_groups=[{protected_col: unprivileged_val}],
    )
    
    print("=== AIF360 Fairness Audit ===")
    print(f"Disparate Impact: {metric.disparate_impact():.3f}")
    print(f"  (1.0 = perfect parity, EEOC threshold: 0.8-1.25)")
    print(f"Equal Opportunity Diff: "
          f"{metric.equal_opportunity_difference():.3f}")
    print(f"Average Odds Diff: "
          f"{metric.average_odds_difference():.3f}")
    print(f"Theil Index: {metric.theil_index():.3f}")
    
    return metric

Legal Foundations

Disparate Impact and Disparate Treatment

US anti-discrimination law distinguishes between disparate treatment (intentionally treating people differently based on protected class) and disparate impact (policies that are facially neutral but disproportionately affect a protected group). Most ML fairness problems fall under disparate impact — nobody intended to discriminate, but the system's outcomes are disproportionate.

The EEOC 80% Rule

The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission's "four-fifths rule" states that a selection rate for a protected group that is less than 80% of the rate for the group with the highest rate constitutes evidence of adverse impact. In ML terms: if your model approves 50% of Group A, it should approve at least 40% of Group B (50% × 0.8). This is the legal operationalization of disparate impact, and it maps directly to the "disparate impact ratio" metric in AIF360.

EU AI Act Risk Categories

The EU AI Act, which entered into force on August 1, 2024, classifies AI systems into four risk tiers. Prohibited systems (social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance with narrow exceptions) were banned as of February 2025. High-risk systems (employment, credit, healthcare, criminal justice) must meet strict requirements for data quality, transparency, human oversight, and documentation — these obligations take effect August 2026. General-purpose AI models (including large language models) face transparency and copyright compliance requirements from August 2025. This is the most comprehensive AI regulation in the world and will set the template for other jurisdictions.

Engineering takeaway on compliance: If your model makes decisions about people (hiring, lending, healthcare, criminal justice), you need a fairness audit pipeline that runs continuously — not a one-time check. The EU AI Act requires ongoing monitoring, and US regulatory agencies are increasingly scrutinizing algorithmic decision-making. Build fairness metrics into your CI/CD pipeline alongside accuracy metrics.

Intersectional and Subgroup Fairness

Compounding Discrimination

Intersectional fairness recognizes that discrimination compounds across attributes. A model might be fair for women overall and fair for Black people overall, but still discriminate against Black women specifically. Kimberlé Crenshaw coined the term "intersectionality" to describe exactly this phenomenon in legal discrimination cases. In ML, you need to check fairness not just on individual attributes but on their combinations.

Gerrymandering in Fairness

Kearns et al. (2018) showed that it's possible to "gerrymander" fairness — to satisfy fairness constraints on predefined groups while still discriminating against subgroups that cross group boundaries. Their solution: algorithms that enforce fairness constraints over exponentially many subgroups, not just the ones you thought to check. This connects to the broader lesson: fairness auditing on predefined groups is necessary but not sufficient.

# Intersectional fairness audit
from fairlearn.metrics import MetricFrame
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score, precision_score
import pandas as pd

def intersectional_audit(y_true, y_pred, sensitive_features_dict):
    """Audit fairness across intersectional groups.
    
    sensitive_features_dict: e.g., {'gender': [...], 'race': [...]}
    """
    # Create intersectional groups
    sf_df = pd.DataFrame(sensitive_features_dict)
    intersectional = sf_df.apply(lambda row: ' × '.join(row.astype(str)), axis=1)
    
    metrics = {
        'accuracy': accuracy_score,
        'selection_rate': lambda y, yp: (yp == 1).mean(),
    }
    
    mf = MetricFrame(
        metrics=metrics,
        y_true=y_true,
        y_pred=y_pred,
        sensitive_features=intersectional,
    )
    
    print("=== Intersectional Fairness Audit ===")
    print(mf.by_group.to_string())
    print(f"\nWorst-group accuracy: {mf.group_min()['accuracy']:.3f}")
    print(f"Best-group accuracy:  {mf.group_max()['accuracy']:.3f}")
    print(f"Gap: {mf.difference()['accuracy']:.3f}")
    
    # Flag groups with significantly lower performance
    mean_acc = mf.overall['accuracy']
    flagged = mf.by_group[mf.by_group['accuracy'] < mean_acc - 0.05]
    if len(flagged) > 0:
        print(f"\n⚠️ Groups with accuracy >5% below average:")
        print(flagged.to_string())
The fairness-accuracy tradeoff is real but often smaller than feared. Research consistently shows that the first few percentage points of fairness improvement come with minimal accuracy cost. It's the extreme fairness requirements that create significant tradeoffs. In practice, a well-designed fairness intervention often improves overall model quality by forcing you to address data quality issues you would otherwise have ignored.
