Sustainability

Chapter 18: Responsible AI and Ethics 3 subtopics
TL;DR

Training a single large language model can emit as much CO₂ as five cars over their entire lifetimes. Sustainability in ML means measuring that impact, reducing it through efficient architectures and training strategies, and building a culture where carbon cost is treated as a first-class metric alongside accuracy. Tools like CodeCarbon make tracking easy, and techniques like transfer learning and compute-optimal scaling (Chinchilla) let you get better results with fewer GPU-hours.

Here is a question nobody asked five years ago: what is the carbon footprint of your model? Today it is unavoidable. Training GPT-4 consumed an estimated 50 GWh of electricity — roughly the annual consumption of thousands of homes — and released around 12,000–19,000 tonnes of CO₂. And that is one model, trained once. Multiply by the thousands of organizations fine-tuning, retraining, and serving models every day, and you start to see the scale of the problem.

This section is about understanding where that energy goes, how to measure it, and — most importantly — how to reduce it without sacrificing model quality.

Carbon Footprint of AI

Training Energy Consumption

Training energy consumption is the electricity used during the model training process — forward passes, backward passes, gradient updates, repeated across billions of examples for days or weeks. The cost scales with three things: the number of parameters in the model, the size of the training dataset, and the number of training steps. A model with 100 billion parameters trained on trillions of tokens for weeks on thousands of GPUs will burn through electricity at industrial scale.

To put numbers on this: training GPT-3 (175B parameters) in 2020 consumed roughly 1,287 MWh and emitted about 552 tonnes of CO₂. By the time GPT-4 arrived in 2023, estimates jumped to 50 GWh — roughly a 40x increase. That is not a trend line any planet can sustain indefinitely.

# Back-of-envelope training energy estimation
def estimate_training_energy(
    num_gpus: int,
    gpu_power_watts: int,
    training_hours: int,
    pue: float = 1.1  # Power Usage Effectiveness of data center
) -> dict:
    """Estimate energy and CO2 for a training run."""
    # Total energy in kWh
    energy_kwh = (num_gpus * gpu_power_watts * training_hours) / 1000
    # Apply PUE (overhead: cooling, networking, storage)
    total_energy_kwh = energy_kwh * pue
    # CO2 estimate (US average grid: ~390 gCO2/kWh)
    co2_kg = total_energy_kwh * 0.390
    return {
        "energy_kwh": total_energy_kwh,
        "co2_kg": co2_kg,
        "co2_tonnes": co2_kg / 1000
    }

# Example: GPT-3 scale training
result = estimate_training_energy(
    num_gpus=1024,
    gpu_power_watts=400,  # A100 TDP
    training_hours=720    # ~30 days
)
print(f"Energy: {result['energy_kwh']:,.0f} kWh")
print(f"CO2: {result['co2_tonnes']:.1f} tonnes")
# Energy: 322,560 kWh
# CO2: 125.8 tonnes

Inference Energy at Scale

Inference energy is the electricity consumed every time a deployed model processes a request. A single GPT-4 query uses about 0.5 Wh — roughly 10x a Google search. That sounds tiny. But when you serve hundreds of millions of queries per day, inference often dominates total energy consumption over a model's lifetime. One study estimated that for popular models, inference accounts for 60–90% of total lifecycle energy.

This is why inference optimization matters so much. Quantization (reducing weight precision from 32-bit to 8-bit or 4-bit), distillation (training a smaller model to mimic a larger one), and speculative decoding all reduce per-query energy. A 4-bit quantized model can serve roughly the same quality at a fraction of the energy cost.

# Comparing inference energy at scale
queries_per_day = 100_000_000  # 100M daily queries

# Energy per query (Wh)
models = {
    "GPT-4": 0.50,
    "GPT-4o (optimized)": 0.30,
    "Distilled 7B model": 0.05,
    "Google Search": 0.03,
}

print("Daily energy consumption at 100M queries/day:")
for model, wh_per_query in models.items():
    daily_kwh = (queries_per_day * wh_per_query) / 1000
    daily_mwh = daily_kwh / 1000
    annual_co2_tonnes = (daily_kwh * 365 * 0.390) / 1000
    print(f"  {model:25s}: {daily_mwh:8,.0f} MWh/day, "
          f"~{annual_co2_tonnes:,.0f} tonnes CO2/year")

Carbon Intensity of Compute Regions

Carbon intensity measures how much CO₂ is emitted per kilowatt-hour of electricity in a given region. It varies wildly. Training in Quebec (mostly hydroelectric, ~20 gCO₂/kWh) produces roughly 20x less carbon than training in a coal-heavy region (~800 gCO₂/kWh). The same training run, the same model, the same number of GPU-hours — but fundamentally different environmental impact based purely on where the servers are located.

Cloud providers now publish carbon intensity data for their regions. Choosing a low-carbon region for a multi-week training run is one of the easiest high-impact decisions you can make. Some organizations go further, scheduling training runs for times when the grid is cleanest (overnight when wind generation peaks, for example).

Region Matters More Than You Think

Moving a training run from US East (coal + gas heavy, ~390 gCO₂/kWh) to Canada Central (hydroelectric, ~20 gCO₂/kWh) can reduce carbon emissions by 95% — with zero impact on model quality. Check your cloud provider's sustainability dashboard before launching large training jobs.

Lifecycle Analysis of ML Models

A lifecycle analysis looks at the total environmental cost of an ML model from start to end: hardware manufacturing, data collection and storage, training, fine-tuning, deployment, inference serving, and eventual decommissioning. Most discussions focus only on training energy, but that is incomplete. The embodied carbon in GPU manufacturing is substantial — producing an NVIDIA A100 chip has a carbon footprint of several hundred kilograms of CO₂ before it ever runs a single computation.

A complete picture also includes the water used for cooling data centers (Microsoft reported using 700,000 gallons of water to train GPT-3), the rare earth minerals in hardware, and the e-waste generated when GPUs are retired. Responsible ML teams account for all of these factors.

Efficient Training

Efficient Architectures

Efficient architectures are model designs that achieve strong performance with fewer computations. The most impactful development here is the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) pattern. Instead of activating all parameters for every input, MoE routes each token to a small subset of specialist sub-networks. Mistral's Mixtral 8x7B, for example, has 46.7B total parameters but only activates about 12.9B per token — giving you large-model quality at small-model inference cost.

Other efficiency wins include sparse attention mechanisms (processing only relevant parts of long contexts instead of full quadratic attention), grouped query attention (sharing key-value heads across multiple query heads), and flash attention (an IO-aware exact attention algorithm that reduces memory reads/writes by 5-20x).

# Conceptual MoE routing — only activate 2 of 8 experts per token
import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class SimpleMoELayer(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_dim, expert_dim, num_experts=8, top_k=2):
        super().__init__()
        self.experts = nn.ModuleList([
            nn.Linear(input_dim, expert_dim) for _ in range(num_experts)
        ])
        self.gate = nn.Linear(input_dim, num_experts)
        self.top_k = top_k

    def forward(self, x):
        # Gate decides which experts to use
        gate_scores = torch.softmax(self.gate(x), dim=-1)
        top_k_scores, top_k_indices = gate_scores.topk(self.top_k, dim=-1)

        # Only compute outputs for selected experts (huge savings)
        output = torch.zeros(x.shape[0], self.experts[0].out_features)
        for i in range(self.top_k):
            expert_idx = top_k_indices[:, i]
            for j, expert in enumerate(self.experts):
                mask = (expert_idx == j)
                if mask.any():
                    output[mask] += top_k_scores[mask, i:i+1] * expert(x[mask])
        return output

Training Efficiency Techniques

Beyond architecture, several training techniques reduce compute requirements. Mixed precision training uses 16-bit (or even 8-bit) floating point for most operations while keeping critical accumulations in 32-bit. This roughly doubles throughput on modern GPUs with negligible accuracy loss. Gradient accumulation lets you simulate larger batch sizes without requiring proportionally more GPU memory. Gradient checkpointing trades compute for memory by recomputing activations during the backward pass instead of storing them all.

Together, these techniques can reduce training time (and therefore energy) by 40-60% compared to naive implementations, with minimal impact on final model quality.

Early Stopping Strategies

Early stopping monitors a validation metric during training and halts when improvement plateaus. The idea is that the model has learned the useful patterns and further training only overfits to noise — wasting energy and degrading generalization. A common approach: stop if validation loss does not improve for N consecutive evaluations (patience). More sophisticated approaches track the rate of improvement and stop when the marginal gain per compute unit drops below a threshold.

Transfer Learning to Reduce Training

Transfer learning is the single biggest efficiency win in modern ML. Instead of training a model from scratch on your specific task, you start with a pre-trained foundation model and fine-tune it on your data. Fine-tuning a 7B parameter model on a domain-specific dataset might take 4 GPU-hours. Training it from scratch would take thousands. That is a 99%+ reduction in compute.

Parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods make this even cheaper. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) freezes the pre-trained weights and adds tiny trainable matrices (often less than 1% of total parameters). QLoRA combines this with 4-bit quantization of the base model. You get fine-tuned performance while training only a few million parameters instead of billions.

# LoRA fine-tuning: train <1% of parameters
from peft import LoraConfig, get_peft_model
from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM

model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained("meta-llama/Llama-2-7b-hf")

lora_config = LoraConfig(
    r=16,                # Rank of low-rank matrices
    lora_alpha=32,       # Scaling factor
    target_modules=["q_proj", "v_proj"],  # Which layers to adapt
    lora_dropout=0.05,
)

peft_model = get_peft_model(model, lora_config)
peft_model.print_trainable_parameters()
# trainable params: 4,194,304 || all params: 6,742,609,920 || trainable%: 0.062%
# That is 0.06% of parameters — enormous energy savings

Green AI Movement

The Green AI movement, sparked by Schwartz et al.'s 2019 paper, argues that the ML community has been too focused on accuracy (Red AI) and not enough on efficiency (Green AI). The core proposal: every paper should report computational cost alongside accuracy. If Model A gets 0.1% better accuracy but costs 10x more compute, is that really progress?

The movement has gained serious traction. Major conferences now encourage or require efficiency reporting. Companies like Hugging Face publish energy consumption estimates for their models. The cultural shift is real — efficiency is no longer an afterthought.

Sustainable ML Practices

Carbon Tracking Tools (CodeCarbon, ML CO2 Impact)

CodeCarbon is an open-source Python library that tracks the energy consumption and carbon emissions of your code in real time. You add two lines to your training script and get a detailed emissions report. It works by monitoring CPU/GPU power draw and multiplying by the carbon intensity of your electricity grid (auto-detected from your IP address or manually configured).

# Track carbon emissions during training with CodeCarbon
from codecarbon import EmissionsTracker

tracker = EmissionsTracker(
    project_name="sentiment-classifier-v2",
    measure_power_secs=30,       # Sample power every 30 seconds
    tracking_mode="process",      # Track this process only
    log_level="warning",
    output_dir="./carbon_logs"
)

tracker.start()

# --- Your training loop ---
for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for batch in train_loader:
        loss = model(batch)
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()
# --- End training loop ---

emissions = tracker.stop()
print(f"Total emissions: {emissions:.4f} kg CO2eq")
print(f"Total energy: {tracker.final_emissions_data.energy_consumed:.4f} kWh")
# Output example:
# Total emissions: 0.2847 kg CO2eq
# Total energy: 0.7300 kWh

ML CO2 Impact is a web calculator where you enter your hardware, training duration, and cloud region to estimate emissions. EcoLogits (by the CodeCarbon team) extends tracking to inference, estimating the carbon cost of API calls to LLM providers. These tools turn an invisible cost into a visible metric — and you cannot optimize what you do not measure.

Model Reuse and Sharing

Model reuse is the most effective sustainability strategy in ML. Platforms like Hugging Face Hub host over 500,000 pre-trained models. Before training anything from scratch, search for an existing model that is close to what you need. Fine-tune it. Adapt it. Share your adapted version back. This creates a virtuous cycle where the enormous one-time cost of pre-training is amortized across thousands of downstream users.

The environmental math is compelling. If 1,000 teams each train a similar model from scratch, you burn 1,000x the energy. If they all fine-tune the same base model, you burn the pre-training cost once plus 1,000 tiny fine-tuning costs. The total savings can be 99%+.

Compute-Optimal Training (Chinchilla Scaling)

DeepMind's Chinchilla scaling laws (2022) showed that most large models were dramatically over-parameterized and under-trained. The prevailing wisdom was "bigger model = better." Chinchilla demonstrated that for a fixed compute budget, you should balance model size and training data roughly equally: a 70B model trained on 1.4 trillion tokens outperformed a 280B model trained on 300 billion tokens — using the same compute budget.

The implication for sustainability is direct. If you allocate your compute budget according to Chinchilla scaling, you get a better model and waste less energy. Over-parameterized models that would have consumed 4x the energy for inferior performance are bad engineering — and now we have the math to prove it.

Compute-Optimal ≠ Inference-Optimal

Chinchilla scaling optimizes for training compute. But a smaller model trained on more data might still be larger than necessary for inference. For deployment, consider further compression (quantization, distillation, pruning) to reduce serving costs. Training and serving have different cost profiles — optimize both.

Reporting Energy and Carbon Metrics

The final piece is transparency. A model card should report not only accuracy, latency, and fairness metrics but also: total training energy (kWh), estimated CO₂ emissions (kg CO₂eq), hardware used (GPU type and count), training duration, and cloud region. This information lets downstream users make informed decisions about whether to use your model, retrain it, or find a more efficient alternative.

Several major ML conferences now encourage carbon reporting. The trend is toward making energy efficiency a standard evaluation dimension — not a nice-to-have, but a required disclosure. As regulatory frameworks like the EU AI Act mature, mandatory environmental reporting for AI systems is likely on the horizon.

The Sustainability Checklist

Before launching a training run: (1) Check if a suitable pre-trained model exists. (2) Use parameter-efficient fine-tuning if possible. (3) Choose a low-carbon compute region. (4) Enable mixed precision training. (5) Track emissions with CodeCarbon. (6) Report energy and carbon in your model card. Each step is small. Together they can cut your carbon footprint by an order of magnitude.
