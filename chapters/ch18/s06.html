Governance & Documentation

Chapter 18: Responsible AI and Ethics EU AI Act · NIST RMF · Model cards · Audit trails
TL;DR

The EU AI Act is the new GDPR for AI — extraterritorial, penalty-heavy, and it will shape global compliance whether you're in Europe or not. Model cards and datasheets are the documentation standards that make your models auditable. Build compliance into your ML pipeline from day one, not as a retrofit. The organizations that treat governance as engineering infrastructure rather than legal paperwork will ship faster, not slower.

EU AI Act — What You Actually Need to Do

The EU AI Act entered into force in August 2024. It's the first comprehensive AI regulation, and like GDPR, it applies to anyone whose systems affect EU residents — regardless of where the company is headquartered. The compliance timeline is staggered, with the earliest obligations (banned practices) enforceable from February 2025.

Risk Classification

The Act classifies AI systems into four risk tiers. Your obligations depend entirely on which tier your system falls into.

Risk Level Examples Requirements Deadline
Unacceptable Social scoring by governments, real-time remote biometric ID in public spaces (with exceptions), manipulative AI targeting vulnerabilities Banned. Full stop. Feb 2025
High Credit scoring, hiring/recruitment tools, medical devices, critical infrastructure, law enforcement, education assessment Risk management system, data governance, technical documentation, human oversight, accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity requirements, conformity assessment Aug 2026
Limited Chatbots, emotion recognition, deepfake generators Transparency obligations — users must be told they're interacting with AI or viewing AI-generated content Aug 2026
Minimal Spam filters, AI in video games, inventory management No specific obligations (voluntary codes of conduct encouraged) N/A

If you're building a hiring tool, a medical AI, or a credit scoring model — you're in the "High" tier. That means conformity assessments, mandatory documentation, and ongoing monitoring. Budget for it.

from dataclasses import dataclass
from enum import Enum
from typing import List

class RiskLevel(Enum):
    UNACCEPTABLE = "unacceptable"  # Banned
    HIGH = "high"                  # Heavy regulation
    LIMITED = "limited"            # Transparency required
    MINIMAL = "minimal"            # No specific obligations

@dataclass
class RiskAssessment:
    level: RiskLevel
    obligations: List[str]
    deadline: str

# Simplified classification logic
HIGH_RISK_DOMAINS = {
    "biometric_identification",
    "critical_infrastructure",
    "education_assessment",
    "employment_recruitment",
    "credit_scoring",
    "law_enforcement",
    "immigration_asylum",
    "judicial_decisions",
    "medical_device",
}

BANNED_USES = {
    "social_scoring_government",
    "realtime_biometric_public",    # With narrow exceptions
    "manipulative_subliminal",
    "vulnerability_exploitation",
    "predictive_policing_profiling", # Based solely on profiling
    "emotion_recognition_workplace", # In workplace/education
    "untargeted_facial_scraping",
}

def classify_ai_risk(use_case: str, domain: str) -> RiskAssessment:
    if use_case in BANNED_USES:
        return RiskAssessment(
            level=RiskLevel.UNACCEPTABLE,
            obligations=["PROHIBITED — do not deploy"],
            deadline="Feb 2025"
        )

    if domain in HIGH_RISK_DOMAINS:
        return RiskAssessment(
            level=RiskLevel.HIGH,
            obligations=[
                "Risk management system (Art. 9)",
                "Data governance and quality (Art. 10)",
                "Technical documentation (Art. 11)",
                "Record-keeping / logging (Art. 12)",
                "Transparency to deployers (Art. 13)",
                "Human oversight design (Art. 14)",
                "Accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity (Art. 15)",
                "Conformity assessment before deployment",
                "Post-market monitoring",
            ],
            deadline="Aug 2026"
        )

    # Chatbots, deepfakes, emotion recognition → limited
    if use_case in {"chatbot", "deepfake_generation", "emotion_recognition"}:
        return RiskAssessment(
            level=RiskLevel.LIMITED,
            obligations=["Transparency: disclose AI interaction to users"],
            deadline="Aug 2026"
        )

    return RiskAssessment(
        level=RiskLevel.MINIMAL,
        obligations=["No mandatory requirements (voluntary codes of conduct)"],
        deadline="N/A"
    )

General-Purpose AI (GPAI) Obligations

Foundation models and general-purpose AI get their own rules. All GPAI providers must provide technical documentation and comply with copyright law. Models trained with compute exceeding 1025 FLOPs (think GPT-4 scale) are classified as having systemic risk — which triggers additional requirements: red-teaming, incident reporting, cybersecurity measures, and energy consumption reporting.

Penalties

These are the numbers that get executive attention:

  • Banned AI practices: Up to €35M or 7% of global annual turnover
  • High-risk non-compliance: Up to €15M or 3% of global turnover
  • Incorrect information to authorities: Up to €7.5M or 1% of global turnover

For SMEs and startups, fines are capped at the lower of the absolute amount or the percentage. Still painful.

The Brussels Effect

Like GDPR, the EU AI Act will become the de facto global standard. Companies won't maintain two versions of their AI systems — one for Europe and one for everywhere else. They'll build to the strictest standard and ship globally. If you're a US or Asian company selling to customers who might be in the EU, this is your regulation too.

NIST AI Risk Management Framework

The US approach is voluntary but increasingly referenced in procurement requirements and legal proceedings as evidence of due diligence. Four functions:

  • Govern: Establish policies, roles, and accountability structures for AI risk
  • Map: Identify and categorize AI risks in your specific context — who's affected, what can go wrong
  • Measure: Quantify identified risks using metrics, testing, and ongoing monitoring
  • Manage: Prioritize and act on risks — mitigate, transfer, accept, or avoid

It's not legally binding, but "we followed NIST AI RMF" is a much stronger defense in court than "we tried our best."

Industry-Specific Regulation

Sector Key Regulation AI-Relevant Requirements
Finance SR 11-7 (US), SS1/23 (UK) Model validation, ongoing monitoring, independent review
Healthcare FDA SaMD, EU MDR Clinical validation, post-market surveillance, change management
Hiring NYC Local Law 144, EU AI Act Annual bias audits, candidate notification, public audit results
Autonomous vehicles UNECE WP.29, sector-specific national laws Safety validation, data recording, incident reporting
Insurance Colorado SB 21-169 Bias testing for life insurance algorithms

If you're in a regulated industry, the AI Act stacks on top of your existing sector rules — it doesn't replace them.

Model Cards & Datasheets

A model without documentation is a liability. Model cards (Mitchell et al., 2019) and datasheets for datasets (Gebru et al., 2021) are the standard formats for documenting what your model is, what it's trained on, and where it should (and shouldn't) be used.

Model Card Template

from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import Dict, List, Optional
from datetime import date

@dataclass
class ModelCard:
    """Structured model documentation — your accountability chain."""

    # Identity
    model_name: str = ""
    version: str = ""
    date_released: str = ""
    model_type: str = ""       # e.g., "fine-tuned BERT classifier"
    owner: str = ""

    # Intended use
    primary_use_cases: List[str] = field(default_factory=list)
    out_of_scope_uses: List[str] = field(default_factory=list)
    target_users: List[str] = field(default_factory=list)

    # Training data
    training_data_description: str = ""
    training_data_size: str = ""
    preprocessing_steps: List[str] = field(default_factory=list)
    known_data_gaps: List[str] = field(default_factory=list)

    # Performance
    metrics: Dict[str, float] = field(default_factory=dict)
    # Key: disaggregated performance by demographic group
    disaggregated_metrics: Dict[str, Dict[str, float]] = field(
        default_factory=dict
    )

    # Limitations and risks
    known_limitations: List[str] = field(default_factory=list)
    ethical_considerations: List[str] = field(default_factory=list)
    failure_modes: List[str] = field(default_factory=list)

    # Governance
    approval_authority: str = ""
    review_date: str = ""
    retirement_criteria: List[str] = field(default_factory=list)


# Example usage
card = ModelCard(
    model_name="loan-approval-v2.3",
    version="2.3.0",
    date_released="2024-11-15",
    model_type="XGBoost binary classifier",
    owner="Credit Risk Team",
    primary_use_cases=[
        "Pre-screening personal loan applications under $50K"
    ],
    out_of_scope_uses=[
        "Mortgage decisions",
        "Applicants under 18",
        "Business loans",
    ],
    metrics={"auroc": 0.89, "precision_at_10pct_fpr": 0.74},
    disaggregated_metrics={
        "gender_male":   {"auroc": 0.90, "fpr": 0.08},
        "gender_female": {"auroc": 0.88, "fpr": 0.09},
        "age_18_30":     {"auroc": 0.84, "fpr": 0.12},
        "age_31_50":     {"auroc": 0.91, "fpr": 0.07},
        "age_51_plus":   {"auroc": 0.87, "fpr": 0.10},
    },
    known_limitations=[
        "Lower accuracy for applicants with thin credit files (<2 years history)",
        "Not validated for self-employment income verification",
    ],
    failure_modes=[
        "Systematic under-scoring of applicants with non-traditional income",
    ],
    retirement_criteria=[
        "AUROC drops below 0.82 on monthly validation",
        "Fairness gap exceeds 5 percentage points on any protected group",
    ],
)

Datasheets for datasets follow the same idea: document the motivation, composition, collection process, preprocessing, intended uses, distribution, and maintenance plan for your dataset. If your training data isn't documented, your model card has a hole in it.

Together, model cards and datasheets form your accountability chain. When a regulator asks "how did you arrive at this decision?", the answer starts with these documents.

Audit Trails

Every prediction your high-risk model makes should be reconstructible. What to log:

  • Input data (or a hash if PII is involved) and the model version that produced the output
  • Raw prediction scores — not just the final decision, the full probability distribution
  • Applied business rules — any post-processing, overrides, or threshold adjustments
  • Human review decisions — who reviewed it, when, what they decided, and why
  • Feature values at prediction time (for drift detection and debugging)

EU AI Act Article 12 requires high-risk systems to maintain logs for the lifetime of the system. Financial regulators (SR 11-7) typically require 5–7 years of retention. Design your logging infrastructure for the strictest requirement you face.

Model Risk Management

Three pillars, borrowed from financial model risk management (SR 11-7) and applicable everywhere:

  • Independent validation: Someone who didn't build the model reviews it — checks methodology, tests on held-out data, probes for bias. "Independent" means organizationally separate, not just a different team member.
  • Ongoing monitoring: Performance metrics, fairness metrics, and input drift tracked continuously in production. Automated alerts when metrics cross thresholds. Monthly or quarterly reviews depending on risk level.
  • Retirement procedures: Every model needs a defined end-of-life. What triggers retirement? What's the fallback? How do you migrate downstream systems? Models don't die gracefully on their own — they degrade silently.
Quick Check
  1. Your company is building a resume screening tool for EU customers. What risk tier does it fall under, and name three specific compliance requirements.
  2. A model card shows overall AUROC of 0.91 but doesn't include disaggregated metrics. Why is this insufficient for compliance?
  3. What's the maximum EU AI Act penalty for deploying a banned AI system, and why does the "Brussels Effect" matter for non-EU companies?
  4. Your audit log captures predictions and final decisions but not feature values at prediction time. What capability does this gap eliminate?
