Text Pipeline: From Raw Text to Features

Chapter 11: Natural Language Processing Classical pipeline · BoW · TF-IDF · Preprocessing decisions
TL;DR

Before any model can learn from text, you need a pipeline: tokenize, normalize, optionally remove stop words and stem/lemmatize, then convert to numbers. Bag of Words counts word occurrences. TF-IDF refines that by down-weighting common words and boosting rare ones. Both ignore word order and semantics — but they're fast, interpretable, and still power many production systems. Understanding this pipeline is what makes the jump to embeddings and transformers make sense.

Tokenization

Why We Need It

Computers don't understand words. They understand numbers. So the very first thing you do with any text is break it into smaller pieces — tokens — that you can map to numbers. A token might be a word, a subword, a character, or even a sentence. The choice matters enormously because it defines the atoms your model will reason over. Get tokenization wrong and everything downstream suffers: your vocabulary explodes, your embeddings become meaningless, and your model wastes capacity learning things it shouldn't have to.

Whitespace Tokenization

The simplest approach is splitting on spaces. In Python, that's just "Hello world".split(). It's fast, it's obvious, and it works surprisingly well for clean English text. But it falls apart quickly. Consider the string "Don't stop — it's $3.50!". Whitespace splitting gives you ["Don't", "stop", "—", "it's", "$3.50!"]. The punctuation is glued to the words. "Don't" stays as one token when you probably want "Do" and "n't" (or "not"). The dollar amount and exclamation mark are fused. For a toy project this is fine. For anything serious, you need more.

Regex-Based Tokenization

A step up is using regular expressions to define what a token looks like. NLTK's regexp_tokenize lets you specify a pattern and extract all matches. For example, the pattern \w+ grabs sequences of alphanumeric characters, effectively stripping punctuation. You can get fancier — \w+(?:'\w+)? keeps contractions together. The advantage is full control. The disadvantage is that you're writing rules by hand, and natural language has more edge cases than you'll ever anticipate. Abbreviations like "U.S.A.", URLs, email addresses, emoticons — each one needs its own regex. This path leads to madness for production systems.

Proper NLP Tokenizers

Libraries like spaCy and NLTK ship with tokenizers that handle the edge cases for you. NLTK's word_tokenize uses a combination of regex rules and the Penn Treebank tokenization standard. It knows that "don't" should become ["do", "n't"] and that "Mr." doesn't end a sentence. spaCy's tokenizer goes further — it uses a rule-based system with exception lists and prefix/suffix/infix rules that handle most English text correctly out of the box.

Here's a practical comparison:

import spacy
from nltk.tokenize import word_tokenize, regexp_tokenize

text = "Dr. Smith didn't pay $3.50 for the U.S.A. trip — he couldn't!"

# Whitespace (naive)
print("Whitespace:", text.split())
# ['Dr.', 'Smith', "didn't", 'pay', '$3.50', 'for', 'the',
#  'U.S.A.', 'trip', '—', 'he', "couldn't!"]

# Regex (alphanumeric only)
print("Regex:", regexp_tokenize(text, r"\w+(?:'\w+)?"))
# ['Dr', 'Smith', "didn't", 'pay', '3', '50', 'for', 'the',
#  'U', 'S', 'A', 'trip', 'he', "couldn't"]

# NLTK word_tokenize
print("NLTK:", word_tokenize(text))
# ['Dr.', 'Smith', 'did', "n't", 'pay', '$', '3.50', 'for',
#  'the', 'U.S.A.', 'trip', '—', 'he', 'could', "n't", '!']

# spaCy
nlp = spacy.load("en_core_web_sm")
doc = nlp(text)
print("spaCy:", [token.text for token in doc])
# ['Dr.', 'Smith', 'did', "n't", 'pay', '$', '3.50', 'for',
#  'the', 'U.S.A.', 'trip', '—', 'he', 'could', "n't", '!']

Notice how the naive approaches mangle contractions and currency, while spaCy and NLTK handle them gracefully. The lesson: don't reinvent the tokenizer unless you have a very good reason.

Sentence Tokenization

Sentence tokenization (also called sentence segmentation) is harder than it looks. You might think you can just split on periods. But "Dr. Smith went to Washington. He arrived at 3 p.m." has three periods that don't end sentences and two that do. Abbreviations, decimal numbers, ellipses, URLs — all contain periods that aren't sentence boundaries. NLTK's sent_tokenize uses the Punkt algorithm, which is a trained unsupervised model that learns abbreviations and sentence starters from data. spaCy handles sentence boundaries during its full parsing pipeline, using dependency parse information to determine where sentences end.

Language-Specific Challenges

Everything above assumes English. Other languages make tokenization even harder. Chinese and Japanese don't use spaces between words at all — the string "我喜欢机器学习" (I like machine learning) is a continuous sequence of characters, and deciding where one word ends and another begins requires a statistical model or dictionary lookup. German creates compound words by smashing nouns together: "Donaudampfschifffahrtsgesellschaft" is one word meaning "Danube steamship company." Turkish is agglutinative — a single word can contain what English expresses as an entire phrase. The takeaway: always use a tokenizer designed for your target language. Universal solutions are rare.

Text Normalization

Lowercasing

Converting all text to lowercase is the most common normalization step. It collapses "The", "the", and "THE" into one token, shrinking your vocabulary and reducing sparsity. For most tasks — topic classification, search, clustering — this is a clear win. But it's not free. Lowercasing destroys information. "US" (the country) becomes "us" (the pronoun). "Apple" (the company) becomes "apple" (the fruit). Named entity recognition, coreference resolution, and any task that cares about proper nouns will suffer. The rule of thumb: lowercase for bag-of-words models and search; keep case for sequence models and anything involving names or acronyms.

Punctuation Removal and Number Handling

Stripping punctuation is another common step, and another one with hidden costs. For sentiment analysis, "great!!!" carries more weight than "great" — those exclamation marks are signal, not noise. For topic modeling, they're probably noise. Numbers are similar. You can remove them, replace them all with a placeholder like <NUM>, or keep them. If you're classifying financial documents, the number "10,000" matters. If you're classifying movie reviews, it probably doesn't. There's no universal right answer. Think about what information your downstream task actually needs.

Unicode Normalization

Unicode normalization is one of those things you don't think about until it bites you. The character "é" can be represented two ways in Unicode: as a single code point (U+00E9, the precomposed form) or as two code points (U+0065 "e" + U+0301 combining accent). They look identical on screen but are different byte sequences. String comparison fails silently. Python's unicodedata.normalize("NFC", text) converts to the precomposed form (NFC — Canonical Decomposition followed by Canonical Composition), and "NFD" does the opposite (NFD — Canonical Decomposition). Always normalize to NFC before doing anything else with your text. It takes milliseconds and prevents bizarre bugs.

Contraction Expansion

Expanding contractions — turning "don't" into "do not", "I'm" into "I am", "they've" into "they have" — is a normalization step that helps some pipelines. It reduces vocabulary size and makes downstream analysis cleaner. But it's also lossy: "don't" has a slightly different pragmatic tone than "do not" in real speech. Most people implement this with a simple dictionary lookup. Libraries like contractions in Python handle common cases. For most NLP pipelines, expansion is a sensible default unless you're doing stylistic or sociolinguistic analysis.

Stop Words

Stop words are the most common words in a language — "the", "is", "at", "which", "on" — that carry little semantic meaning on their own. Both NLTK and spaCy ship with standard stop word lists. NLTK's English list has about 179 words; spaCy's has around 326. The idea is that removing them reduces noise and dimensionality. If you're building a TF-IDF search engine or a topic model, stop word removal usually helps because these words dominate frequency counts without adding topical information.

But here's where people get into trouble: stop word removal is not always safe. In sentiment analysis, "not" is on most stop word lists, yet "not good" means the opposite of "good." Removing "not" flips the sentiment. Phrases like "to be or not to be" become meaningless. For deep learning models that learn their own representations, stop word removal is often unnecessary and sometimes harmful — the model can learn to ignore irrelevant words on its own, and you're just throwing away context it might use.

Domain-specific stop words are also worth considering. In a corpus of legal documents, "court", "plaintiff", and "defendant" might appear in nearly every document. They're not on any standard stop word list, but they carry no discriminating power within that domain. You can identify them by looking at document frequency — words that appear in more than 90% of documents are usually good candidates. But be careful: removing them is irreversible, and you might want them later for a different task.

When NOT to Remove Stop Words

If your task depends on word order, negation, or phrase-level meaning, keep stop words. Modern transformer models (BERT, GPT) were trained with stop words present, and removing them before feeding text to these models will degrade performance. Stop word removal is most useful for classical bag-of-words approaches: TF-IDF, topic models, keyword extraction. When in doubt, keep them and let the model decide what matters.

Stemming

Stemming is the process of reducing a word to its root form by chopping off suffixes (and sometimes prefixes). It's a crude, rule-based operation — no dictionary lookup, no understanding of grammar. The goal is to collapse related word forms: "running", "runs", and "ran" should all map to something like "run" so that your model treats them as the same concept.

Porter Stemmer and Snowball Stemmer

The most famous stemmer is the Porter Stemmer, designed by Martin Porter in 1980. It applies a series of rules in phases: strip plurals, strip past tenses, strip derivational suffixes. NLTK makes it one import away. The Snowball Stemmer (also called Porter2) is a refined version that fixes some of the original's quirks and supports multiple languages.

Stemming is fast — it's just string manipulation. That speed makes it attractive for search engines and information retrieval where you're processing millions of documents. But the tradeoff is accuracy.

Over-Stemming and Under-Stemming

Over-stemming happens when the stemmer collapses words that shouldn't be grouped. "University" becomes "univers", and so does "universal" — but a university and something universal are very different things. "Operating" and "operation" both stem to "oper", but an operating system and a surgical operation share little meaning. Under-stemming is the opposite: failing to collapse words that should be grouped. "Alumnus" and "alumni" won't get stemmed to the same root because the Latin plural doesn't follow English suffix rules. Stemmers work best for regular English morphology and struggle with irregular forms and borrowed words.

Lemmatization

Lemmatization is the smarter sibling of stemming. Instead of blindly chopping suffixes, it uses a dictionary (or morphological analysis) to return the actual base form of a word — its lemma. "Better" lemmatizes to "good". "Ran" lemmatizes to "run". "Mice" lemmatizes to "mouse". A stemmer would never get these right because they require knowing the word, not just the suffix pattern.

Dictionary-Based vs Morphological Analysis

The simplest lemmatizers just do a dictionary lookup: they have a table mapping word forms to lemmas. WordNet-based lemmatization in NLTK works this way. More sophisticated approaches (like what spaCy uses) perform morphological analysis — understanding that "unhappiness" is "un-" + "happy" + "-ness" and the lemma is "happy". The dictionary approach is fast but brittle (if the word isn't in the dictionary, it returns it unchanged). Morphological analysis is more robust but slower.

POS-Aware Lemmatization

POS-aware lemmatization uses the word's part of speech to disambiguate. The word "saw" can be a noun (a cutting tool) or a verb (past tense of "see"). Without POS information, the lemmatizer has to guess. With it, verb-"saw" correctly becomes "see" and noun-"saw" stays "saw". NLTK's WordNet lemmatizer accepts a POS tag argument; spaCy does this automatically because its pipeline runs POS tagging before lemmatization.

Here's a direct comparison of stemming and lemmatization:

from nltk.stem import PorterStemmer, SnowballStemmer
from nltk.stem import WordNetLemmatizer
from nltk import pos_tag, word_tokenize
import nltk
nltk.download('averaged_perceptron_tagger_eng', quiet=True)
nltk.download('wordnet', quiet=True)
nltk.download('punkt_tab', quiet=True)

porter = PorterStemmer()
snowball = SnowballStemmer("english")
lemmatizer = WordNetLemmatizer()

words = ["running", "better", "universities", "mice", "saw",
         "happily", "studies", "geese", "operating", "flies"]

print(f"{'Word':<15} {'Porter':<15} {'Snowball':<15} {'Lemma (n)':<15} {'Lemma (v)':<15}")
print("-" * 75)
for word in words:
    print(f"{word:<15} "
          f"{porter.stem(word):<15} "
          f"{snowball.stem(word):<15} "
          f"{lemmatizer.lemmatize(word, pos='n'):<15} "   # as noun
          f"{lemmatizer.lemmatize(word, pos='v'):<15}")    # as verb

# Output:
# Word            Porter          Snowball        Lemma (n)       Lemma (v)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# running         run             run             running         run
# better          better          better          better          better
# universities    univers         univers         university      university
# mice            mice            mice            mouse           mice
# saw             saw             saw             saw             see
# happily         happili         happili         happily         happily
# studies         studi           studi           study           study
# geese           gees            gees            goose           geese
# operating       oper            oper            operating       operate
# flies           fli             fli             fly             fly

The table makes the tradeoffs obvious. Stemming is aggressive and sometimes wrong ("univers", "happili", "fli" are not real words). Lemmatization produces actual words but needs POS context to get the right one — look at how "saw" and "mice" change depending on whether you treat them as nouns or verbs.

Stemming vs Lemmatization — When to Use Which

For search engines and information retrieval, stemming is often good enough. Speed matters when you're indexing millions of documents, and the rough collapsing is acceptable. For text classification and sentiment analysis, lemmatization usually performs better because it preserves more meaning. For modern deep learning pipelines (BERT, GPT), you typically need neither — these models use subword tokenizers (BPE, WordPiece) that handle morphological variation implicitly. The model learns from context that "running" and "run" are related without you having to tell it.

Stemming vs Lemmatization at a Glance

Stemming: fast, rule-based, no dictionary needed, produces non-words, language-specific rules, best for search and IR. Lemmatization: slower, dictionary or model-based, produces real words, benefits from POS tags, best for tasks needing semantic precision. Neither: modern transformer models use subword tokenization and don't need either step — applying stemming or lemmatization before BERT will likely hurt performance.

From Clean Text to Numbers

You've tokenized, normalized, and cleaned your text. Now what? A machine learning model needs numbers — vectors it can multiply, distances it can compute. The simplest family of techniques for this conversion has been around for decades and still works remarkably well.

The Problem: Text Is Not Numbers

A machine learning model is, at its core, a function that takes numbers in and pushes numbers out. Matrix multiplications, dot products, gradient updates — all of it requires numerical input. But text is a sequence of characters grouped into words grouped into sentences. There's no obvious way to multiply "the cat sat on the mat" by a weight matrix.

So we need a translation step. We need to convert a document — whether it's a tweet, a legal contract, or a product review — into a fixed-length vector of numbers that a model can chew on. The simplest family of techniques for doing this is called classical text representations, and the two workhorses are Bag of Words and TF-IDF. They've been around for decades, they're dead simple, and they still power a surprising number of production systems.

Let's build the intuition from scratch.

Bag of Words

Vocabulary Construction

The first step is deciding which words we care about. You collect every unique word across all your documents. This set is your vocabulary. If your dataset contains 50,000 unique words, your vocabulary has 50,000 entries. Each entry gets a fixed position — an index. "apple" might be index 0, "banana" index 1, "cat" index 2, all the way up to "zebra" at index 49,999.

This vocabulary becomes the backbone of your representation. Every document, regardless of its actual length, will be turned into a vector of length 50,000 — one slot per vocabulary word.

Count Vectorization

The Bag of Words model is almost comically simple. For each document, you count how many times each vocabulary word appears. That's it. You literally ignore word order, grammar, sentence structure, punctuation — everything except raw word counts.

Let's walk through a concrete example. Suppose we have three tiny documents:

Doc 1: "the cat sat on the mat"
Doc 2: "the dog sat on the log"
Doc 3: "the cat chased the dog"

First, we build the vocabulary from all three documents: {the, cat, sat, on, mat, dog, log, chased}. That's 8 unique words, so each document becomes a vector of length 8.

Now we count. Doc 1 has "the" twice, "cat" once, "sat" once, "on" once, "mat" once, and zero occurrences of "dog", "log", and "chased". So Doc 1's vector is [2, 1, 1, 1, 1, 0, 0, 0]. Doc 2 becomes [2, 0, 1, 1, 0, 1, 1, 0]. Doc 3 becomes [2, 1, 0, 0, 0, 1, 0, 1].

Notice what just happened. We turned variable-length text into fixed-length numerical vectors. A model can now compute distances between documents, feed them into logistic regression, run k-means clustering — whatever it wants. The representation is crude, but it works.

Binary Bag of Words

Sometimes you don't care about counts — just presence. The binary BoW variant replaces every nonzero count with 1. Doc 1 becomes [1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 0, 0, 0]. This can actually work better for short documents where word frequency isn't very informative. If a word appears once or three times in a tweet, the fact that it appears at all is the signal.

N-grams: Capturing Word Pairs

Plain BoW has a glaring weakness: it loses all word order. The sentences "the dog bit the man" and "the man bit the dog" produce identical BoW vectors. That's a problem.

N-grams offer a partial fix. Instead of treating individual words (unigrams) as your vocabulary units, you also include consecutive word pairs (bigrams) or triples (trigrams). With bigrams, your vocabulary grows to include entries like "not good", "very happy", "machine learning". Now "not good" is a distinct vocabulary item from just "good", so the model can learn that they carry opposite sentiment.

The tradeoff is vocabulary size. If you have 50,000 unigrams, adding bigrams might push you to 500,000 entries. Trigrams push you into the millions. Each document vector gets correspondingly longer and sparser.

The Sparsity Problem

Here's the uncomfortable truth about BoW. If your vocabulary has 50,000 words and a typical document contains 200 words, then roughly 49,800 entries in that document's vector are zero. That's 99.6% zeros. This is what we call a sparse representation.

Sparsity isn't automatically bad — sparse matrix libraries handle it efficiently — but it creates real issues. Models need more data to learn meaningful patterns because the signal is spread thinly across a huge space. Distance metrics start misbehaving in very high dimensions (the curse of dimensionality). And storage scales with vocabulary size, not document length. These limitations motivate everything that comes later: TF-IDF, dimensionality reduction, and eventually dense embeddings.

Code: Bag of Words with sklearn

from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import CountVectorizer

docs = [
    "the cat sat on the mat",
    "the dog sat on the log",
    "the cat chased the dog",
]

# Unigram BoW
vectorizer = CountVectorizer()
X = vectorizer.fit_transform(docs)

print("Vocabulary:", vectorizer.get_feature_names_out())
# ['cat', 'chased', 'dog', 'log', 'mat', 'on', 'sat', 'the']

print("BoW matrix:\n", X.toarray())
# [[1 0 0 0 1 1 1 2]
#  [0 0 1 1 0 1 1 2]
#  [1 1 1 0 0 0 0 2]]

# Bigram extension — capture word pairs
bigram_vec = CountVectorizer(ngram_range=(1, 2))
X_bigram = bigram_vec.fit_transform(docs)
print(f"\nUnigrams: {X.shape[1]} features")
print(f"Uni+Bigrams: {X_bigram.shape[1]} features")

# Binary BoW
binary_vec = CountVectorizer(binary=True)
X_binary = binary_vec.fit_transform(docs)
print("\nBinary BoW:\n", X_binary.toarray())

Notice that CountVectorizer returns a sparse matrix by default. You call .toarray() only for display. In production with large vocabularies, you keep it sparse to save memory.

TF-IDF

The Intuition

BoW treats every word equally. "The" and "quantum" get the same treatment — both are just counts. But obviously "quantum" carries far more information than "the." If a word appears in nearly every document, it's not useful for distinguishing one document from another. If a word appears in only a handful of documents, it's a strong signal.

TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency) formalizes this intuition. It takes the raw count from BoW and multiplies it by a factor that penalizes common words and rewards rare ones.

Term Frequency

The term frequency TF(t, d) measures how often term t appears in document d. In its simplest form, it's just the raw count — exactly what BoW gives us. Some variants normalize by document length (divide by total words in d) to prevent long documents from dominating, but the raw count is the most common starting point.

Inverse Document Frequency

The inverse document frequency IDF(t) measures how rare a term is across the entire corpus. The formula is:

IDF(t) = log(N / df(t))

where N is the total number of documents and df(t) is the number of documents that contain term t. If a word appears in every document, df(t) = N, so IDF = log(1) = 0 — the word gets completely zeroed out. If a word appears in only 1 out of 1,000 documents, IDF = log(1000) ≈ 6.9 — it gets a big boost.

In practice, sklearn uses a slightly smoothed version — log((1 + N) / (1 + df(t))) + 1 — to avoid division by zero and to prevent terms from being completely eliminated. But the core idea is identical: rare words get higher weights.

The Combined Formula

The final TF-IDF score for term t in document d is simply:

TF-IDF(t, d) = TF(t, d) × IDF(t)

A high TF-IDF score means the word is frequent in this particular document but rare across the corpus. That's exactly the kind of word that characterizes what this document is about.

Worked Example

Let's reuse our three documents. We have N = 3 documents.

Consider the word "cat". It appears in Doc 1 and Doc 3, so df("cat") = 2. Its IDF is log(3/2) ≈ 0.405. In Doc 1, TF("cat", Doc 1) = 1. So TF-IDF = 1 × 0.405 = 0.405.

Now consider "the". It appears in all 3 documents, so df("the") = 3. Its IDF is log(3/3) = log(1) = 0. No matter how many times "the" appears in a document, its TF-IDF is always zero. The word is completely uninformative for distinguishing documents — which is exactly right.

Now consider "mat". It appears only in Doc 1, so df("mat") = 1. Its IDF is log(3/1) ≈ 1.099. In Doc 1, TF("mat", Doc 1) = 1, so TF-IDF = 1 × 1.099 = 1.099. This is the highest score so far, because "mat" is the most discriminative word — it uniquely identifies Doc 1.

This is the power of IDF. Without it, "the" and "mat" look equally important. With it, the model automatically learns to focus on the words that actually matter.

Document Similarity with Cosine Similarity

Once you have TF-IDF vectors, you can measure how similar two documents are using cosine similarity. This computes the cosine of the angle between two vectors — 1.0 means identical direction (same topic), 0.0 means completely unrelated, and -1.0 means opposite (rare in practice since TF-IDF values are non-negative).

Why cosine and not Euclidean distance? Because cosine is length-invariant. A 5,000-word article and a 50-word abstract about the same topic will have very different vector magnitudes but similar directions. Cosine captures that shared direction. Euclidean distance would say they're far apart just because one vector is longer.

Code: TF-IDF with sklearn

from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer
from sklearn.metrics.pairwise import cosine_similarity

docs = [
    "the cat sat on the mat",
    "the dog sat on the log",
    "the cat chased the dog",
]

# Fit TF-IDF
tfidf = TfidfVectorizer()
X = tfidf.fit_transform(docs)

print("Vocabulary:", tfidf.get_feature_names_out())
print("TF-IDF matrix (rounded):")
print(X.toarray().round(3))

# Document similarity
sim_matrix = cosine_similarity(X)
print("\nCosine similarity matrix:")
print(sim_matrix.round(3))
# Doc 1 vs Doc 2 will be moderately similar (shared "sat", "on")
# Doc 1 vs Doc 3 will be somewhat similar (shared "cat")
# Doc 2 vs Doc 3 will be somewhat similar (shared "dog")
Why sklearn's TF-IDF Numbers Look Different

If you compute TF-IDF by hand and compare with sklearn's output, the numbers won't match exactly. That's because TfidfVectorizer applies smoothing to the IDF formula, adds 1 to prevent zero divisions, and then L2-normalizes each document vector so they all have unit length. This normalization bakes cosine similarity into the representation itself — the dot product of two L2-normalized vectors is their cosine similarity. The conceptual idea is identical; sklearn just adds engineering guardrails.

Limitations of TF-IDF

TF-IDF is a massive improvement over raw counts, but it has clear limitations you should know about.

First, there is no word order. "Dog bites man" and "man bites dog" produce identical TF-IDF vectors. N-grams help somewhat, but they blow up the vocabulary and only capture local ordering.

Second, there is no semantics. "Happy" and "joyful" are as unrelated as "happy" and "refrigerator" in TF-IDF space. The method has no notion of meaning, synonymy, or even that words can be conceptually related. Each word is an independent dimension.

Third, the curse of dimensionality bites hard. Real-world vocabularies easily reach 100,000+ terms. Every document lives in a 100,000-dimensional space where most coordinates are zero. Models struggle to learn in such high-dimensional, sparse spaces without enormous amounts of training data.

These limitations are precisely what motivated the development of dense word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe) and eventually contextual representations (ELMo, BERT, GPT) — all of which you'll encounter in the next sections.

When BoW and TF-IDF Still Work (and When They Don't)

Despite being decades old, these methods haven't retired. TF-IDF remains the backbone of many information retrieval systems, including search engines. If you're building a document search feature, a spam filter, or a topic classifier with limited labeled data, TF-IDF plus logistic regression is a shockingly strong baseline. It trains in seconds, requires no GPU, and is fully interpretable — you can inspect exactly which words drove a prediction.

TF-IDF works best when the task depends on which words appear, not on subtle relationships between them. Spam detection ("buy", "discount", "click here"), topic classification ("election", "candidate", "vote"), and document deduplication are all good fits. When you have small datasets — hundreds or a few thousand labeled examples — TF-IDF often beats neural approaches that are starved for data.

Where do they fail? Anything that requires understanding meaning. Sentiment analysis stumbles because "not great" looks similar to "great" in BoW space. Paraphrase detection fails because "the movie was fantastic" and "the film was excellent" share zero words. Question answering, summarization, and machine translation are all beyond reach. For these tasks, you need representations that capture semantics — and that's where embeddings and transformers come in.

The Practical Rule of Thumb

Always start with TF-IDF. Seriously. Before reaching for BERT or a large language model, build a TF-IDF baseline. It takes five minutes, costs nothing to run, and gives you a performance floor. If TF-IDF gets you 90% of the way there, you might not need a transformer at all. If it only gets you 60%, now you know exactly how much the fancy model is buying you — and you can justify the added complexity and cost.
