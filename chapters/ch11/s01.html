Text Preprocessing

Chapter 11: Natural Language Processing 5 subtopics
TL;DR

Raw text is messy. Before any model can learn from it, you need to chop it into pieces (tokenization), normalize the surface forms (lowercasing, Unicode cleanup), remove noise (stop words, punctuation), and reduce words to their roots (stemming or lemmatization). Every choice here is a tradeoff — aggressive preprocessing loses information, too little preprocessing leaves noise for the model to fight through. Understanding these tradeoffs is what separates a pipeline that works from one that silently fails.

Tokenization

Why We Need It

Computers don't understand words. They understand numbers. So the very first thing you do with any text is break it into smaller pieces — tokens — that you can map to numbers. A token might be a word, a subword, a character, or even a sentence. The choice matters enormously because it defines the atoms your model will reason over. Get tokenization wrong and everything downstream suffers: your vocabulary explodes, your embeddings become meaningless, and your model wastes capacity learning things it shouldn't have to.

Whitespace Tokenization

The simplest approach is splitting on spaces. In Python, that's just "Hello world".split(). It's fast, it's obvious, and it works surprisingly well for clean English text. But it falls apart quickly. Consider the string "Don't stop — it's $3.50!". Whitespace splitting gives you ["Don't", "stop", "—", "it's", "$3.50!"]. The punctuation is glued to the words. "Don't" stays as one token when you probably want "Do" and "n't" (or "not"). The dollar amount and exclamation mark are fused. For a toy project this is fine. For anything serious, you need more.

Regex-Based Tokenization

A step up is using regular expressions to define what a token looks like. NLTK's regexp_tokenize lets you specify a pattern and extract all matches. For example, the pattern \w+ grabs sequences of alphanumeric characters, effectively stripping punctuation. You can get fancier — \w+(?:'\w+)? keeps contractions together. The advantage is full control. The disadvantage is that you're writing rules by hand, and natural language has more edge cases than you'll ever anticipate. Abbreviations like "U.S.A.", URLs, email addresses, emoticons — each one needs its own regex. This path leads to madness for production systems.

Proper NLP Tokenizers

Libraries like spaCy and NLTK ship with tokenizers that handle the edge cases for you. NLTK's word_tokenize uses a combination of regex rules and the Penn Treebank tokenization standard. It knows that "don't" should become ["do", "n't"] and that "Mr." doesn't end a sentence. spaCy's tokenizer goes further — it uses a rule-based system with exception lists and prefix/suffix/infix rules that handle most English text correctly out of the box.

Here's a practical comparison:

import spacy
from nltk.tokenize import word_tokenize, regexp_tokenize

text = "Dr. Smith didn't pay $3.50 for the U.S.A. trip — he couldn't!"

# Whitespace (naive)
print("Whitespace:", text.split())
# ['Dr.', 'Smith', "didn't", 'pay', '$3.50', 'for', 'the',
#  'U.S.A.', 'trip', '—', 'he', "couldn't!"]

# Regex (alphanumeric only)
print("Regex:", regexp_tokenize(text, r"\w+(?:'\w+)?"))
# ['Dr', 'Smith', "didn't", 'pay', '3', '50', 'for', 'the',
#  'U', 'S', 'A', 'trip', 'he', "couldn't"]

# NLTK word_tokenize
print("NLTK:", word_tokenize(text))
# ['Dr.', 'Smith', 'did', "n't", 'pay', '$', '3.50', 'for',
#  'the', 'U.S.A.', 'trip', '—', 'he', 'could', "n't", '!']

# spaCy
nlp = spacy.load("en_core_web_sm")
doc = nlp(text)
print("spaCy:", [token.text for token in doc])
# ['Dr.', 'Smith', 'did', "n't", 'pay', '$', '3.50', 'for',
#  'the', 'U.S.A.', 'trip', '—', 'he', 'could', "n't", '!']

Notice how the naive approaches mangle contractions and currency, while spaCy and NLTK handle them gracefully. The lesson: don't reinvent the tokenizer unless you have a very good reason.

Sentence Tokenization

Sentence tokenization (also called sentence segmentation) is harder than it looks. You might think you can just split on periods. But "Dr. Smith went to Washington. He arrived at 3 p.m." has three periods that don't end sentences and two that do. Abbreviations, decimal numbers, ellipses, URLs — all contain periods that aren't sentence boundaries. NLTK's sent_tokenize uses the Punkt algorithm, which is a trained unsupervised model that learns abbreviations and sentence starters from data. spaCy handles sentence boundaries during its full parsing pipeline, using dependency parse information to determine where sentences end.

Language-Specific Challenges

Everything above assumes English. Other languages make tokenization even harder. Chinese and Japanese don't use spaces between words at all — the string "我喜欢机器学习" (I like machine learning) is a continuous sequence of characters, and deciding where one word ends and another begins requires a statistical model or dictionary lookup. German creates compound words by smashing nouns together: "Donaudampfschifffahrtsgesellschaft" is one word meaning "Danube steamship company." Turkish is agglutinative — a single word can contain what English expresses as an entire phrase. The takeaway: always use a tokenizer designed for your target language. Universal solutions are rare.

Text Normalization

Lowercasing

Converting all text to lowercase is the most common normalization step. It collapses "The", "the", and "THE" into one token, shrinking your vocabulary and reducing sparsity. For most tasks — topic classification, search, clustering — this is a clear win. But it's not free. Lowercasing destroys information. "US" (the country) becomes "us" (the pronoun). "Apple" (the company) becomes "apple" (the fruit). Named entity recognition, coreference resolution, and any task that cares about proper nouns will suffer. The rule of thumb: lowercase for bag-of-words models and search; keep case for sequence models and anything involving names or acronyms.

Punctuation Removal and Number Handling

Stripping punctuation is another common step, and another one with hidden costs. For sentiment analysis, "great!!!" carries more weight than "great" — those exclamation marks are signal, not noise. For topic modeling, they're probably noise. Numbers are similar. You can remove them, replace them all with a placeholder like <NUM>, or keep them. If you're classifying financial documents, the number "10,000" matters. If you're classifying movie reviews, it probably doesn't. There's no universal right answer. Think about what information your downstream task actually needs.

Unicode Normalization

Unicode normalization is one of those things you don't think about until it bites you. The character "é" can be represented two ways in Unicode: as a single code point (U+00E9, the precomposed form) or as two code points (U+0065 "e" + U+0301 combining accent). They look identical on screen but are different byte sequences. String comparison fails silently. Python's unicodedata.normalize("NFC", text) converts to the precomposed form (NFC — Canonical Decomposition followed by Canonical Composition), and "NFD" does the opposite (NFD — Canonical Decomposition). Always normalize to NFC before doing anything else with your text. It takes milliseconds and prevents bizarre bugs.

Contraction Expansion

Expanding contractions — turning "don't" into "do not", "I'm" into "I am", "they've" into "they have" — is a normalization step that helps some pipelines. It reduces vocabulary size and makes downstream analysis cleaner. But it's also lossy: "don't" has a slightly different pragmatic tone than "do not" in real speech. Most people implement this with a simple dictionary lookup. Libraries like contractions in Python handle common cases. For most NLP pipelines, expansion is a sensible default unless you're doing stylistic or sociolinguistic analysis.

Stop Words

Stop words are the most common words in a language — "the", "is", "at", "which", "on" — that carry little semantic meaning on their own. Both NLTK and spaCy ship with standard stop word lists. NLTK's English list has about 179 words; spaCy's has around 326. The idea is that removing them reduces noise and dimensionality. If you're building a TF-IDF search engine or a topic model, stop word removal usually helps because these words dominate frequency counts without adding topical information.

But here's where people get into trouble: stop word removal is not always safe. In sentiment analysis, "not" is on most stop word lists, yet "not good" means the opposite of "good." Removing "not" flips the sentiment. Phrases like "to be or not to be" become meaningless. For deep learning models that learn their own representations, stop word removal is often unnecessary and sometimes harmful — the model can learn to ignore irrelevant words on its own, and you're just throwing away context it might use.

Domain-specific stop words are also worth considering. In a corpus of legal documents, "court", "plaintiff", and "defendant" might appear in nearly every document. They're not on any standard stop word list, but they carry no discriminating power within that domain. You can identify them by looking at document frequency — words that appear in more than 90% of documents are usually good candidates. But be careful: removing them is irreversible, and you might want them later for a different task.

When NOT to Remove Stop Words

If your task depends on word order, negation, or phrase-level meaning, keep stop words. Modern transformer models (BERT, GPT) were trained with stop words present, and removing them before feeding text to these models will degrade performance. Stop word removal is most useful for classical bag-of-words approaches: TF-IDF, topic models, keyword extraction. When in doubt, keep them and let the model decide what matters.

Stemming

Stemming is the process of reducing a word to its root form by chopping off suffixes (and sometimes prefixes). It's a crude, rule-based operation — no dictionary lookup, no understanding of grammar. The goal is to collapse related word forms: "running", "runs", and "ran" should all map to something like "run" so that your model treats them as the same concept.

Porter Stemmer and Snowball Stemmer

The most famous stemmer is the Porter Stemmer, designed by Martin Porter in 1980. It applies a series of rules in phases: strip plurals, strip past tenses, strip derivational suffixes. NLTK makes it one import away. The Snowball Stemmer (also called Porter2) is a refined version that fixes some of the original's quirks and supports multiple languages.

Stemming is fast — it's just string manipulation. That speed makes it attractive for search engines and information retrieval where you're processing millions of documents. But the tradeoff is accuracy.

Over-Stemming and Under-Stemming

Over-stemming happens when the stemmer collapses words that shouldn't be grouped. "University" becomes "univers", and so does "universal" — but a university and something universal are very different things. "Operating" and "operation" both stem to "oper", but an operating system and a surgical operation share little meaning. Under-stemming is the opposite: failing to collapse words that should be grouped. "Alumnus" and "alumni" won't get stemmed to the same root because the Latin plural doesn't follow English suffix rules. Stemmers work best for regular English morphology and struggle with irregular forms and borrowed words.

Lemmatization

Lemmatization is the smarter sibling of stemming. Instead of blindly chopping suffixes, it uses a dictionary (or morphological analysis) to return the actual base form of a word — its lemma. "Better" lemmatizes to "good". "Ran" lemmatizes to "run". "Mice" lemmatizes to "mouse". A stemmer would never get these right because they require knowing the word, not just the suffix pattern.

Dictionary-Based vs Morphological Analysis

The simplest lemmatizers just do a dictionary lookup: they have a table mapping word forms to lemmas. WordNet-based lemmatization in NLTK works this way. More sophisticated approaches (like what spaCy uses) perform morphological analysis — understanding that "unhappiness" is "un-" + "happy" + "-ness" and the lemma is "happy". The dictionary approach is fast but brittle (if the word isn't in the dictionary, it returns it unchanged). Morphological analysis is more robust but slower.

POS-Aware Lemmatization

POS-aware lemmatization uses the word's part of speech to disambiguate. The word "saw" can be a noun (a cutting tool) or a verb (past tense of "see"). Without POS information, the lemmatizer has to guess. With it, verb-"saw" correctly becomes "see" and noun-"saw" stays "saw". NLTK's WordNet lemmatizer accepts a POS tag argument; spaCy does this automatically because its pipeline runs POS tagging before lemmatization.

Here's a direct comparison of stemming and lemmatization:

from nltk.stem import PorterStemmer, SnowballStemmer
from nltk.stem import WordNetLemmatizer
from nltk import pos_tag, word_tokenize
import nltk
nltk.download('averaged_perceptron_tagger_eng', quiet=True)
nltk.download('wordnet', quiet=True)
nltk.download('punkt_tab', quiet=True)

porter = PorterStemmer()
snowball = SnowballStemmer("english")
lemmatizer = WordNetLemmatizer()

words = ["running", "better", "universities", "mice", "saw",
         "happily", "studies", "geese", "operating", "flies"]

print(f"{'Word':<15} {'Porter':<15} {'Snowball':<15} {'Lemma (n)':<15} {'Lemma (v)':<15}")
print("-" * 75)
for word in words:
    print(f"{word:<15} "
          f"{porter.stem(word):<15} "
          f"{snowball.stem(word):<15} "
          f"{lemmatizer.lemmatize(word, pos='n'):<15} "   # as noun
          f"{lemmatizer.lemmatize(word, pos='v'):<15}")    # as verb

# Output:
# Word            Porter          Snowball        Lemma (n)       Lemma (v)
# ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
# running         run             run             running         run
# better          better          better          better          better
# universities    univers         univers         university      university
# mice            mice            mice            mouse           mice
# saw             saw             saw             saw             see
# happily         happili         happili         happily         happily
# studies         studi           studi           study           study
# geese           gees            gees            goose           geese
# operating       oper            oper            operating       operate
# flies           fli             fli             fly             fly

The table makes the tradeoffs obvious. Stemming is aggressive and sometimes wrong ("univers", "happili", "fli" are not real words). Lemmatization produces actual words but needs POS context to get the right one — look at how "saw" and "mice" change depending on whether you treat them as nouns or verbs.

Stemming vs Lemmatization — When to Use Which

For search engines and information retrieval, stemming is often good enough. Speed matters when you're indexing millions of documents, and the rough collapsing is acceptable. For text classification and sentiment analysis, lemmatization usually performs better because it preserves more meaning. For modern deep learning pipelines (BERT, GPT), you typically need neither — these models use subword tokenizers (BPE, WordPiece) that handle morphological variation implicitly. The model learns from context that "running" and "run" are related without you having to tell it.

Stemming vs Lemmatization at a Glance

Stemming: fast, rule-based, no dictionary needed, produces non-words, language-specific rules, best for search and IR. Lemmatization: slower, dictionary or model-based, produces real words, benefits from POS tags, best for tasks needing semantic precision. Neither: modern transformer models use subword tokenization and don't need either step — applying stemming or lemmatization before BERT will likely hurt performance.

Sentence Segmentation

We touched on sentence tokenization earlier, but it's worth revisiting because sentence segmentation is often its own pipeline stage, especially for tasks like machine translation, summarization, and document parsing.

Rule-Based Approaches

Rule-based segmenters use handcrafted patterns: split on periods, question marks, and exclamation marks, but not when preceded by known abbreviations (Mr., Dr., U.S., etc.), not inside quotation marks, not when the period follows a single capital letter (initials). You maintain an ever-growing list of exceptions. This works surprisingly well for clean, formal English text. It breaks down on informal text (social media, chat logs), multilingual content, and any domain with unusual abbreviation patterns (legal, medical).

ML-Based Approaches

ML-based segmenters train a classifier to decide whether each period (or other punctuation) is a sentence boundary. NLTK's Punkt tokenizer is one — it's an unsupervised model that learns abbreviations and sentence starters from the text itself. spaCy's sentencizer component can be rule-based or trained. For difficult domains, you can train your own model on annotated data. The advantage is adaptability: an ML segmenter can learn that "Fig. 3" doesn't end a sentence in a scientific paper without you writing a rule for it.

In practice, spaCy's default sentence segmentation (based on the dependency parse) is good enough for most English text. For other languages or specialized domains, you might need to train a custom model. The key insight is that sentence segmentation is a classification problem — each candidate boundary is either a true boundary or not — and you should treat it as one rather than trying to enumerate every edge case with regex.

Putting It All Together

A typical classical NLP preprocessing pipeline runs in this order: (1) Unicode normalization, (2) tokenization, (3) lowercasing, (4) stop word removal, (5) stemming or lemmatization. But this is not a universal recipe. Each step is optional, and the right combination depends on your task, your model, and your data. A TF-IDF pipeline benefits from aggressive preprocessing. A BERT pipeline needs almost none — just tokenization with the model's own tokenizer. The worst mistake is applying a standard preprocessing template without thinking about what information each step destroys and whether your task needs that information. Preprocessing is not a checklist; it's a series of engineering decisions.
