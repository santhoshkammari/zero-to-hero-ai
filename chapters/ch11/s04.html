The NLP Task Landscape

Chapter 11: Natural Language Processing Classification · NER · QA · Summarization · The task taxonomy
TL;DR

NLP is a toolbox, not a single trick. Text classification assigns labels to documents. Sentiment analysis gauges opinion. NER finds named entities in text. POS tagging labels grammatical roles. Relation extraction links entities together. Question answering locates or generates answers. Summarization compresses documents. Information retrieval finds relevant documents for a query. Semantic similarity measures meaning overlap between sentences. Coreference resolution figures out who "he" or "it" refers to. Each task has a classical baseline, a neural middle era, and a modern transformer-based approach — and knowing the full stack helps you pick the right tool for your budget and latency constraints.

Text Classification

Text classification is the most bread-and-butter NLP task there is. You have a document — an email, a tweet, a support ticket — and you want to assign it one or more labels. Is this email spam or not spam? Is this support ticket about billing, shipping, or a technical issue? Is this news article about politics, sports, or technology? Every time you've seen an email land in your spam folder, a text classifier made that call.

The classical approach is simple and surprisingly effective. You represent each document as a bag-of-words (BoW) vector — just count how many times each word appears, ignoring order. A fancier version uses TF-IDF (term frequency–inverse document frequency), which downweights common words like "the" and upweights rare, informative ones. Then you feed that vector into a Naive Bayes classifier or an SVM (support vector machine). Naive Bayes is fast and works shockingly well for spam detection. SVMs are slower to train but tend to generalize better, especially when you have limited data.

The neural era brought TextCNN, introduced by Yoon Kim in 2014. The idea is elegant. You represent each word as an embedding vector, stack them into a matrix (one row per word), and then slide convolutional filters over the matrix. Different filter sizes — say 3, 4, and 5 words wide — capture n-gram patterns of different lengths. After convolution, you apply max-over-time pooling: for each filter, keep only the strongest activation across the entire sequence. This gives you a fixed-size representation regardless of document length. A final fully-connected layer maps that representation to your label set. TextCNN is fast, parallelizable, and surprisingly competitive even by today's standards.

The modern approach is to fine-tune a pretrained transformer like BERT or RoBERTa. You take the [CLS] token's representation, add a classification head on top, and fine-tune the whole thing on your labeled data. With just a few hundred examples, you can often beat classical methods by a wide margin. If you have no labeled data at all, you can try zero-shot classification with an LLM — just prompt it with "Is this text about sports or politics?" and read the answer. It won't match a fine-tuned model, but it requires zero training.

Sub-variants are everywhere. Spam detection is binary classification. Intent classification powers voice assistants — figuring out if you want to set a timer, play music, or check the weather. Topic classification organizes news feeds and content recommendation systems. The task is the same skeleton; the labels change.

Sentiment Analysis

Sentiment analysis is text classification's most famous cousin. The goal is to determine the opinion or emotion expressed in a piece of text. The simplest version is binary — positive or negative. A step up is multi-class — rating a review from 1 to 5 stars. The most nuanced version is aspect-based sentiment analysis, where you identify sentiment toward specific aspects of an entity. "The food was amazing but the service was terrible" has positive sentiment toward food and negative toward service. Same review, two different sentiments.

The classical approach uses a lexicon — a dictionary that assigns sentiment scores to individual words. VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner) is the most widely used lexicon for English. It handles things like capitalization ("GREAT" is more intense than "great"), exclamation marks, and basic negation. For something more robust, you combine BoW or TF-IDF features with an SVM or logistic regression classifier trained on labeled reviews.

The neural era leaned on recurrent models. A BiLSTM (bidirectional LSTM) reads the sentence forward and backward, capturing context from both directions. Adding an attention mechanism lets the model focus on the most sentiment-bearing words — "terrible" matters more than "the" for predicting negative sentiment.

Today, you fine-tune a transformer. Models like cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-sentiment come pre-fine-tuned and work out of the box. For quick experiments, few-shot prompting with GPT-4 or Claude is remarkably good — just show the model two or three labeled examples and let it classify the rest.

The hard problems remain hard. Sarcasm is brutal — "Oh great, another meeting" is negative despite the word "great." Negation flips sentiment — "not bad" is positive despite "bad." And domain shift is real: a model trained on movie reviews may fail on restaurant reviews because "unpredictable" is positive for a thriller but negative for a chef. Always evaluate on in-domain data.

Named Entity Recognition (NER)

Named Entity Recognition is the task of finding and classifying named entities in text. Given the sentence "Barack Obama was born in Honolulu, Hawaii on August 4, 1961," a NER system should identify "Barack Obama" as a person (PER), "Honolulu" and "Hawaii" as locations (LOC), and "August 4, 1961" as a date (DATE). Standard entity types also include organization (ORG), and many domain-specific schemes add things like gene names, drug names, or monetary values.

NER is a sequence labeling problem. Every token in the sentence gets a tag. The standard tagging scheme is BIO (also called IOB): B-XXX marks the beginning of an entity of type XXX, I-XXX marks tokens inside the entity, and O marks tokens that aren't part of any entity. Here's how our example looks:

# BIO tagging example
# Token          Tag
# ─────────      ──────
# Barack         B-PER
# Obama          I-PER
# was            O
# born           O
# in             O
# Honolulu       B-LOC
# ,              O
# Hawaii         B-LOC
# on             O
# August         B-DATE
# 4              I-DATE
# ,              I-DATE
# 1961           I-DATE

The classical approach uses a CRF (Conditional Random Field) or an HMM (Hidden Markov Model) with hand-crafted features — capitalization patterns, word shape, surrounding words, gazetteer lookups (is this word in a list of known city names?). CRFs are discriminative models that learn transition probabilities between tags, so they naturally enforce constraints like "I-PER can't follow B-LOC."

The breakthrough neural architecture was the BiLSTM-CRF. A BiLSTM encodes each token with context from both directions. Then, instead of predicting each tag independently, you add a CRF decoding layer on top. The CRF models tag-to-tag transition scores, and at inference time, the Viterbi algorithm efficiently finds the globally optimal tag sequence. This combination — neural encoder plus structured prediction — was the dominant approach from roughly 2015 to 2018.

Modern NER systems use BERT-based models. You feed the sentence through BERT, take the hidden state for each token, and pass it through a linear classification layer. Some systems still add a CRF on top of BERT; others find it unnecessary because BERT's contextual representations are so strong. Span-based approaches enumerate candidate spans and classify each span as an entity type or "not an entity." This naturally handles the nested NER problem — "Bank of America" is an ORG, and "America" inside it is a LOC. BIO tagging can't represent overlapping entities; span-based methods can.

from transformers import pipeline

ner = pipeline("ner", model="dslim/bert-base-NER", aggregation_strategy="simple")
text = "Elon Musk founded SpaceX in Hawthorne, California in 2002."
entities = ner(text)

for ent in entities:
    print(f"{ent['word']:<20} {ent['entity_group']:<8} {ent['score']:.3f}")
# Elon Musk            PER      0.998
# SpaceX               ORG      0.994
# Hawthorne            LOC      0.997
# California           LOC      0.999
Why BIO and not just entity labels?

Without the B/I distinction, you can't tell where one entity ends and another begins. If "New York City Council" appears in a sentence, is it one ORG or a LOC ("New York City") followed by an ORG ("Council")? The B tag marks the start of a new entity, even if the previous token was also part of an entity of the same type. Without B tags, consecutive entities of the same type would merge into one.

POS Tagging

Part-of-speech (POS) tagging assigns a grammatical category to every word in a sentence — noun, verb, adjective, adverb, preposition, and so on. "The cat sat on the mat" becomes DET NOUN VERB ADP DET NOUN. It sounds simple, but ambiguity makes it interesting. "Book" is a noun in "I read a book" and a verb in "Book a flight." Context matters.

The Universal POS Tagset defines 17 coarse tags that work across languages: NOUN, VERB, ADJ, ADV, PRON, DET, ADP, NUM, CONJ, PART, INTJ, and a few others. Finer-grained tagsets like the Penn Treebank set have 36+ tags, distinguishing between past-tense verbs (VBD), gerunds (VBG), and so on. The universal tagset trades granularity for cross-lingual consistency.

Classical POS taggers used HMMs, modeling the probability of a tag given the previous tag (transition) and the probability of a word given its tag (emission). CRF-based taggers replaced HMMs by using richer features without the independence assumptions. Both approaches achieve around 97% accuracy on English — which sounds great until you realize the remaining 3% are the hard, ambiguous cases that actually matter for downstream tasks.

Modern POS tagging is just another sequence labeling problem for transformers. Fine-tune BERT on a POS-tagged corpus and you get state-of-the-art accuracy with minimal effort. In fact, most practitioners never build a standalone POS tagger anymore — libraries like spaCy ship fast, accurate taggers out of the box.

Why does POS tagging still matter if transformers can do everything end-to-end? Three reasons. First, POS tags feed into syntactic parsing — knowing a word is a verb constrains what parse trees are valid. Second, lemmatization (reducing "running" to "run") needs POS information because "running" the verb lemmatizes differently than "running" the noun. Third, POS features improve NER and other downstream tasks in low-resource settings where you don't have enough data for the transformer to learn these patterns on its own.

Relation Extraction

Relation extraction identifies semantic relationships between entities in text. Given "Barack Obama was born in Honolulu," the system should extract the triple ("Barack Obama", "born_in", "Honolulu"). This is how knowledge graphs get built. Every fact in Wikidata or Google's Knowledge Graph was either curated by hand or extracted automatically from text by a relation extraction system.

The simplest approach is supervised RE. You label a training set with entity pairs and their relationship types, then train a classifier. The features typically include the entities themselves, the words between them, their dependency path, and their entity types (a PER and a LOC are more likely linked by "born_in" than "works_at"). The downside: labeled data is expensive, and you can only extract relationship types you've seen in training.

Distant supervision is a clever workaround. You take an existing knowledge base (like Freebase or Wikidata), find all entity pairs that have a known relationship, then find sentences in a large corpus that mention both entities. You assume — often incorrectly, but usefully on average — that those sentences express the known relationship. This gives you noisy but abundant training data without manual labeling.

Neural RE models typically encode the sentence with a transformer, then apply attention over the two entity mentions to capture their interaction. Some models insert special marker tokens around entities — like [E1] Barack Obama [/E1] was born in [E2] Honolulu [/E2] — so the model can directly attend to entity boundaries.

The modern approach uses LLMs with prompting. You write a prompt like "Given the text: '...'. What is the relationship between 'Barack Obama' and 'Honolulu'? Choose from: born_in, works_at, lives_in, none." This works surprisingly well for common relation types and requires zero training data. For production systems processing millions of documents, you still fine-tune a smaller model for speed and cost.

Question Answering

Question answering (QA) comes in several flavors, and confusing them causes trouble. Let's untangle them.

Extractive QA is the most constrained form. You're given a passage and a question, and the answer must be a contiguous span within the passage. The Stanford Question Answering Dataset (SQuAD) popularized this format. The model reads the passage, reads the question, and predicts start and end positions of the answer span. BERT-based models dominate here — you feed the question and passage as a single sequence, and two linear heads predict the start and end token positions.

Abstractive QA lets the model generate an answer in its own words rather than copying a span. This is harder because the model needs to synthesize information and produce fluent text. Seq2seq models like T5 and BART handle this well — you encode the question and context, then decode the answer token by token.

Open-domain QA removes the luxury of a given passage. The question is "What year was the Eiffel Tower built?" and the model has to find the answer from a large corpus — say, all of Wikipedia. The standard approach is a retrieve-then-read pipeline: first, a retriever finds the top-k most relevant passages; then, a reader extracts or generates the answer from those passages.

The modern incarnation is retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). A dense retriever (like DPR — Dense Passage Retrieval) encodes queries and passages into the same vector space, retrieves relevant chunks, and feeds them as context to a generative LLM. RAG is the architecture behind most chatbot knowledge systems today. It separates what the model "knows" (its parameters) from what it can "look up" (the retrieval corpus), which makes it easy to update knowledge without retraining.

Extractive vs. Abstractive — when to pick which

If you need verifiable answers with provenance (legal, medical, compliance), use extractive QA. The answer comes verbatim from the source, so you can always point to where it came from. If you need natural, conversational answers and the source material is messy or fragmented, use abstractive QA. But know that abstractive models can hallucinate — they might generate fluent answers that aren't supported by the source. Always add a verification step in production.

Summarization

Summarization compresses a long document into a shorter version that preserves the key information. Like QA, it splits into two camps: extractive and abstractive.

Extractive summarization selects the most important sentences from the original document and concatenates them. The original text is never rewritten — just pruned. TextRank is the classic algorithm. It builds a graph where each sentence is a node and edge weights represent sentence similarity (usually cosine similarity of TF-IDF vectors). Then it runs a variant of PageRank to score each sentence by importance. High-scoring sentences become the summary. LexRank is a similar graph-based approach. BertSumExt modernized this by using BERT embeddings for sentence representations and a classification head to select summary-worthy sentences.

import numpy as np
from sklearn.metrics.pairwise import cosine_similarity
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer

def textrank_summarize(sentences, top_n=3):
    """Simplified TextRank: build similarity graph, run power iteration."""
    # Step 1: TF-IDF sentence vectors
    vectorizer = TfidfVectorizer()
    tfidf_matrix = vectorizer.fit_transform(sentences)

    # Step 2: Similarity matrix (adjacency weights)
    sim_matrix = cosine_similarity(tfidf_matrix)
    np.fill_diagonal(sim_matrix, 0)  # no self-loops

    # Step 3: Power iteration (simplified PageRank)
    n = len(sentences)
    scores = np.ones(n) / n
    damping = 0.85

    for _ in range(30):
        row_sums = sim_matrix.sum(axis=1, keepdims=True)
        row_sums[row_sums == 0] = 1  # avoid division by zero
        transition = sim_matrix / row_sums
        scores = (1 - damping) / n + damping * transition.T @ scores

    # Step 4: Pick top-n sentences (preserve original order)
    ranked_idx = np.argsort(scores)[::-1][:top_n]
    ranked_idx = sorted(ranked_idx)
    return [sentences[i] for i in ranked_idx]

# Example usage
doc = [
    "Machine learning has transformed natural language processing.",
    "Early NLP systems relied on hand-crafted rules and lexicons.",
    "The introduction of word embeddings was a turning point.",
    "Transformers replaced recurrent models as the dominant architecture.",
    "Today, large language models can perform most NLP tasks zero-shot.",
    "However, smaller fine-tuned models remain more practical for production.",
]

summary = textrank_summarize(doc, top_n=2)
for s in summary:
    print(s)

Abstractive summarization generates new text that captures the document's meaning. Seq2seq models with attention were the first neural approach. Modern systems use pretrained encoder-decoder models. BART (denoising autoencoder pretraining) and T5 (text-to-text pretraining) are the workhorses. Pegasus was pretrained specifically for summarization by masking entire sentences and learning to generate them — a pretraining objective that directly mirrors the downstream task.

Evaluation uses ROUGE scores (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation). ROUGE-1 measures unigram overlap between the generated summary and a reference summary. ROUGE-2 measures bigram overlap. ROUGE-L measures the longest common subsequence. ROUGE captures surface-level overlap but misses semantic equivalence — two summaries can say the same thing with different words and get low ROUGE scores. Newer metrics like BERTScore use embedding similarity instead.

LLM-based summarization is now the default for most applications. You prompt GPT-4 or Claude with "Summarize this document in 3 sentences" and get a surprisingly good result. The trade-off is cost and latency — for summarizing millions of documents, a fine-tuned BART model at 400M parameters is far cheaper than an API call to a 100B+ parameter model.

Information Retrieval

Information retrieval (IR) is the task of finding relevant documents for a given query. Every search engine is an IR system. When you type a query into Google or search your company's internal docs, an IR system decides which documents to show you and in what order.

The classical workhorse is BM25, which evolved from TF-IDF. BM25 scores a document by summing a weighted term frequency for each query term that appears in the document, with diminishing returns for repeated occurrences and a length normalization factor so short documents aren't penalized. It's a sparse retrieval method — it only matches on exact term overlap. "Dog" won't match "canine." Despite this limitation, BM25 is fast, well-understood, and remains a tough baseline to beat.

Neural IR introduced dense retrieval. The idea: encode both queries and documents as dense vectors using a neural encoder, then retrieve documents whose vectors are closest to the query vector (usually by cosine similarity or dot product). DPR (Dense Passage Retrieval) uses two separate BERT encoders — a bi-encoder architecture — one for queries and one for documents. At indexing time, you encode all documents and store their vectors. At query time, you encode the query and find the nearest vectors using approximate nearest neighbor search (FAISS, ScaNN). Dense retrieval handles synonymy and paraphrase because semantically similar texts land near each other in vector space.

For maximum precision, you add a cross-encoder reranker. A cross-encoder takes the query and a candidate document as a single input and outputs a relevance score. It's much more accurate than a bi-encoder because it can model fine-grained token interactions, but it's too slow to score every document. So you use the bi-encoder to retrieve the top 100 candidates and the cross-encoder to rerank them.

Modern production systems use hybrid retrieval: run BM25 (sparse) and dense retrieval in parallel, then fuse the results. This captures both exact term matches (BM25's strength) and semantic matches (dense retrieval's strength). Reciprocal rank fusion (RRF) is a simple, effective way to merge ranked lists from both systems.

Semantic Textual Similarity

Semantic textual similarity (STS) measures how similar two sentences are in meaning on a continuous scale. "A cat is sitting on the mat" and "A feline rests on a rug" are semantically very similar despite sharing almost no words. "A cat is sitting on the mat" and "A cat is not sitting on the mat" share almost every word but have opposite meanings. STS captures meaning, not surface form.

The simplest approach is to compute word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe) for each word, average them across the sentence, and compute cosine similarity between the two averages. This works better than you'd expect, but averaging throws away word order — "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" get the same embedding.

Sentence-BERT (SBERT) was the breakthrough. It fine-tunes BERT using a Siamese network architecture: feed both sentences through the same BERT encoder, pool each into a single vector (usually mean pooling over token embeddings), and train with a contrastive or regression objective. The result is a model that produces high-quality sentence embeddings in a single forward pass. Computing similarity then reduces to cosine similarity between two vectors — which is orders of magnitude faster than running a cross-encoder on every pair.

Applications are everywhere. Duplicate detection: are these two support tickets describing the same issue? Semantic search: find documents whose meaning matches the query, not just the keywords. Clustering: group similar documents together by clustering their sentence embeddings.

The STS Benchmark is the standard evaluation dataset. It contains sentence pairs from captions, news headlines, and forum posts, each scored from 0 (unrelated) to 5 (perfectly equivalent) by human annotators. Models are evaluated by Pearson or Spearman correlation between their predicted scores and the human scores. State-of-the-art models now achieve correlations above 0.90 on this benchmark.

Coreference Resolution

Coreference resolution determines which mentions in a text refer to the same real-world entity. Consider: "John went to the store. He bought milk. The man returned home happy." A coreference system should link "John," "He," and "The man" into one cluster — they all refer to the same person. "The store" and "milk" are singleton mentions that don't co-refer with anything else.

The task has two stages. Mention detection identifies all noun phrases that could refer to an entity — pronouns ("he," "it"), named entities ("John"), and nominal mentions ("the store," "the man"). Mention clustering then groups mentions that refer to the same entity.

Classical systems used rule-based sieves or feature-engineered classifiers that scored pairs of mentions on features like gender agreement, number agreement, distance, and string matching. These were brittle. "The CEO" and "she" co-refer only if you know the CEO is female — a fact the coreference system can't determine from local features alone.

The landmark neural approach is the end-to-end model by Lee et al. (2017). It considers all spans in the document as potential mentions, scores each span's likelihood of being a mention, and then scores pairs of mentions for coreference. The key innovation is doing mention detection and coreference jointly — no pipeline, no error propagation between stages. The model uses span representations built from BERT (or earlier, from LSTMs) and learns to rank antecedent candidates for each mention. This approach and its successors remain the backbone of modern coreference systems.

Why does coreference matter? Because almost every downstream task benefits from it. Summarization needs to know that "he" and "the president" refer to "Obama" to produce a coherent summary. Question answering needs coreference to connect information spread across sentences. Relation extraction can only find that "Obama was born in Honolulu" if it resolves "He" back to "Obama" in a sentence like "He was born in Honolulu." Coreference is the glue that holds document-level understanding together.

The common thread

Every task in this chapter follows the same evolutionary arc: hand-crafted features → shallow neural models → pretrained transformers → LLM prompting. The classical methods teach you what the task actually requires. The neural methods teach you the right inductive biases. And the transformer revolution teaches you that scale and pretraining can substitute for task-specific architecture — up to a point. In production, the right choice is often the simplest approach that meets your accuracy, latency, and cost requirements.
