Subword Tokenization

Chapter 11: Natural Language Processing 5 subtopics
TL;DR

Word-level tokenization chokes on rare words. Character-level tokenization makes sequences painfully long. Subword tokenization splits the difference — common words stay whole, rare words get broken into reusable pieces. BPE (used in GPT) merges frequent character pairs bottom-up. WordPiece (used in BERT) merges by likelihood gain. Unigram (used in T5 via SentencePiece) starts big and prunes down. Byte-level BPE eliminates unknown tokens entirely by operating on raw bytes. The choice of tokenizer and vocabulary size quietly shapes everything downstream.

The Vocabulary Problem

Before a neural network can process text, every word must become a number. The simplest approach is word-level tokenization: assign an integer to each unique word in your training corpus. "The" gets 1, "cat" gets 2, "sat" gets 3, and so on. This works until you encounter a word the model has never seen. Maybe someone writes "catsat" by accident. Maybe a user types "pretraining" and your corpus only had "pre-training." Maybe you're processing German, where compound words like "Handschuhschneeballschlacht" (a snowball fight with gloves) are perfectly normal. Every unseen word collapses into a single [UNK] token. The model learns nothing from it.

The scale of this problem is brutal. English has over a million distinct word forms if you count inflections, derivations, and compound words. A vocabulary of 50,000 words — already large — still misses a huge fraction of them. Misspellings, brand names, code identifiers, technical jargon, and morphological variants all become [UNK]. In some domains, 10–15% of tokens end up unknown. That's like reading a book with every seventh word blacked out.

The opposite extreme is character-level tokenization. Every character — "a", "b", "c", space, punctuation — gets its own token. Now nothing is unknown. The vocabulary is tiny (a few hundred entries). But the sequences become absurdly long. The sentence "The cat sat" is 11 characters instead of 3 words. Transformers have a fixed context window, so you've just cut your effective capacity by roughly 4x. Worse, the model must learn long-range dependencies across many more positions. Learning that "c-a-t" means the same animal whether it appears at position 5 or position 500 is hard when each letter is a separate step.

Subword tokenization is the sweet spot. The idea: keep common words intact, but break rare words into smaller pieces that the model has seen before. "Unbelievable" might split into "un" + "believ" + "able." Each piece is meaningful and reusable. "Unbreakable" shares the "un" and "able" pieces. The model learns morphological patterns automatically, without anyone hand-coding rules. Vocabulary sizes are manageable (typically 30,000–50,000 tokens), sequences stay short, and nothing is ever truly unknown.

Byte Pair Encoding (BPE)

Byte Pair Encoding was originally a data compression algorithm from 1994, repurposed for NLP by Sennrich, Haddow, and Birch in 2016. The core idea is elegant: start with individual characters, then iteratively merge the most frequent adjacent pair into a new token. Repeat until you hit your desired vocabulary size. That's it.

A Worked Example

Let's walk through BPE step by step. Suppose our entire training corpus is four words: "low", "lower", "newest", "widest". First, we split every word into characters and add a special end-of-word symbol _. We also note how many times each word appeared (let's say once each for simplicity):

# Initial vocabulary: individual characters + end-of-word marker
# Word frequencies (each appears once in our tiny corpus)

"low"    → l o w _
"lower"  → l o w e r _
"newest" → n e w e s t _
"widest" → w i d e s t _

Now we count every adjacent pair across all words. The pair ("e", "s") appears twice — once in "newest" and once in "widest." The pair ("s", "t") also appears twice. The pair ("l", "o") appears twice. Let's say we break ties alphabetically and merge ("e", "s") first.

# Merge 1: ("e", "s") → "es"

"low"    → l o w _
"lower"  → l o w e r _
"newest" → n e w es t _
"widest" → w i d es t _

Now we recount. The pair ("es", "t") appears twice (in "newest" and "widest"). Merge it.

# Merge 2: ("es", "t") → "est"

"low"    → l o w _
"lower"  → l o w e r _
"newest" → n e w est _
"widest" → w i d est _

Recount again. ("l", "o") appears twice ("low" and "lower"). Merge it.

# Merge 3: ("l", "o") → "lo"

"low"    → lo w _
"lower"  → lo w e r _
"newest" → n e w est _
"widest" → w i d est _

Now ("lo", "w") appears twice. Merge it.

# Merge 4: ("lo", "w") → "low"

"low"    → low _
"lower"  → low e r _
"newest" → n e w est _
"widest" → w i d est _

After just four merges, "low" is a single token. "Lower" is "low" + "e" + "r" + "_". The model now knows that "lower" contains "low" — it learned a morphological relationship purely from frequency statistics. If we continued merging, "low_" would fuse next, then perhaps "est_", and so on.

The number of merges is a hyperparameter that directly controls vocabulary size. Start with ~256 base characters, add one new token per merge. 32,000 merges gives you roughly 32,256 tokens. GPT-2 uses about 50,257 tokens. GPT-4 uses around 100,000. More tokens means shorter sequences but a larger embedding table.

BPE in Practice

BPE is the tokenization method behind GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4, LLaMA, and most modern large language models. It's also the standard in neural machine translation. The HuggingFace tokenizers library makes it straightforward to train one from scratch:

from tokenizers import Tokenizer
from tokenizers.models import BPE
from tokenizers.trainers import BpeTrainer
from tokenizers.pre_tokenizers import Whitespace

# Initialize a BPE tokenizer
tokenizer = Tokenizer(BPE(unk_token="[UNK]"))
tokenizer.pre_tokenizer = Whitespace()

# Configure training
trainer = BpeTrainer(
    vocab_size=8000,
    special_tokens=["[UNK]", "[PAD]", "[CLS]", "[SEP]", "[MASK]"],
    min_frequency=2,          # ignore pairs that appear only once
    show_progress=True
)

# Train on your corpus (list of file paths)
tokenizer.train(["my_corpus.txt"], trainer)

# Test it
output = tokenizer.encode("unbelievably newest tokenization")
print(output.tokens)
# Might produce: ['un', 'believ', 'ably', 'new', 'est', 'token', 'ization']

tokenizer.save("my_bpe_tokenizer.json")
Why Merge Order Matters at Inference

During tokenization of new text, BPE replays the merge rules in the exact order they were learned during training. It doesn't just greedily find the longest match. The merge priority list is the tokenizer's "memory." This is why you must always pair a model with its exact tokenizer — using a different merge table produces completely different token IDs, and the model's embeddings become meaningless.

WordPiece

WordPiece was introduced by Schuster and Nakajima in 2012 for Japanese and Korean speech processing, and later became famous as BERT's tokenizer. It looks similar to BPE at first glance — start with characters, iteratively merge pairs — but the merge criterion is different. BPE merges the most frequent pair. WordPiece merges the pair that maximizes the likelihood of the training data.

Concretely, WordPiece scores each candidate merge by how much it increases the language model's probability of the corpus. If tokens "a" and "b" appear often individually but rarely together, merging them doesn't help much. If they appear often together relative to their individual frequencies, the merge is valuable. This is similar to pointwise mutual information. The result is a vocabulary that's slightly more "linguistically aware" than BPE — it favors merges that capture genuine sub-word units rather than just frequent collocations.

The ## Convention

WordPiece uses a special prefix — ## — to mark tokens that are continuations of a previous token (i.e., they don't start a new word). The first piece of any word has no prefix. All subsequent pieces get ##. This makes it trivial to reconstruct the original text: strip ## and join.

For example, BERT tokenizes "unbelievable" like this:

# BERT's WordPiece tokenization of "unbelievable"
from transformers import BertTokenizer

tokenizer = BertTokenizer.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
tokens = tokenizer.tokenize("unbelievable")
print(tokens)
# ['un', '##bel', '##ie', '##va', '##ble']

# Another example
print(tokenizer.tokenize("tokenization is fascinating"))
# ['token', '##ization', 'is', 'fascinating']

# Common words stay whole
print(tokenizer.tokenize("the cat sat on the mat"))
# ['the', 'cat', 'sat', 'on', 'the', 'mat']

Notice how "the", "cat", "is" — common words — survive as single tokens. "Unbelievable" breaks into pieces, but each piece carries meaning. "un" is a negation prefix the model sees in thousands of words. "##ble" is a suffix shared by "readable", "capable", "comfortable." The model implicitly learns morphology without any explicit linguistic rules.

Unigram Language Model

The Unigram approach, introduced by Taku Kudo in 2018, works in the opposite direction from BPE. Instead of starting small and building up, you start with a very large initial vocabulary — say, all substrings up to a certain length plus all individual characters — and iteratively remove tokens that contribute least to the overall corpus likelihood.

Each token in the vocabulary has an associated probability (trained via expectation-maximization). Given a sentence, there are often multiple valid ways to segment it into tokens. The Unigram model picks the segmentation that maximizes the total log-probability. For "unbreakable", possible segmentations include ["un", "break", "able"], ["unbreak", "able"], ["u", "n", "b", "r", "e", "a", "k", "a", "b", "l", "e"], and many more. Each segmentation has a total probability (product of individual token probabilities), and the model picks the highest one.

This multiplicity of segmentations is actually useful. During training, you can sample different segmentations of the same word — a technique called subword regularization. Sometimes "unbreakable" segments as ["un", "break", "able"], sometimes as ["unbreak", "able"]. This acts as data augmentation, making the model more robust to different tokenizations at test time. BPE and WordPiece, by contrast, always produce the same deterministic segmentation.

SentencePiece

SentencePiece, also by Kudo, is a language-agnostic tokenization library that implements both Unigram and BPE. Its key innovation: it treats the input as a raw byte stream rather than a sequence of pre-segmented words. Most tokenizers assume you've already split text on whitespace — SentencePiece doesn't. It treats spaces as just another character (represented as the Unicode character ▁, "lower one eighth block"). This means it works identically on languages like Chinese and Japanese that don't use spaces, languages like English that do, and everything in between.

SentencePiece is the tokenizer behind T5, ALBERT, XLNet, and many multilingual models. The "no pre-tokenization" design is especially important for multilingual models — you don't need language-specific rules for word boundaries.

Byte-Level BPE

GPT-2 introduced a clever twist: Byte-level BPE. Instead of starting with Unicode characters (of which there are ~150,000), you start with the 256 raw UTF-8 byte values. Every possible text — English, Chinese, Arabic, emoji, even binary data — is first converted to a sequence of bytes, and BPE merges operate on those bytes.

The result: there is no unknown token. Ever. Any byte sequence is representable, because the base vocabulary covers every possible byte. This eliminates the [UNK] problem completely. You never need to worry about out-of-vocabulary words, unseen scripts, or unexpected Unicode characters. GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4, and many other models use this approach.

The tradeoff is efficiency. A common English word like "the" is 3 bytes, and the BPE merge table will quickly learn to represent it as a single token. But a Chinese character in UTF-8 is 3 bytes, and a rare emoji can be 4 bytes. If the merge table hasn't seen a particular character often enough to merge its bytes, that single character might consume 3–4 tokens instead of 1. In practice, byte-level BPE tokenizers use about 1.3–1.5x more tokens for non-Latin scripts compared to a dedicated tokenizer for that language.

The Hidden Cost of Tokenization

Tokenizer choice affects cost in production. If your tokenizer takes 1.5x more tokens to encode Hindi text compared to English, Hindi users effectively get 1.5x less context window and pay 1.5x more per API call. This is a real equity issue in LLM deployment. Some newer models (like Gemma and LLaMA 3) use larger vocabularies (128k–256k tokens) specifically to improve efficiency on non-English languages.

Tokenizer Training

Vocabulary Size Tradeoffs

Vocabulary size is one of those hyperparameters that quietly affects everything. Too small (say, 4,000 tokens) and common words get split into many pieces — "understanding" might become five tokens. Sequences get long, the model needs more compute per sentence, and the effective context window shrinks. Too large (say, 500,000 tokens) and the embedding table becomes enormous. Each token has a dense vector (768 or 4,096 dimensions), so 500k tokens means billions of parameters just in the embedding layer. Worse, rare tokens in a huge vocabulary get very few training examples, so their embeddings are undertrained and noisy.

The sweet spot for most English-centric models has been 30,000–50,000 tokens. Multilingual models push higher: 100,000–256,000 tokens, because they need to efficiently represent multiple scripts. The trend in 2023–2024 is toward larger vocabularies as model sizes grow — the relative cost of a bigger embedding table shrinks when the rest of the model has 70 billion parameters.

Domain-Specific Tokenizers

A tokenizer trained on Wikipedia and web text does a poor job on code. The identifier getAllUsersByOrganizationId might split into 8 tokens when a code-aware tokenizer could handle it in 3. Biomedical text is full of terms like "deoxyribonucleic" that a general tokenizer shreds into tiny pieces. Legal documents repeat long Latin phrases that a general tokenizer handles inefficiently.

Training a domain-specific tokenizer is straightforward — you just train BPE or Unigram on your domain corpus. The HuggingFace tokenizers library handles all of it. But there's a catch: if you change the tokenizer, you must retrain (or at least fine-tune) the model, because the embedding layer maps token IDs to vectors. Swapping tokenizers without retraining is like shuffling the labels on a filing cabinet — the contents no longer match the index.

A pragmatic middle ground is to start from a pretrained model and extend its tokenizer with domain-specific tokens. Add new tokens to the vocabulary, initialize their embeddings (often as the mean of their sub-token embeddings), and fine-tune. This preserves most of the pretrained knowledge while improving efficiency on domain text.

Comparison

Here's how the four approaches stack up side by side:

Method Direction Merge Criterion Used By Key Trait
BPE Bottom-up (merge) Most frequent pair GPT-2/3/4, LLaMA, most LLMs Simple, deterministic, widely adopted
WordPiece Bottom-up (merge) Max likelihood gain BERT, DistilBERT, Electra ## prefix for continuation tokens
Unigram Top-down (prune) Remove least useful token T5, ALBERT, XLNet (via SentencePiece) Multiple segmentations; subword regularization
Byte-level BPE Bottom-up (merge) Most frequent byte pair GPT-2/3/4, RoBERTa No [UNK] tokens ever; any input representable

In practice, the differences between these methods matter less than you might expect. With the same vocabulary size and training data, BPE, WordPiece, and Unigram produce surprisingly similar downstream performance. The bigger decisions are vocabulary size, training corpus composition, and whether to go byte-level. If you're starting from scratch, BPE with a byte-level fallback (as in GPT-2) is the safest default. If you're using an existing pretrained model, just use whatever tokenizer it shipped with — consistency is more important than optimality.
