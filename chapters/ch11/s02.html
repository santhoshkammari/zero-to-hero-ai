Embeddings & Tokenization

Chapter 11: Natural Language Processing Word Embeddings · Subword Tokenization
Section Overview

This section combines Word Embeddings and Subword Tokenization into one comprehensive reference.

Word Embeddings

TL;DR

Words need to become numbers before any model can touch them. One-hot vectors are wasteful and blind to meaning. Word embeddings map each word to a dense, low-dimensional vector where similar words land near each other. Word2Vec learns embeddings by predicting context words (or vice versa), using negative sampling to make training feasible. GloVe achieves similar results by factorizing a global co-occurrence matrix. FastText goes further by representing words as bags of character n-grams, handling misspellings and rare words gracefully. All static embeddings share one flaw: a single vector per word, regardless of context. ELMo fixed this with bidirectional LSTMs, paving the road to transformers.

Why Embeddings?

Before we can do anything interesting with text — classify it, translate it, generate it — we need to turn words into numbers. The simplest approach is a one-hot vector: if your vocabulary has 50,000 words, each word becomes a vector of length 50,000 with a single 1 and 49,999 zeros. "cat" might be [0, 0, ..., 1, ..., 0] and "dog" might be [0, 0, ..., 0, 1, ..., 0].

This has three devastating problems. First, the vectors are enormous. A vocabulary of 100,000 words means each word is a 100,000-dimensional vector. Multiply that by the number of words in your dataset, and you've got a storage nightmare. Second, the vectors are sparse — almost entirely zeros. That's wasteful for computation. Third, and most critically, every pair of one-hot vectors is equally distant from every other pair. The dot product of "cat" and "dog" is zero. The dot product of "cat" and "refrigerator" is also zero. The representation carries no information about meaning, similarity, or relationships between words.

What we want instead is a representation where "cat" and "dog" are close together (both animals, both pets), "king" and "queen" are close (both royalty), and "king" and "banana" are far apart. We want geometry to reflect semantics. That's what word embeddings give us: dense, low-dimensional vectors (typically 50–300 dimensions) where proximity encodes similarity. The question is how to learn them.

Word2Vec

The Core Idea

In 2013, Tomas Mikolov and colleagues at Google published a paper that changed NLP. The insight behind Word2Vec was borrowed from linguistics: "you shall know a word by the company it keeps" (J.R. Firth, 1957). If two words consistently appear in similar contexts — surrounded by the same neighboring words — they probably mean similar things. "Cat sat on the mat" and "dog sat on the rug" tell us that "cat" and "dog" play similar roles.

Word2Vec doesn't parse grammar or look up definitions. It just slides a window across massive amounts of text and learns to predict which words appear near which. The result is a set of vectors that capture remarkable semantic relationships, all from raw co-occurrence statistics.

Skip-gram

The skip-gram model works like this: pick a target word from your text, then try to predict the words that surround it within a fixed window. Suppose your sentence is "the cat sat on the mat" and your window size is 2. If the target word is "sat," the context words are "the," "cat," "on," and "the." The model's job is: given "sat," predict those four neighbors.

Internally, the model has two weight matrices. The first maps each word to its embedding vector (the one we actually want). The second maps from embedding space back to vocabulary space to produce predictions. During training, you feed in "sat," look up its embedding, and use it to predict each context word in turn. The loss function measures how well the model predicts the actual context words versus random words. Gradients flow back and adjust the embedding, nudging "sat" closer to words it co-occurs with.

You slide this window across billions of words of text. After enough iterations, words that appear in similar contexts end up with similar vectors — not because anyone told the model about meaning, but because the prediction task forced it to organize words by their distributional patterns.

CBOW

Continuous Bag of Words (CBOW) is the mirror image of skip-gram. Instead of predicting context from a target, you predict the target from its context. Given "the," "cat," "on," "the," predict "sat." The context word embeddings are averaged (or summed) into a single vector, and that vector is used to predict the center word.

CBOW trains faster than skip-gram because it processes one prediction per window position instead of several. Skip-gram, on the other hand, tends to do better on rare words because each word gets more training signal (it appears as both a target and a context word for its neighbors). In practice, skip-gram is more commonly used, but the quality difference is often small.

The Softmax Problem and Negative Sampling

Here's the practical problem. To predict a context word, the model needs to produce a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary. That means computing a softmax over 100,000+ words for every single training example. The denominator of the softmax — the sum of exponentials across the whole vocabulary — is brutally expensive.

Negative sampling sidesteps this entirely. Instead of asking "what's the probability of each word in the vocabulary?", it asks a simpler question: "is this word-context pair real, or did I make it up?" For each real pair (like "sat" and "cat"), you sample k random words from the vocabulary (say, k = 5–15) that probably didn't appear in that context. Then you train a binary classifier to distinguish real pairs from fake ones. The loss for a single training example is:

# Negative sampling objective (simplified)
# For a real (target, context) pair and k negative samples:
#
# L = -log(sigmoid(v_context . v_target))
#     - sum_{i=1}^{k} log(sigmoid(-v_neg_i . v_target))
#
# v_context . v_target should be LARGE  (real pair → sigmoid → 1)
# v_neg_i   . v_target should be SMALL  (fake pair → sigmoid → 0)

This reduces each training step from O(V) to O(k), where V is vocabulary size and k is typically 5–15. The negative words are sampled proportional to their frequency raised to the 3/4 power — this slightly oversamples rare words so they get enough training signal. Hierarchical softmax is another trick that arranges the vocabulary in a binary tree and only computes log(V) probabilities per step, but negative sampling is simpler and more widely used.

One more trick: subsampling frequent words. Words like "the," "a," and "is" appear so often that they dominate the training data without contributing much information. Word2Vec randomly discards frequent words during training with a probability that increases with word frequency. This speeds up training and, counterintuitively, improves embedding quality because the remaining training pairs are more informative.

The King–Queen Analogy

The most famous result from Word2Vec is the analogy task. Compute vec("king") - vec("man") + vec("woman") and the closest vector in the embedding space is vec("queen"). The vector arithmetic captures the relationship: "king" is to "man" as "queen" is to "woman." The difference vector vec("king") - vec("man") encodes something like "royalty," and adding it to vec("woman") gives you a female royal.

from gensim.models import KeyedVectors

# Load pretrained Word2Vec embeddings (Google News, 300d)
wv = KeyedVectors.load_word2vec_format(
    "GoogleNews-vectors-negative300.bin", binary=True
)

# The classic analogy: king - man + woman ≈ queen
result = wv.most_similar(positive=["king", "woman"], negative=["man"], topn=3)
for word, score in result:
    print(f"  {word}: {score:.4f}")
# Output (typical):
#   queen: 0.7118
#   monarch: 0.6189
#   princess: 0.5902

# More analogies
print(wv.most_similar(positive=["paris", "germany"], negative=["france"], topn=1))
# [('berlin', 0.7327)]

print(wv.most_similar(positive=["bigger", "cold"], negative=["big"], topn=1))
# [('colder', 0.7254)]

This is impressive, but let's be honest about the limitations. The king–queen analogy works because gender is a prominent, consistent axis in the training data. Many other analogies fail silently. "doctor" minus "man" plus "woman" often returns "nurse" — not because that's the correct analogy, but because the training corpus reflects societal biases. The embeddings have absorbed stereotypes from the text they were trained on.

Bias in Word Embeddings

Word embeddings are a mirror of the data they were trained on, including its prejudices. Bolukbasi et al. (2016) showed that Word2Vec embeddings associate "programmer" and "engineer" with male names, and "homemaker" and "nurse" with female names. Debiasing techniques exist — projecting out the gender subspace, for example — but they're imperfect and sometimes just hide the bias rather than removing it. If you deploy embeddings in a production system, audit them for harmful associations. This is not optional.

GloVe

Global Co-occurrence

GloVe (Global Vectors for Word Representation), developed by Pennington, Socher, and Manning at Stanford in 2014, takes a different angle. Instead of sliding a window across text and learning from local predictions, GloVe first builds a co-occurrence matrix: a giant table where entry (i, j) counts how often word i appears near word j across the entire corpus.

Then it factorizes that matrix. The goal is to learn vectors w_i and w_j such that their dot product approximates the logarithm of the co-occurrence count: w_i · w_j + b_i + b_j ≈ log(X_ij), where X_ij is the co-occurrence count. The loss function weights each entry by a function of X_ij so that very frequent co-occurrences don't dominate and very rare ones don't add noise.

GloVe vs. Word2Vec

The key difference is philosophical. Word2Vec learns from local context windows — it never explicitly constructs a global view of co-occurrence. GloVe starts with the global picture and factorizes it. In theory, GloVe captures long-range statistics that a fixed-size window might miss.

In practice? The results are remarkably similar. Multiple studies have shown that well-tuned Word2Vec and well-tuned GloVe produce embeddings of comparable quality on most benchmarks. GloVe sometimes wins on analogy tasks; Word2Vec sometimes wins on similarity tasks. The choice often comes down to which pretrained embeddings are available for your language and domain, not which algorithm is inherently better.

FastText

Subword Information

Both Word2Vec and GloVe treat each word as an atomic unit. The word "running" has no relationship to "run" in the model's eyes — it's just another entry in the vocabulary. If a word didn't appear in the training data, you're out of luck. It gets no vector at all.

FastText, published by Bojanowski et al. at Facebook AI Research in 2017, fixes this by representing each word as a bag of character n-grams. The word "where" is decomposed into its character n-grams (with special boundary markers): <wh, whe, her, ere, re>, plus the whole word <where>. Each n-gram gets its own vector, and the word's embedding is the sum of all its n-gram vectors.

This has two massive advantages. First, it handles out-of-vocabulary words. Even if "unfriendliness" never appeared in training, FastText can construct a reasonable vector from its character n-grams — many of which did appear in other words like "unfriendly," "friendliness," and "kindness." Second, it naturally captures morphology. Words that share prefixes or suffixes (like "running," "runner," "runs") will share n-grams and therefore have similar embeddings. This makes FastText particularly powerful for morphologically rich languages like Turkish, Finnish, and German, where a single root can generate dozens of word forms that Word2Vec would treat as completely unrelated.

The training procedure is essentially the same as Word2Vec's skip-gram with negative sampling — the only difference is how word vectors are computed (sum of n-gram vectors instead of a single lookup).

Choosing Between Word2Vec, GloVe, and FastText

If your text is clean, your vocabulary is well-covered, and you just need solid general-purpose embeddings, any of the three will work. Use GloVe or Word2Vec pretrained vectors for a quick start. Use FastText when your data contains misspellings, slang, domain-specific jargon, or when you're working with a morphologically rich language. FastText is also the best choice when your vocabulary is open-ended — user-generated content, medical terminology, code identifiers — because it can always construct a vector, even for words it's never seen.

Embedding Properties

Dimensionality

How many dimensions should your embedding vectors have? Common choices are 50, 100, 200, and 300. Smaller dimensions train faster and use less memory, but may not capture enough semantic distinctions. Larger dimensions can represent finer-grained relationships but risk overfitting on small datasets and show diminishing returns past 300 dimensions.

A practical rule of thumb: 100–300 dimensions work well for most English NLP tasks. If your vocabulary is small or your downstream task is simple (sentiment classification, for example), 50 dimensions might suffice. If you're doing fine-grained semantic work on a large corpus, 300 is a good ceiling. Going to 500 or 1000 rarely helps and often hurts.

Cosine Similarity

Once you have embeddings, you need a way to measure how similar two words are. The standard choice is cosine similarity: the cosine of the angle between two vectors. It ranges from -1 (opposite directions) to 1 (same direction), with 0 meaning orthogonal (unrelated). The formula is cos(a, b) = (a · b) / (||a|| × ||b||).

Why cosine instead of Euclidean distance? Because cosine ignores vector magnitude and focuses on direction. Two vectors that point the same way but have different lengths will have cosine similarity 1.0. This is desirable because embedding magnitude often reflects word frequency rather than meaning — common words tend to have larger norms. Cosine factors that out.

import numpy as np
from gensim.models import KeyedVectors

# Load pretrained GloVe vectors (converted to word2vec format)
wv = KeyedVectors.load_word2vec_format("glove.6B.100d.word2vec.txt")

# Cosine similarity between word pairs
pairs = [
    ("cat", "dog"),        # both animals → high similarity
    ("cat", "computer"),   # unrelated → low similarity
    ("good", "great"),     # synonyms → high similarity
    ("good", "bad"),       # antonyms → moderate (both adjectives!)
]

for w1, w2 in pairs:
    sim = wv.similarity(w1, w2)
    print(f"  sim({w1}, {w2}) = {sim:.4f}")
# Typical output:
#   sim(cat, dog) = 0.8798
#   sim(cat, computer) = 0.1820
#   sim(good, great) = 0.7192
#   sim(good, bad) = 0.5437  ← surprisingly high!

# Note: "good" and "bad" are similar because they appear
# in identical contexts ("the food was ___"). Embeddings
# capture distributional similarity, not sentiment polarity.

That last result — "good" and "bad" having moderate similarity — trips people up. But it makes sense. Distributional similarity means "used in similar contexts," and antonyms are often used interchangeably in the same syntactic slot. Embeddings don't distinguish "similar meaning" from "similar usage." Keep this in mind when using embeddings for sentiment analysis.

Contextual Embeddings: ELMo

The Problem with Static Embeddings

Every method we've discussed so far — Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText — assigns exactly one vector per word type. The word "bank" gets the same embedding whether it means a financial institution or the side of a river. The word "play" gets the same vector whether it's a noun (a theater play) or a verb (play a game). This is a fundamental limitation. Language is full of polysemy (multiple meanings) and the correct meaning depends entirely on context.

For many tasks, this limitation is tolerable. If most occurrences of "bank" in your dataset refer to financial institutions, the embedding will capture that dominant sense reasonably well. But for tasks that require precise word-sense disambiguation, static embeddings hit a ceiling.

ELMo: Embeddings from Language Models

ELMo (Embeddings from Language Models), published by Peters et al. in 2018, broke through this ceiling. Instead of a fixed lookup table, ELMo runs the input sentence through a deep bidirectional LSTM and uses the internal states as word representations. Because the LSTM reads the entire sentence, each word's representation is influenced by all other words in the sentence. The word "bank" in "I deposited money at the bank" gets a different vector than "bank" in "I sat on the river bank."

ELMo's architecture is a two-layer bidirectional LSTM trained on a language modeling objective — predict the next word (forward) and the previous word (backward). At inference time, for each word, ELMo produces three vectors: one from the character-level CNN (capturing morphology), one from the first LSTM layer (capturing syntax), and one from the second LSTM layer (capturing semantics). The final embedding is a learned weighted sum of all three.

Layer-wise Representations

This layer-wise structure turned out to be important. Researchers found that lower layers capture syntactic information — part-of-speech, syntactic roles — while higher layers capture semantic information — word sense, sentiment. Different downstream tasks benefit from different layers. A POS tagger works best with lower-layer representations; a sentiment classifier works best with higher-layer representations. ELMo's learned weighting lets each task find its own optimal combination.

ELMo improved the state of the art on six NLP benchmarks the day it was published. It was a proof of concept for a transformative idea: don't learn fixed word vectors — learn a function that produces word vectors conditioned on context. This idea is the direct ancestor of BERT, GPT, and every transformer-based language model that followed. Those models replaced ELMo's LSTMs with self-attention (which we'll cover in the chapter on Transformers), but the core principle — contextual representations — was ELMo's contribution.

Static vs. Contextual: When to Use What

Static embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText) are still useful. They're fast, lightweight, and require no GPU at inference time. For simple tasks like document similarity, keyword clustering, or information retrieval on constrained hardware, they're often the right choice. Contextual embeddings (ELMo, BERT) shine on tasks that require understanding nuance: question answering, named entity recognition in ambiguous contexts, and anything where word sense matters. The tradeoff is always compute cost and latency versus accuracy.

From one-hot vectors to Word2Vec to ELMo, each step added more intelligence to how we represent words. One-hot vectors know nothing about meaning. Word2Vec captures similarity from co-occurrence. ELMo captures context-dependent meaning by reading entire sentences. The next leap — transformers and self-attention — will take this idea even further, but that's a story for a later chapter.

Subword Tokenization

TL;DR

Word-level tokenization chokes on rare words. Character-level tokenization makes sequences painfully long. Subword tokenization splits the difference — common words stay whole, rare words get broken into reusable pieces. BPE (used in GPT) merges frequent character pairs bottom-up. WordPiece (used in BERT) merges by likelihood gain. Unigram (used in T5 via SentencePiece) starts big and prunes down. Byte-level BPE eliminates unknown tokens entirely by operating on raw bytes. The choice of tokenizer and vocabulary size quietly shapes everything downstream.

The Vocabulary Problem

Before a neural network can process text, every word must become a number. The simplest approach is word-level tokenization: assign an integer to each unique word in your training corpus. "The" gets 1, "cat" gets 2, "sat" gets 3, and so on. This works until you encounter a word the model has never seen. Maybe someone writes "catsat" by accident. Maybe a user types "pretraining" and your corpus only had "pre-training." Maybe you're processing German, where compound words like "Handschuhschneeballschlacht" (a snowball fight with gloves) are perfectly normal. Every unseen word collapses into a single [UNK] token. The model learns nothing from it.

The scale of this problem is brutal. English has over a million distinct word forms if you count inflections, derivations, and compound words. A vocabulary of 50,000 words — already large — still misses a huge fraction of them. Misspellings, brand names, code identifiers, technical jargon, and morphological variants all become [UNK]. In some domains, 10–15% of tokens end up unknown. That's like reading a book with every seventh word blacked out.

The opposite extreme is character-level tokenization. Every character — "a", "b", "c", space, punctuation — gets its own token. Now nothing is unknown. The vocabulary is tiny (a few hundred entries). But the sequences become absurdly long. The sentence "The cat sat" is 11 characters instead of 3 words. Transformers have a fixed context window, so you've just cut your effective capacity by roughly 4x. Worse, the model must learn long-range dependencies across many more positions. Learning that "c-a-t" means the same animal whether it appears at position 5 or position 500 is hard when each letter is a separate step.

Subword tokenization is the sweet spot. The idea: keep common words intact, but break rare words into smaller pieces that the model has seen before. "Unbelievable" might split into "un" + "believ" + "able." Each piece is meaningful and reusable. "Unbreakable" shares the "un" and "able" pieces. The model learns morphological patterns automatically, without anyone hand-coding rules. Vocabulary sizes are manageable (typically 30,000–50,000 tokens), sequences stay short, and nothing is ever truly unknown.

Byte Pair Encoding (BPE)

Byte Pair Encoding was originally a data compression algorithm from 1994, repurposed for NLP by Sennrich, Haddow, and Birch in 2016. The core idea is elegant: start with individual characters, then iteratively merge the most frequent adjacent pair into a new token. Repeat until you hit your desired vocabulary size. That's it.

A Worked Example

Let's walk through BPE step by step. Suppose our entire training corpus is four words: "low", "lower", "newest", "widest". First, we split every word into characters and add a special end-of-word symbol _. We also note how many times each word appeared (let's say once each for simplicity):

# Initial vocabulary: individual characters + end-of-word marker
# Word frequencies (each appears once in our tiny corpus)

"low"    → l o w _
"lower"  → l o w e r _
"newest" → n e w e s t _
"widest" → w i d e s t _

Now we count every adjacent pair across all words. The pair ("e", "s") appears twice — once in "newest" and once in "widest." The pair ("s", "t") also appears twice. The pair ("l", "o") appears twice. Let's say we break ties alphabetically and merge ("e", "s") first.

# Merge 1: ("e", "s") → "es"

"low"    → l o w _
"lower"  → l o w e r _
"newest" → n e w es t _
"widest" → w i d es t _

Now we recount. The pair ("es", "t") appears twice (in "newest" and "widest"). Merge it.

# Merge 2: ("es", "t") → "est"

"low"    → l o w _
"lower"  → l o w e r _
"newest" → n e w est _
"widest" → w i d est _

Recount again. ("l", "o") appears twice ("low" and "lower"). Merge it.

# Merge 3: ("l", "o") → "lo"

"low"    → lo w _
"lower"  → lo w e r _
"newest" → n e w est _
"widest" → w i d est _

Now ("lo", "w") appears twice. Merge it.

# Merge 4: ("lo", "w") → "low"

"low"    → low _
"lower"  → low e r _
"newest" → n e w est _
"widest" → w i d est _

After just four merges, "low" is a single token. "Lower" is "low" + "e" + "r" + "_". The model now knows that "lower" contains "low" — it learned a morphological relationship purely from frequency statistics. If we continued merging, "low_" would fuse next, then perhaps "est_", and so on.

The number of merges is a hyperparameter that directly controls vocabulary size. Start with ~256 base characters, add one new token per merge. 32,000 merges gives you roughly 32,256 tokens. GPT-2 uses about 50,257 tokens. GPT-4 uses around 100,000. More tokens means shorter sequences but a larger embedding table.

BPE in Practice

BPE is the tokenization method behind GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4, LLaMA, and most modern large language models. It's also the standard in neural machine translation. The HuggingFace tokenizers library makes it straightforward to train one from scratch:

from tokenizers import Tokenizer
from tokenizers.models import BPE
from tokenizers.trainers import BpeTrainer
from tokenizers.pre_tokenizers import Whitespace

# Initialize a BPE tokenizer
tokenizer = Tokenizer(BPE(unk_token="[UNK]"))
tokenizer.pre_tokenizer = Whitespace()

# Configure training
trainer = BpeTrainer(
    vocab_size=8000,
    special_tokens=["[UNK]", "[PAD]", "[CLS]", "[SEP]", "[MASK]"],
    min_frequency=2,          # ignore pairs that appear only once
    show_progress=True
)

# Train on your corpus (list of file paths)
tokenizer.train(["my_corpus.txt"], trainer)

# Test it
output = tokenizer.encode("unbelievably newest tokenization")
print(output.tokens)
# Might produce: ['un', 'believ', 'ably', 'new', 'est', 'token', 'ization']

tokenizer.save("my_bpe_tokenizer.json")
Why Merge Order Matters at Inference

During tokenization of new text, BPE replays the merge rules in the exact order they were learned during training. It doesn't just greedily find the longest match. The merge priority list is the tokenizer's "memory." This is why you must always pair a model with its exact tokenizer — using a different merge table produces completely different token IDs, and the model's embeddings become meaningless.

WordPiece

WordPiece was introduced by Schuster and Nakajima in 2012 for Japanese and Korean speech processing, and later became famous as BERT's tokenizer. It looks similar to BPE at first glance — start with characters, iteratively merge pairs — but the merge criterion is different. BPE merges the most frequent pair. WordPiece merges the pair that maximizes the likelihood of the training data.

Concretely, WordPiece scores each candidate merge by how much it increases the language model's probability of the corpus. If tokens "a" and "b" appear often individually but rarely together, merging them doesn't help much. If they appear often together relative to their individual frequencies, the merge is valuable. This is similar to pointwise mutual information. The result is a vocabulary that's slightly more "linguistically aware" than BPE — it favors merges that capture genuine sub-word units rather than just frequent collocations.

The ## Convention

WordPiece uses a special prefix — ## — to mark tokens that are continuations of a previous token (i.e., they don't start a new word). The first piece of any word has no prefix. All subsequent pieces get ##. This makes it trivial to reconstruct the original text: strip ## and join.

For example, BERT tokenizes "unbelievable" like this:

# BERT's WordPiece tokenization of "unbelievable"
from transformers import BertTokenizer

tokenizer = BertTokenizer.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
tokens = tokenizer.tokenize("unbelievable")
print(tokens)
# ['un', '##bel', '##ie', '##va', '##ble']

# Another example
print(tokenizer.tokenize("tokenization is fascinating"))
# ['token', '##ization', 'is', 'fascinating']

# Common words stay whole
print(tokenizer.tokenize("the cat sat on the mat"))
# ['the', 'cat', 'sat', 'on', 'the', 'mat']

Notice how "the", "cat", "is" — common words — survive as single tokens. "Unbelievable" breaks into pieces, but each piece carries meaning. "un" is a negation prefix the model sees in thousands of words. "##ble" is a suffix shared by "readable", "capable", "comfortable." The model implicitly learns morphology without any explicit linguistic rules.

Unigram Language Model

The Unigram approach, introduced by Taku Kudo in 2018, works in the opposite direction from BPE. Instead of starting small and building up, you start with a very large initial vocabulary — say, all substrings up to a certain length plus all individual characters — and iteratively remove tokens that contribute least to the overall corpus likelihood.

Each token in the vocabulary has an associated probability (trained via expectation-maximization). Given a sentence, there are often multiple valid ways to segment it into tokens. The Unigram model picks the segmentation that maximizes the total log-probability. For "unbreakable", possible segmentations include ["un", "break", "able"], ["unbreak", "able"], ["u", "n", "b", "r", "e", "a", "k", "a", "b", "l", "e"], and many more. Each segmentation has a total probability (product of individual token probabilities), and the model picks the highest one.

This multiplicity of segmentations is actually useful. During training, you can sample different segmentations of the same word — a technique called subword regularization. Sometimes "unbreakable" segments as ["un", "break", "able"], sometimes as ["unbreak", "able"]. This acts as data augmentation, making the model more robust to different tokenizations at test time. BPE and WordPiece, by contrast, always produce the same deterministic segmentation.

SentencePiece

SentencePiece, also by Kudo, is a language-agnostic tokenization library that implements both Unigram and BPE. Its key innovation: it treats the input as a raw byte stream rather than a sequence of pre-segmented words. Most tokenizers assume you've already split text on whitespace — SentencePiece doesn't. It treats spaces as just another character (represented as the Unicode character ▁, "lower one eighth block"). This means it works identically on languages like Chinese and Japanese that don't use spaces, languages like English that do, and everything in between.

SentencePiece is the tokenizer behind T5, ALBERT, XLNet, and many multilingual models. The "no pre-tokenization" design is especially important for multilingual models — you don't need language-specific rules for word boundaries.

Byte-Level BPE

GPT-2 introduced a clever twist: Byte-level BPE. Instead of starting with Unicode characters (of which there are ~150,000), you start with the 256 raw UTF-8 byte values. Every possible text — English, Chinese, Arabic, emoji, even binary data — is first converted to a sequence of bytes, and BPE merges operate on those bytes.

The result: there is no unknown token. Ever. Any byte sequence is representable, because the base vocabulary covers every possible byte. This eliminates the [UNK] problem completely. You never need to worry about out-of-vocabulary words, unseen scripts, or unexpected Unicode characters. GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4, and many other models use this approach.

The tradeoff is efficiency. A common English word like "the" is 3 bytes, and the BPE merge table will quickly learn to represent it as a single token. But a Chinese character in UTF-8 is 3 bytes, and a rare emoji can be 4 bytes. If the merge table hasn't seen a particular character often enough to merge its bytes, that single character might consume 3–4 tokens instead of 1. In practice, byte-level BPE tokenizers use about 1.3–1.5x more tokens for non-Latin scripts compared to a dedicated tokenizer for that language.

The Hidden Cost of Tokenization

Tokenizer choice affects cost in production. If your tokenizer takes 1.5x more tokens to encode Hindi text compared to English, Hindi users effectively get 1.5x less context window and pay 1.5x more per API call. This is a real equity issue in LLM deployment. Some newer models (like Gemma and LLaMA 3) use larger vocabularies (128k–256k tokens) specifically to improve efficiency on non-English languages.

Tokenizer Training

Vocabulary Size Tradeoffs

Vocabulary size is one of those hyperparameters that quietly affects everything. Too small (say, 4,000 tokens) and common words get split into many pieces — "understanding" might become five tokens. Sequences get long, the model needs more compute per sentence, and the effective context window shrinks. Too large (say, 500,000 tokens) and the embedding table becomes enormous. Each token has a dense vector (768 or 4,096 dimensions), so 500k tokens means billions of parameters just in the embedding layer. Worse, rare tokens in a huge vocabulary get very few training examples, so their embeddings are undertrained and noisy.

The sweet spot for most English-centric models has been 30,000–50,000 tokens. Multilingual models push higher: 100,000–256,000 tokens, because they need to efficiently represent multiple scripts. The trend in 2023–2024 is toward larger vocabularies as model sizes grow — the relative cost of a bigger embedding table shrinks when the rest of the model has 70 billion parameters.

Domain-Specific Tokenizers

A tokenizer trained on Wikipedia and web text does a poor job on code. The identifier getAllUsersByOrganizationId might split into 8 tokens when a code-aware tokenizer could handle it in 3. Biomedical text is full of terms like "deoxyribonucleic" that a general tokenizer shreds into tiny pieces. Legal documents repeat long Latin phrases that a general tokenizer handles inefficiently.

Training a domain-specific tokenizer is straightforward — you just train BPE or Unigram on your domain corpus. The HuggingFace tokenizers library handles all of it. But there's a catch: if you change the tokenizer, you must retrain (or at least fine-tune) the model, because the embedding layer maps token IDs to vectors. Swapping tokenizers without retraining is like shuffling the labels on a filing cabinet — the contents no longer match the index.

A pragmatic middle ground is to start from a pretrained model and extend its tokenizer with domain-specific tokens. Add new tokens to the vocabulary, initialize their embeddings (often as the mean of their sub-token embeddings), and fine-tune. This preserves most of the pretrained knowledge while improving efficiency on domain text.

Comparison

Here's how the four approaches stack up side by side:

Method Direction Merge Criterion Used By Key Trait
BPE Bottom-up (merge) Most frequent pair GPT-2/3/4, LLaMA, most LLMs Simple, deterministic, widely adopted
WordPiece Bottom-up (merge) Max likelihood gain BERT, DistilBERT, Electra ## prefix for continuation tokens
Unigram Top-down (prune) Remove least useful token T5, ALBERT, XLNet (via SentencePiece) Multiple segmentations; subword regularization
Byte-level BPE Bottom-up (merge) Most frequent byte pair GPT-2/3/4, RoBERTa No [UNK] tokens ever; any input representable

In practice, the differences between these methods matter less than you might expect. With the same vocabulary size and training data, BPE, WordPiece, and Unigram produce surprisingly similar downstream performance. The bigger decisions are vocabulary size, training corpus composition, and whether to go byte-level. If you're starting from scratch, BPE with a byte-level fallback (as in GPT-2) is the safest default. If you're using an existing pretrained model, just use whatever tokenizer it shipped with — consistency is more important than optimality.
