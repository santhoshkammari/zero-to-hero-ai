BERT Internals

NLP Transformers Pre-training Transfer Learning BERT
TL;DR

BERT is an encoder-only transformer pre-trained with two objectives: predicting masked tokens and guessing whether two sentences are adjacent. Because it reads text in both directions simultaneously, it builds richer representations than left-to-right models like GPT. You pre-train it once on a massive corpus, then fine-tune a thin output layer for classification, named-entity recognition, question answering, or nearly any NLP task. Its 2018 release triggered an avalanche of variants — RoBERTa, ALBERT, DeBERTa — each fixing a specific weakness. Understanding BERT's internals is the fastest way to understand the entire modern NLP stack.

In October 2018, a team at Google published a paper titled "BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding." Within months it shattered records on eleven NLP benchmarks. The reason was simple but profound: previous language models read text left-to-right (like GPT) or right-to-left, but never both at the same time. BERT broke that rule. It looks at the entire sentence at once — left context and right context — when deciding what a word means. That bidirectional trick, combined with a clever training objective and the raw power of the transformer architecture, is what made BERT a turning point. Let's open the hood and see exactly how it works.

Architecture Overview

If you've read about the original transformer, you know it has an encoder and a decoder. BERT throws away the decoder. It is an encoder-only transformer. Why? Because BERT's job during pre-training is not to generate text. Its job is to understand text — to build a rich vector representation for every token in a sentence. Generation requires a decoder that outputs one token at a time. Understanding requires looking at the full input, which is exactly what the encoder does.

BERT comes in two sizes. BERT-base has 12 transformer layers (also called blocks), a hidden size of 768 dimensions, 12 attention heads per layer, and about 110 million parameters total. BERT-large doubles down: 24 layers, 1024-dimensional hidden states, 16 attention heads, and roughly 340 million parameters. BERT-base was designed to match the size of OpenAI's original GPT so the comparison would be fair. BERT-large was designed to show what happens when you scale up. In practice, most people start with BERT-base because it fits on a single GPU and still delivers excellent results.

Each layer is structurally identical: multi-head self-attention, followed by a feed-forward network, with layer normalization and residual connections wrapping both. Nothing exotic. The magic is in how the model is trained, not in the architecture itself.

WordPiece Tokenization

Before any text enters BERT, it has to be broken into tokens. You might think we could just split on spaces and call each word a token. That works until you hit the word "unbelievable" — a word that might appear rarely in your training data but whose parts ("un", "believe", "able") are everywhere. A whole-word vocabulary would need hundreds of thousands of entries to cover English, and it would still choke on rare words, misspellings, and new slang. That's why BERT uses WordPiece tokenization, a subword method.

WordPiece starts with individual characters as its vocabulary. Then it repeatedly merges the most frequent pair of adjacent tokens into a single new token. After enough merges, you end up with a vocabulary of about 30,000 subword units that can represent virtually any English text. Common words like "the" stay as a single token. Rarer words get split. The prefix ## signals a continuation piece — it means "this piece is attached to whatever came before it."

For example, the word "unbelievable" becomes ["un", "##believe", "##able"]. The model has seen each of these pieces thousands of times during training, so it knows what they mean individually and can compose them. Even a completely novel word like "ungooglable" would get split into familiar pieces. This is how BERT handles unknown words gracefully: there is no [UNK] token in practice, because WordPiece can always fall back to individual characters if needed.

Special Tokens: [CLS] and [SEP]

BERT's input isn't just raw tokens. Every input sequence starts with a special [CLS] token (short for "classification"). This token doesn't correspond to any real word. Instead, its final hidden state is used as an aggregate representation of the entire input. When you fine-tune BERT for sentiment analysis, spam detection, or any sentence-level task, you take the vector at the [CLS] position and feed it into a classification head. Think of it as a designated parking spot for "the meaning of the whole sequence."

The second special token is [SEP] (separator). It goes between two sentences when you feed a pair into BERT. Many tasks require reasoning about pairs — natural language inference ("Does sentence B follow from sentence A?"), question answering ("Given this passage, answer this question"), or paraphrase detection. The [SEP] token tells the model where one sentence ends and the next begins.

But BERT goes further. It adds segment embeddings — a learned vector that tells each token whether it belongs to sentence A or sentence B. These are also called token type IDs: 0 for sentence A, 1 for sentence B. On top of that, every token gets a position embedding, because transformers have no built-in sense of order. The final input representation for each token is the sum of three embeddings: Input = Token Embedding + Segment Embedding + Position Embedding. Each of these is a learned 768-dimensional vector (for BERT-base), and their elementwise sum is what actually enters the first transformer layer.

Masked Language Modeling (MLM)

This is the core trick. Traditional language models learn by predicting the next word. But if you read left to right, you can never use the right context to help you understand the left. BERT solves this with Masked Language Modeling. Before feeding a sentence into the model, you randomly select 15% of the tokens and hide them. The model's job is to predict the original token at each masked position, using the full bidirectional context — everything to the left and everything to the right.

There's a subtlety. If you always replaced selected tokens with [MASK], the model would learn to only pay attention when it sees that special token. But during fine-tuning, there are no [MASK] tokens in the input. To bridge this gap, BERT uses the 80/10/10 rule: of the 15% selected tokens, 80% are replaced with [MASK], 10% are replaced with a random token from the vocabulary, and 10% are left unchanged. The random replacement forces the model to maintain a good representation for every token (it can't be sure which ones are corrupted), and keeping some unchanged ensures the model doesn't learn that "correct token = never needs prediction."

Here's a quick example using HuggingFace's transformers library to see MLM in action: 

from transformers import BertTokenizer, BertForMaskedLM
import torch

tokenizer = BertTokenizer.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
model = BertForMaskedLM.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
model.eval()

text = "The capital of France is [MASK]."
inputs = tokenizer(text, return_tensors="pt")

with torch.no_grad():
    outputs = model(**inputs)
    logits = outputs.logits  # shape: (1, seq_len, vocab_size)

# Find the position of [MASK]
mask_index = (inputs["input_ids"] == tokenizer.mask_token_id).nonzero(as_tuple=True)[1]
predicted_id = logits[0, mask_index, :].argmax(dim=-1)
print(tokenizer.decode(predicted_id))  # "paris"

The model confidently predicts "paris" because it has seen enough text during pre-training to associate "capital of France" with "Paris." That knowledge is baked into the weights. No one explicitly told it geography — it learned from context.

Why 15% and Not More?

Masking too many tokens makes the task too hard — the model doesn't have enough context to make reasonable guesses. Masking too few means the model sees very few training signals per sentence, making pre-training slow. The 15% figure is a sweet spot found through experimentation. It gives enough context for accurate prediction while still providing a meaningful learning signal on each pass.

Next Sentence Prediction (NSP)

BERT's second pre-training objective is Next Sentence Prediction. The idea is straightforward: give the model two sentences, and ask it to predict whether sentence B actually follows sentence A in the original document, or whether B is a random sentence pulled from elsewhere. During training, 50% of the pairs are genuine consecutive sentences and 50% are random. The model uses the [CLS] token's representation to make this binary prediction.

The motivation was that many downstream tasks — question answering, natural language inference — require understanding the relationship between two sentences, not just individual sentences. NSP was supposed to teach BERT about inter-sentence coherence.

But here's the honest truth: NSP turned out to be controversial. When the RoBERTa team at Facebook (2019) carefully ablated BERT's design choices, they found that removing NSP actually improved performance on most benchmarks. The problem? The "random sentence" negative examples were too easy. A sentence from a completely different document is obviously unrelated — the model could cheat by just detecting topic mismatch rather than learning genuine discourse structure. Later models like RoBERTa, ALBERT, and most modern variants either drop NSP entirely or replace it with harder objectives.

Fine-Tuning Strategy

The entire point of BERT is the pre-train once, fine-tune many paradigm. Pre-training is extremely expensive — the original BERT-base was trained on 16 TPUs for 4 days on the BooksCorpus and English Wikipedia (about 3.3 billion words). But you only do that once. Fine-tuning is cheap. You take the pre-trained weights, attach a small task-specific output layer, and train for a few epochs on your labeled dataset. The pre-trained layers already understand language; fine-tuning just teaches them your specific task.

For sentence classification (sentiment analysis, spam detection), you take the [CLS] token's final hidden vector and pass it through a single linear layer that maps from 768 dimensions to the number of classes. For token-level tasks like Named Entity Recognition (NER), you take each token's final hidden vector and pass it through a linear layer. Each token gets its own label: is this token a person name, an organization, a location, or nothing? For question answering (SQuAD-style), you feed in a question-passage pair and predict two things: the start position and the end position of the answer span within the passage. Two separate linear layers, each producing a score for every token position.

The typical fine-tuning recipe is remarkably consistent: 2 to 4 epochs, a learning rate between 2e-5 and 5e-5, batch sizes of 16 or 32, and the Adam optimizer with weight decay. Going much higher on the learning rate risks catastrophic forgetting — the model "unlearns" its pre-trained knowledge. 

from transformers import BertForSequenceClassification, BertTokenizer
from torch.optim import AdamW

tokenizer = BertTokenizer.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
model = BertForSequenceClassification.from_pretrained(
    "bert-base-uncased", num_labels=2
)

# Example: fine-tuning on a binary classification task
texts = ["I love this movie", "This was terrible"]
labels = torch.tensor([1, 0])

inputs = tokenizer(texts, padding=True, truncation=True, return_tensors="pt")
inputs["labels"] = labels

optimizer = AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=2e-5, weight_decay=0.01)

model.train()
for epoch in range(3):
    outputs = model(**inputs)
    loss = outputs.loss
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    print(f"Epoch {epoch + 1}, Loss: {loss.item():.4f}")

That's it. In a real scenario you'd use a DataLoader, a validation set, and a learning rate scheduler. But the core loop is exactly this simple. The heavy lifting was already done during pre-training.

Watch the Learning Rate

A learning rate of 2e-5 sounds absurdly small if you're used to training models from scratch. But BERT's weights are already well-initialized. A large learning rate would stomp over the pre-trained representations in a few gradient steps. If your fine-tuning loss drops fast but validation accuracy is terrible, your learning rate is almost certainly too high. Start at 2e-5 and only go up if the model is clearly underfitting.

BERT Variants

BERT spawned an entire family of models, each fixing a specific weakness. Understanding the key variants tells you what BERT got wrong, which is just as instructive as understanding what it got right.

RoBERTa (Robustly Optimized BERT Approach, Facebook 2019) asked a simple question: what if we just trained BERT better? The answer was striking. RoBERTa removed NSP entirely, used dynamic masking (a different random mask for each epoch instead of a fixed mask), trained on 10x more data with larger batch sizes (8K sequences), and trained for much longer. No architectural changes at all — just better training. It outperformed BERT on every benchmark. The lesson: BERT was significantly undertrained.

ALBERT (A Lite BERT, Google 2019) attacked BERT's size. It introduced two ideas. First, factorized embedding parameterization: instead of directly mapping each token to a 768-dimensional vector (30K vocabulary × 768 = 23 million parameters just for embeddings), ALBERT maps tokens to a smaller 128-dimensional space first, then projects up to 768. This cuts embedding parameters by 80%. Second, cross-layer parameter sharing: all 12 transformer layers share the same weights. The model applies the same transformation 12 times. This drastically reduces the parameter count — ALBERT-xxlarge has only 12 million unique parameters compared to BERT-large's 340 million — while maintaining competitive performance by using a much larger hidden size.

DeBERTa (Decoding-enhanced BERT with Disentangled Attention, Microsoft 2020) made a deeper architectural change. In standard BERT, the attention score between two tokens is computed from their combined content-and-position embeddings. DeBERTa disentangles these: it maintains separate vectors for content and position, and computes attention using content-to-content, content-to-position, and position-to-content interactions independently. This lets the model reason more precisely about how the meaning of a word depends on where it appears. DeBERTa also adds an enhanced mask decoder that incorporates absolute position information right before the output layer, compensating for the relative-only position encoding in the attention layers. DeBERTa consistently beats RoBERTa on downstream tasks and was the first model to surpass human performance on the SuperGLUE benchmark.

Sentence Embeddings

You might expect that BERT's [CLS] vector would be a great sentence embedding — a single vector capturing the meaning of the whole sentence. In practice, it's mediocre. The [CLS] token was trained for NSP, not for producing general-purpose sentence representations. If you compute cosine similarity between [CLS] vectors of different sentences, the results are often worse than simple word-embedding averages. This was a surprising and somewhat embarrassing finding.

Sentence-BERT (SBERT) fixed this. The idea is to fine-tune BERT with a siamese or triplet network structure. You feed two sentences through the same BERT model, pool the outputs (typically by averaging all token vectors, which is called mean pooling and works better than using [CLS]), and train with a contrastive loss that pushes similar sentences closer and dissimilar sentences apart in the embedding space. After this training, you can compare sentences by simple cosine similarity — thousands of times faster than feeding every pair through BERT for cross-encoding.

SBERT made BERT practical for semantic search, clustering, and retrieval. Before SBERT, comparing 10,000 sentences pairwise with BERT required about 50 million forward passes. With SBERT, you compute 10,000 embeddings once and then use cosine similarity, which takes milliseconds.

Distillation

BERT-base has 110 million parameters and takes noticeable time for inference, especially on CPUs or mobile devices. Knowledge distillation is the technique of training a smaller "student" model to mimic the behavior of the larger "teacher" model. The key insight is that the teacher's soft targets — the full probability distribution over the vocabulary for each prediction — carry far more information than hard labels. When BERT predicts a masked token and assigns 0.7 probability to "paris," 0.1 to "lyon," and 0.05 to "france," those relative probabilities reveal what the model has learned about the relationships between words. A hard label just says "paris" and throws away all that nuance.

DistilBERT (HuggingFace 2019) takes BERT-base's 12 layers and distills them into 6 layers. The result is 40% smaller, 60% faster at inference, and retains about 97% of BERT's performance on downstream tasks. The student is initialized from every other layer of the teacher, then trained to match the teacher's output distributions. This is one of the best effort-to-performance tradeoffs in NLP.

TinyBERT (Huawei 2020) goes further. It doesn't just match output distributions — it also distills the intermediate representations. The student is trained to match the teacher's attention matrices and hidden states at each layer, in addition to the final predictions. This two-stage distillation (general distillation on unlabeled data, then task-specific distillation) produces a 4-layer model that's 7.5x smaller and 9.4x faster than BERT-base, while retaining over 96% performance. The tradeoff is a more complex training pipeline. 

from transformers import pipeline

# DistilBERT is a drop-in replacement — same API, much faster
classifier = pipeline(
    "sentiment-analysis",
    model="distilbert-base-uncased-finetuned-sst-2-english"
)

results = classifier([
    "BERT internals are actually pretty elegant.",
    "I hate debugging tokenizer issues at 3 AM."
])
for r in results:
    print(f"{r['label']}: {r['score']:.4f}")
# POSITIVE: 0.9998
# NEGATIVE: 0.9995

Notice you don't need to change your inference code at all. DistilBERT has the same API as BERT. You just swap the model name and get a 60% speedup for free. That's the power of distillation done right.

BERT Limitations

BERT is powerful, but it has real limitations you should understand before choosing it for a project.

First, the 512-token maximum. BERT's position embeddings are learned vectors for positions 0 through 511. If your input is longer, you can't just extend them — the model has never seen positions beyond 511 during pre-training. You have to truncate your input, or use a sliding window and aggregate the results, or switch to a model designed for long documents (like Longformer or BigBird). For many real-world tasks — legal documents, research papers, long conversations — 512 tokens is painfully short.

Second, the pre-train/fine-tune mismatch. During pre-training, 15% of input tokens are [MASK]. During fine-tuning and inference, there are no [MASK] tokens at all. The model has never seen a fully "clean" sentence during pre-training. The 80/10/10 rule mitigates this somewhat, but it's fundamentally a hack. This mismatch doesn't cause catastrophic failures, but it does mean BERT's representations aren't perfectly optimized for the downstream setting.

Third, BERT can't generate text. It's an encoder-only model. It can fill in blanks ([MASK]), score sentences, and classify things, but it can't write a paragraph. If you need generation, you need a decoder (GPT-style) or an encoder-decoder (T5-style). Trying to do autoregressive generation with BERT requires iterative masking and is both slow and poor quality.

Fourth, pre-training cost. Even though you don't usually pre-train BERT yourself, if you need to pre-train on domain-specific data (legal, medical, scientific text), you're looking at significant compute costs. BERT-base took 4 days on 16 TPU v3 chips. That's roughly $10,000 to $50,000 in cloud compute, depending on your setup. For most practitioners, the answer is to start with a pre-trained checkpoint and fine-tune. But if your domain is sufficiently different from Wikipedia and books, fine-tuning alone may not be enough.

Don't Use BERT for Everything

BERT is a fantastic understanding model, but it's not the right tool for every NLP task. If you need text generation, use GPT or T5. If you need to process documents longer than 512 tokens, use Longformer or BigBird. If you need fast inference on a mobile device, start with DistilBERT or TinyBERT, not BERT-base. And if you're starting a new project in 2024, consider whether a modern model like DeBERTa-v3 or a general-purpose model like a fine-tuned LLaMA might serve you better. BERT was a breakthrough, but the field has moved on, and understanding why it was surpassed teaches you as much as understanding how it works.

Despite these limitations, BERT remains one of the most important models in NLP history. It proved that bidirectional pre-training works, that the pre-train-then-fine-tune paradigm is extraordinarily effective, and that a single model architecture can serve dozens of different tasks. Every encoder model you use today — RoBERTa, DeBERTa, sentence transformers — is a direct descendant. Understanding BERT's internals isn't just historical knowledge. It's the foundation for understanding everything that came after.
