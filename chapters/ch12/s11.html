Reasoning & Inference-Time Scaling

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 4 subtopics
TL;DR

LLMs default to fast, pattern-matching "System 1" answers. You can unlock dramatically better reasoning by spending more compute at inference time — chain-of-thought prompting, self-consistency, tree search over reasoning paths, and dedicated thinking tokens. This creates a new scaling axis: instead of only making models bigger, you can make them think longer. Process reward models score each reasoning step, reinforcement learning teaches models to reason from scratch, and best-of-N sampling exploits the fact that checking an answer is easier than finding one. Smaller models that think harder can beat larger models that answer reflexively.

Why Reasoning Matters

Ask GPT-4 to write a poem and it nails it. Ask it to figure out how many Rs are in "strawberry" and it stumbles. The gap is revealing. LLMs are superb at pattern matching — they've seen so many poems that generating one is almost lookup. But multi-step reasoning, where each conclusion depends on the previous one, is a different beast. One wrong step early on cascades into a completely wrong answer.

There's a useful analogy from cognitive science. Daniel Kahneman describes two modes of human thinking. System 1 is fast, intuitive, automatic — you instantly know that 2 + 2 = 4. System 2 is slow, deliberate, effortful — you need it for 17 × 24. Standard LLM inference is almost entirely System 1. The model reads the prompt, does a single forward pass through billions of parameters, and produces an answer in one shot. No scratch work, no backtracking, no "hmm, let me reconsider."

The key insight that launched an entire research direction: you can improve answers by spending more compute at inference time, not just training time. Traditional scaling laws said "make the model bigger, train it on more data." That still works, but it has diminishing returns and enormous cost. The new idea is to let the model think longer on hard problems. Instead of a single forward pass, give it multiple attempts, let it show its work, let it explore different reasoning paths. This is inference-time scaling, and it's reshaping how we build and deploy AI systems.

Chain-of-Thought Prompting

The Breakthrough

In 2022, Wei et al. published a deceptively simple finding: if you show a language model examples of step-by-step reasoning, it learns to reason step-by-step too. The technique is called chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting. Instead of asking the model to jump straight to the answer, you prompt it to decompose the problem into intermediate steps.

Why does this work? Think about it this way. When you solve a word problem in math, you don't look at the question and write the final number. You identify what's given, figure out what operation to apply, compute intermediate results, then combine them. Each step is individually easy even if the whole problem is hard. CoT prompting forces the model to do the same thing — break a hard problem into a chain of easy sub-problems. Each sub-problem sits within the context window, so the model can "read" its own earlier reasoning to inform the next step.

Here's a concrete before-and-after. Without CoT, a model might fail a question like "Roger has 5 tennis balls. He buys 2 more cans, each with 3 balls. How many does he have now?" The model might blurt out "10" or "8." With CoT, you prompt it to reason explicitly:

# Standard prompting — model jumps to answer
prompt_standard = """Q: Roger has 5 tennis balls. He buys 2 more cans of
3 tennis balls each. How many tennis balls does he have now?
A:"""
# Model output: "10"  (wrong)

# Chain-of-thought prompting — model shows its work
prompt_cot = """Q: Janet has 3 apples. She buys 2 bags of 4 apples each.
How many apples does she have now?
A: Janet starts with 3 apples. She buys 2 bags × 4 apples = 8 apples.
Total = 3 + 8 = 11 apples. The answer is 11.

Q: Roger has 5 tennis balls. He buys 2 more cans of
3 tennis balls each. How many tennis balls does he have now?
A:"""
# Model output: "Roger starts with 5 tennis balls. He buys
# 2 cans × 3 balls = 6 balls. Total = 5 + 6 = 11. The answer is 11."

That's few-shot CoT — you provide worked examples as demonstrations. But Kojima et al. (2022) found something even simpler. Just appending the phrase "Let's think step by step" to the prompt — with no examples at all — triggers the model to generate its own chain of thought. This is zero-shot CoT, and it works shockingly well for a technique that requires zero effort.

from openai import OpenAI
client = OpenAI()

def ask_with_cot(question: str) -> str:
    """Zero-shot chain-of-thought: just add the magic phrase."""
    response = client.chat.completions.create(
        model="gpt-4o",
        messages=[
            {"role": "system", "content": "You are a helpful assistant."},
            {"role": "user", "content": f"{question}\n\nLet's think step by step."}
        ],
        temperature=0.0
    )
    return response.choices[0].message.content

answer = ask_with_cot(
    "A bat and a ball cost $1.10 in total. The bat costs $1.00 more "
    "than the ball. How much does the ball cost?"
)
print(answer)
# Without CoT, models often say "$0.10" (wrong).
# With CoT, the model reasons through the algebra and gets "$0.05".
KEY INSIGHT

Chain-of-thought doesn't add new knowledge to the model. It changes how the model uses what it already knows. The reasoning capability was always latent — CoT just provides a format that lets it surface. This is why CoT works better on larger models: they have more latent reasoning ability to unlock.

Self-Consistency

Chain-of-thought is great, but it has a problem: the model might reason its way to the wrong answer through a plausible-sounding but flawed chain. One bad reasoning path, one bad answer. Self-consistency (Wang et al., 2022) fixes this with a beautifully simple idea: generate multiple reasoning chains and take a majority vote on the final answer.

You run the same prompt through the model, say, 10 times with a higher temperature (typically 0.7–1.0) to get diverse reasoning paths. Each path arrives at some answer. Maybe 7 paths say "11," two say "13," and one says "8." You pick "11." The intuition is that correct reasoning is more likely to be consistent across diverse attempts, while errors tend to be random and scattered. If most roads lead to the same answer, that answer is probably right.

Self-consistency improves over single-CoT by 5–15 percentage points on math benchmarks. That's enormous for a technique that requires zero changes to the model — you're just sampling more and counting. The tradeoff is cost: 10 samples means 10× the inference compute. But for high-stakes reasoning tasks, the accuracy gain is worth it.

Tree of Thoughts

Chain-of-thought is a single path from question to answer. Self-consistency runs multiple independent paths. Tree of Thoughts (ToT) (Yao et al., 2023) takes the next logical step: explore reasoning as a branching tree, where you can evaluate partial solutions and backtrack when a path looks unpromising.

Think of it like solving a maze. CoT picks a direction and keeps walking — if it hits a dead end, too bad. ToT keeps a map. At each step, it generates several possible "thoughts" (next reasoning steps), evaluates each one ("does this look like it's heading somewhere useful?"), and uses search algorithms — breadth-first search (BFS) or depth-first search (DFS) — to explore the tree systematically. BFS explores all options at the current depth before going deeper, which is thorough but expensive. DFS goes deep first and backtracks, which is cheaper but might miss good branches.

ToT shines on problems that require planning, exploration, or creative problem-solving — puzzles like the Game of 24 (make 24 from four numbers using arithmetic), crossword puzzles, or complex multi-step proofs. For straightforward factual questions, the overhead isn't worth it. But for genuinely hard reasoning, the ability to backtrack and try different approaches is game-changing.

Scratchpad Reasoning & Thinking Tokens

All the techniques above work with existing models by clever prompting. The next evolution bakes reasoning into the model itself. Thinking tokens are special tokens the model generates internally before producing its visible answer. You, the user, don't see them — they're the model's private scratchpad.

OpenAI's o1 and o3 models pioneered this approach. When you ask o1 a hard math question, it doesn't immediately answer. It generates a long chain of internal reasoning — sometimes thousands of tokens — exploring approaches, checking its work, reconsidering. Only after this hidden deliberation does it produce its final answer. The result is dramatically better performance on math, coding, and science benchmarks.

DeepSeek's R1 model took a different path to the same destination. They started with DeepSeek-R1-Zero, a model trained purely with reinforcement learning on reasoning tasks — no supervised fine-tuning on human-written reasoning chains at all. The model discovered chain-of-thought reasoning on its own. Researchers observed an "aha moment" during training: at some point, the model spontaneously started generating longer, more structured reasoning before answering, and accuracy jumped. The model learned that thinking before answering gets more reward.

This led to reasoning distillation — taking the reasoning traces from a large, powerful reasoning model and using them to fine-tune a smaller model. The smaller model can't discover these reasoning patterns on its own, but it can learn to imitate them. DeepSeek showed that a 7B parameter model distilled from R1 could outperform much larger models that hadn't learned to reason explicitly.

TRADEOFF ALERT

Thinking tokens dramatically improve accuracy but also dramatically increase latency and cost. An o1 response might use 10,000+ internal tokens on a hard problem. You're paying for all those tokens even though you never see them. For simple tasks — summarization, translation, basic Q&A — thinking tokens are wasteful overkill. Match the model to the task.

Inference-Time Scaling Laws

This is the paradigm shift. For years, the AI field ran on a single recipe: scale up training. More parameters, more data, more GPU-hours. The Chinchilla scaling laws told you the optimal ratio of model size to training tokens. But all of that governs pre-training compute. What happens at inference time was an afterthought — just do a single forward pass and return the answer.

Inference-time scaling flips this on its head. The new finding: for reasoning tasks, you can get log-linear improvements in accuracy by scaling inference compute — generating more tokens, sampling more candidates, doing more search. This means you have two knobs to turn: training compute and inference compute. And crucially, they're partially substitutable.

The practical implication is powerful. Imagine you have a budget of $1,000 to answer a set of hard reasoning questions. You could spend $900 on training a bigger model and $100 on inference. Or you could spend $500 on training a smaller model and $500 on inference-time compute — letting the smaller model think harder. For sufficiently hard problems, the second strategy wins. The smaller model with more thinking time beats the bigger model with less.

Adaptive compute makes this even more efficient. Not every question needs deep reasoning. "What's the capital of France?" doesn't need 10,000 thinking tokens. "Prove that there are infinitely many primes" does. Smart systems detect problem difficulty and allocate inference compute accordingly — spending more tokens on harder problems and fewer on easy ones. This is how human cognition works too: you don't deliberate over every decision.

Process Reward Models vs Outcome Reward Models

If you're going to search over reasoning paths, you need a way to score them. There are two philosophies. An Outcome Reward Model (ORM) looks at the final answer and gives a thumbs up or thumbs down. Did the model get the right answer? Great, positive reward. Wrong answer? Negative reward. ORMs are simple to train (you just need question-answer pairs) but they have a critical weakness: they can't tell the difference between a correct answer reached by flawed reasoning (got lucky) and a wrong answer from mostly-correct reasoning (one small slip).

A Process Reward Model (PRM) scores each individual reasoning step. Did step 1 make a valid deduction? Was step 3 a correct arithmetic operation? PRMs are harder to train — you need human annotations at the step level, not just the final answer — but they enable much better search over reasoning. When you're exploring a tree of possible reasoning paths, a PRM tells you early whether a branch is going off the rails, so you can prune it before wasting compute.

This connects naturally to Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), the algorithm that powered AlphaGo. Instead of playing Go moves, you're playing "reasoning moves." At each node, you use the PRM to estimate the value of the current reasoning state, then expand the most promising branches. MCTS with a PRM can systematically search through thousands of reasoning paths, focusing compute where it matters most. The combination is remarkably effective for math competition problems and formal proofs.

WHY THIS MATTERS

PRMs shift the reward signal from "did you get the right answer?" to "did you reason correctly?" This is the same distinction between a teacher who only checks the final answer on an exam and one who gives partial credit for correct work. The partial-credit teacher produces students who understand the material better, because they learn which steps matter.

Best-of-N Sampling & Verification

Best-of-N sampling is the brute-force version of inference-time scaling: generate N complete solutions and pick the best one. It's the simplest technique that works, and it works surprisingly well. With N=100 on math benchmarks, even modest models see massive accuracy improvements.

The key question is: how do you pick the "best"? If you have a reliable verifier — a reward model, a code executor, a proof checker — you can score each candidate and select the winner. For math, you can often execute the solution to check if the final numerical answer is correct. For code, you can run test cases. This exploits a fundamental asymmetry: generation-verification asymmetry. It's much harder to generate a correct proof than to check one. It's much harder to write correct code than to run tests on it.

import random

def best_of_n_sampling(model, prompt: str, n: int = 16, temperature: float = 0.8):
    """Generate N candidate solutions and pick the best one via verification."""
    candidates = []
    for _ in range(n):
        response = model.generate(prompt, temperature=temperature)
        answer = extract_final_answer(response)
        # Score via a reward model or verifier
        score = reward_model.score(prompt, response)
        candidates.append({"response": response, "answer": answer, "score": score})

    # Return highest-scoring candidate
    best = max(candidates, key=lambda c: c["score"])
    return best["response"]

def self_consistency_vote(model, prompt: str, n: int = 16, temperature: float = 0.8):
    """Self-consistency: majority vote over N sampled answers."""
    answers = []
    for _ in range(n):
        response = model.generate(prompt, temperature=temperature)
        answer = extract_final_answer(response)
        answers.append(answer)

    # Majority vote
    from collections import Counter
    vote = Counter(answers).most_common(1)[0][0]
    return vote

Self-verification is a variant where the model checks its own work. After generating a solution, you prompt the model: "Is this solution correct? Check each step." The model sometimes catches its own errors. It's not perfect — models have blind spots about their own mistakes — but it's a cheap additional filter that catches some errors for free.

Reinforcement Learning for Reasoning

Chain-of-thought prompting coaxes reasoning out of pre-trained models. But what if you could train models to reason better from the ground up? This is where reinforcement learning enters the picture.

The standard approach uses algorithms like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) or the newer Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). The setup: give the model a math problem, let it generate a solution, check if the answer is correct, and give a reward signal. Over thousands of problems, the model learns to generate reasoning chains that lead to correct answers more often.

DeepSeek-R1-Zero is the most striking example. They took a base language model and trained it purely with RL on reasoning tasks — no supervised fine-tuning, no human-written chain-of-thought examples. The reward was simple: rule-based rewards that checked whether the final answer was correct and whether the response was properly formatted. No neural reward model, no human preference data. Just "did you get the right answer?"

What emerged was remarkable. The model spontaneously learned to generate chain-of-thought reasoning, allocate more tokens to harder problems, and even re-examine its work when it detected inconsistencies. It discovered reasoning strategies that humans had hand-designed in prompting techniques. The "aha moment" the researchers described was when the model, mid-training, started producing noticeably longer and more structured outputs for hard problems — it had learned that thinking harder leads to more reward.

OPEN PROBLEM

RL-trained reasoning models can develop "reasoning pathologies" — verbose but circular thinking, repetitive restating of the problem, or hallucinated verification steps where the model claims to check its work but doesn't actually catch errors. Regularization, format rewards, and careful reward shaping are active research areas. We're still in the early days of understanding how to train robust reasoning reliably.

Practical Implications

All of this has real consequences for how you build and deploy LLM systems. The biggest shift: model size is no longer the only variable that matters. A well-orchestrated smaller model — one that uses chain-of-thought, self-consistency, and best-of-N sampling — can outperform a much larger model that just answers in one shot. This democratizes access. You don't need a 405B-parameter model for everything; a 7B model with smart inference-time techniques might be enough.

But there are honest tradeoffs. More inference compute means higher latency. A user waiting for an o1-style response might wait 10–30 seconds instead of 1–2. It means higher cost per query — you're burning tokens on thinking that the user never sees. For latency-sensitive applications (autocomplete, real-time chat), heavy reasoning is impractical. For high-value applications (code generation, medical diagnosis, legal analysis), the accuracy gain justifies the cost.

The engineering challenge is building systems that adapt. Easy questions should get fast, cheap answers. Hard questions should trigger deeper reasoning. This requires either a routing model that estimates difficulty upfront, or a cascading approach where you start cheap and escalate if the initial answer seems uncertain. Both are active areas of development.

We're at an inflection point. For years, the AI scaling story was simple: bigger models, more data, more training compute. Now there's a second act. The models we've already trained contain more reasoning capability than we know how to extract with a single forward pass. Inference-time scaling is how we unlock it. The next generation of AI breakthroughs won't just come from building bigger models — they'll come from teaching models to think.
