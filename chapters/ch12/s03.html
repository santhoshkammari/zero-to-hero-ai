GPT Family

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 7 subtopics
TL;DR

The GPT family is the story of one bet — scale a decoder-only transformer — validated again and again across five generations. GPT-1 proved unsupervised pre-training works. GPT-2 showed that scale enables zero-shot task transfer. GPT-3 revealed that few-shot in-context learning emerges at 175B parameters. InstructGPT/ChatGPT proved that alignment via RLHF makes raw capability actually useful. GPT-4 added multimodal input and longer context. Each generation kept the same core architecture (causal decoder, BPE tokenizer) and simply pushed scale, data quality, and alignment further. The lesson: a simple idea, executed relentlessly, can reshape an entire field.

The One Bet That Kept Paying Off

Before we walk through each model, understand the throughline. In 2018, OpenAI made a specific architectural gamble: use a decoder-only transformer, train it to predict the next token on a large corpus, and then fine-tune it for downstream tasks. Every other lab at the time was exploring encoder-only models (BERT) or encoder-decoder architectures (T5). OpenAI kept doubling down on the decoder. That bet — that a left-to-right generative model would eventually outperform everything else if you scaled it enough — turned out to be spectacularly correct.

GPT-1 (2018): The Proof of Concept

Alec Radford and colleagues published "Improving Language Understanding by Generative Pre-Training" in mid-2018. The idea was deceptively simple: take an unlabeled text corpus, train a transformer to predict the next word, then fine-tune the resulting model on specific tasks like sentiment analysis or textual entailment. This two-stage recipe — unsupervised pre-training followed by supervised fine-tuning — was GPT-1's core contribution.

The model itself was modest by today's standards: 12 transformer decoder layers, 768-dimensional hidden states, 12 attention heads, totaling 117 million parameters. It trained on BooksCorpus, a collection of roughly 7,000 unpublished books (~5GB of text). The choice of books was deliberate — long-form text with coherent narrative structure teaches a model about long-range dependencies better than random web snippets.

For tokenization, GPT-1 used Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE), a subword tokenization method that starts with individual characters and iteratively merges the most frequent adjacent pairs. BPE hits a sweet spot: it handles rare words by breaking them into known subwords, keeps the vocabulary manageable (~40,000 tokens), and avoids the out-of-vocabulary problem entirely. Every subsequent GPT model kept BPE. It just worked.

The results were solid but not earth-shattering. GPT-1 achieved state-of-the-art on 9 of 12 NLP benchmarks it was tested on. The real significance was the proof of concept: a generative pre-training objective — simply predicting the next token — produced representations that transferred well to discriminative tasks. You didn't need task-specific architectures. You needed a good language model and a thin fine-tuning layer on top.

GPT-2 (2019): Zero-Shot Transfer

GPT-2 scaled everything by roughly 10x. The model jumped to 1.5 billion parameters across 48 layers with 1,600-dimensional hidden states. But the bigger change was the training data. OpenAI curated WebText, a dataset of 8 million web pages (about 40GB of text) sourced by following outbound links from Reddit posts with at least 3 upvotes — a clever heuristic for "content that humans found interesting enough to share."

The tokenizer grew too: a BPE vocabulary of 50,257 tokens, operating on byte-level representations. This meant the model could encode any text without unknown tokens — even code, URLs, or emoji. The byte-level approach was a quiet innovation that made the model far more robust in practice.

GPT-2's headline result wasn't about fine-tuning at all. It was about zero-shot task transfer: the model could perform tasks it was never explicitly trained for, simply by conditioning on a natural-language description. Want it to translate? Prefix the input with "Translate English to French:" and it would produce reasonable translations — despite never seeing a single labeled translation pair. Want a summary? Write "TL;DR:" after a paragraph. The paper's title said it all: "Language Models are Unsupervised Multitask Learners."

This was a paradigm shift. Before GPT-2, the recipe was: collect labeled data → design a task-specific head → fine-tune. GPT-2 suggested a different future: train one big language model and prompt it. No labeled data. No task-specific architecture. Just natural language instructions.

GPT-2 also became famous for its controversial staged release. OpenAI initially withheld the full 1.5B model, releasing only a 124M version, citing concerns about misuse — generating fake news, impersonating people, automating spam. This triggered a fierce debate: was this genuine safety caution or publicity theater? The community was divided. In retrospect, GPT-2's generation quality seems tame compared to modern models, but the precedent it set about responsible disclosure shaped how every subsequent large model was released.

GPT-3 (2020): The Scaling Leap

If GPT-2 hinted that scale mattered, GPT-3 proved it emphatically. At 175 billion parameters — over 100x GPT-2 — it was by far the largest language model ever trained. It used 96 layers, 12,288-dimensional hidden states, and 96 attention heads. Training consumed roughly 3.14 × 10²³ FLOPs and cost an estimated $4.6 million in compute alone.

The training data was a filtered version of Common Crawl (~570GB) mixed with WebText2, Books1, Books2, and English Wikipedia — about 300 billion tokens total. Data quality mattered enormously. OpenAI trained a classifier to filter low-quality pages out of Common Crawl, keeping roughly 7% of the original data. The intuition is simple: garbage in, garbage out, and Common Crawl has a lot of garbage.

GPT-3's breakthrough was in-context learning: the ability to learn new tasks at inference time from a few examples stuffed into the prompt, with zero gradient updates. You don't fine-tune anything. You just show the model what you want through examples, and it generalizes. This is sometimes called few-shot prompting.

The paper systematically tested zero-shot (just instructions), one-shot (one example), and few-shot (several examples) performance across dozens of benchmarks. The pattern was consistent: bigger models benefited more from in-context examples. A small model barely improved with more examples in the prompt, but GPT-3 at 175B showed dramatic gains. This was the first clear evidence of emergent abilities — capabilities that appear suddenly at a certain scale threshold rather than improving gradually.

OpenAI also changed the access model. GPT-3's weights were never publicly released. Instead, it was offered through an API — you sent text in, got completions back, and paid per token. This was a business decision as much as a safety one: the API model became enormously profitable and spawned hundreds of startups building on top of GPT-3's capabilities. It also meant that no one outside OpenAI could fully inspect, reproduce, or audit the model. That tension between commercial value and scientific openness persists today.

InstructGPT & GPT-3.5: The Alignment Breakthrough

Here's the problem with raw GPT-3: it's a next-token predictor, not an assistant. Ask it "What's the capital of France?" and it might respond with "What's the capital of Germany?" — because in its training data, questions are often followed by more questions. It's completing text patterns, not answering you. It was capable but not helpful. Sometimes it was actively harmful — generating toxic content, making up facts confidently, or ignoring the user's actual intent.

InstructGPT (published early 2022) fixed this through a technique called Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). The pipeline has three stages:


  RLHF Pipeline
  ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  STEP 1: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)
  ──────────────────────────────────────
  Human labelers write ideal responses to prompts.
  Fine-tune GPT-3 on these (prompt, ideal_response) pairs.
  Result: A model that tries to be helpful but is limited
          by the small amount of human-written data.

       [Prompt] ──► [Human writes ideal answer] ──► Fine-tune model

  STEP 2: Reward Model Training                    SFT Model
  ──────────────────────────────────────
  For each prompt, the SFT model generates multiple responses.
  Humans rank these responses from best to worst.
  Train a separate reward model to predict human preferences.

                         │

  Use the reward model as a scoring function.

                    InstructGPT (aligned model)

The results were striking. InstructGPT, based on a 1.3B parameter model (over 100x smaller than GPT-3 175B), was preferred by human evaluators 85% of the time over the full-size GPT-3. Read that again: a smaller model with alignment training beat a much larger model without it. This proved that alignment isn't a nice-to-have — it's a multiplier on raw capability. A model that understands what you want is more useful than a model that's smarter but oblivious to your intent.

The reward model deserves special attention. It's a neural network that takes a (prompt, response) pair and outputs a scalar score predicting how much a human would like that response. Training it requires thousands of human comparisons. The quality of the reward model is the ceiling for the entire RLHF pipeline — if it rewards the wrong things, PPO will gleefully optimize for the wrong things. This is why reward hacking (the model finding unexpected ways to score highly without actually being helpful) is an active research concern.

ChatGPT (November 2022) was essentially GPT-3.5 — a more capable base model — put through this same RLHF pipeline and wrapped in a conversational interface. The technical novelty was modest, but the product impact was explosive: it reached 100 million users in two months, the fastest adoption of any consumer application in history. ChatGPT didn't invent anything new. It productized the alignment breakthrough that InstructGPT had demonstrated months earlier.

GPT-4 (2023): Multimodal and Mysterious

GPT-4 arrived in March 2023 with dramatically improved capabilities and dramatically reduced transparency. The technical report was conspicuously vague — no architecture details, no dataset description, no training compute figures. OpenAI cited competitive pressures and safety concerns. The AI research community was not thrilled.

What we do know: GPT-4 is multimodal. It accepts both text and images as input (though initially it only produced text output). You can feed it a photograph of a handwritten math problem and it will solve it. You can give it a screenshot of a UI mockup and it will generate the HTML. This wasn't just "bolting on a vision model" — the image understanding was deeply integrated into the model's reasoning.

Context length grew substantially: from GPT-3's 4,096 tokens to 8,192, then 32,768, and eventually 128,000 tokens with GPT-4 Turbo. Longer context means the model can process entire codebases, full research papers, or lengthy conversations without losing track. But context length comes with costs — attention is quadratic in sequence length, and longer contexts require more memory and compute at inference time.

GPT-4 introduced system prompts as a first-class concept: a special message at the beginning of the conversation that instructs the model on its persona, constraints, and behavior. "You are a helpful assistant that responds only in JSON." "You are a tutor who never gives the answer directly but asks guiding questions." System prompts gave developers fine-grained behavioral control without any fine-tuning.

On benchmarks, GPT-4's performance was remarkable. It passed the Uniform Bar Exam in the 90th percentile (GPT-3.5 scored 10th percentile). It scored in the 88th percentile on the LSAT, 80th on the GRE Quantitative section. These aren't toy benchmarks — they're standardized exams designed to test human reasoning. The improvement over GPT-3.5 was not incremental; it was a qualitative jump.

The architecture is widely rumored to be a Mixture of Experts (MoE) design — multiple smaller "expert" sub-networks, with a routing mechanism that activates only a subset for each token. MoE lets you scale total parameters (and therefore knowledge capacity) without proportionally increasing compute per token. This is how you get GPT-4-level quality at manageable inference costs. OpenAI has never confirmed this, but multiple independent analyses point to it.

GPT-4o and Beyond: The Efficiency Turn

GPT-4o ("o" for "omni") arrived in May 2024 as a natively multimodal model. Unlike GPT-4, which processed images through a separate pathway, GPT-4o handles text, vision, and audio in a single unified architecture. You can speak to it and it responds with natural-sounding speech — not through a text-to-speech pipeline, but as a native output modality. Latency dropped dramatically: GPT-4o responds to audio input in about 320 milliseconds on average, comparable to human conversational response times.

The efficiency theme intensified with GPT-4o mini, a distilled version optimized for cost and speed. It scores higher than GPT-3.5 Turbo on most benchmarks while being significantly cheaper to run. This reflects a broader industry trend: once you have a very capable large model, you can use it to train smaller, faster models that retain most of the capability. This process — distillation — involves training the small model to mimic the large model's outputs rather than learning from raw data. The teacher's "dark knowledge" (its probability distribution over all possible tokens, not just the top-1) transfers surprisingly well.

The trajectory is clear. Early GPT models pushed the frontier of what's possible. Later GPT models push the frontier of what's practical — making those capabilities faster, cheaper, and accessible to more applications. The $20/month ChatGPT subscription that processes text, images, and audio in real time would have required millions in API costs just two years earlier.

Scaling Decisions & Architecture Choices

Step back and look at what stayed constant across every GPT model. The core architecture is always a decoder-only transformer with causal attention (each token can only attend to tokens before it, never ahead). The tokenizer is always BPE-based. The training objective is always next-token prediction. These choices, made in 2018, still underpin GPT-4o in 2024. That's remarkable stability in a field where architectures usually have a shelf life of months.

What changed is everything around that core. Training data evolved from curated books (GPT-1) to filtered web crawls (GPT-2/3) to undisclosed mixtures (GPT-4). Context length grew from 512 tokens to 128,000. Alignment techniques progressed from nothing (GPT-1/2) to basic RLHF (InstructGPT) to sophisticated multi-stage pipelines (GPT-4). Serving infrastructure shifted from "download the weights and run it yourself" to API-only access with rate limits, content filters, and usage policies.

The API-versus-open-source tradeoff is worth understanding. OpenAI chose the closed-API model for GPT-3 onward. Benefits: controlled access, revenue generation, ability to patch vulnerabilities quickly. Costs: no independent auditing, no reproducibility, vendor lock-in for developers, and no community improvements. Meta's LLaMA family took the opposite approach — open weights — and catalyzed an enormous ecosystem of fine-tuned, adapted, and improved models. Neither approach is strictly better. Closed models can invest more in safety and alignment. Open models enable faster scientific progress and prevent concentration of power. The industry needs both.

One final observation. The GPT family demonstrates that architecture innovation matters less than you'd think compared to three other factors: training data quality, scale (compute and parameters), and post-training alignment. GPT-4 probably has architectural improvements over GPT-1, but the transformer decoder it's built on is recognizably the same design from 2017. The secret sauce was never a clever new attention variant — it was the relentless willingness to 10x the data, 10x the compute, and invest in making the model actually do what users want.

The GPT story is ultimately simple. Take a proven architecture. Feed it more data. Make it bigger. Align it to human preferences. Repeat. That simplicity is deceptive — executing each step at scale involves staggering engineering challenges — but the strategic clarity is what made the GPT family the most influential lineage in modern AI. One bet. Validated again and again.
