Tokenizer Training

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 6 subtopics
TL;DR

LLMs don't read text — they read tokens. The tokenizer is the lens through which a model sees language, and training it well matters more than most people realize. Subword tokenization (BPE, WordPiece, Unigram) won because it balances vocabulary size against sequence length. BPE merges the most frequent character pairs bottom-up; WordPiece merges by likelihood gain; Unigram starts huge and prunes down. Vocabulary size is a tradeoff — too small means painfully long sequences, too large means sparse embeddings. Multilingual models suffer a "fertility tax" where non-English text explodes into far more tokens. Getting tokenization right is the invisible foundation that everything else sits on.

Why Tokenization Matters

Before a large language model can process a single word, that word must be converted into numbers. Not just any numbers — specific integer IDs from a fixed vocabulary. The component that does this conversion is the tokenizer, and it determines what the model can "see." A model is literally blind to distinctions its tokenizer doesn't make. If your tokenizer lumps two different concepts into the same token, the model can never tell them apart.

Consider the word "unhappiness." A character-level tokenizer would split it into ['u', 'n', 'h', 'a', 'p', 'p', 'i', 'n', 'e', 's', 's'] — eleven tokens for one word. A word-level tokenizer might keep it as one token, but then it needs a separate entry for "unhappy," "happiness," "happier," and thousands of other variations. A subword tokenizer might split it into ['un', 'happiness'] or ['un', 'happi', 'ness'] — capturing morphological structure in just two or three tokens. That difference matters. Every extra token means a longer sequence, more computation, and less room in the model's context window for actual content.

Here's the part that surprises people: the same sentence can look wildly different to different models. GPT-2's tokenizer splits "tokenization" into ['token', 'ization'] (2 tokens), while a character-level model needs 12 tokens. Feed in some Python code like self.attention_heads and GPT-2 gives you ['self', '.', 'attention', '_', 'heads'] — five tokens. A poorly trained tokenizer might fragment that into eight or nine pieces, wasting context window capacity on syntax rather than semantics.

KEY INSIGHT

The tokenizer is trained before the model. Once training begins, the vocabulary is frozen. You cannot change it later without retraining from scratch. This means tokenizer decisions made on day one permanently shape what the model can learn.

The Tokenization Landscape

Character-Level Tokenization

The simplest approach: every character is a token. Your vocabulary is tiny — maybe 256 entries for ASCII, or a few thousand for Unicode. You'll never encounter an unknown word because every word is just a sequence of known characters. The problem? Sequences become extremely long. A 500-word paragraph might turn into 2,500+ tokens. Since transformer attention is quadratic in sequence length, this is computationally brutal. The model also has to learn spelling from scratch — it gets no hint that "cat" and "cats" are related.

Word-Level Tokenization

The other extreme: every word is a token. Sequences are short and meaningful. But now your vocabulary explodes — English alone has hundreds of thousands of words, and that's before you add misspellings, technical terms, proper nouns, and other languages. Worse, any word not in the vocabulary becomes an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) token, usually mapped to a generic [UNK]. The moment you hit [UNK], the model has lost information permanently. If your user types "ChatGPT" and it wasn't in the training vocabulary, the model sees [UNK] and has no idea what was meant.

Subword Tokenization: The Sweet Spot

Subword tokenization splits text into pieces that are larger than characters but often smaller than words. Common words stay whole. Rare words get split into meaningful pieces. The word "unhappiness" becomes ['un', 'happi', 'ness'] — and now the model can share knowledge between "unhappy," "happiness," and "unhappiness" because they share subword components. This is why virtually every modern LLM uses subword tokenization. It solves both the long-sequence problem and the OOV problem simultaneously.

BPE (Byte Pair Encoding) Training

Byte Pair Encoding is the most widely used subword algorithm. GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4, and many other models use it. The algorithm is elegantly simple: start with individual characters, then repeatedly merge the most frequent pair of adjacent tokens. That's it. Let's walk through a concrete example.

Suppose your training corpus, after counting word frequencies, looks like this: "hug" appears 10 times, "pug" 5 times, "pun" 12 times, "bun" 4 times, and "hugs" 7 times. Your initial vocabulary is every unique character: {h, u, g, p, n, b, s}. Now you scan the corpus for the most frequent adjacent pair. The pair (u, g) appears in "hug" (10), "pug" (5), and "hugs" (7) — 22 times total. The pair (u, n) appears in "pun" (12) and "bun" (4) — 16 times. So (u, g) wins. You merge it into a new token ug. Now "hug" is represented as ['h', 'ug'], "pug" as ['p', 'ug'], and "hugs" as ['h', 'ug', 's'].

Next iteration: scan again. Now (h, ug) appears 17 times (10 from "hug" plus 7 from "hugs"). Merge into hug. Next, maybe (u, n) at 16 times gets merged into un. You keep going until you hit your target vocabulary size — say, 32,000 tokens. Each merge rule is saved, and at inference time, you apply those same rules in the same order to tokenize new text.

Here's how you train a BPE tokenizer in practice using the HuggingFace tokenizers library:

from tokenizers import Tokenizer
from tokenizers.models import BPE
from tokenizers.trainers import BpeTrainer
from tokenizers.pre_tokenizers import Whitespace

# Initialize a blank BPE tokenizer
tokenizer = Tokenizer(BPE(unk_token="[UNK]"))
tokenizer.pre_tokenizer = Whitespace()

# Configure training: vocab size, special tokens
trainer = BpeTrainer(
    vocab_size=32000,
    special_tokens=["[UNK]", "[CLS]", "[SEP]", "[PAD]", "[MASK]"],
    min_frequency=2,  # ignore pairs appearing only once
)

# Train on your corpus files
tokenizer.train(files=["corpus_part1.txt", "corpus_part2.txt"], trainer=trainer)

# Save for later use
tokenizer.save("my-bpe-tokenizer.json")

# Test it out
output = tokenizer.encode("unhappiness is temporary")
print(output.tokens)
# e.g. ['un', 'happiness', 'is', 'temporary']
print(output.ids)
# e.g. [347, 8291, 25, 6104]

The min_frequency=2 parameter is important — it tells the trainer to ignore pairs that appear only once, which prevents overfitting to rare typos or artifacts in your corpus.

WordPiece Training

WordPiece is the algorithm behind BERT, DistilBERT, and most of Google's encoder models. It looks similar to BPE on the surface — you still merge tokens iteratively — but the merge criterion is different. Instead of merging the most frequent pair, WordPiece merges the pair that maximizes the likelihood of the training data. Concretely, it picks the pair (A, B) that maximizes the score:

score(A, B) = frequency(AB) / (frequency(A) * frequency(B))

This formula favors merging pairs where the combination is unexpectedly common relative to the individual pieces. A frequent pair like (t, h) might not score highest if both "t" and "h" are individually very common — because the denominator is large. But a pair like (##ness, ##ly) might win if the combination is disproportionately common relative to each piece alone. This tends to produce linguistically cleaner merges.

The other distinctive feature of WordPiece is the ## prefix convention. Any subword that doesn't start a word is prefixed with ##. The word "tokenization" might become ['token', '##iza', '##tion']. This tells you unambiguously which pieces are word-initial and which are continuations. When you decode back to text, you just strip the ## and concatenate. It's a small detail but it matters for tasks like named entity recognition where word boundaries are important.

Let's trace through a small example. Start with the word "hugging" split into characters: ['h', 'u', 'g', 'g', 'i', 'n', 'g']. Suppose the likelihood-based score ranks (g, g) highest (because "gg" is rare individually, making the combination surprisingly frequent relative to each part). Merge into ['h', 'u', 'gg', 'i', 'n', 'g']. Next, perhaps (u, gg) scores highest, giving ['h', 'ugg', 'i', 'n', 'g']. Then (h, ugg) → ['hugg', 'i', 'n', 'g']. Eventually you might get ['hugg', '##ing'] if the full word isn't common enough to stay intact.

Unigram Training

Unigram takes the opposite approach from BPE and WordPiece. Instead of starting small and merging up, it starts with a massive vocabulary — perhaps 1 million subword candidates — and prunes down. The algorithm assigns a probability to each token, then asks: "If I remove this token, how much does the overall likelihood of the training corpus decrease?" Tokens whose removal barely hurts get dropped. You repeat until you reach your target vocabulary size.

This is the algorithm behind SentencePiece, used by T5, ALBERT, Llama, and Mistral. One elegant property: Unigram naturally handles ambiguity. The word "unbreakable" could be tokenized as ['un', 'break', 'able'] or ['unbreak', 'able'] or ['un', 'breakable']. A BPE tokenizer has one deterministic answer based on its merge rules. A Unigram tokenizer considers all possible segmentations and picks the one with the highest total probability. During training, it can even sample different segmentations to regularize the model — a technique called subword regularization. This makes the downstream model more robust because it sees multiple tokenizations of the same word.

WATCH OUT

Unigram's probabilistic nature means the same word can be tokenized differently on different runs if you enable sampling. This is a feature during training (it acts as data augmentation), but at inference time you should use the Viterbi (most probable) segmentation for deterministic results.

Vocabulary Size Selection

Choosing vocabulary size is one of those decisions that seems simple but has deep consequences. Too small a vocabulary — say 8,000 tokens — means every sentence becomes a long sequence of small pieces. Your context window fills up fast, and the model spends its capacity reassembling fragments rather than understanding meaning. Too large a vocabulary — say 500,000 tokens — means most tokens are rare, their embeddings are barely trained, and you're burning memory on a massive embedding matrix that's mostly noise.

The sweet spot for most modern LLMs falls between 32,000 and 128,000 tokens. Here's how popular models compare:

Model Tokenizer Type Vocab Size Notes
GPT-2 BPE 50,257 Byte-level BPE, no UNK tokens
GPT-4 / GPT-4o BPE (cl100k / o200k) 100,256 / 200,019 Larger vocab for efficiency on code and multilingual
BERT WordPiece 30,522 Cased and uncased versions
T5 Unigram (SentencePiece) 32,000 Shared vocabulary for input and output
Llama 2 BPE (SentencePiece) 32,000 Standard size, English-focused
Llama 3 BPE (tiktoken) 128,256 4x larger vocab for multilingual and code
Mistral 7B BPE (SentencePiece) 32,000 Efficient for English-centric workloads
Gemma 2 Unigram (SentencePiece) 256,000 Very large vocab for broad language coverage

Notice the trend: newer models use larger vocabularies. Llama 3 quadrupled its vocab from 32K to 128K compared to Llama 2. Why? A bigger vocabulary means more common words and phrases are single tokens, which shortens sequences and lets the model fit more content in its context window. The cost is a larger embedding matrix (vocab_size × hidden_dim), but for models with billions of parameters, this overhead is relatively small. The selection is ultimately empirical — you train multiple tokenizers at different sizes, measure downstream performance, and pick the best tradeoff.

Special Tokens

Special tokens are artificial tokens that don't represent natural language but serve as signals to the model. They're injected by the tokenizer during preprocessing and tell the model about the structure of its input. Every model family has its own set, but the concepts are universal.

BERT uses [CLS] (classification token) at the start of every input — the model's representation at this position serves as a summary for classification tasks. [SEP] separates two segments (like a question and its context). [PAD] fills shorter sequences in a batch to the same length. [MASK] replaces tokens during masked language modeling — the model's pre-training objective is to predict what's behind the mask.

GPT-style models use <|endoftext|> to mark document boundaries during training. The model learns that this token means "what comes next is unrelated to what came before." Llama uses <s> for beginning-of-sequence and </s> for end-of-sequence. Chat models add tokens like <|im_start|> and <|im_end|> to delineate system prompts, user messages, and assistant responses.

These tokens are added to the vocabulary manually after tokenizer training. Their embeddings are randomly initialized and learned during model pre-training, just like any other token. Forgetting to include them or mishandling them during fine-tuning is a common source of mysterious bugs — the model produces garbage because it's not seeing the structural signals it was trained on.

Multilingual Tokenization

Here's where tokenization gets political. If you train a BPE tokenizer primarily on English text, common English words become single tokens. But words from other languages — say Hindi, Japanese, or Swahili — get fragmented into many small pieces. This is the fertility problem: the number of tokens per word varies dramatically across languages. English text might average 1.3 tokens per word. The same semantic content in Tamil might average 4.2 tokens per word.

This isn't just an efficiency issue — it creates real unfairness. A model with a 4,096-token context window can process roughly 3,000 words of English but only 1,000 words of Tamil. The Tamil user pays more per API call (pricing is per token), gets shorter effective context, and experiences slower inference — all because the tokenizer was trained on English-heavy data. Research by Petrov et al. showed that for some African languages, API costs can be 10x higher than English for the same semantic content.

SentencePiece was designed to address this by treating the input as a raw byte stream, with no language-specific pre-processing. It doesn't assume whitespace-separated words — critical for languages like Chinese, Japanese, and Thai that don't use spaces. Byte-level BPE, used by GPT-4's tokenizer, takes this further: the base vocabulary starts with all 256 byte values, so any UTF-8 encoded text can be represented without UNK tokens. This doesn't solve the fertility problem entirely — you still need balanced training data — but it guarantees that no input is unrepresentable.

COMMON PITFALL

If you're fine-tuning a model for a non-English language, check the tokenizer's fertility on your target language before committing. A fertility above 3.0 tokens per word is a red flag — you may need to either extend the vocabulary or choose a different base model with better multilingual coverage.

Tokenizer Efficiency and Evaluation

How do you know if your tokenizer is good? There are two key metrics. Fertility is the average number of tokens per word. Lower is better — it means your tokenizer is efficiently representing language. Compression ratio is the number of bytes in the original text divided by the number of tokens. Higher is better — it means each token carries more information.

A good English tokenizer typically achieves a fertility around 1.2–1.5 and a compression ratio of 3.5–4.5 bytes per token. For code, you want even better compression — code has a lot of repetitive structure that a well-trained tokenizer can exploit. Let's write some code to evaluate a tokenizer:

from transformers import AutoTokenizer

def analyze_tokenizer(model_name, texts):
    tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_name)
    total_tokens = 0
    total_words = 0
    total_bytes = 0

    for text in texts:
        tokens = tokenizer.encode(text)
        words = text.split()
        total_tokens += len(tokens)
        total_words += len(words)
        total_bytes += len(text.encode("utf-8"))

    fertility = total_tokens / total_words
    compression = total_bytes / total_tokens
    print(f"Model: {model_name}")
    print(f"  Fertility:    {fertility:.2f} tokens/word")
    print(f"  Compression:  {compression:.2f} bytes/token")
    print(f"  Total tokens: {total_tokens}")
    return fertility, compression

sample_texts = [
    "The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog.",
    "Tokenization is a critical preprocessing step for LLMs.",
    "Machine learning models learn patterns from data.",
    "def train_model(data, epochs=10): return model.fit(data)",
]

# Compare across models
for model in ["gpt2", "bert-base-uncased", "meta-llama/Llama-2-7b-hf"]:
    analyze_tokenizer(model, sample_texts)
    print()

You can also inspect how a specific tokenizer fragments a particular sentence, which is invaluable for debugging:

from transformers import AutoTokenizer

tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained("gpt2")

sentence = "The unbelievably complex tokenization process"
tokens = tokenizer.tokenize(sentence)
ids = tokenizer.encode(sentence)

print("Tokens:", tokens)
# ['The', ' un', 'believ', 'ably', ' complex', ' token', 'ization', ' process']
print("IDs:   ", ids)
print(f"Words: {len(sentence.split())}, Tokens: {len(tokens)}")

# Decode back to verify round-trip
decoded = tokenizer.decode(ids)
print("Decoded:", decoded)
assert decoded == sentence, "Round-trip failed!"

When building a tokenizer for a new domain — say, biomedical text or legal documents — run this analysis on a representative sample. Compare fertility against GPT-2's tokenizer as a baseline. If your domain-specific tokenizer achieves 20% lower fertility on your target text, that translates directly into 20% shorter sequences, 20% faster training, and 20% more content per context window. That's a significant return for a preprocessing step that takes minutes to train.

One final thought: tokenization is the one part of the LLM pipeline that is completely deterministic and fully inspectable. You can look at exactly how any input gets split, verify round-trip consistency, and measure efficiency on your specific data. In a field full of black boxes, the tokenizer is refreshingly transparent. Use that transparency. Inspect it, measure it, and make sure it's working for your use case before you spend millions of GPU-hours training on top of it.
