BERT Family

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 6 subtopics
TL;DR

BERT showed that bidirectional context crushes left-to-right language models on understanding tasks. Then the community spent years fixing its rough edges. RoBERTa proved BERT was undertrained, not under-designed. ALBERT shrank the parameter count without killing accuracy. DistilBERT distilled it into a production-friendly size. DeBERTa separated position from content and beat humans on SuperGLUE. XLNet avoided the [MASK] token mismatch entirely. ELECTRA made every token a training signal instead of just 15%. Each model in the family is a lesson in what happens when you question one assumption at a time.

BERT: The Model That Changed NLP

Before 2018, language models read text in one direction. GPT read left-to-right. ELMo stitched together a forward and backward LSTM, but the two directions never actually talked to each other during computation. This meant that when the model processed the word "bank" in "I went to the bank to deposit my check," it either hadn't yet seen "deposit" (left-to-right) or hadn't yet seen "I went to the" (right-to-left). Neither direction alone captures the full picture.

BERT — Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers — fixed this by doing something deceptively simple: it masked out random words and asked the model to predict them using context from both sides simultaneously. This is Masked Language Modeling (MLM). During training, 15% of input tokens are selected. Of those, 80% are replaced with a special [MASK] token, 10% are replaced with a random word, and 10% are left unchanged. The model must predict the original token at each masked position using the full surrounding context.

BERT came in two sizes: BERT-Base (12 transformer layers, 768 hidden dimensions, 12 attention heads, 110M parameters) and BERT-Large (24 layers, 1024 hidden, 16 heads, 340M parameters). Both used a WordPiece tokenizer with a 30,522-token vocabulary, trained on BookCorpus (800M words) and English Wikipedia (2,500M words). The second pre-training objective was Next Sentence Prediction (NSP): given two sentences, predict whether the second actually follows the first in the original text, or is a random sentence from elsewhere.

Once pre-trained, you fine-tune BERT on your specific task by adding a thin classification head on top. Sentiment analysis? Take the [CLS] token's representation, add a linear layer, fine-tune for three epochs. Named entity recognition? Add a token-level classifier. Question answering? Predict the start and end positions of the answer span. This pre-train-then-fine-tune paradigm swept every NLU benchmark — GLUE, SQuAD, NER — by huge margins. The era of training task-specific architectures from scratch was over.

from transformers import BertTokenizer, BertForMaskedLM
import torch

tokenizer = BertTokenizer.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
model = BertForMaskedLM.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")

text = "The capital of France is [MASK]."
inputs = tokenizer(text, return_tensors="pt")
mask_idx = (inputs["input_ids"] == tokenizer.mask_token_id).nonzero(as_tuple=True)[1]

with torch.no_grad():
    logits = model(**inputs).logits

predicted_id = logits[0, mask_idx, :].argmax(dim=-1)
print(tokenizer.decode(predicted_id))  # → "paris"

RoBERTa: BERT Done Right

In 2019, Facebook AI published a paper with a blunt thesis: BERT's architecture is fine, but its training is sloppy. RoBERTa (Robustly Optimized BERT Approach) changed nothing about the model architecture. Zero new ideas in the transformer itself. Instead, it fixed the training recipe.

First, they dropped NSP. Careful ablations showed that NSP either didn't help or actively hurt downstream performance. The model was wasting capacity learning to distinguish real sentence pairs from fake ones — a task too easy to provide a useful training signal. Second, they introduced dynamic masking: instead of masking the same positions every time a training example is seen (static masking), they generate a new random mask each epoch. This effectively multiplies the diversity of training data the model sees. Third — and this was the biggest factor — they scaled up training dramatically: 160GB of text (10× BERT's data), batch sizes of 8,192 sequences, and training for far more steps.

The result? RoBERTa matched or beat every BERT-Large result on GLUE, SQuAD, and RACE, without any architectural change. The lesson was uncomfortable but important: before you invent a clever new mechanism, make sure you've actually trained your existing model properly. BERT was undertrained, not under-designed.

ALBERT: Shrinking the Giant

BERT-Large has 340 million parameters. Most of them sit in two places: the embedding matrix and the repeated transformer layers. ALBERT (A Lite BERT) asked: can we compress both?

The first trick is factorized embedding parameterization. In BERT, the embedding matrix maps each of 30,000 vocabulary tokens directly to a 768-dimensional (or 1024-dimensional) vector. That's a V×H matrix — enormous. ALBERT splits this into two smaller matrices: V×E and E×H, where E is a much smaller embedding dimension (say, 128). The token first maps to a low-dimensional space, then projects up to the hidden size. Instead of 30,000 × 1,024 = 30.7M parameters, you get 30,000 × 128 + 128 × 1,024 = 3.97M. An 87% reduction in embedding parameters alone.

The second trick is cross-layer parameter sharing. Instead of having 12 (or 24) independent transformer layers each with their own weights, every layer shares the same set of parameters. The model still has 12 layers — it still runs the input through 12 sequential transformer blocks — but all 12 blocks use identical weights. This collapses the transformer parameters by a factor of 12.

ALBERT also replaced NSP with Sentence Order Prediction (SOP). Instead of asking "are these two sentences from the same document?" (which is trivially solved by topic matching), SOP asks "are these two consecutive sentences in the right order or swapped?" This forces the model to learn fine-grained discourse coherence — a harder and more useful signal.

The tradeoff is honest and worth understanding. ALBERT-xxlarge has only 235M parameters (vs. BERT-Large's 340M) but achieves better benchmark scores. However, inference speed doesn't improve. You still run 12 sequential transformer layers. The parameter sharing saves memory and helps with regularization, but it doesn't reduce computation. ALBERT is smaller to store and easier to train, but not faster to run.

DistilBERT: Knowledge Distillation for Production

Research models are great for papers. Production needs something that fits in a container, runs on a CPU, and responds in under 50 milliseconds. DistilBERT was Hugging Face's answer: use knowledge distillation to compress BERT into a smaller model that retains nearly all of its ability.

The idea behind knowledge distillation is elegant. You have a large, well-trained "teacher" model (BERT-Base). You train a smaller "student" model (6 layers instead of 12, same hidden dimension) to mimic the teacher's outputs, not just the hard labels. The student learns from three signals simultaneously:

  • Distillation loss: the KL divergence between the teacher's and student's softmax distributions (using a temperature parameter to soften the probabilities, exposing the teacher's uncertainty about wrong answers — this is where most of the knowledge transfer happens)
  • MLM loss: the standard masked language modeling loss, so the student also learns from the raw data
  • Cosine embedding loss: aligning the student's hidden representations with the teacher's, so internal features match, not just outputs

The result: DistilBERT is 40% smaller (66M vs. 110M parameters), 60% faster at inference, and retains 97% of BERT's performance on GLUE. For many real-world applications — chatbot intent classification, email routing, search ranking — that 3% accuracy loss is invisible, but the 60% speed gain is the difference between a viable product and one that can't meet latency requirements.

# Using DistilBERT is identical to BERT — same API, smaller model
from transformers import pipeline

# BERT-Base: 110M params, ~410MB
classifier_bert = pipeline("sentiment-analysis", model="bert-base-uncased")

# DistilBERT: 66M params, ~250MB, 60% faster
classifier_distil = pipeline("sentiment-analysis", model="distilbert-base-uncased")

text = "This movie was surprisingly good despite the bad reviews."
print(classifier_distil(text))  # same API, faster response

DeBERTa: Disentangling Position and Content

Here's a subtle problem with BERT's attention mechanism. When BERT computes attention between two tokens, it mixes their content (what the token means) and their position (where it sits in the sequence) into a single vector from the start. The attention score between "cat" at position 3 and "sat" at position 5 is computed from fused content-position embeddings. But conceptually, the relevance between two words has multiple components: is "cat" semantically related to "sat"? Does position 3 typically attend to position 5? Does "cat" tend to attend to things two positions ahead?

DeBERTa (Decoding-enhanced BERT with Disentangled Attention) from Microsoft separates these components into distinct computations. Each token gets two separate vectors: a content vector (what it means) and a position vector (where it is). Attention is then computed as the sum of three terms:

DeBERTa Disentangled Attention:

  Attention(i, j) = Content_i → Content_j    (word-to-word relevance)
                  + Content_i → Position_j   (word-to-where relevance)
                  + Position_i → Content_j   (where-to-word relevance)

  ┌─────────┐     ┌─────────┐
  │ Token i  │     │ Token j  │
  └────┬─────┘     └────┬─────┘
       │                │
   ┌───┴───┐        ┌───┴───┐
   │Content│        │Content│
   │ Vec_i │        │ Vec_j │
   └───┬───┘        └───┬───┘
       │    ╲      ╱    │
       │     ╲    ╱     │
       │      ╲  ╱      │
   ┌───┴───┐   ╳   ┌───┴───┐
   │Posit. │  ╱ ╲  │Posit. │
   │ Vec_i │ ╱   ╲ │ Vec_j │
   └───┬───┘      └───┬───┘
       │               │
       └───────┬───────┘
               │
        ┌──────┴──────┐
        │  C→C + C→P  │
        │   + P→C     │
        │  (3 scores) │
        └─────────────┘

Notice the missing fourth term: position-to-position (P→P). DeBERTa drops it because relative position-to-position interactions are the same for every sentence — they carry no useful signal.

There's a second innovation: the Enhanced Mask Decoder. During pre-training, DeBERTa uses only relative positions throughout its layers (which helps with generalization). But for the final MLM prediction, absolute position matters — "the" at position 0 is more likely than "the" at position 50. So DeBERTa adds absolute position information only at the very end, right before the prediction head. This gives the model the best of both worlds: relative position reasoning throughout, absolute position awareness at decision time.

DeBERTa was the first model to surpass human performance on the SuperGLUE benchmark. Its v3 variant (DeBERTa-v3) combined the disentangled attention with ELECTRA-style training (more on that below) and remains one of the strongest encoder models available today.

XLNet: Permutation Language Modeling

BERT's MLM training has a subtle flaw. During pre-training, the model sees [MASK] tokens. During fine-tuning and inference, there are no [MASK] tokens. This pretrain-finetune mismatch means the model is trained on a slightly different data distribution than it's tested on. Also, because masked tokens are predicted independently (BERT doesn't model dependencies between masked positions), the model misses correlations. If both "New" and "York" are masked in "I live in [MASK] [MASK]", BERT predicts each independently — it might predict "New" and "Jersey" because it doesn't condition one masked prediction on the other.

XLNet solved this with Permutation Language Modeling (PLM). Instead of masking tokens, XLNet considers all possible orderings (permutations) of the input sequence. For each permutation, it trains an autoregressive model that predicts each token given only the tokens that come before it in that particular permutation. Across all permutations, every token gets to condition on every possible subset of other tokens — achieving bidirectional context without ever using a [MASK] token.

To make this work, XLNet uses two-stream self-attention. The content stream is standard self-attention — it can see the current token and all previous tokens in the permuted order. The query stream can see all previous tokens but not the current token (because that's the one being predicted). Without this separation, the model would trivially look at the answer.

XLNet also inherits segment recurrence from Transformer-XL. Instead of processing each fixed-length segment independently, it caches hidden states from the previous segment and lets the current segment attend to them. This gives the model a much longer effective context window without quadratic memory cost. In practice, XLNet outperformed BERT on 20 benchmarks. The downside? It's significantly more expensive to train (all those permutations), and the implementation is complex enough that the community largely moved on to simpler approaches like RoBERTa for everyday use.

ELECTRA and the Road to ModernBERT

Here's the fundamental inefficiency of MLM: only 15% of tokens get masked, so only 15% of tokens produce a training signal. The other 85% pass through the model, consume compute, and contribute nothing to the loss function. ELECTRA (Efficiently Learning an Encoder that Classifies Token Replacements Accurately) fixed this by giving every token a training signal.

ELECTRA uses a generator-discriminator setup, loosely inspired by GANs but trained differently. A small generator (a lightweight MLM model) fills in masked tokens with plausible but sometimes wrong replacements. Then a larger discriminator examines every token in the resulting sequence and predicts, for each one, whether it's the original token or a replacement. This is replaced token detection — a binary classification at every position.

ELECTRA: Generator-Discriminator Training

Original:    "the chef cooked the meal"
Masked:      "the [M]  cooked the [M]"
                   │                │
           ┌───────┴────────┐      │
           │   Generator    │      │
           │  (small MLM)   │      │
           └───────┬────────┘      │
                   │               │
Generated:   "the artist cooked the soup"
                   │                │
                   ▼                ▼
             ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
             │        Discriminator            │
             │   (full-size encoder model)     │
             │                                 │
             │  "the"    → original  ✓         │
             │  "artist" → replaced  ✓         │
             │  "cooked" → original  ✓         │
             │  "the"    → original  ✓         │
             │  "soup"   → replaced  ✓         │
             │                                 │
             │  Every token gets a signal!     │
             └─────────────────────────────────┘

The result is striking. ELECTRA-Small (14M parameters) matches GPT-Large (774M parameters) on GLUE when both are trained with the same compute budget. ELECTRA-Base matches RoBERTa-Large with 1/4 the compute. The sample efficiency gain is enormous: roughly 4× more learning per FLOP compared to MLM.

ModernBERT and the Family Today

ModernBERT (2024) represents the latest evolution: it combines the best ideas from the entire family — ELECTRA-style training, rotary positional embeddings (from the GPT lineage), Flash Attention for efficiency, an 8,192 token context window, and modern training infrastructure. It's trained on 2 trillion tokens of English text. ModernBERT-Base (149M params) and ModernBERT-Large (395M params) set new state-of-the-art results across classification, retrieval, and code search tasks, while being significantly faster than DeBERTa due to architectural optimizations.

The BERT family has also branched into specialized domains. BioBERT was trained on PubMed abstracts and PMC full-text articles for biomedical tasks. SciBERT trained on 1.14M papers from Semantic Scholar for scientific NLP. FinBERT was fine-tuned on financial news and earnings calls for sentiment analysis in finance. The recipe is always the same: take a BERT variant, continue pre-training on domain-specific text, and fine-tune on domain tasks. The sentence-transformers library turned BERT-family models into powerful embedding engines, enabling semantic search and clustering by training with contrastive objectives on sentence pairs.

The Evolution Story

Step back and look at the arc. BERT proved that bidirectional pre-training works. RoBERTa showed the architecture was fine — we just needed better training. ALBERT attacked parameter bloat. DistilBERT attacked inference cost. DeBERTa questioned how position and content should interact. XLNet eliminated the [MASK] mismatch entirely. ELECTRA questioned whether we should be masking at all. ModernBERT combined the survivors of these experiments with modern engineering.

Each model in this family didn't just propose a new technique — it identified a specific weakness in the previous approach and provided an empirical answer. That's how good research works. You don't need to use all these models. For most practitioners today, the choice is simple: DeBERTa-v3 or ModernBERT if you need maximum accuracy on understanding tasks, DistilBERT or a small ELECTRA if you need speed, and a sentence-transformer if you need embeddings. But knowing the evolution helps you understand why these models are built the way they are — and that understanding is what lets you pick the right tool when the next project lands on your desk.
