Quantization & Model Compression

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 12 subtopics
TL;DR

Large models are powerful but obscenely expensive to run. Quantization shrinks weights from 16-bit floats to 4- or 8-bit integers, cutting memory by 2–4× with surprisingly small accuracy loss. Pruning removes unimportant weights entirely. Distillation trains a small model to mimic a big one. Together these techniques let you run 70-billion-parameter models on a single GPU — or even a laptop. This section walks through the math, the key algorithms (GPTQ, AWQ, SmoothQuant, QLoRA), and the practical tradeoffs you'll face in production.

Why Compression Matters

Let's start with a number that should make you uncomfortable. A 70-billion-parameter model stored in FP16 (two bytes per weight) needs 140 GB of RAM just to load. That's two A100 GPUs at $2/hour each, minimum. And that's before you account for activations, KV cache, or the optimizer states if you're fine-tuning. Most teams — and most individuals — simply cannot afford this.

Now imagine you could represent each weight with 4 bits instead of 16. That same 70B model drops to about 35 GB. That fits on a single 48 GB GPU. Drop to 2-bit with some cleverness and you're running it on a gaming laptop. This isn't a hypothetical — people do it every day with tools like llama.cpp. Model compression is the set of techniques that make this possible, and it's one of the most practically important areas in modern AI.

There are three main families of compression. Quantization reduces the numerical precision of weights (and sometimes activations) — from 32-bit floats down to 8-bit integers or lower. Pruning removes weights entirely, setting them to zero and hoping the model doesn't notice. Knowledge distillation trains a smaller model from scratch to mimic the behavior of the large one. Each has different tradeoffs, and in practice you often combine them.

Quantization Fundamentals

At its core, quantization is about representing continuous values with a smaller set of discrete values. Your model weights are 16-bit or 32-bit floating-point numbers — that's a huge amount of precision. Quantization asks: do we really need all those bits? For most weights, the answer is no.

Symmetric Quantization

The simplest scheme is symmetric quantization. You find the maximum absolute value in a tensor, then linearly map the range [-max, max] onto the integer range [-127, 127] for INT8. The scale factor is s = max / 127. To quantize a weight w, you compute q = round(w / s). To dequantize, you compute w ≈ q × s. Simple, fast, and symmetric around zero.

Asymmetric Quantization

Asymmetric quantization handles distributions that aren't centered on zero. You map the actual range [min, max] onto [0, 255] for unsigned INT8, using both a scale factor and a zero-point offset: s = (max - min) / 255 and z = round(-min / s). This is more expressive but slightly more expensive at inference time because every multiply-add now includes the zero-point correction.

Group Quantization

Here's the problem with per-tensor quantization: one outlier weight can ruin everything. If 99.9% of your weights live in [-0.5, 0.5] but one weight is 10.0, your scale factor gets stretched to accommodate that outlier, and all the small weights get squashed into a tiny fraction of your integer range. Group quantization solves this by splitting the weight tensor into small groups — typically 32 or 128 values — and computing a separate scale factor for each group. This dramatically improves accuracy at the cost of storing one extra scale factor per group.

# Basic symmetric quantization in NumPy
import numpy as np

def symmetric_quantize(weights, bits=8):
    """Quantize weights to signed integer range."""
    qmax = 2 ** (bits - 1) - 1          # 127 for INT8, 7 for INT4
    scale = np.max(np.abs(weights)) / qmax
    quantized = np.clip(np.round(weights / scale), -qmax, qmax).astype(np.int8)
    return quantized, scale

def dequantize(quantized, scale):
    return quantized.astype(np.float32) * scale

# Example: quantize a small weight tensor
weights = np.array([0.12, -0.37, 0.85, -0.02, 0.41], dtype=np.float32)
q, s = symmetric_quantize(weights, bits=8)
restored = dequantize(q, s)
print(f"Original:  {weights}")
print(f"Quantized: {q}  (scale={s:.6f})")
print(f"Restored:  {restored}")
print(f"Max error: {np.max(np.abs(weights - restored)):.6f}")

Post-Training Quantization (PTQ)

Post-training quantization is exactly what it sounds like: you take a fully trained model and quantize it, no retraining required. This is the path of least resistance, and for INT8, it works shockingly well. Most models lose less than 0.1% accuracy going from FP16 to INT8 with simple PTQ.

INT4 is where it gets interesting. You're going from 16 bits to 4 — that's a 4× memory reduction, but you only have 16 possible values per weight. Naive round-to-nearest INT4 quantization often causes noticeable quality drops, especially for larger models where a few outlier weights carry disproportionate importance. This is why the smarter algorithms below were invented.

One key concept for quantization-aware approaches is the Straight-Through Estimator (STE). Quantization is a staircase function — it has zero gradient almost everywhere, which breaks backpropagation. The STE simply pretends the gradient of the rounding operation is 1, passing gradients straight through as if the rounding didn't happen. It's mathematically unjustified but works remarkably well in practice.

GPTQ

GPTQ (GPT Quantization) is the gold standard for weight-only quantization of large language models. It's based on a family of methods called Optimal Brain Quantization, and the core idea is deceptively elegant: quantize weights one at a time, and after each quantization, adjust the remaining unquantized weights to compensate for the error you just introduced.

GPTQ processes the model one layer at a time. For each layer, it uses a small calibration dataset (typically 128 samples) to measure how the layer's output changes when weights are quantized. The Hessian matrix — the second-derivative of the reconstruction error with respect to the weights — tells you which weights are most sensitive to perturbation. GPTQ uses this information to choose a quantization order and to compute optimal error-compensating updates for the remaining weights.

In practice, GPTQ achieves 4-bit quantization of models like LLaMA-70B with perplexity increases of less than 0.5 on WikiText. The quantized models run with GPU-accelerated inference through libraries like AutoGPTQ and ExLlama, often at 3–4× the throughput of FP16 because you're moving less data from GPU memory to compute cores. Memory bandwidth is the bottleneck for LLM inference, so smaller weights means faster generation.

AWQ (Activation-Aware Weight Quantization)

AWQ starts from a different insight than GPTQ. Not all weights are equally important, and the way to find the important ones isn't to look at the weights themselves — it's to look at the activations they multiply. A weight channel that consistently multiplies against large activation values has an outsized impact on the output. Quantization error in that channel gets amplified by the large activations.

AWQ's solution is almost comically simple. It identifies important weight channels by measuring activation magnitudes on a calibration set. Then it scales those important channels up by a factor before quantization, effectively giving them more of the integer range to work with. It scales the corresponding activations down by the same factor, so the math stays equivalent. After quantization, the important weights have lower relative error because they occupied more of the quantization grid.

The results are impressive. AWQ often matches or beats GPTQ in perplexity benchmarks, especially at aggressive bit widths like 3-bit. It's also faster to run the quantization process itself — GPTQ needs to solve a series of least-squares problems per layer, while AWQ just needs to compute channel-wise activation statistics. For deployment, AWQ models pair well with GPU inference engines like vLLM and TensorRT-LLM.

GGUF and llama.cpp

If GPTQ and AWQ are the GPU-optimized path, GGUF and llama.cpp are the democratization path. llama.cpp is a C/C++ inference engine that runs quantized LLMs on CPUs using SIMD instructions — no GPU required. GGUF (GPT-Generated Unified Format) is its model format: a single self-contained file with all weights, tokenizer, and metadata.

What makes GGUF especially clever is its K-quant system. Instead of quantizing every tensor to the same bit width, K-quants assign different precisions to different tensor types. Attention weights, which are more sensitive, might get 6-bit quantization. Feed-forward network weights, which are more redundant, get 4-bit. The naming convention tells you the scheme: Q4_K_M means 4-bit with K-quant, medium quality (meaning the important tensors get more bits). Q5_K_S means 5-bit, small quality tier.

llama.cpp also supports partial GPU offloading — you can put some layers on a GPU and keep the rest on CPU. This is what makes it possible to run a 70B model on a MacBook with 64 GB of unified memory or a desktop with 32 GB RAM and a 12 GB GPU. The performance won't match a datacenter A100, but you're generating tokens locally, privately, for free. This is genuinely a big deal.

SmoothQuant

All the methods above focus on quantizing weights. But for maximum speed, you want to quantize activations too — then both sides of your matrix multiplications are integers, and you can use dedicated INT8 tensor cores. The problem: activations have massive outliers. A few channels might have values 100× larger than the rest, making them near-impossible to quantize.

SmoothQuant handles this with a mathematically elegant trick. It observes that the difficulty of quantizing activations and weights is somewhat complementary — if activations are hard (outliers), the corresponding weights tend to be easy (smooth), and vice versa. SmoothQuant introduces a per-channel scaling factor s that divides the activations and multiplies the weights: Y = (X diag(s)⁻¹) · (diag(s) W). Since diag(s)⁻¹ · diag(s) = I, the output is unchanged, but the quantization difficulty has been migrated from activations to weights. With the right s, both sides become easy to quantize.

This enables W8A8 quantization — 8-bit weights and 8-bit activations — with negligible accuracy loss for models up to 530B parameters. On hardware with INT8 tensor cores (like NVIDIA A100 or H100), this translates to nearly 2× inference speedup over FP16.

QLoRA

QLoRA bridges quantization and fine-tuning in a beautiful way. The idea: keep your base model frozen in 4-bit quantized form, but add LoRA adapters (small trainable low-rank matrices) in full BFloat16 precision. During the forward pass, you dequantize the base weights on the fly, add the LoRA contribution, and compute the output. Only the LoRA adapters receive gradient updates.

QLoRA introduces three key innovations. First, NormalFloat4 (NF4) is an information-theoretically optimal 4-bit data type for normally distributed data. Instead of uniformly spacing the 16 quantization levels, NF4 spaces them according to the quantiles of a standard normal distribution, so that each level represents an equal probability mass. Since neural network weights are approximately normally distributed, NF4 minimizes expected quantization error. Second, double quantization quantizes the quantization constants themselves — the per-group scale factors get quantized from FP32 to FP8, saving an additional 0.37 bits per parameter. Third, paged optimizers use CPU memory as overflow when GPU memory spikes during gradient computation, preventing out-of-memory crashes.

The upshot: you can fine-tune a 65B parameter model on a single 48 GB GPU (like an A6000 or A100). Before QLoRA, this would have required multiple 80 GB A100s. The quality is remarkably close to full-precision fine-tuning — typically within 1% on downstream benchmarks.

# Loading a GPTQ-quantized model with transformers + auto-gptq
from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, AutoTokenizer

model_id = "TheBloke/Llama-2-7B-GPTQ"
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_id)
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
    model_id,
    device_map="auto",        # automatically distribute across GPUs
    trust_remote_code=False,
)

prompt = "Explain quantization in one sentence:"
inputs = tokenizer(prompt, return_tensors="pt").to(model.device)
outputs = model.generate(**inputs, max_new_tokens=50)
print(tokenizer.decode(outputs[0], skip_special_tokens=True))

# Loading a GGUF model with llama-cpp-python
from llama_cpp import Llama

llm = Llama(
    model_path="./models/llama-2-7b.Q4_K_M.gguf",
    n_ctx=2048,          # context window
    n_gpu_layers=20,     # offload 20 layers to GPU, rest on CPU
)

output = llm(
    "Explain quantization in one sentence:",
    max_tokens=50,
    temperature=0.7,
)
print(output["choices"][0]["text"])

Pruning & Sparsity

Quantization keeps all the weights but makes them smaller. Pruning takes the opposite approach: remove weights entirely. Set them to zero, and if the model barely notices, those weights weren't doing much anyway.

Magnitude Pruning

The simplest form is magnitude pruning: sort all weights by absolute value, and zero out the smallest ones. You'd be surprised how far this goes — many models can lose 50–80% of their weights with minimal accuracy drop if you prune gradually during training and allow the model to adapt.

The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis

The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis, proposed by Frankle and Carlin in 2019, makes a bolder claim: within a large randomly initialized network, there exists a small subnetwork (a "winning ticket") that, if trained in isolation from the same initialization, would reach the same accuracy as the full model. The large network isn't powerful because it has more capacity — it's powerful because it has more chances of containing a good subnetwork. This is a profound insight about why overparameterization helps in training even though the final model is redundant.

Structured vs. Unstructured Pruning

Unstructured pruning zeros individual weights wherever they are. This creates sparse matrices, but standard hardware can't exploit random sparsity efficiently — you still need to load the full matrix into memory and skip the zeros during computation. Structured pruning removes entire rows, columns, attention heads, or channels. The resulting model is genuinely smaller and faster on standard hardware, but the accuracy cost is higher because you're removing entire functional units.

SparseGPT and Movement Pruning

SparseGPT applies GPTQ-like logic to pruning: remove weights one at a time and adjust remaining weights to compensate for the reconstruction error. It can prune LLMs to 50–60% sparsity in one shot without retraining, something that was previously thought impossible for models this large.

Movement pruning takes a different angle entirely. Instead of removing the smallest weights, it removes the weights that are moving toward zero during fine-tuning. The intuition: if the training process is trying to shrink a weight, it's telling you that weight isn't useful for this task. Weights that are small but growing might be very important. This consistently outperforms magnitude pruning for transfer learning scenarios.

Knowledge Distillation

Knowledge distillation is fundamentally different from quantization and pruning. Instead of compressing an existing model, you train a new, smaller student model to mimic a large teacher model. The student doesn't learn from the ground-truth labels directly — it learns from the teacher's predictions.

White-Box vs. Black-Box Distillation

In white-box distillation, you have access to the teacher's internal representations. The student learns to match the teacher's full probability distribution over the vocabulary — not just the top prediction. This is far more informative than a one-hot label because the distribution reveals relationships between classes. When the teacher says "this is 70% cat, 20% lynx, 5% tiger," that 20% lynx signal teaches the student about the similarity between cats and lynxes.

Black-box distillation only uses the teacher's outputs, treating it as an API. This is how most distillation of proprietary models works — you query GPT-4 and train a smaller model on its outputs. Less information gets transferred, but it works with any teacher, even closed-source ones.

Temperature Scaling

Temperature scaling is the secret sauce. Before the softmax, you divide the logits by a temperature parameter T. At T=1, you get the normal output distribution. At higher temperatures like T=3 or T=5, the distribution gets softer — the differences between probabilities shrink. This reveals more information about the teacher's "dark knowledge" — its opinions about the relative likelihood of wrong answers. The distillation loss typically combines a soft-target loss (student vs. teacher at high temperature) with a hard-target loss (student vs. ground truth).

Success Stories

DistilBERT is perhaps the most famous distillation success. It's 40% smaller than BERT, 60% faster, and retains 97% of BERT's language understanding. TinyBERT goes further by also distilling the intermediate representations (attention matrices and hidden states), not just the final output. Self-distillation is a curious variant where the teacher and student are the same model — you use predictions from earlier training epochs, or from deeper layers of the same network, as soft targets. It consistently improves performance at no additional inference cost.

Speculative Decoding

Speculative decoding isn't compression — the model stays the same size. But it's too useful a speedup to skip in a section about making LLMs practical. The insight: autoregressive decoding generates one token per forward pass, and most of the cost is moving weights from memory, not computation. What if you could generate multiple tokens per pass?

Here's how it works. You run a small, fast draft model (maybe a 1B model) to generate K candidate tokens autoregressively (cheap because the model is tiny). Then you feed all K tokens into the large model in a single forward pass, verifying them in parallel. If the large model agrees with a token, you keep it. If it disagrees, you reject it and all subsequent tokens, then sample a corrected token from the large model's distribution.

The elegant part: rejection sampling guarantees that the output distribution is mathematically identical to running the large model alone. There is zero quality loss — the output is the same, token for token in distribution. You're just getting there faster. In practice, if the draft model agrees with the large model 70–80% of the time, you get 2–3× speedup. The better the draft model approximates the large model, the bigger the win.

Practical Tradeoffs — Choosing Your Approach

After all this theory, here's the decision framework I'd use in practice.

If you want the easiest win with minimal risk, start with INT8 post-training quantization. It's nearly lossless, halves your memory, and most frameworks support it out of the box. Literally one line of configuration in many serving frameworks.

If you need serious compression for GPU inference, GPTQ or AWQ at 4-bit are your workhorses. AWQ tends to edge out GPTQ on newer models and is faster to quantize. Both deliver roughly 4× memory reduction with very small quality loss. Pair them with vLLM or TGI for production serving.

If you're running on consumer hardware — a laptop, a desktop, or a Mac — GGUF with llama.cpp is the answer. The K-quant system gives you fine-grained control over the quality-size tradeoff. Start with Q4_K_M for a good balance, go to Q5_K_M if you want better quality, or Q3_K_S if you're really tight on memory.

If you need to fine-tune a large model on limited hardware, QLoRA is the clear choice. It lets you fine-tune models that are 4× larger than what your GPU would otherwise support. The quality is surprisingly close to full fine-tuning.

If you need both weight and activation quantization for maximum throughput on datacenter hardware, SmoothQuant W8A8 unlocks INT8 tensor core performance.

If you need a genuinely smaller, faster model for production — not a compressed version of a big model, but an architecturally small model — knowledge distillation is the right tool. It requires more upfront effort (you need training data and compute for the distillation process) but produces a model that's fast on any hardware.

And if you just want faster generation without changing the model at all, speculative decoding is a free lunch. Zero quality cost, 2–3× speedup, and it stacks with all other compression techniques.

The overarching theme: you don't have to choose one technique. Production systems routinely combine a distilled model, quantized to 4-bit, served with speculative decoding. The compression techniques are complementary, and the gap between "what fits in a datacenter" and "what runs on your laptop" keeps shrinking. That's the real story here — not just efficiency, but access.
