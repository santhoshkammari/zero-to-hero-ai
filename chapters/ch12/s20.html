Inference Optimization

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 3 subtopics
TL;DR

Training a model is only half the battle — serving it fast and cheaply is where the real engineering lives. Decoding strategies control what the model says (greedy, beam search, sampling with temperature/top-k/top-p). Serving systems control how fast it says it — vLLM's PagedAttention eliminates GPU memory waste, TensorRT-LLM fuses and quantizes kernels for raw throughput, and continuous batching keeps the GPU saturated instead of idling while tokens drip out. In production, you balance latency budgets (P50/P99), cost-per-token, and autoscaling to keep your CFO and your users both happy.

You've spent weeks training a 70B-parameter model. It scores great on benchmarks. Then you deploy it and a single request takes 12 seconds, your GPU utilization hovers at 30%, and your cloud bill makes your manager cry. Welcome to the inference problem. Training is embarrassingly parallel — you throw batches at the GPU and life is good. Inference is sequential, memory-bound, and latency-sensitive. Every token the model generates depends on the previous one. This section is about making that sequential nightmare as fast and cheap as possible.

Decoding Strategies

Before we optimize how fast the model generates, we need to understand what it generates. A language model outputs a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary at each step. The decoding strategy is the algorithm that turns that distribution into an actual token. Different strategies produce wildly different outputs from the same model.

Greedy and Beam Search

Greedy decoding is the simplest strategy: pick the token with the highest probability at each step. It's fast, deterministic, and often boring. It tends to produce repetitive, safe text because it never explores alternatives.

Beam search keeps the top-k partial sequences (called the beam width) at each step and expands all of them. With a beam width of 5, you're tracking the 5 best sequences simultaneously. More beams means better search but linear cost increase. The tradeoff: beam width 4–5 is the sweet spot for translation tasks. Beyond 10, quality often degrades because beam search has a length bias — it prefers shorter sequences since each additional token multiplies in more probabilities less than 1.0. Length normalization fixes this by dividing the log-probability by the sequence length raised to a tunable power α (typically 0.6–0.7).

# Beam search score with length normalization
import math

def normalized_score(log_prob, length, alpha=0.6):
    """Divide cumulative log-prob by length penalty to avoid short-sequence bias."""
    length_penalty = ((5 + length) / 6) ** alpha
    return log_prob / length_penalty

# Sequence A: short but confident
score_a = normalized_score(log_prob=-2.1, length=5)
# Sequence B: longer, slightly less confident per-token
score_b = normalized_score(log_prob=-4.8, length=12)
print(f"Seq A: {score_a:.3f}, Seq B: {score_b:.3f}")

Sampling: Temperature, Top-k, Top-p

For creative tasks — chatbots, storytelling, brainstorming — you don't want the single "best" answer. You want diversity. Temperature scales the logits before softmax. Temperature = 1.0 is the raw distribution. Below 1.0, the distribution gets sharper (more deterministic). Above 1.0, it flattens out (more random). Temperature = 0.0 is equivalent to greedy.

Top-k sampling restricts the pool to the k most probable tokens, then re-normalizes and samples. Setting k=50 means the model can only pick from its top 50 candidates. The problem: sometimes the distribution is very peaked (only 3 good choices) and sometimes it's flat (200 reasonable choices). A fixed k handles neither well.

Top-p (nucleus) sampling solves this by dynamically choosing the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds p. If the model is confident, that might be 5 tokens. If it's uncertain, it might be 500. A typical value is p=0.9 or p=0.95. In practice, most production systems combine temperature with top-p.

Typical sampling takes a different angle: instead of filtering by probability mass, it filters by information content. It selects tokens whose surprisal (negative log probability) is close to the expected surprisal of the distribution. This tends to produce text that feels more natural because it avoids both the boringly predictable tokens and the wildly improbable ones.

Repetition Control and Advanced Methods

Repetition penalties reduce the logit of any token that already appeared in the generated text. Without them, models love to get stuck in loops — "The cat sat on the mat on the mat on the mat." A penalty multiplier of 1.1–1.3 usually breaks the cycle without making the text incoherent.

Contrastive decoding uses a smaller "amateur" model alongside your main model. It boosts tokens where the expert and amateur disagree most — the intuition being that the expert's unique knowledge lives in those gaps. Minimum Bayes Risk (MBR) decoding generates many candidates and picks the one most similar to all others — a consensus approach. Constrained decoding forces the output to satisfy a grammar, like valid JSON or a specific schema. This is critical for tool-calling LLMs where malformed output breaks your pipeline.

Stop sequences are simple but essential: you tell the model "when you generate this string, stop." For chat models, that's usually the end-of-turn token. For code generation, it might be a closing backtick fence. Without stop sequences, the model will happily generate forever.

Inference Serving Systems

Decoding strategy is about quality. Serving is about speed and cost. A raw PyTorch model doing model.generate() in a loop will serve maybe 5–10 requests per second on an A100. Production systems need 10–100x that. Here's how.

vLLM and PagedAttention — Deep Dive

vLLM is the open-source serving engine that changed the game. Its core insight: the KV cache (the stored key-value pairs from previous tokens in the attention mechanism) is the bottleneck. In a naive implementation, you pre-allocate a contiguous block of GPU memory for the maximum possible sequence length for every request. If your max length is 4096 tokens but the average response is 200 tokens, you're wasting 95% of your KV cache memory. That wasted memory means fewer concurrent requests, which means lower throughput.

PagedAttention borrows virtual memory concepts from operating systems. Instead of contiguous allocation, the KV cache is split into fixed-size pages (blocks). Each sequence gets a page table that maps logical positions to physical memory blocks. Pages are allocated on demand as the sequence grows. When a sequence finishes, its pages are immediately freed for reuse. This eliminates both internal fragmentation (wasted space within an allocation) and external fragmentation (gaps between allocations).

# vLLM serving — dead simple API
from vllm import LLM, SamplingParams

# Load model with PagedAttention enabled by default
llm = LLM(
    model="meta-llama/Llama-3-8B-Instruct",
    tensor_parallel_size=2,       # split across 2 GPUs
    gpu_memory_utilization=0.90,  # use 90% of GPU RAM for KV cache
    max_model_len=8192,
)

# Batch of prompts — vLLM handles scheduling internally
prompts = [
    "Explain quantum computing in one paragraph.",
    "Write a Python function to merge two sorted lists.",
    "What causes the northern lights?",
]
params = SamplingParams(temperature=0.7, top_p=0.9, max_tokens=256)

# This runs continuous batching under the hood
outputs = llm.generate(prompts, params)
for out in outputs:
    print(out.outputs[0].text[:80], "...")

The second key innovation in vLLM is continuous batching (also called iteration-level batching). Traditional batching waits for an entire batch of requests to finish before starting the next batch. If one request generates 500 tokens and another generates 20, the short request's GPU slot sits idle for 480 steps. Continuous batching inserts new requests into empty slots at every iteration. The moment a request finishes, a waiting request takes its place. This can improve throughput by 2–4x compared to static batching.

vLLM also supports prefix caching: if multiple requests share the same system prompt (common in chatbot deployments), the KV cache for that prefix is computed once and shared across all requests. For a 1000-token system prompt, this saves both compute and memory for every single request.

📊 Real-World Numbers

On a single A100-80GB, vLLM can serve Llama-3-8B at roughly 2000–3000 output tokens per second across concurrent users — about 10–24x faster than naive HuggingFace generation. The gains come almost entirely from PagedAttention + continuous batching eliminating memory waste and GPU idle time.

TensorRT-LLM — Deep Dive

TensorRT-LLM is NVIDIA's purpose-built inference compiler for large language models. Where vLLM optimizes memory management and scheduling, TensorRT-LLM optimizes the actual computation at the CUDA kernel level. It takes your model and compiles it into a fused, optimized execution plan that squeezes every FLOP out of the hardware.

The key optimizations include: kernel fusion — combining multiple small operations (LayerNorm + Linear + GELU) into a single GPU kernel to eliminate memory round-trips; quantization — running weights and activations in FP8, INT8, or INT4 with calibrated scaling factors to halve or quarter memory bandwidth requirements; and flash attention integration — using memory-efficient attention kernels that avoid materializing the full attention matrix.

# TensorRT-LLM: build an optimized engine from a HuggingFace model
# Step 1: Convert checkpoint
# python convert_checkpoint.py --model_dir ./llama-3-8b \
#     --output_dir ./trt_ckpt --dtype float16

# Step 2: Build the TRT-LLM engine with quantization
# trtllm-build --checkpoint_dir ./trt_ckpt \
#     --output_dir ./trt_engine \
#     --gemm_plugin float16 \
#     --max_batch_size 64 \
#     --max_input_len 2048 \
#     --max_seq_len 4096 \
#     --use_fp8_context_fmha enable

# Step 3: Serve with the Triton backend
import tensorrt_llm
from tensorrt_llm.runtime import ModelRunner

runner = ModelRunner.from_dir("./trt_engine")
outputs = runner.generate(
    batch_input_ids=tokenized_prompts,
    max_new_tokens=256,
    temperature=0.7,
    top_p=0.9,
)

The tradeoff with TensorRT-LLM is build complexity. You must compile an engine for each specific GPU architecture (H100 vs A100 vs L40S), each quantization level, and each maximum sequence length. Changing any of these means rebuilding. vLLM is "pip install and go"; TensorRT-LLM is "invest an afternoon, get 30–50% more throughput." For high-volume production, that throughput gain justifies the effort. For prototyping, vLLM wins on developer experience.

⚠️ Engine Portability

A TensorRT-LLM engine compiled for A100 will NOT run on H100, and vice versa. Each GPU architecture has different tensor core configurations. Always build on the same GPU type you deploy to, or maintain separate engine builds per target.

Other Serving Frameworks

Text Generation Inference (TGI) by HuggingFace is a batteries-included serving solution. It supports continuous batching, flash attention, quantization (GPTQ, AWQ, bitsandbytes), and has first-class integration with the HuggingFace model hub. It's the easiest path from "I found a model on HuggingFace" to "it's running behind an API."

Triton Inference Server by NVIDIA is the Swiss Army knife — it can serve TensorRT-LLM engines, PyTorch models, ONNX models, and even custom Python backends behind a unified gRPC/HTTP API. It handles model versioning, ensemble pipelines (run a tokenizer model then a LLM model then a detokenizer), and dynamic batching. It's the production standard for multi-model deployments.

Advanced Serving Optimizations

Disaggregated serving separates the prefill phase (processing the input prompt) from the decode phase (generating output tokens). Prefill is compute-bound — it processes all input tokens in parallel. Decode is memory-bandwidth-bound — it generates one token at a time. By running these on different GPU pools optimized for each workload, you can improve overall efficiency by 20–40%.

Model pruning removes weights that contribute little to output quality. Structured pruning removes entire attention heads or feed-forward neurons, yielding actual speedups without custom kernels. Unstructured pruning zeros out individual weights, which requires sparse matrix support to see real gains.

The roofline model is a performance analysis framework that tells you whether your workload is compute-bound or memory-bandwidth-bound. Plot your operation's arithmetic intensity (FLOPs per byte of memory accessed) against the hardware's peak compute and peak bandwidth. LLM decode is almost always bandwidth-bound, which is why quantization (reducing bytes per weight) helps more than faster math.

Production Infrastructure

Latency Budgets and SLOs

Production systems care about tail latency, not average latency. Your P50 (median) might be 200ms time-to-first-token, but your P99 (99th percentile) might be 2 seconds because of KV cache eviction or queue buildup. Users don't remember the average — they remember the worst experience. Set SLOs on P99 and work backward: if your P99 budget for time-to-first-token is 500ms, that constrains your maximum batch size, model size, and queuing depth.

Autoscaling and Cost-per-Token

Load balancing for LLM inference is trickier than for stateless web servers. Requests have wildly different compute costs — a 10-token prompt generating 500 tokens costs 50x more than a 10-token prompt generating 10 tokens. Least-connections balancing works better than round-robin because it naturally routes to the GPU with the most capacity.

Autoscaling needs to account for GPU spin-up time (1–3 minutes for loading model weights). Scale proactively based on queue depth, not reactively based on latency. By the time latency spikes, you're already dropping requests.

Cost-per-token is the metric that ties everything together. A rough formula: take your hourly GPU cost, divide by throughput (tokens/second × 3600), and that's your cost per token. On an A100 at $2/hr running vLLM with Llama-3-8B at ~2500 tok/s, that's about $0.0000002 per token. Every optimization in this section — PagedAttention, continuous batching, quantization, TensorRT-LLM compilation — exists to push that number down.

🔥 The Cold Start Trap

Loading a 70B model takes 60–90 seconds even from local NVMe. If your autoscaler launches a new pod and a user is waiting, that's a timeout. Keep at least one warm replica running at all times, and pre-load models during instance startup — not on first request. Some teams use model weight sharding across nodes and parallel loading to cut this to 15–20 seconds.
