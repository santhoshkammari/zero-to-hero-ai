Scaling Laws

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 4 subtopics
TL;DR

Scaling laws are empirical equations that predict how a language model's loss decreases as you increase compute, data, or parameters. They turn billion-dollar training runs from coin flips into engineering. Kaplan et al. (2020) found power-law relationships and argued you should scale model size fastest. Chinchilla (2022) corrected this: the optimal ratio is roughly 20 training tokens per parameter, meaning many models were undertrained. These laws also reveal a looming data wall and help explain why some capabilities emerge only at certain scales. If you train large models — or even decide whether to — scaling laws are the single most important planning tool you have.

Why Scaling Laws Matter

Before 2020, training a large language model was essentially a very expensive bet. You'd pick a model size, guess at how much data you needed, burn through millions of dollars of GPU time, and hope the result was good. If it wasn't, you had very little theory to tell you what went wrong. Was the model too small? Was the data too little? Was it just a bad hyperparameter? Nobody really knew. The field advanced by trial-and-error at a scale where each trial cost a fortune.

Scaling laws changed that. They are empirical relationships — discovered by actually running many experiments at different scales — that describe how a model's loss (its prediction error) decreases as you increase three things: the number of parameters (model size), the number of training tokens (dataset size), and the total compute (measured in FLOPs). The relationships turn out to be remarkably clean: straight lines on log-log plots, which means they follow power laws.

Why does this matter practically? Because power laws are predictable. If you train a small model (say, 100M parameters) on a modest dataset, you can measure the loss. Then you can extrapolate — with surprising accuracy — what the loss would be for a 10B parameter model trained on more data. You can plan your compute budget before spending it. You can compare architectures at small scale and predict which one will win at large scale. Scaling laws turn LLM training from gambling into engineering.

Kaplan et al. (2020) — The OpenAI Scaling Laws

The landmark paper came from OpenAI in January 2020. Jared Kaplan and colleagues trained hundreds of transformer language models ranging from 768 parameters to 1.5 billion parameters and measured how cross-entropy loss varied with three factors independently.

They found three clean power law relationships. A power law means that when you plot two quantities on logarithmic axes, you get a straight line. In plain English: every time you multiply your resource by some factor, you get a fixed additive reduction in log-loss. The returns never truly stop — they just get more expensive.

The Three Power Laws

First, loss versus parameters (with effectively unlimited data and compute):

L(N) ≈ (Nc / N)^0.076

    where N = number of parameters
          Nc ≈ 8.8 × 10^13 (a fitted constant)

In words: every time you multiply the model size by 10, the loss drops by a fixed amount on a log scale. The exponent 0.076 is small, which means you need a lot more parameters to get a little less loss — but the relationship is reliable.

Second, loss versus dataset size (with a large enough model):

L(D) ≈ (Dc / D)^0.095

    where D = number of training tokens
          Dc ≈ 5.4 × 10^13

Third, loss versus compute (optimally allocated between N and D):

L(C) ≈ (Cc / C)^0.050

    where C = compute in FLOPs
          Cc ≈ 3.1 × 10^8

Kaplan's Key Conclusion

Here's the punchline that shaped the field for two years: Kaplan found that parameters mattered more than data. Given a fixed compute budget, you should make the model bigger rather than training on more data. The optimal allocation, they claimed, was to scale model size much faster than dataset size — roughly, if you 10× your compute, you should 5× your parameters but only 2× your data.

This led directly to the "make it bigger" era. GPT-3 (175B parameters, 300B tokens) was trained on far fewer tokens per parameter than what we'd later learn is optimal. The thinking was: more parameters is what matters, don't worry too much about data. This turned out to be wrong — or at least, incomplete.

Chinchilla Scaling (Hoffmann et al., 2022)

In March 2022, a team at DeepMind led by Jordan Hoffmann published a paper that reshaped the field overnight. The title was dry — "Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models" — but the implication was explosive: almost every existing large model was undertrained.

The problem with Kaplan's analysis was subtle but critical. Their experiments didn't vary model size and training tokens jointly in a fully balanced way. When Hoffmann's team ran a proper sweep — training over 400 models ranging from 70M to 16B parameters on varying amounts of data, keeping total compute fixed — a different picture emerged.

The Chinchilla Optimal Ratio

The core finding: given a fixed compute budget, the compute-optimal strategy is to scale parameters and training tokens equally. Roughly speaking, the optimal number of training tokens is about 20 times the number of parameters:

Optimal ratio:  D ≈ 20 × N

    N = number of parameters
    D = number of training tokens

Given compute budget C ≈ 6 × N × D  (approximate FLOPs for training):
    Optimal N ∝ C^0.5
    Optimal D ∝ C^0.5

Both scale as the square root of compute — equally.

This was a direct contradiction of Kaplan's recommendation. Kaplan said: prioritize parameters. Chinchilla said: balance parameters and data equally. And Chinchilla backed it up with a stunning demonstration.

The Chinchilla vs Gopher Showdown

DeepMind had recently trained Gopher, a 280B parameter model on 300B tokens — a large model by Kaplan-era logic. Then they trained Chinchilla: only 70B parameters (4× smaller), but on 1.4T tokens (4.7× more data). Both models used roughly the same compute budget — about 5.76 × 10²³ FLOPs.

Chinchilla beat Gopher on virtually every benchmark — a 4× smaller model, cheaper to serve, with better performance. The lesson was brutal: Gopher had been massively undertrained. It had the capacity to learn more but wasn't given enough data to fill that capacity.

Here's an intuitive way to think about it. Imagine you're packing a suitcase (the model) for a trip (a task). Kaplan said: buy a bigger suitcase. Chinchilla said: actually, your suitcase is half-empty — pack more stuff before buying a bigger one.

The Compute-Optimal Frontier

   │
   │                          ★ Gopher (280B, 300B tok)
   │                            wasteful — too big, too little data
   │
   │              ╱ Compute-optimal frontier
   │            ╱    (Chinchilla line: D ≈ 20N)
   │          ╱

   │      ╱    optimal for its compute budget
   │    ╱
   │  ╱    ★ Overtrained small models
   │╱        (good data efficiency, but limited capacity)
   │──────────────────────────── Tokens (log)

Everything above and to the left of the frontier is undertrained (too many parameters, not enough data). Everything below and to the right is overtrained (wasting compute on a model that's too small to benefit). The frontier itself is where you get the most performance per FLOP.

This paper transformed the industry. Meta's LLaMA models, Mistral, and most subsequent open-source models follow Chinchilla-optimal ratios or go even further — deliberately "overtraining" smaller models on extra data to make them cheaper at inference time. A 7B model trained on 8T tokens costs much less to serve than a 70B model, even if the 70B model is slightly better.

The Data Wall

Chinchilla's ratio creates an inconvenient math problem. If you want to train a model with 1 trillion parameters at the compute-optimal ratio, you need 20 trillion tokens of training data. A 10T parameter model would need 200T tokens. Where does all that text come from?

The best estimates suggest the entire public internet contains roughly 10–15 trillion tokens of deduplicated, reasonably high-quality text. That includes web pages, books, academic papers, code repositories, and forums. We're already bumping up against this ceiling. The data wall is the point where scaling laws demand more data than actually exists.

This is not a theoretical concern. Llama 3 was trained on 15T tokens — close to the estimated total supply of high-quality text. Several approaches are being explored to push past this wall:

Synthetic data generation uses existing models to create new training data. You can have a strong model generate solutions to math problems, write code, or rephrase existing text. This works surprisingly well for structured domains like math and code, where you can verify correctness. For general text, it's riskier — training on synthetic data can create feedback loops where the model reinforces its own biases and errors, a phenomenon called model collapse.

Multi-epoch training means showing the model the same data more than once. Traditional wisdom said this was wasteful — the model would just memorize. But recent work shows that repeating data 4–8× with the right learning rate schedule actually works, though with diminishing returns. Each repetition extracts less new learning than the last.

Data quality over quantity is perhaps the most impactful lever. A carefully curated 1T token dataset can outperform a noisy 10T token dataset. Techniques include aggressive deduplication, quality filtering (classifiers that score text quality), domain balancing, and removing toxic or low-information content. The Phi models from Microsoft demonstrated that textbook-quality data can train remarkably capable small models.

Multimodal data — images, video, audio — represents a vast untapped reservoir. The internet has far more visual data than text. Models like GPT-4V and Gemini consume images alongside text, effectively expanding the training data pool into a different modality. Whether visual tokens help language understanding is still debated, but they certainly help with visual reasoning tasks.

Emergent Abilities

Scaling laws describe smooth, predictable curves. But some of the most interesting capabilities of large models don't follow smooth curves at all. They appear suddenly.

Emergent abilities are capabilities that are absent or near-random in smaller models but appear sharply once a model crosses a certain scale threshold. The model doesn't get gradually better at the task — it jumps from can't-do-it to can-do-it over a narrow range of model sizes.

Examples of Emergence

Three-digit addition is a clean example. Models under about 13B parameters essentially guess randomly on 3-digit arithmetic. Above 13B, accuracy jumps to well above 50%. The model hasn't been explicitly taught addition — it learned the algorithm from seeing arithmetic in text.

Chain-of-thought reasoning is another. Small models asked to "think step by step" produce incoherent intermediate steps that don't help. Models above roughly 60–100B parameters actually produce useful reasoning chains that improve their final answers. The ability to decompose a problem, work through it, and synthesize an answer emerges rather than being trained in.

Few-shot learning — the ability to perform a new task given just a few examples in the prompt — is essentially absent below ~1B parameters. It appears weakly around 10B and becomes reliable around 100B+. This is what makes GPT-3 feel qualitatively different from GPT-2: not just better text, but the ability to follow instructions and learn from context.

The Debate: Real or Artifact?

In 2023, Schaeffer, Miranda, and Sanborn published a provocative paper arguing that many claimed emergent abilities are measurement artifacts, not genuine phase transitions. Their argument: if you measure with a sharp metric (like exact-match accuracy, where the answer is either perfectly right or completely wrong), you see sudden jumps. But if you measure with a smooth metric (like token-level edit distance — how close was the answer?), the improvement is gradual all along. The "emergence" was in the metric, not the model.

This is a genuinely important critique and partially correct. Some claimed emergent abilities do disappear under smoother metrics. The model was getting better all along — you just couldn't see it through the all-or-nothing lens. However, this doesn't explain all cases. Some tasks require a minimum level of capability to produce any useful output — you can't half-solve a multi-step reasoning problem and get credit. For these tasks, the jump is real in any practical sense, even if the underlying representations improve smoothly.

The honest answer is: both sides have a point. Some "emergence" is metric-driven, and researchers should use continuous metrics when possible. But some capabilities genuinely require a critical mass of model capacity, and below that threshold, the model truly cannot do the task. The field hasn't fully settled this debate yet.

Practical Implications for Engineers

Scaling laws aren't just academic — they're planning tools. Here's how practitioners actually use them.

Train Small, Extrapolate Big

The standard workflow: train a series of small models (say, 10M, 50M, 200M, 1B parameters) on proportionally sized datasets, following the Chinchilla ratio. Plot their losses on a log-log chart. Fit a line. Extrapolate to your target model size. This gives you a reliable estimate of what loss to expect before you spend millions of dollars on the full training run.

# Simplified scaling law fit
import numpy as np

# Small-scale experiments: (parameters, measured_loss)
experiments = [
    (1e7,  3.5),   # 10M params
    (5e7,  3.1),   # 50M params
    (2e8,  2.8),   # 200M params
    (1e9,  2.5),   # 1B params
]

log_N = np.array([np.log10(n) for n, _ in experiments])
log_L = np.array([np.log10(l) for _, l in experiments])

# Fit line on log-log scale: log(L) = α * log(N) + β
α, β = np.polyfit(log_N, log_L, 1)

# Predict loss at 70B parameters
target_N = 7e10
predicted_loss = 10 ** (α * np.log10(target_N) + β)
print(f"Predicted loss at 70B: {predicted_loss:.2f}")
# This saves you from a blind $10M+ training run

Compute-Optimal Budgeting

Given a fixed GPU budget (say, 1,000 A100 GPUs for 3 months — roughly 10²⁴ FLOPs), you can use the Chinchilla ratio to determine your model size. With C ≈ 6ND, and D ≈ 20N, you get C ≈ 120N². Solving for N: N ≈ √(C/120). For 10²⁴ FLOPs, that suggests about 91B parameters trained on 1.8T tokens. This is remarkably close to what Llama 2 70B actually used.

When to Stop Scaling

Scaling laws show diminishing returns. Going from 7B to 70B parameters (10× more) might reduce loss from 2.1 to 1.8 — meaningful but not revolutionary. Going from 70B to 700B costs 10× more again, and might only get you from 1.8 to 1.6. At some point the cost of the marginal improvement exceeds its value. The right answer depends on your application: for a product that serves billions of queries, even a tiny quality improvement has huge aggregate value. For a research prototype, you might stop earlier.

Training Compute vs Inference Compute

There's a tradeoff scaling laws don't capture directly: a bigger model costs more to serve, not just to train. Training is a one-time cost. Inference runs every time a user sends a query. For applications with high query volume, inference cost dominates. This is why many teams deliberately overtrain smaller models beyond the Chinchilla ratio — a 7B model trained on 8T tokens is slightly worse than a Chinchilla-optimal 30B model, but it's 4× cheaper to serve.

  70B → $3M/mo inference     ← 10× more, for ~15% better quality

Inference-Time Scaling: A New Axis

Recent work (OpenAI's o1 and o3 series) has introduced a different kind of scaling: instead of making the model bigger, give it more time to think at inference. The model generates a long chain of reasoning before producing its final answer. This trades inference compute for better answers on hard problems — a form of scaling that doesn't require retraining.

This is a fundamentally different axis. Traditional scaling laws predict: bigger model → lower loss. Inference-time scaling says: same model, more compute per query → better answers on specific hard queries. The two approaches are complementary. You might use a Chinchilla-optimal 70B model but let it "think" for 10× longer on math and reasoning tasks. The optimal allocation between training-time compute and inference-time compute is an open research question that will likely reshape scaling law research in the coming years.

The bottom line: scaling laws don't tell you to mindlessly make models bigger. They give you a framework for allocating resources rationally — across parameters, data, training compute, and now inference compute. The teams that use them well ship better models for less money. The teams that ignore them burn cash and hope for the best.
