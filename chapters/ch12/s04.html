Other Foundation Models

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 4 subtopics
TL;DR

GPT didn't stay alone for long. Google's T5 reframed every NLP task as text-to-text. Meta's LLaMA blew the gates open with powerful open-weights models. Mistral showed a 7B model could beat one twice its size, and Mixtral proved Mixture of Experts is the way to scale without burning proportionally more compute. Google countered with PaLM and then Gemini (natively multimodal). Anthropic's Claude pioneered Constitutional AI for safety. Meanwhile, Falcon, Phi, Qwen, and RWKV carved out their own niches. We're living through a Cambrian explosion of foundation models — and understanding the lineage matters because each model introduced ideas that ended up everywhere.

T5 and FLAN-T5

Everything Is Text-to-Text

Most models before T5 treated different NLP tasks as structurally different problems. A classifier outputs a class index. A translator outputs tokens in another language. A summarizer outputs shorter text. Google's T5 (Text-to-Text Transfer Transformer), released in 2019, asked a radical question: what if we made every task look exactly the same? Feed in text, get out text. Classification? The output is the literal string "positive" or "negative." Translation? The output is the translated sentence. Summarization? A shorter version of the input. Same model, same loss function, same architecture — for everything.

This works through task prefixes. You prepend a short instruction to the input that tells the model what to do:

# T5 task prefix examples
"translate English to German: The house is wonderful."
→ "Das Haus ist wunderbar."

"summarize: State authorities dispatched emergency crews Tuesday..."
→ "Emergency crews were dispatched following severe weather."

"stsb sentence1: The cat sat on the mat. sentence2: A cat is sitting on a mat."
→ "4.2"   # semantic similarity score as text

T5 uses an encoder-decoder architecture — unlike GPT (decoder-only) or BERT (encoder-only). The encoder reads the entire input bidirectionally, building rich representations. The decoder generates the output autoregressively. This design is natural for tasks where you read something fully, then produce something new — translation, summarization, question-answering.

For pre-training, T5 uses span corruption. Random contiguous spans of the input are replaced with sentinel tokens (like <extra_id_0>), and the model learns to predict the original spans. This is different from BERT's single-token masking — corrupting entire spans forces the model to reason about longer-range dependencies.

T5 came in five sizes: Small (60M), Base (220M), Large (770M), 3B, and 11B parameters. Google systematically tested everything — architectures, pre-training objectives, data mixtures, transfer strategies — using the C4 dataset (a cleaned version of Common Crawl). The paper is practically a field guide to the design decisions that matter.

Instruction Tuning with FLAN

FLAN-T5 took the base T5 and fine-tuned it on over 1,800 tasks phrased as natural instructions. The idea behind instruction tuning is simple: if you train a model on a huge variety of tasks described in plain English, it gets better at following new instructions it hasn't seen before. FLAN-T5 didn't just memorize 1,800 tasks — it learned the meta-skill of understanding what a human is asking for.

For a while, FLAN-T5 was arguably the best openly available model. It could do zero-shot tasks that required no fine-tuning, ran on reasonable hardware (the 3B version fits on a single consumer GPU), and handled structured output well. It's still a solid choice when you need a reliable encoder-decoder model for tasks like extraction or summarization.

The LLaMA Family

LLaMA-1: Open Weights Change Everything

In February 2023, Meta released LLaMA (Large Language Model Meta AI) and fundamentally changed who gets to play with large language models. The key insight: you don't need proprietary data. LLaMA-1 was trained exclusively on publicly available datasets — Common Crawl, Wikipedia, GitHub, ArXiv, Books3, Stack Exchange. And it was good. LLaMA-65B matched or exceeded GPT-3 on most benchmarks, while LLaMA-13B outperformed GPT-3 (175B) on several tasks despite being 13 times smaller.

How? By training longer. The Chinchilla scaling laws (from DeepMind) showed that most models are over-parameterized and under-trained. LLaMA followed this insight, feeding more tokens through a smaller model. LLaMA-1 was trained on 1.4 trillion tokens — about 7x more data per parameter than GPT-3 used.

LLaMA also introduced several architecture choices that became standard across the open-source ecosystem:

RoPE (Rotary Position Embedding) encodes position information by rotating the query and key vectors in attention. Unlike absolute position embeddings (which cap out at the training length), RoPE naturally generalizes to longer sequences because it encodes relative distance between tokens through rotation angles. This is why LLaMA-based models can be extended to longer contexts without retraining from scratch.

SwiGLU activation replaces the standard ReLU or GELU in the feed-forward layers with a gated variant: SwiGLU(x) = Swish(xW₁) ⊙ (xV). The gating mechanism (element-wise multiplication with a linear projection) gives the network more expressive power. In practice, SwiGLU consistently yields better loss for the same compute budget.

RMSNorm (Root Mean Square Layer Normalization) simplifies LayerNorm by removing the mean-centering step. Standard LayerNorm computes mean and variance, then normalizes. RMSNorm only divides by the root mean square — fewer operations, faster training, and empirically just as good.

LLaMA-2 and LLaMA-3

LLaMA-2 (July 2023) came in 7B, 13B, and 70B sizes, with a genuinely permissive license — you could actually use it commercially. It shipped with a chat model that had been aligned using RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback), making it the first open model with a serious alignment effort baked in. Context length went from 2K to 4K, with community extensions pushing it to 32K.

LLaMA-3 (April 2024) pushed further: 8B and 70B sizes, 128K context window, and a dramatically better tokenizer with 128K vocabulary (up from 32K). It was trained on 15 trillion tokens — over 10x more than LLaMA-1. The bigger tokenizer means better handling of non-English languages and code. LLaMA-3 70B is competitive with GPT-3.5-turbo on many benchmarks.

But LLaMA's biggest impact isn't any single model. It's the ecosystem it spawned. Within weeks of LLaMA-1 leaking, the community produced Alpaca (Stanford's instruction-tuned variant for $600), Vicuna (fine-tuned on ChatGPT conversations), Koala, WizardLM, and dozens more. LLaMA proved that open-weight models, with community fine-tuning, could approach closed-model quality at a fraction of the cost.

Mistral and Mixtral

Mistral-7B: The Efficient Upstart

In September 2023, a Paris-based startup called Mistral AI released Mistral-7B with no paper, no blog post — just a torrent link. It outperformed LLaMA-2 13B on every benchmark despite having half the parameters. How does a 7B model beat a 13B model? Better architecture choices and better training data.

The first key innovation is Sliding Window Attention (SWA). Standard attention lets every token attend to every other token — O(n²) cost, and you need to store KV pairs for the entire sequence. SWA restricts each token to attending only to the previous W tokens (where W is the window size, 4,096 for Mistral-7B). But here's the trick: because information propagates through layers, after L layers, a token can effectively access information from L × W tokens back. With 32 layers and a window of 4,096, that's 131K tokens of effective context — through a fixed-size attention window.

Grouped-Query Attention (GQA) is the second innovation. Standard multi-head attention has separate key, value, and query heads. Multi-query attention (MQA) shares a single key-value head across all query heads — fast but loses quality. GQA is the middle ground: multiple query heads share a smaller number of key-value heads (e.g., 8 KV heads for 32 query heads). You get most of MQA's speed with most of MHA's quality.

Mistral also uses a rolling buffer KV cache. Instead of storing KV pairs for the entire sequence, you keep only the last W entries in a fixed-size circular buffer. Position i is stored at index i mod W. Memory usage stays constant regardless of sequence length — you can generate indefinitely without running out of cache memory.

Mixtral 8x7B: Mixture of Experts

Mixtral 8x7B introduced the Mixture of Experts (MoE) architecture to the open-source world. The idea of Mixture of Experts isn't new (it dates to the 1990s), but Mixtral made it practical at scale.

In a standard transformer, every token passes through the same feed-forward network (FFN) in each layer. In an MoE model, you replace that single FFN with multiple "expert" FFNs and a router that decides which experts to use for each token. Here's how it works:

Mixtral MoE Routing — Per Layer, Per Token
══════════════════════════════════════════════

  Input Token Embedding
          │
          ▼
  ┌──────────────┐
  │    Router     │  (small linear layer → softmax over 8 experts)
  │  (gate net)   │
  └──────────────┘
          │
          ▼  Scores: [0.05, 0.35, 0.02, 0.08, 0.40, 0.03, 0.04, 0.03]
                             ↑                    ↑
                          Expert 2             Expert 5
                         (top-2 selection)
          │
     ┌────┴─────┐
     ▼          ▼
┌─────────┐ ┌─────────┐
│Expert 2 │ │Expert 5 │   ← Each expert is a full FFN block
│  FFN    │ │  FFN    │      (same size as a 7B model's FFN)
└─────────┘ └─────────┘
     │          │
     ▼          ▼
   out_2      out_5
     │          │
     ▼          ▼
  0.47×out_2 + 0.53×out_5   ← weighted sum (normalized top-2 scores)
          │
          ▼
    Final Output

Each token activates only 2 of the 8 experts. So Mixtral has 47B total parameters (8 expert FFNs × ~7B each, plus shared attention layers), but only ~13B are active for any given token. You get the knowledge capacity of a much larger model with the inference cost of a smaller one. The router learns during training which expert specializes in what — one expert might handle code, another handles French text, another handles reasoning. Tokens are routed to the experts best suited for them.

Why does this matter? MoE gives you a bigger brain without proportionally more compute. A dense 47B model would need 47B parameters of compute per token. Mixtral needs only 13B. In practice, Mixtral 8x7B matches or exceeds LLaMA-2 70B on most benchmarks while being much faster at inference. The tradeoff: you still need enough RAM (or VRAM) to hold all 47B parameters, even though only 13B are active. MoE saves compute, not memory.

PaLM and Gemini

PaLM: Scaling Decoder-Only

Google's PaLM (Pathways Language Model), released in April 2022, was a 540B-parameter decoder-only transformer — the largest dense model at the time. It was trained on Google's Pathways system, which distributed computation across 6,144 TPU v4 chips. PaLM showed breakthrough performance on reasoning tasks, code generation, and math. It was the first model to demonstrate convincing "chain of thought" reasoning at scale: given a math word problem, PaLM could produce multi-step solutions rather than just guessing the answer.

PaLM-2 (May 2023) was smaller but smarter. Google didn't disclose exact sizes, but it was trained on better data with a better mixture — more multilingual text, more code, more math. The Chinchilla lesson again: better data beats bigger models. PaLM-2 powered Google's Bard chatbot and was the backbone of Vertex AI for enterprise use.

Gemini: Natively Multimodal

Gemini (December 2023) was Google's answer to GPT-4. The critical difference: Gemini was designed as natively multimodal from the start. Previous multimodal models typically bolted a vision encoder onto a language model (like connecting CLIP to an LLM). Gemini was pre-trained jointly on text, images, audio, and video from day one. The model doesn't "translate" images into text — it processes them in a shared representation space alongside language.

Gemini came in three sizes: Ultra (the flagship, competing with GPT-4), Pro (the workhorse for most applications), and Nano (designed to run on-device, like phones). Gemini Ultra matched or exceeded GPT-4 on most benchmarks at launch, and Google has been iterating fast with Gemini 1.5 (which introduced a million-token context window using a MoE architecture internally).

Claude

Constitutional AI and Safety-First Design

Anthropic, founded by former OpenAI researchers, built Claude around a core belief: powerful AI needs to be aligned with human values before it's deployed widely. Their key contribution is Constitutional AI (CAI), which replaces part of the human feedback pipeline with AI feedback.

Standard RLHF requires thousands of human ratings on model outputs — expensive, slow, and inconsistent. Constitutional AI works differently. First, you write a "constitution" — a set of principles like "be helpful," "be harmless," "be honest," "don't assist with illegal activity." Then you generate multiple responses, have the AI critique its own responses against the constitution, and revise. Finally, you use RLAIF (Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback) — the AI itself rates outputs based on the constitutional principles. Humans write the rules; the AI enforces them at scale.

Claude evolved through several generations. Claude 1 was competitive with early ChatGPT. Claude 2 improved significantly on coding, math, and reasoning. Claude 3 (March 2024) came in three tiers — Haiku (fast and cheap), Sonnet (balanced), and Opus (flagship) — and Opus competed directly with GPT-4 on complex reasoning. Claude's standout feature has been its long context window: 200K tokens, meaning you can feed it an entire codebase or a full novel and ask questions about it.

The "helpful, harmless, honest" framework is deceptively simple but practically powerful. It gives model developers a clear rubric for evaluating tradeoffs. Should the model help someone with a borderline request? "Helpful" says yes, "harmless" says maybe not. The tension between these principles — and the explicit, principled way Claude resolves them — is what makes Constitutional AI a genuine contribution to alignment research, not just a marketing term.

The Wider Ecosystem

The models above are the headliners, but the ecosystem is far broader. Here are several models that carved out important niches.

Falcon

Falcon, from the Technology Innovation Institute (Abu Dhabi), showed that a well-resourced research lab outside the usual US-China axis could compete at the frontier. Falcon-40B and Falcon-180B were trained on the RefinedWeb dataset — a carefully cleaned version of Common Crawl that emphasized data quality over quantity. Falcon-180B was briefly the top model on the Hugging Face Open LLM Leaderboard.

Phi Series

Microsoft's Phi series asks a contrarian question: what if small models trained on extremely high-quality data could rival much larger ones? Phi-1 (1.3B) was trained on "textbook quality" data — synthetic and curated content chosen for its educational value. Phi-2 (2.7B) matched or exceeded models 25x its size on reasoning benchmarks. Phi-3 continued the trend. The Phi papers are a strong argument that data quality trumps data quantity, especially at smaller scales. If you need a model that fits on a laptop, Phi is worth serious consideration.

Qwen

Qwen (Alibaba Cloud) is the leading open model from the Chinese AI ecosystem. Qwen-2 comes in sizes from 0.5B to 72B, supports 29 languages, and includes both base and instruction-tuned variants. Qwen matches or exceeds LLaMA-3 on many benchmarks, particularly for Chinese and multilingual tasks. It's a reminder that the foundation model race is global.

RWKV

RWKV is a fascinating hybrid: it combines the training parallelism of a transformer with the linear-time inference of an RNN. During training, RWKV uses an attention-like mechanism that can be parallelized across the sequence. At inference time, it reformulates as a recurrent model — each token update is O(1) regardless of context length. This means constant memory usage during generation, no KV cache explosion. The tradeoff is slightly lower quality on tasks that require precise long-range retrieval, but for many applications the efficiency gain is worth it.

Switch Transformer

Google's Switch Transformer simplified the Mixture of Experts concept by routing each token to just one expert (instead of top-2 or top-4). This reduced communication overhead and made MoE easier to scale. Switch Transformer achieved a 7x speedup over the dense T5 model at the same compute budget. It was an early proof point that MoE is not just a trick — it's a scalable architecture paradigm.

The Cambrian Explosion

Step back and look at the timeline. In early 2022, if you wanted a powerful language model, you called the OpenAI API. By the end of 2024, you could choose from dozens of high-quality models — open and closed, 1B to 540B parameters, dense or sparse, text-only or multimodal, safety-focused or performance-focused. This is a Cambrian explosion. And like the biological one, it's being driven by a few key innovations propagating rapidly across the ecosystem.

The pattern is clear. Each generation introduces a few key ideas — Chinchilla scaling, MoE routing, sliding window attention, instruction tuning, Constitutional AI — and within months those ideas show up everywhere. LLaMA introduced RoPE and SwiGLU; now virtually every open model uses them. Mistral demonstrated GQA and sliding window attention; these became standard features. Mixtral validated sparse MoE; Google and others immediately scaled the approach.

The honest tradeoff you face as a practitioner: closed models (GPT-4, Gemini Ultra, Claude Opus) still tend to lead on the hardest benchmarks. But the gap is shrinking fast, and open models offer something closed models never will — control. You can fine-tune them on your data, run them on your hardware, inspect their weights, and guarantee that your data never leaves your infrastructure. For many real-world applications, a fine-tuned LLaMA-3 or Mixtral running on your own servers is a better choice than an API call to a model you can't control.

The foundation model landscape won't consolidate around one winner. It's going to keep diversifying — different architectures for different constraints, different training recipes for different domains, different alignment strategies for different risk profiles. Understanding the ideas behind each model family, not just their benchmark scores, is what lets you make the right choice when the next wave arrives.
