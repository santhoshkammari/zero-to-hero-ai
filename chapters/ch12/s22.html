Emerging AI Patterns (2024-2025)

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 10 subtopics
TL;DR

The AI landscape shifted dramatically in 2024-2025. Models learned to "think longer" at inference via test-time compute scaling (OpenAI o1/o3). Small language models like Phi-4 and Gemma-3 brought near-frontier performance to consumer GPUs. Multimodal models (GPT-4o, Gemini 2.0) unified text, vision, and audio in a single transformer. The industry moved from monolithic models to compound AI systems — agents that route queries, use tools, and maintain memory. Meanwhile, open-source models closed the gap with closed-source, the EU AI Act became law, and synthetic data emerged as a first-class training strategy. This section maps the ten patterns reshaping how we build and deploy AI.

If you froze yourself in early 2023 and thawed out in mid-2025, you'd barely recognize the field. GPT-4 was the pinnacle back then — a single monolithic model you threw prompts at. Today, the frontier has splintered into a constellation of techniques: reasoning models that burn extra compute at inference instead of training, tiny models that run on your laptop, agents that orchestrate dozens of tool calls, and regulatory frameworks that dictate what you can ship and where. Let's walk through each pattern, starting from the one that surprised everyone most.

Test-Time Compute Scaling

For years, the scaling law story was simple: bigger models trained on more data get smarter. Then OpenAI's o1 model (September 2024) introduced a different lever: test-time compute scaling. Instead of making the model larger, you let it think longer on each query. The model generates an internal chain-of-thought, backtracks when it hits contradictions, and explores multiple solution paths before committing to a final answer. It's the difference between answering a math problem in two seconds versus spending ten minutes with scratch paper.

The mechanism is deceptively simple. During training, o1 used reinforcement learning to develop reasoning strategies — not just pattern matching, but something closer to deliberate problem-solving. At inference, the model allocates a variable amount of compute per query. Easy questions get a short chain-of-thought. Hard problems (competitive math, complex coding tasks) trigger extended reasoning that can consume 10-100x the tokens of a standard response.

The results were dramatic: up to 50% improvement on hard reasoning benchmarks compared to standard prompting of the same base model. OpenAI followed with o3 (April 2025) and the cost-optimized o4-mini, each generation getting better at deciding how much to think. The key insight is budget forcing — you can control the compute-accuracy tradeoff at inference time. Need a quick answer? Cap the reasoning tokens. Need to be really sure? Let it think.

# Conceptual: budget forcing with reasoning tokens
response = client.chat.completions.create(
    model="o4-mini",
    messages=[{"role": "user", "content": hard_math_problem}],
    max_completion_tokens=16384,   # total budget including reasoning
    reasoning_effort="high"        # low | medium | high
)
# The model decides internally how many "thinking" tokens to use
# before producing the visible answer

The open-source community replicated this pattern quickly. The s1-32B model demonstrated that you could distill reasoning capabilities from a larger model into a 32-billion parameter model, then apply budget forcing at inference. The recipe: take a strong base model, fine-tune it on traces of extended reasoning (often generated by a frontier model), and add a simple mechanism to control how long it "thinks." This democratized test-time scaling beyond OpenAI's API.

💡 Key Insight

Test-time compute scaling decouples model capability from model size. A smaller model that thinks longer can outperform a larger model that answers immediately. This changes the economics: you pay per-query compute instead of upfront training compute, and you can dial the knob based on task difficulty.

Reasoning Models: System 2 Thinking for LLMs

The o1 and o3 family represent a broader pattern: reasoning models that exhibit System 2-style deliberation. The analogy comes from Daniel Kahneman's framework — System 1 is fast, intuitive pattern matching (what standard LLMs do), and System 2 is slow, deliberate, step-by-step reasoning (what o1/o3 attempt).

Concretely, reasoning models do three things that standard LLMs struggle with. First, chain-of-thought decomposition: they break complex problems into sub-steps and solve each one explicitly. Second, self-consistency verification: they check their own intermediate answers, notice contradictions, and backtrack. Third, agentic tool use: they recognize when a sub-problem requires external computation (running code, querying a database) and invoke tools mid-reasoning.

This isn't just prompting tricks. The reinforcement learning training teaches the model when to invest more thought, when to verify, and when to abandon a failing approach. It's a fundamental shift from "predict the next token" to "solve the problem, whatever it takes." The tradeoff? Latency and cost. A reasoning model can take 30-60 seconds on a hard query where a standard model answers in 2 seconds. For many applications — customer support chatbots, simple Q&A — this is overkill. For math competitions, code generation, and scientific reasoning, it's transformative.

Small Language Models

While reasoning models push the ceiling, small language models (SLMs) are quietly reshaping the floor. The thesis: you don't need 400 billion parameters to summarize an email, extract entities from an invoice, or classify customer intent. Models under 30B parameters — sometimes under 4B — handle most enterprise NLP tasks competently.

The SLM Landscape

Three models defined this wave. Microsoft's Phi-4 (14B parameters) demonstrated that training methodology matters more than raw size. Phi-4 was trained heavily on synthetic data — textbook-quality examples generated by larger models, plus curated reasoning traces. It matched or exceeded models 3-5x its size on reasoning benchmarks, and ships under an MIT license. Google's Gemma-3 family spans 1B to 27B parameters, supports 128K token context windows, and is Apache 2.0 licensed. It's designed for fine-tuning: the 1B variant fits on a phone, the 27B variant on a single GPU. Mistral's Small 3.1 is multilingual (24 languages), supports 128K context, function calling, and is also Apache 2.0.

Industry analysts project that 40% of enterprise NLP workloads will run on SLMs by 2027. The economics are compelling: a 14B model costs roughly 20x less per token than a frontier model, runs on a single RTX 4090, and can be self-hosted — no API dependency, no data leaving your infrastructure.

# Running a small model locally with llama-cpp-python
from llama_cpp import Llama

# Phi-4 quantized to 4-bit fits in ~8GB VRAM
model = Llama(
    model_path="phi-4-Q4_K_M.gguf",
    n_ctx=4096,
    n_gpu_layers=-1   # offload all layers to GPU
)

response = model.create_chat_completion(
    messages=[{"role": "user", "content": "Extract entities from this invoice..."}],
    temperature=0.1
)
# Runs locally, ~40 tokens/sec on RTX 4090, zero API costs
⚠️ Tradeoff Alert

SLMs excel at narrow, well-defined tasks but struggle with open-ended reasoning, multi-step planning, and tasks requiring broad world knowledge. Always benchmark on YOUR specific task before committing to a smaller model. A 14B model that scores 85% on a generic benchmark might score 60% on your domain-specific evaluation.

Multimodal Foundation Models

The most visible shift in 2024 was multimodal foundation models — single models that natively process text, images, audio, and video through one unified architecture. No more bolting a vision encoder onto a text model. No more separate ASR pipeline feeding into an LLM.

GPT-4o (May 2024) was the flagship. The "o" stands for "omni." It's a single transformer that ingests text, image, and audio tokens directly. Audio-in to audio-out latency dropped to 320 milliseconds — fast enough for natural conversation. It supports 128K tokens of context and set new benchmarks across 30+ evaluation tasks. The architecture unifies modalities at the token level: images become patches mapped to tokens, audio becomes spectral features mapped to tokens, and the same attention mechanism processes them all.

Google's Gemini 2.0 (December 2024) pushed two frontiers: a 1-million token context window and dramatically lower cost — roughly 25x cheaper than GPT-4o for equivalent tasks. It added native cross-modal generation: the model doesn't just understand images, it reasons across modalities in a single forward pass. Ask it to "listen to this audio clip, look at this chart, and write a summary" and it processes all three inputs natively, without routing through separate sub-models.

For engineers, the practical impact is pipeline simplification. A task that previously required an OCR model, a layout parser, an NER model, and an LLM can now be a single API call with an image attachment. The debugging surface shrinks. The latency drops. The tradeoff is control: when something goes wrong in a monolithic multimodal model, you can't isolate which "module" failed.

Long-Context Windows vs. RAG

Gemini 2.0's million-token context window forced a reckoning with a technique everyone assumed was here to stay: Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). If you can stuff an entire codebase or document corpus directly into the context window, do you still need a retrieval pipeline?

The honest answer: it depends. Long context wins on simplicity — no chunking strategy, no embedding model, no vector database, no retrieval relevance tuning. You dump your documents in and ask questions. For corpuses under 500K tokens (~375K words), this works surprisingly well. Gemma-3's 128K context and Gemini 2.0's 1M context make many RAG pipelines unnecessary.

But RAG still wins on three fronts. Cost: processing 1M tokens on every query is expensive even at Gemini's lower prices. Freshness: RAG pipelines can index new documents in seconds; re-prompting with updated context is instant. Scale: once your corpus exceeds the context window, you need retrieval. Many enterprise knowledge bases are 10-100M tokens.

# The decision framework
def should_use_rag(corpus_size_tokens, queries_per_day, freshness_hours):
    """When does RAG beat long-context stuffing?"""
    long_context_cost = corpus_size_tokens * queries_per_day * COST_PER_TOKEN
    rag_cost = (RETRIEVAL_COST + TOP_K_TOKENS * COST_PER_TOKEN) * queries_per_day
    
    if corpus_size_tokens > 1_000_000:
        return True   # exceeds context window
    if freshness_hours < 1:
        return True   # need real-time updates
    if long_context_cost > 10 * rag_cost:
        return True   # cost prohibitive at scale
    return False      # long context is simpler, use it
💡 Hybrid Approach

The emerging best practice is hybrid: use RAG to retrieve the most relevant chunks, then stuff those chunks into a long-context model that can reason over all of them simultaneously. You get retrieval's efficiency with long-context's cross-document reasoning. This is what most production systems shipping in 2025 actually do.

Compound AI Systems

Perhaps the deepest architectural shift: the move from monolithic models to compound AI systems. Instead of one giant model handling everything, you build a system of specialized components — a router, multiple models, tools, memory stores, and evaluation layers — that collaborate on each request.

A typical compound system looks like this: a query arrives, a lightweight router classifies its complexity and domain, the router dispatches to an appropriate model (small model for simple tasks, reasoning model for hard ones), the model may invoke tools (code execution, web search, database queries), results flow through a verification layer, and the final answer is assembled. Each component can be swapped, tested, and scaled independently.

Two protocol standards emerged to make this work. Google's Agent2Agent (A2A) protocol defines how autonomous agents discover each other, negotiate capabilities, and delegate tasks. Anthropic's Model Context Protocol (MCP) standardizes how models connect to external tools and data sources — think of it as a USB standard for AI tools. Both are open specifications, and both are gaining rapid adoption.

# Model routing based on query complexity
def route_query(query: str, complexity_score: float) -> str:
    """Route to the right model based on estimated difficulty."""
    if complexity_score < 0.3:
        return "phi-4"              # fast, cheap, good enough
    elif complexity_score < 0.7:
        return "gpt-4o"             # balanced performance
    else:
        return "o4-mini"            # hard problem, needs reasoning
    
# Compound system: router + model + tools + verifier
class CompoundAISystem:
    def __init__(self):
        self.router = ComplexityClassifier()
        self.models = {"phi-4": ..., "gpt-4o": ..., "o4-mini": ...}
        self.tools = ToolRegistry()       # MCP-compatible
        self.memory = ConversationStore() # persistent across sessions
    
    def process(self, query: str) -> str:
        score = self.router.estimate_complexity(query)
        model_name = route_query(query, score)
        response = self.models[model_name].generate(
            query, 
            tools=self.tools,
            context=self.memory.retrieve(query)
        )
        self.memory.store(query, response)
        return response

The Reliability-Autonomy Tradeoff

As agents get more autonomous — making tool calls, browsing the web, writing and executing code — reliability becomes the central engineering challenge. The reliability-autonomy tradeoff states: the more decisions you let an agent make without human oversight, the higher the probability of a catastrophic error. A chatbot that only answers questions is very reliable. An agent that deploys code to production is very autonomous. You cannot maximize both.

Production systems handle this with graduated autonomy. Low-risk actions (reading files, searching) are fully autonomous. Medium-risk actions (writing files, sending messages) require confirmation in some contexts. High-risk actions (deploying, deleting, spending money) always require human approval. The art is drawing these lines correctly for your domain.

Agent Benchmarks

How do you know if your agent actually works? Three benchmarks emerged as the standard. SWE-bench tests whether an agent can resolve real GitHub issues — find the relevant code, write a patch, and pass the existing tests. Top agents solve ~50% of verified issues. WebArena tests web navigation: can an agent book a flight, fill out forms, and extract information from real websites? GAIA tests general AI assistants on tasks requiring multi-step reasoning, tool use, and real-world knowledge. These benchmarks matter because they test the full system — not just the model, but the routing, tool use, and error recovery.

Synthetic Data for Training

Phi-4's success revealed an open secret: synthetic data — training examples generated by AI models rather than collected from humans — is now a first-class training strategy. The approach has several variants.

Distillation: a large frontier model generates high-quality responses, and a smaller model trains on them. This is how most SLMs reach competitive quality. Self-play: a model generates both questions and answers, with a verifier filtering for correctness. This works especially well for math and code, where you can programmatically check answers. Textbook generation: a model writes structured, curriculum-like explanations of concepts, producing training data that's more pedagogically organized than web scrapes.

The Phi series from Microsoft leaned heavily into synthetic textbook-style data, and the quality gains were measurable. But there's a risk: model collapse, where training on AI-generated data causes the model to lose diversity and converge to a narrow distribution. The mitigation is mixing synthetic data with real-world data and carefully controlling the ratio — typically 30-50% synthetic works well.

⚠️ Data Contamination

When your synthetic data comes from a frontier model, you might accidentally leak that model's benchmark answers into your training set. Always audit synthetic datasets against your evaluation benchmarks. If your small model suspiciously matches the frontier model's exact phrasing on benchmark questions, you have a contamination problem.

Open-Source vs. Closed-Source Models

The gap between open and closed models narrowed dramatically. Meta's Llama 3 family (8B to 405B parameters), Mistral's models, and DeepSeek's releases all demonstrated that open-weight models can compete with proprietary offerings on most tasks. DeepSeek-V2 and V3 in particular shocked the field by matching GPT-4-class performance while being fully open and trainable.

For engineers, the choice maps to a tradeoff triangle: capability, control, and cost. Closed models (GPT-4o, Claude) offer the highest raw capability and the simplest API, but you have zero control over the model weights, training data, or inference infrastructure. Open models (Llama 3, Mistral) give you full control — fine-tune on your data, deploy on your hardware, modify the architecture — at the cost of engineering effort and potentially lower peak capability.

The economic argument for open-source is strongest in two scenarios: high-volume inference (where API costs compound) and sensitive domains (healthcare, legal, finance) where data cannot leave your infrastructure. The argument for closed-source is strongest for low-volume, high-complexity tasks where the frontier gap still matters.

AI Regulation: The EU AI Act and Beyond

On August 1, 2024, the EU AI Act entered into force — the world's first comprehensive AI regulation. It uses a risk-tiered framework: unacceptable risk (banned — social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance in public), high risk (strict requirements — healthcare diagnostics, hiring tools, credit scoring), limited risk (transparency obligations — chatbots must disclose they're AI), and minimal risk (no requirements — spam filters, video game AI).

The Act is extraterritorial: if your AI system affects EU residents, you're subject to it regardless of where your company is based. Fines reach up to 7% of global annual revenue — higher than GDPR.

Open-source gets a partial exemption. If you release model weights under an open license, you're exempt from most requirements — unless your model is classified as posing "systemic risk," defined as training compute exceeding 10^25 FLOPs. This threshold catches only the very largest models (GPT-4-class and above), leaving most open-source projects in the clear.

In the US, the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) provides voluntary guidelines. It's not law, but it's increasingly referenced in procurement requirements and industry standards. The framework emphasizes governance, risk mapping, measurement, and management — practical steps rather than prohibitions.

🚨 Compliance Reality

If you're building a high-risk application under the EU AI Act, you need: a conformity assessment, technical documentation of your training data and methodology, human oversight mechanisms, and ongoing monitoring. The grace period for most provisions ends in August 2026. Start your compliance work now — retrofitting is far more expensive than building it in.

Edge AI: Models on Consumer Hardware

Edge AI means running models directly on the device — a laptop, phone, or IoT sensor — rather than calling a cloud API. The SLM revolution made this practical. A 4-bit quantized Phi-4 runs at 40+ tokens per second on an RTX 4090. Gemma-3 1B runs on a smartphone. Apple's on-device models power features across iOS.

The toolchain has matured. ONNX Runtime provides a hardware-agnostic inference engine — export your model once, run it on CPU, GPU, or NPU. TensorFlow Lite (TFLite) targets mobile and embedded devices with aggressive quantization and operator fusion. llama.cpp and its ecosystem (GGUF format, Metal/CUDA backends) became the de facto standard for running LLMs locally.

The benefits go beyond cost savings. Edge inference provides hard privacy guarantees — user data never leaves the device. Latency drops to single-digit milliseconds for small models. There's no network dependency, so the system works offline. For applications like real-time translation, on-device voice assistants, and medical devices in low-connectivity environments, edge deployment isn't optional — it's the only viable path.

# Exporting a model for edge deployment with ONNX
import torch
from transformers import AutoModelForSequenceClassification

model = AutoModelForSequenceClassification.from_pretrained("my-fine-tuned-slm")
dummy_input = torch.randint(0, 1000, (1, 128))  # batch=1, seq_len=128

torch.onnx.export(
    model,
    dummy_input,
    "model_edge.onnx",
    input_names=["input_ids"],
    output_names=["logits"],
    dynamic_axes={"input_ids": {0: "batch", 1: "seq_len"}},
    opset_version=17
)
# Then quantize: onnxruntime.quantization.quantize_dynamic(...)
# Result: ~4x smaller model, ~2x faster inference, minimal accuracy loss

Full-Duplex Voice AI

GPT-4o's 320ms audio response time enabled a new interaction pattern: full-duplex voice AI. Unlike traditional voice assistants that follow a strict listen → process → speak pipeline, full-duplex systems can be interrupted mid-sentence, detect emotional tone in real-time, and adjust their response while still speaking. It's the difference between a walkie-talkie and a phone call.

The architecture is speech-to-speech: audio goes in, audio comes out, with no intermediate text transcription step. The model processes spectral features directly and generates audio tokens directly. This preserves paralinguistic information — tone, pace, emphasis, hesitation — that gets lost in an ASR → LLM → TTS pipeline. Streaming audio means the model starts responding before the user finishes speaking, enabling natural conversational overlap.

ML/LLM Model Lifecycle Management

With all these models proliferating — reasoning models, SLMs, multimodal models, fine-tuned variants — model lifecycle management became an urgent operational concern. You're no longer managing one model. You're managing a fleet: different models for different query types, A/B testing new versions, rolling back when a fine-tune regresses, tracking which training data went into which checkpoint.

Evaluation-driven development is the core practice. Before changing anything — the model, the prompt, the retrieval pipeline — you define an evaluation suite that captures what "good" looks like for your application. Every change is measured against this suite before deployment. No vibes-based shipping. The evaluation suite becomes the single source of truth, more important than any individual model choice.

The lifecycle looks like this: define evals → establish baseline → experiment (new model, new prompt, new pipeline) → measure against evals → deploy if improved → monitor production metrics → feed failures back into evals. It's the same build-measure-learn loop from software engineering, adapted for probabilistic systems where "correct" is a distribution, not a binary.

💡 The Meta-Pattern

Every trend in this section — reasoning models, SLMs, multimodal, compound systems, edge AI — increases the number of choices you face. The meta-pattern of 2024-2025 isn't any single technology. It's the shift from "which model should I use?" to "which system of models, tools, protocols, and evaluation frameworks gives me the best reliability-cost-latency tradeoff for my specific use case?" The answer is always a system, never a single model.
