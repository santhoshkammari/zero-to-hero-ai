The converged 2024 recipe: the standard LLM architecture stack

Every week, new model drops — LLaMA-something, Mistral-whatever, another GPT variant. Different labs, different budgets — all converged on same internal components. This section: those shared components, where families diverge.

The Decoder-Only Bet

Scenario: ML engineer at small startup TinyChat. Job: build chatbot for restaurant chain. Modest GPU budget, launch deadline. Open Hugging Face, type "language model" — thousands of results. Where to start?

First decision narrows field: architecture type. Original 2017 transformer had two halves — encoder reads input, decoder produces output. Field split into three camps, each keeping different halves.

Encoder-only models (BERT family) kept encoder half. Read full input at once — every token sees every other token, both directions. Bidirectional view → exceptional at understanding text: sentiment classification, NER, sentence embeddings. No built-in generation mechanism.

Encoder-decoder models (T5, BART) kept both halves. Encoder reads input, decoder generates output. Most faithful descendant of original transformer. T5's "text-to-text" framing — every task reformulated as "translate this input into that output" — looked like winner for a while.

Decoder-only models (GPT family) kept decoder half. Process tokens left-to-right, each token attends only to past. Causal attention — see past, not future — enables autoregressive generation. Predict one token, append, predict next.

TinyChat needs to generate answers. Rules out encoder-only. Real question: encoder-decoder or decoder-only?

Decoder-only won. Reasons shaped entire LLM landscape. First: one stack scales easier than two — every simplification matters from 1B to 100B parameters. Second: autoregressive objective (predict next token) is universal task interface. Translation? "Translate to French: [text]". Classification? "Is this review positive or negative?". No encoder-decoder boundary needed when prompting handles routing. Third: decoder-only gets KV cache — compute each token's key-value pair once, reuse for all subsequent tokens. Efficient inference. Encoder-decoder re-processes full input through encoder — wasteful for long conversations.

Encoder-decoder felt more principled — clean machine translation architecture from original paper. Engineering beat elegance. One stack, scaled far enough, flexible training objective, ate the world.

Encoder-only isn't dead — specialized. TinyChat needs complaint classification (billing, food quality, delivery)? 110M DeBERTa beats 7B decoder at fraction of cost. Right tool, right problem.

The GPT Lineage — Scaling as a Strategy

Decoder-only established as winner. Question: what to do with it? OpenAI's answer: make it bigger, train it longer.

GPT story: removing constraints, one generation at a time. Trace through each era — what each GPT gave you.

GPT-1 (2018, 117M params) proved one idea: pre-train decoder-only transformer on book corpus with next-token prediction, fine-tune on specific task, beats scratch-trained models. Architecture: 12 layers, 12 heads, 768-dim hidden states. 2018: download GPT-1, fine-tune on restaurant Q&A, get reasonable results. Fine-tuning required — base model rambles incoherently without it.

GPT-2 (2019, 1.5B params) changed calculus. Title: "Language Models are Unsupervised Multitask Learners." Scale 13×, train on larger web corpus (WebText, ~40GB) — model performs tasks never explicitly trained for. Summarization, translation, QA — respectable quality, zero gradient updates on task data. Zero-shot capability from scale. 2019: prompt with "Q: What are your hours? A:" — coherent answer. Not great, but coherent. No restaurant-specific training.

OpenAI withheld GPT-2 full weights, worried about misuse. That debate: first serious public conversation about responsible AI release.

GPT-3 (2020, 175B params) leaped to few-shot in-context learning. Key discovery: give model few examples in prompt, it infers pattern and continues. No fine-tuning. No gradient updates. Task spec lives in prompt.

# The GPT-3 breakthrough: few-shot in-context learning
# No training required — the examples ARE the task specification

prompt = """
Customer: What time do you close on Sundays?
Answer: We close at 9 PM on Sundays.

Customer: Do you have vegan options?
Answer: Yes! We have a full vegan menu including our popular jackfruit tacos.

Customer: Is there parking available?
Answer:"""

# GPT-3 completes: "Yes, we have a free parking lot behind the restaurant
# that can accommodate up to 30 vehicles."
#
# It inferred the format (Q&A), the domain (restaurant), the tone
# (helpful, specific), and generated a plausible answer — all from
# three examples in the prompt. That's in-context learning.

GPT-3 training cost: ~$4.6M. 96 layers, 96 heads, 12,288-dim hidden. Created API-as-product model — no weights download, pay per token. For TinyChat: no GPUs, no training expertise, no ML infra needed. Need credit card and good prompt.

InstructGPT / GPT-3.5 (2022) added human alignment. Raw GPT-3 capable but erratic — helpful, offensive, or confidently wrong at random. RLHF fixed this: fine-tune to follow instructions, be helpful, harmless, honest. Powered early ChatGPT. Alignment didn't dumb model down — made it more useful. Model following intent beats smarter-but-unpredictable.

GPT-4 (2023) and GPT-4o (2024) added multimodal inputs (images, audio, text) and improved reasoning. GPT-4 passes bar exam, writes production code. Architecture undisclosed, widely rumored Mixture-of-Experts. GPT-4o: native audio + vision, omni-modal, 2× faster, half cost of GPT-4 Turbo.

Throughline: architecture barely changed across six years. Each generation removed a constraint via scale or training signal. GPT-1: pre-training transfers. GPT-2: skip fine-tuning. GPT-3: use few-shot prompting. InstructGPT: align models. GPT-4: add modalities. OpenAI core thesis: good enough architecture, scaled far enough, beats any amount of architectural cleverness at small scale.

Whether thesis holds forever — hotly debated. From 2018 to 2024, scaling camp won every round. Every competitor — Google, Meta, Mistral — had to respond.

The LLaMA Revolution — Open Weights Change Everything

Response came from unexpected direction. February 2023: Meta released LLaMA-1 — models (7B, 13B, 33B, 65B) competitive with GPT-3-class. Weights restricted to researchers. Leaked within a week. Open-source LLM explosion began.

LLaMA-1 wasn't breakthrough architecture — breakthrough training philosophy. DeepMind's 2022 Chinchilla paper: most large models undertrained. Field kept making models bigger, not training on enough data. Chinchilla showed smaller model trained on more tokens matches or beats larger model on fewer tokens. LLaMA-1 applied this aggressively: 65B model trained on 1.4T tokens of public data. GPT-3 (175B — nearly 3× larger) trained on ~300B tokens.

Scaling labs built bigger engines, same amount of fuel. LLaMA: smaller engine, full tank goes further.

LLaMA-1 introduced component combination that became modern standard: RoPE (position encoding), RMSNorm (normalization), SwiGLU (FFN activation), pre-normalization throughout. None were novel — each proposed in earlier papers — but LLaMA first widely-available model to package them into clean, reproducible recipe.

LLaMA-2 (July 2023) shipped with commercial license — first frontier-class open model available for businesses. Added Grouped-Query Attention (GQA) for 70B variant (memory optimization, covered later). RLHF-tuned chat variants included — drop-in alternative to API chatbots.

LLaMA-3 (April 2024) jumped to 15T training tokens — 10× LLaMA-1 — with 128K vocabulary (up from 32K). Ships in 8B, 70B, 405B. 8B rivals LLaMA-2 70B. 405B competes with GPT-4-class. All open weights.

LLaMA moment was pivotal. Before LLaMA: pay OpenAI API costs forever, or spend millions training own model. After LLaMA: download 7B model, fine-tune on restaurant data for few hundred dollars of GPU time, deploy on own servers. No API dependency. No per-token costs. Full behavior control. AI economics changed overnight.

7B model runs on gaming laptop, carries coherent restaurant conversation. In 2020 that required 175B params and data center. Engine got smaller. Fuel got better.

Inside the Modern LLM Block — RMSNorm and SwiGLU

Every model — LLaMA, Mistral, Qwen, Phi — built from same building block: transformer decoder layer. Stack 32 → 7B model. Stack 80 → 70B model. Differences between families: specific components inside each block.

Build single block from scratch. Hidden dimension of 4 — absurdly small, lets us see every number. Real model like LLaMA-2 7B uses 4,096-dim, mechanics identical.

Transformer decoder layer does two things: tokens talk to each other (attention), then each token processed independently (FFN). Original 2017 transformer: sequential, normalization after each step:

# Post-norm (original transformer, GPT-2 era):
# Token comes in as vector x (dimension 4 in our toy example)

x = x + Attention(x)       # tokens talk to each other
x = LayerNorm(x)           # normalize
x = x + FFN(x)             # each token processed independently
x = LayerNorm(x)           # normalize again

Worked for 12-layer models. Stack 60, 80, 96 layers — post-norm became minefield. Gradients through dozens of LayerNorm ops accumulated instabilities. Training runs collapsed without warning. Needed careful LR warmup; even then, runs diverged at step 50,000.

Fix: put normalization before each sub-layer instead of after.

# Pre-norm (LLaMA, Mistral, every modern LLM):
x = x + Attention(RMSNorm(x))   # normalize BEFORE attention
x = x + FFN(RMSNorm(x))         # normalize BEFORE FFN

Why it helps: look at residual path. Pre-norm: main highway (x = x + ...) is clean sum. Gradients flow straight through addition, no normalization in path. Normalization on side road, inside sub-layer — stabilizes without disrupting gradient highway. Post-norm: normalization sits on highway. Every layer forces gradients through another nonlinear op. Compounds over 80 layers.

Moving layer norm up by one line looks trivially cosmetic. Gradient math is unambiguous: pre-norm preserves residual stream identity. That's what makes 80-layer training stable.

Standard LayerNorm: compute mean and variance, subtract mean, divide by std dev. RMSNorm (Root Mean Square Norm): drop mean-centering entirely, normalize by root-mean-square alone:

# LayerNorm: center then scale
#   mean = (x₁ + x₂ + x₃ + x₄) / 4
#   variance = Σ(xᵢ - mean)² / 4
#   LayerNorm(x) = (x - mean) / √(variance + ε) * γ
#
# RMSNorm: scale only (no centering)
#   rms = √(x₁² + x₂² + x₃² + x₄²) / 4)
#   RMSNorm(x) = x / rms * γ
#
# For our tiny vector x = [2.0, -1.0, 0.5, 1.5]:
#   rms = √((4.0 + 1.0 + 0.25 + 2.25) / 4) = √(1.875) ≈ 1.37
#   RMSNorm(x) ≈ [1.46, -0.73, 0.37, 1.10] * γ
#
# The mean subtraction in LayerNorm costs extra compute and,
# empirically, doesn't help training quality in deep transformers.
# RMSNorm saves ~10-15% of normalization compute for free.

Original transformer FFN: two-layer MLP with ReLU. Project to wider dimension (4×), apply ReLU, project back. Works. Treats every dimension identically — activation applies element-wise, no interaction between dimensions.

SwiGLU (Shazeer, 2020) adds gating mechanism. Network learns which dimensions to amplify, which to suppress, per token:

# Original FFN:
#   FFN(x) = ReLU(x @ W₁) @ W₂
#   Two weight matrices. Simple. Each dimension independent.
#
# SwiGLU FFN:
#   SwiGLU(x) = (Swish(x @ W_gate) ⊙ (x @ W₁)) @ W₂
#
#   Three weight matrices now: W_gate, W₁, W₂
#   ⊙ means element-wise multiplication (the "gating")
#   Swish(z) = z × sigmoid(z) — a smooth, non-monotonic activation
#
# Walkthrough with our tiny 4-dim example, x = [2.0, -1.0, 0.5, 1.5]:
#
#   gate_values = Swish(x @ W_gate)   → e.g., [0.9, 0.1, 0.7, 0.3]
#   main_values = x @ W₁              → e.g., [1.5, -2.0, 0.8, 1.2]
#   gated = gate_values ⊙ main_values → [1.35, -0.2, 0.56, 0.36]
#                                         ↑ amplified  ↑ suppressed
#   output = gated @ W₂               → back to model dimension
#
# The gate decides: "Let this dimension through strongly,
# choke that one off." The network learns WHAT to gate
# during training. Different tokens get different gates.

No one fully knows why SwiGLU works so well. Shazeer tested dozen gated activation variants. SwiGLU won empirically: ~1–3% better performance for same compute budget. Field adopted it because numbers spoke — no airtight theoretical explanation.

Gating costs third weight matrix (W_gate). To keep same total params, shrink inner dimension from 4×d_model to ~(8/3)×d_model. Parameter-neutral swap, better performance. Everyone takes that deal.

How RoPE Encodes Position Through Rotation

Attention has no built-in sense of order. Shuffle tokens — attention scores don't change. Each token attends to every other based on content alone, no notion of before/after. Transformers need position encoding: inject where each token sits in sequence.

Early approaches: two options. Learned absolute position embeddings (BERT, GPT-2) assign trainable vector to each position. Works, but fatal flaw: model never saw position 513 during training (if max 512 tokens) — falls off cliff on longer text.

Sinusoidal position embeddings (original transformer): fixed sine/cosine waves at different frequencies. No learning needed, but encode absolute positions. Relative relationships not baked in — model must learn "positions 5 and 8 are 3 apart" on its own.

What's needed: attention that naturally understands relative positions. "Word 3 positions back" matters more than "word at position 847." Rotary Position Embeddings — RoPE (Su et al., 2021) — achieve this via geometric trick.

Core idea: imagine 2D vector — arrow on flat plane. Rotate it by some angle. Rotation in 2D: multiply by rotation matrix.

# A 2D vector [x₁, x₂] rotated by angle θ:
#
#   ┌ cos(θ)  -sin(θ) ┐   ┌ x₁ ┐     ┌ x₁·cos(θ) - x₂·sin(θ) ┐
#   │                  │ × │    │  =  │                          │
#   └ sin(θ)   cos(θ) ┘   └ x₂ ┘     └ x₁·sin(θ) + x₂·cos(θ) ┘
#
# RoPE's insight: use position as the rotation angle.
# Token at position m → rotate by angle m·θ₀
# Token at position n → rotate by angle n·θ₀
#
# So the query at position 5 gets rotated by 5·θ₀
# And the key at position 3 gets rotated by 3·θ₀

Elegant part: query at position m and key at position n compute dot product (attention scores). Two rotations combine. Dot product of rotated vectors depends on difference in angles: m·θ₀ − n·θ₀ = (m−n)·θ₀. Attention score → function of (m−n), relative distance, not absolute positions.

Relative position encoding, free, from rotation.

Real models use 128-dim head vectors. RoPE pairs up dimensions: (dim 1, dim 2), (dim 3, dim 4), etc. Each pair rotates at different frequency — like clock with fast second hand and slow hour hand. Different frequencies → distinguish positions at different scales: nearby tokens via high-frequency, distant via low-frequency.

# RoPE for a d-dimensional vector (d=128 in LLaMA):
# Pair dimensions: (0,1), (2,3), (4,5), ..., (126,127)
# Each pair i gets frequency: θᵢ = 10000^(-2i/d)
#
# Position m rotates pair i by angle m · θᵢ
#
# Low i (early pairs): high frequency — changes fast with position
#   → good for distinguishing nearby tokens
# High i (late pairs): low frequency — changes slowly
#   → good for distinguishing distant tokens
#
# Like a clock:
#   Second hand (high freq) = tells you "3 seconds ago"
#   Hour hand (low freq)    = tells you "2 hours ago"
#   Both together           = full position information

RoPE's superpower: context length extension. Positions encoded as rotation frequencies — scale them for longer sequences. Linear interpolation: divide all position indices by factor (4K→16K: divide by 4). NTK-aware scaling: adjust base frequency. YaRN: scaling + attention temperature adjustments per dimension. LLaMA-3 trains at 8K, extends to 128K — RoPE structure supports graceful stretching.

Alternative: ALiBi (Attention with Linear Biases), used in Falcon and MPT. Adds linear penalty to attention scores by distance — farther tokens get bigger score reduction. Simpler, extrapolates well. Less expressive than RoPE. By 2024, RoPE won: LLaMA, Mistral, Qwen, Phi all use it. ALiBi fades from new architectures.

GQA, sliding window attention, MoE, and design philosophies of Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek — all covered next.

The KV Cache Problem and Grouped-Query Attention

LLaMA-2 7B deployed, working well. Restaurant chain wants longer conversations — booking flows, menu exploration, dietary restrictions. Increase context 4K→32K. GPU runs out of memory.

Culprit: not model weights. It's KV cache. During autoregressive generation, model stores key and value vectors for every processed token — no recompute needed. Cache grows linearly with sequence length and number of attention heads.

Real arithmetic. Standard Multi-Head Attention (MHA): every head has own query, key, value projections. LLaMA-2 70B: 64 heads, head dim 128, 80 layers, 32K sequence length, float16:

# KV cache = 2 (keys + values) × num_heads × head_dim × seq_len × layers × bytes
#
# MHA (64 KV heads, the standard):
#   2 × 64 × 128 × 32768 × 80 × 2 bytes
#   = ~85 GB
#
# An A100 GPU has 80 GB of memory total.
# The KV cache alone doesn't fit. And we haven't loaded
# the model weights yet (~140 GB in fp16).
#
# For TinyChat, this means: with standard attention,
# you literally cannot serve a 70B model with 32K context
# on anything less than 4 GPUs.

First fix: Multi-Query Attention (MQA) — 64 query heads share single KV projections. KV cache drops 64×. Quality drop noticeable — one key, one value loses expressiveness.

Grouped-Query Attention (GQA) is the compromise that stuck. Instead of 64 KV heads (MHA) or 1 KV head (MQA), you use a small number of KV head groups. Each group serves multiple query heads:

# Think of it like a restaurant kitchen (our TinyChat theme):
#
# MHA = 64 chefs, each with their own pantry (64 pantries)
#   Expensive. Every chef stores their own ingredients.
#
# MQA = 64 chefs, one shared pantry (1 pantry)
#   Cheap, but everyone's fighting over the same spice rack.
#   Quality drops.
#
# GQA = 64 chefs, 8 shared pantries (8 groups of 8 chefs)
#   8 chefs share one well-stocked pantry. Enough variety,
#   way less storage.
#
# In attention terms:
#   MHA:  Q₁→KV₁   Q₂→KV₂   ...  Q₆₄→KV₆₄  (64 KV heads)
#   GQA:  Q₁ thru Q₈ → KV₁,  Q₉ thru Q₁₆ → KV₂, ... (8 KV heads)
#   MQA:  Q₁ thru Q₆₄ → KV₁                          (1 KV head)
#
# LLaMA-2 70B: 64 query heads, 8 KV heads → 8× KV cache reduction
# KV cache drops from 85 GB to ~10.5 GB. Fits on one GPU.

GQA quality impact: negligible — within noise of full MHA. KV cache savings: enormous. GQA now standard: LLaMA-2 70B, LLaMA-3 (all sizes), Mistral-7B (8 KV groups, 32 query heads), most new models.

Counterintuitive: share KV projections across 8 query heads and lose nothing. Working intuition: KV captures "what info is available at each position" — doesn't need query-level diversity. Queries need diversity — different heads search different patterns. KV can be shared — underlying info at each position is same regardless of which query asks.

One more: Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA) from DeepSeek-V2. Instead of grouping KV heads, MLA compresses entire KV representation into low-dimensional latent space before caching. More aggressive compression, larger memory savings. Less widely adopted, but signals direction: increasingly creative KV cache shrinking.

Mistral's Tricks — Sliding Windows and Mixture-of-Experts

September 2023: Mistral AI released 7B model that beat LLaMA-2 13B on every benchmark — half the parameter count. Two architectural innovations: both aimed at doing more with less memory.

Sliding Window Attention

Standard causal attention: every token attends to all previous tokens. Token 8,000 looks back at 0–7,999. 8,000 attention entries per token, grows linearly → total computation quadratic in sequence length.

Mistral-7B: restrict each token to local window W = 4,096. Token 8,000 attends to 3,904–7,999, ignores everything before.

Immediate objection: "model forgets everything beyond window?" Answer more subtle — depends on what happens across layers.

# Layer 1: Token 8000 attends to tokens [3904, 7999]
#          Token 5000 attends to tokens [904, 4999]
#
# Layer 2: Token 8000 attends to tokens [3904, 7999]
#          BUT those tokens already saw [0, 7999] in layer 1.
#          So token 8000 now has INDIRECT access to [0, 7999].
#
# Layer 3: The receptive field widens further...
#
# After L layers: effective receptive field = L × W
# Mistral-7B: 32 layers × 4096 window = 131K tokens
#
# Think of it like binoculars (window) vs. telescope (full attention):
# Binoculars see a limited field, but if you hand what you see
# to the next person up a tower (next layer), and they use
# their binoculars too... the person at the top of a 32-story
# tower has information from the entire horizon.

Early token info propagates up through layer stack, compressed at each layer. Lossy vs. full attention, but quality difference small empirically. Memory benefit: Mistral pairs sliding window with rolling KV cache — fixed-size circular buffer. Old positions overwritten. Memory usage constant regardless of sequence length. KV cache never exhausted during inference.

Binoculars analogy: limited local view per layer, information passed upward. 32-layer tower has info from entire horizon.

Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)

MoE appears everywhere — Mixtral, GPT-4 (rumored), Gemini. Key: how model has 47B params but runs at speed of 13B.

Standard transformer: every token passes through same FFN each layer. FFN = biggest parameter hog (~2/3 of total params). MoE: replace single FFN with N parallel FFNs ("experts"), small router network selects which experts process each token:

# Standard FFN: Every token goes through the same kitchen.
#   y = FFN(x)
#
# MoE FFN: Multiple kitchens, each with a specialty.
#   A host (router) directs each dish (token) to the right kitchen.
#
#   router_scores = softmax(x @ W_router)   → scores for each expert
#   top_2 = pick the 2 highest-scoring experts
#   y = weight₁ × Expert₁(x) + weight₂ × Expert₂(x)
#
# Mixtral 8×7B:
#   8 expert FFNs per layer, 2 active per token
#   Total parameters: ~46.7B (all experts counted)
#   Active parameters: ~13B per token (only 2 experts fire)
#
# Result: The model has the knowledge of a 47B model
# but the inference speed of a 13B model.

Router learns to specialize during training. Researchers found different experts activate for different content — code, math reasoning, conversational text. Not perfectly clean specialization, but empirical results consistent.

Main engineering challenge: load balancing. Without intervention, router collapses — picks 2 favorites, sends everything to them, other six undertrained. Fix: auxiliary loss penalizing uneven usage. Also: communication overhead in distributed training — experts on different GPUs, tokens routed across network.

MoE deal: Mixtral 8×7B gives GPT-3.5-class quality, fraction of GPU compute cost — only 13B params active per token. Catch: full 47B params still need to fit in memory — saving compute, not memory. Quantization (covered later) makes that manageable.

The Broader Landscape — Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek, Phi

GPT, LLaMA, Mistral not alone. Other families introduced distinctive ideas worth knowing — interviews, production decisions, field evolution.

Claude (Anthropic) built on Constitutional AI (CAI). Most LLMs aligned via RLHF: humans rank outputs, model fine-tuned to match. Claude: model critiques own outputs against written principles ("constitution"), revises them, trains on revisions. Self-critique loop reduces dependence on human labelers, makes alignment criteria explicit and auditable. Claude 3 (2024): Haiku (fast, cheap), Sonnet (balanced), Opus (max capability) — 200K context. Safety-first → more cautious, more reliable for sensitive applications.

Gemini (Google): natively multimodal. GPT-4 trained on text, vision added later. Gemini trained from ground up on interleaved text, images, audio, video. Cross-modal reasoning more fluid — diagrams, video Q&A. Gemini 1.5 Pro: 1M token context via MoE. Google also released Gemma (2B, 7B) as open-weight derivative for community use.

DeepSeek (China): introduced MLA in DeepSeek-V2, compressing KV cache via low-dimensional latent projection. DeepSeek-V3 combined MLA + MoE for remarkably efficient inference. Architectural innovation is global.

Phi (Microsoft) challenged "scale is all you need." Phi-1 (1.3B) trained on textbook-quality data + synthetic examples from larger models. Matched models 10× its size on coding benchmarks. Phi-2 (2.7B), Phi-3 (3.8B) continued thesis: data quality > data quantity. Phone, laptop, edge deployment — small, well-trained model is surprisingly capable.

Qwen (Alibaba) and Yi (01.AI): leading Chinese open-weight families. Strong multilingual + coding. Qwen-2 ships 0.5B–72B, competes with LLaMA-3 class on many benchmarks.

Strip away names and marketing: Claude, Gemini, LLaMA, Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek all share same engine block — decoder-only transformer, pre-norm + RMSNorm, SwiGLU FFN, RoPE. Differ in fuel (training data), tuning (alignment), turbochargers (MoE, MLA), paint job (API vs. open weights). Same engine.

Open vs. Closed — The Great Divide

LLM world has fundamental fault line. More important than any single architecture choice.

Closed models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini): frontier capabilities. API access, pay per token. No weights. Can't fine-tune (mostly). Can't self-host. Subject to provider policies, pricing, uptime. OpenAI changes content policy — chatbot behavior changes overnight.

Open-weight models (LLaMA, Mistral, Qwen, Phi): weights available. Fine-tune on own data. Quantize for cheaper hardware. Deploy anywhere — on-prem, any cloud, laptop. Full behavior control. Need food allergy accuracy? Fine-tune specifically — API models don't allow this.

Capability gap between closed and open shrinking fast. LLaMA-3 405B approaches GPT-4 on many benchmarks. Mistral punches above weight. For most production use cases: question isn't "highest benchmark score?" — it's "what are constraints?" Latency, cost, data privacy, deployment control, fine-tuning needs usually matter more than last few points on MMLU.

Prototyping: use GPT-4 API. Fastest path to working demo. Production at scale: own open-weight model — dramatically cheaper, more controllable. No universal right answer. Right answer for specific constraints.

No clean framework for this decision. Every project: spreadsheet weighing latency, cost, privacy, quality — answer always "it depends." Honest ambiguity. Anyone saying always use open or always use closed APIs is selling something.

Model Cards and Responsible Release

We've been talking about architecture and capabilities, but there's a dimension we haven't addressed: the information that ships alongside a model. This matters more than most technical sections acknowledge.

A model card is a document that accompanies a model release, describing what the model is, how it was trained, what it's good at, what it's bad at, and what risks it carries. The concept was formalized in a 2019 paper by Margaret Mitchell et al., and it's become the standard practice for responsible model releases.

A good model card includes the training data composition (or at least a high-level description), benchmark results with methodology, known limitations and failure modes, intended use cases and out-of-scope uses, bias evaluations, and environmental impact (compute used). LLaMA-3's model card, for example, explicitly lists failure modes around code generation in certain languages, limitations in mathematical reasoning, and the fact that the model may produce culturally biased outputs for non-English languages.

The practice matters for TinyChat because deploying a model without understanding its limitations is how you end up with a restaurant chatbot that confidently tells a customer with a peanut allergy that the pad thai is nut-free (when it isn't). Model cards won't prevent every failure, but they're the minimum baseline for responsible deployment — a checklist that forces you to think about what could go wrong before it does.

The open vs. closed divide shows up here too. Open-weight releases like LLaMA ship detailed model cards. Closed models like GPT-4 publish a "system card" that's substantially less detailed about training data and architecture. Some releases — particularly from smaller labs racing to publish — ship with minimal documentation. The trend is toward more transparency, but we're not there yet.

The Converged Recipe

After all that, let's make the convergence explicit. If you're reading a new model release in 2024 and the paper doesn't call out a specific design choice, it's almost certainly using this default stack:

The 2024 LLM Standard Recipe:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Architecture:     Decoder-only transformer
Normalization:    Pre-norm with RMSNorm
FFN Activation:   SwiGLU (d_ff ≈ 8/3 × d_model)
Position Enc:     RoPE (Rotary Position Embeddings)
Attention:        GQA (Grouped-Query Attention)
Bias terms:       None (removed from attention and FFN)
Vocabulary:       32K–128K BPE tokens
Context window:   8K–128K (RoPE enables extension)
Training:         AdamW optimizer, cosine LR schedule
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

When you read a new paper, the deviations from this recipe
are the interesting parts. Everything else is assumed.

This recipe is the engine block. The engine is the same across almost every family. LLaMA, Mistral, Qwen, Phi, Gemma — they all run this engine. The differences that matter are:

Training data — How much, how clean, what languages, what domains. This is probably the single biggest differentiator and also the most opaque. Most labs won't tell you exactly what's in the training set.

Scale — More parameters and more tokens generally help, up to a point. But Phi showed that data quality can compensate for scale, and Mistral showed that architectural efficiency can compensate for raw size.

Alignment method — RLHF (OpenAI), Constitutional AI (Anthropic), DPO (used by many open models), or various combinations. This determines how the model behaves, not what it knows.

Efficiency innovations — MoE (Mixtral, Gemini), sliding window attention (Mistral), MLA (DeepSeek). These are the turbochargers — they don't change what the engine does, but they change how fast and cheap it runs.

When you understand this recipe, reading new model papers becomes dramatically faster. You scan for deviations. DeepSeek-V2's MLA replaces GQA — that's interesting, read that section. Mamba's state-space layers replace the entire attention mechanism — that's a major departure. Jamba mixes Mamba and transformer layers in one model — hybrid approach. The recipe is your baseline. Deviations from it are the signal.
