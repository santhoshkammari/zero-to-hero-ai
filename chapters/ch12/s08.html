Embeddings & Semantic Search

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 5 subtopics
TL;DR

Embeddings turn text into dense vectors where meaning lives as geometry — similar sentences land near each other. Word2Vec started the revolution; Sentence-BERT made it practical for full sentences. Modern embedding models let you search millions of documents by meaning, not keywords. Cosine similarity is the default metric. Brute-force search doesn't scale, so you need approximate nearest neighbor algorithms like HNSW or IVF. The retrieve-then-rerank pattern — fast bi-encoder retrieval followed by accurate cross-encoder scoring — is the backbone of every modern semantic search system and RAG pipeline.

Why Embeddings Matter

Imagine you're building a customer support search engine. A user types "my payment didn't go through." Your database has a FAQ titled "Troubleshooting failed transactions." A keyword search won't match — there's zero word overlap. But the two sentences mean almost the same thing. How do you teach a computer that?

This is the core problem. Computers see text as strings of characters. They have no notion of meaning. The word "bank" is just four bytes. It doesn't know whether you're talking about a river bank or a financial institution, let alone that "payment didn't go through" is semantically close to "failed transactions."

Embeddings solve this by mapping text into a high-dimensional vector space — typically 384 to 3072 floating-point numbers. The key insight: the mapping is trained so that texts with similar meanings end up with similar vectors. "My payment didn't go through" and "Troubleshooting failed transactions" will land close together in that space, even though they share no words. "The cat sat on the mat" will land far away from both. Meaning becomes geometry. And once meaning is geometry, search becomes a nearest-neighbor lookup.

This idea underpins everything in modern NLP: semantic search, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), recommendation systems, clustering, and deduplication. If you understand embeddings, you understand the plumbing behind every LLM application that needs to find relevant information.

From Word2Vec to Sentence Embeddings

The Word Embedding Revolution

The story starts in 2013 with Word2Vec. Tomáš Mikolov and his team at Google showed that you could train a simple neural network on a massive text corpus to produce vectors for individual words. The training objective was elegant: predict a word from its neighbors (CBOW) or predict the neighbors from a word (Skip-gram). The result was a vector space where "king" minus "man" plus "woman" landed near "queen." This wasn't programmed — it emerged from the statistics of language.

GloVe (Global Vectors) from Stanford took a different approach. Instead of a neural network, it factorized a word co-occurrence matrix. The result was similar: dense vectors that captured semantic relationships. Both produced vectors of 100–300 dimensions per word, and they were wildly useful for downstream tasks.

But there was a glaring limitation. These were word-level embeddings. How do you get a vector for "The movie was surprisingly good despite the terrible trailer"? The naive approach is to average all the word vectors. This works better than you'd expect, but it throws away word order and nuance. "The dog bit the man" and "The man bit the dog" would get the same embedding. That's clearly wrong.

Sentence-BERT and Contrastive Learning

In 2019, Nils Reimers and Iryna Gurevych introduced Sentence-BERT (SBERT), and it changed everything. The idea: take a pretrained BERT model, feed in a full sentence, and use the output as a sentence embedding. But raw BERT embeddings are actually terrible for similarity — they cluster in a narrow cone and don't separate meanings well. The fix was contrastive learning with a siamese network architecture.

Here's how it works. You take two copies of BERT that share the same weights. You feed sentence A into one copy and sentence B into the other. Each produces an embedding. During training, you push embeddings of similar pairs closer together and pull dissimilar pairs apart. The training data is pairs of sentences labeled as similar or dissimilar — drawn from NLI (natural language inference) datasets, paraphrase corpora, or search query-document pairs. After fine-tuning, the model produces embeddings where cosine similarity actually correlates with semantic similarity.

Why was this such a big deal? Before SBERT, comparing two sentences with BERT required feeding both into the model together — a cross-encoder approach that takes O(n²) comparisons for n sentences. SBERT lets you encode each sentence independently and store the vectors. Comparing 10,000 sentences goes from 50 million BERT inference calls to 10,000 encoding calls plus cheap vector math.

Modern Embedding Models

The field has moved fast since SBERT. Today you have many options, each with different tradeoffs in quality, speed, dimensionality, and cost. Here's a practical comparison:

A notable recent innovation is Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL). Named after Russian nesting dolls, MRL trains embeddings so that you can truncate them to fewer dimensions without retraining. An embedding trained at 1024 dimensions still works reasonably well if you chop it to 256 dimensions. This is huge for production systems: you can store smaller vectors to save memory and only use full-size vectors when you need maximum precision. OpenAI's text-embedding-3 models support this natively — you just pass a dimensions parameter to the API.

PRACTICAL TIP

Start with an open model like all-MiniLM-L6-v2 for prototyping. It runs on a CPU, produces 384-dim vectors, and is surprisingly good. Switch to a larger model (BGE, E5, or an API model) only when you've measured that the quality gap actually matters for your use case.

Similarity Metrics

Once you have embeddings, you need a way to measure how close two vectors are. There are three common choices, and which one you pick matters more than you might think.

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors. It ranges from -1 (opposite directions) to 1 (same direction). Crucially, it ignores magnitude — a vector of length 10 pointing north is just as similar to a unit vector pointing north as another unit vector pointing north. This makes it robust to variations in embedding norms, which is why it's the default choice for most embedding models.

Euclidean distance (L2 distance) measures the straight-line distance between two points in vector space. Unlike cosine, it cares about magnitude. If one vector is twice as long as another but points in the same direction, Euclidean distance will say they're far apart while cosine similarity says they're identical. Use Euclidean distance when magnitude carries meaning — for example, if longer vectors represent "more confident" embeddings.

Dot product is the simplest: multiply corresponding dimensions and sum. It's equivalent to cosine similarity multiplied by the magnitudes of both vectors. When your embeddings are L2-normalized (which many models produce by default), dot product and cosine similarity are exactly the same. Dot product is faster to compute because you skip the normalization step, so many production systems normalize embeddings at index time and then use dot product at query time.

import numpy as np

def cosine_similarity(a, b):
    return np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b))

def euclidean_distance(a, b):
    return np.linalg.norm(a - b)

def dot_product(a, b):
    return np.dot(a, b)

# Example: encode three sentences
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer

model = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")
sentences = [
    "How do I reset my password?",
    "I forgot my login credentials",
    "What is the weather today?"
]
embeddings = model.encode(sentences)

# Compare sentence 0 with sentence 1 (similar meaning)
print(f"Similar pair (cosine):  {cosine_similarity(embeddings[0], embeddings[1]):.4f}")
print(f"Similar pair (L2 dist): {euclidean_distance(embeddings[0], embeddings[1]):.4f}")

# Compare sentence 0 with sentence 2 (different meaning)
print(f"Different pair (cosine):  {cosine_similarity(embeddings[0], embeddings[2]):.4f}")
print(f"Different pair (L2 dist): {euclidean_distance(embeddings[0], embeddings[2]):.4f}")

# Output (approximate):
# Similar pair (cosine):  0.8352
# Similar pair (L2 dist): 0.5741
# Different pair (cosine):  0.1247
# Different pair (L2 dist): 1.3221

Notice how cosine similarity cleanly separates the similar pair (0.84) from the unrelated pair (0.12). That's embeddings doing their job — turning semantic similarity into a number you can sort by.

Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) Search

Why Brute-Force Doesn't Scale

Say you have 10 million document embeddings, each with 768 dimensions. A user sends a query. You embed it, then compute cosine similarity against all 10 million vectors. That's 10 million dot products on 768-dimensional vectors. On a modern CPU, this takes roughly 2–5 seconds. For a search engine that needs sub-100ms responses, that's unacceptable. And 10 million is small — production systems often have billions of vectors.

The solution is approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search. Instead of checking every vector, you build an index structure that lets you find the approximate top-k neighbors in milliseconds — with 95-99% recall compared to brute force. You trade a tiny amount of accuracy for enormous speed gains.

HNSW: Navigable Small-World Graphs

HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) is the most popular ANN algorithm today. The intuition is beautiful. Imagine you're in a city trying to find a specific address. At first, you take long-distance highways that get you to the right neighborhood quickly. Then you switch to local streets for the final approach. HNSW works the same way but with vectors.

It builds a multi-layer graph. The top layer is sparse — only a few vectors, connected by long-range links. Lower layers have more vectors and shorter-range connections. The bottom layer has every vector. To find the nearest neighbor of a query, you start at the top layer, greedily hop to whichever neighbor is closest to the query, then drop to the next layer and repeat. Each layer refines the search. The result: you examine maybe 200 vectors out of 10 million to find the true nearest neighbors with over 95% recall.

IVF and Product Quantization

IVF (Inverted File Index) takes a different approach. It clusters all your vectors into, say, 1,000 groups using k-means. At query time, you find the 10 closest cluster centroids, then brute-force search only the vectors in those clusters. This narrows the search from 10 million to maybe 100,000 vectors — a 100x speedup.

Product Quantization (PQ) tackles memory. Storing 10 million 768-dim float32 vectors takes about 30 GB. PQ compresses each vector by splitting it into sub-vectors and replacing each with a codebook index. This can shrink memory 10–50x with modest accuracy loss. In practice, you combine IVF with PQ (called IVF-PQ) to get both speed and memory savings.

FAISS in Practice

Facebook's FAISS library is the workhorse for vector search in production. Here's a practical walkthrough:

import faiss
import numpy as np

# Simulate 1 million embeddings of dimension 384
dim = 384
n_vectors = 1_000_000
database = np.random.randn(n_vectors, dim).astype("float32")
faiss.normalize_L2(database)  # normalize for cosine similarity

# --- Flat index (brute force, for comparison) ---
flat_index = faiss.IndexFlatIP(dim)  # IP = inner product (= cosine after normalization)
flat_index.add(database)

# --- HNSW index (fast approximate) ---
hnsw_index = faiss.IndexHNSWFlat(dim, 32)  # 32 = links per node
hnsw_index.add(database)

# --- IVF index (clustering-based) ---
n_clusters = 1024
quantizer = faiss.IndexFlatIP(dim)
ivf_index = faiss.IndexIVFFlat(quantizer, dim, n_clusters)
ivf_index.train(database)  # learn clusters
ivf_index.add(database)
ivf_index.nprobe = 10       # search 10 nearest clusters at query time

# Query
query = np.random.randn(1, dim).astype("float32")
faiss.normalize_L2(query)

# Search each index for top 5 results
for name, idx in [("Flat", flat_index), ("HNSW", hnsw_index), ("IVF", ivf_index)]:
    distances, indices = idx.search(query, 5)
    print(f"{name:5s} → top-5 indices: {indices[0]}, scores: {distances[0].round(3)}")
TRADEOFF

HNSW gives the best recall but uses more memory (it stores the full graph plus the vectors). IVF-PQ uses the least memory but has lower recall. For most applications under 50 million vectors, HNSW is the sweet spot. Beyond that, consider IVF-PQ or a managed vector database like Pinecone, Weaviate, or Qdrant that handles the scaling for you.

Bi-Encoder vs Cross-Encoder

There are two fundamentally different ways to use transformers for comparing text pairs, and understanding the difference is critical for system design.

A bi-encoder encodes each text independently. Query goes in one pass, document goes in another. You get two vectors, and you compare them with cosine similarity. This is what embedding models like SBERT produce. The huge advantage: you can precompute all document embeddings offline. At query time, you only encode the query once and compare against the stored vectors. This is what makes ANN search possible.

A cross-encoder feeds both texts into the model together, separated by a special token. The model sees both texts simultaneously and can attend across them — comparing them word-by-word. It outputs a single relevance score instead of two vectors. This joint attention makes cross-encoders significantly more accurate. They can catch subtle interactions like negation, conditional statements, and nuanced paraphrases that bi-encoders miss.

The catch? Cross-encoders can't be precomputed. You must run the model for every query-document pair at inference time. Comparing a query against 10 million documents with a cross-encoder would take hours.

The standard solution is the retrieve-then-rerank pattern. First, use a bi-encoder to retrieve the top 100 candidates from your full corpus via ANN search. Then, run a cross-encoder on just those 100 pairs to rerank them. You get the speed of bi-encoders and the accuracy of cross-encoders. This two-stage pattern is used in virtually every production search system, including RAG pipelines.

Training and Fine-Tuning Embeddings

How Embedding Models Learn

The core training objective for embedding models is contrastive loss. The idea: given an anchor sentence, pull its positive pair closer and push negative pairs farther away. InfoNCE loss (the most common variant) frames this as a classification problem — given one positive and N negatives, maximize the probability of selecting the positive. The temperature parameter controls how "sharp" the probability distribution is.

In-batch negatives are a training efficiency trick. In a batch of 256 sentence pairs, each pair's positive document is a negative for every other query in the batch. You get 255 negatives "for free" without any extra computation. This is why larger batch sizes often lead to better embedding models — more negatives means a harder training signal.

Hard negative mining pushes further. Random negatives are too easy — the model learns to distinguish "reset my password" from "the weather in Tokyo" without breaking a sweat. Hard negatives are documents that are superficially similar but actually irrelevant — like "password requirements for new accounts" when the query is "reset my password." Mining these from your bi-encoder's own top-k results produces the most informative training signal.

Domain-Specific Fine-Tuning

Off-the-shelf models are trained on general web text. If your domain uses specialized vocabulary — medical records, legal contracts, semiconductor datasheets — the general model's embeddings may not separate your concepts well. Fine-tuning adapts the model to your domain.

from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer, InputExample, losses
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader

# Load a pretrained model
model = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")

# Your domain-specific training pairs
# (query, positive_document, negative_document)
train_examples = [
    InputExample(texts=[
        "patient presents with acute MI",
        "myocardial infarction treatment protocol",
        "automobile insurance claim form"
    ]),
    InputExample(texts=[
        "elevated troponin levels",
        "cardiac biomarker interpretation guide",
        "protein structure visualization"
    ]),
    # ... hundreds or thousands more pairs
]

train_dataloader = DataLoader(train_examples, shuffle=True, batch_size=32)

# Triplet loss: anchor, positive, negative
train_loss = losses.TripletLoss(model=model)

model.fit(
    train_objectives=[(train_dataloader, train_loss)],
    epochs=3,
    warmup_steps=100,
    output_path="./medical-embeddings"
)

When should you fine-tune versus use off-the-shelf? The honest answer: try off-the-shelf first. Modern models like BGE and E5 are remarkably robust across domains. Fine-tune only if you measure a meaningful quality gap on your evaluation set. You need at least a few hundred high-quality pairs, and ideally a few thousand. If you have fewer than 100 pairs, fine-tuning will almost certainly overfit and make things worse.

EVALUATION TIP

Always evaluate on a held-out test set of query-document pairs from your actual domain. The standard metrics are Recall@k (what fraction of relevant docs appear in the top-k results?) and NDCG@10 (are the most relevant docs ranked highest?). Never evaluate only on the training data — you'll get misleadingly optimistic numbers.

Embedding Pitfalls

Embeddings look like magic when they work. When they fail, the failures are subtle and hard to debug. Here are the landmines.

Dimensionality tradeoffs are real. Higher dimensions capture more nuance but cost more to store and search. A 3072-dim embedding takes 8x the memory of a 384-dim one. For most tasks, the quality difference between 384 and 1024 dimensions is modest — maybe 2-3% on benchmarks. Going from 1024 to 3072 gives even less. Don't default to the biggest model. Profile your actual recall on your actual data.

Domain mismatch is the most common failure mode. A model trained on web search queries won't understand that in your medical corpus, "positive" means a bad test result, not a good sentiment. Code embeddings are another minefield — natural language models treat variable names as random strings, not meaningful identifiers. If your retrieval quality is poor, domain mismatch is the first thing to investigate.

Embedding drift is a production concern that catches teams off guard. You build your index with model version A. Six months later, you upgrade to model version B. The new model produces different vectors that aren't compatible with the old ones. Your search quality silently degrades — or crashes entirely if the dimensionality changed. Every model upgrade requires re-embedding your entire corpus, which for large datasets can take days and significant compute.

CRITICAL PITFALL

Never mix embeddings from different models in the same index. Even two versions of the same model can produce incompatible vectors. If you update your embedding model, you must re-embed every document and rebuild the entire index. Plan for this in your infrastructure from day one — store the raw text alongside the vectors so you can always regenerate.

Another subtle issue: input length truncation. Most embedding models have a maximum token limit (typically 256–512 tokens). If your documents are longer — and many are — the model silently truncates them. The embedding only represents the first few paragraphs. For long documents, consider chunking strategies: split documents into overlapping passages of 200–300 tokens, embed each chunk separately, and retrieve at the chunk level. This is a core technique in RAG pipelines that we'll explore further in the next sections.

Embeddings aren't perfect. They collapse nuance. They lose details. A 384-dimensional vector can't capture everything about a 2,000-word document. But they're the best tool we have for turning the fuzzy concept of "meaning" into something a computer can work with at scale. Master them, and you'll understand the backbone of every modern search and retrieval system.
