Transformer Training Fundamentals

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 8 subtopics
TL;DR

Training a transformer is more craft than science. You need AdamW (not Adam) for weight decay that actually works, a learning rate schedule with warmup to avoid early instability, mixed precision to fit models in memory, and distributed strategies to spread work across GPUs. Embedding scaling by √d_model keeps gradients healthy. Padding masks prevent the model from attending to garbage. Gradient checkpointing trades compute for memory. And the data you feed matters as much as the architecture — deduplication, quality filtering, and careful data mixing are non-negotiable. Get any one of these wrong and your multi-week training run diverges at 3 AM.

Architecture is the easy part. Seriously. You can implement a transformer from scratch in an afternoon. But training one — getting it to actually converge to something useful without wasting thousands of GPU-hours — that's where the real engineering lives. This section is about everything that happens between "I have a model definition" and "I have a working model." These are the tricks, schedules, and infrastructure decisions that separate a toy experiment from a production training run.

Data Preparation

Deduplication and Quality Filtering

Garbage in, garbage out. Before you touch an optimizer, you need to worry about what you're feeding the model. Large-scale web scrapes contain enormous amounts of duplicated text — boilerplate headers, copy-pasted articles, template pages. If you don't deduplicate, your model memorizes these repeated passages and wastes capacity. Deduplication typically operates at two levels: exact dedup (hashing documents and removing identical ones) and fuzzy dedup (using MinHash or SimHash to catch near-duplicates that differ by a few words).

Quality filtering removes documents that are too short, too repetitive, in the wrong language, or full of HTML artifacts and boilerplate. Common heuristics include perplexity filtering (a language model scores each document; gibberish gets high perplexity and is dropped), classifier-based filtering (train a small model to distinguish "high quality" from "low quality" text), and rule-based filters (minimum word count, maximum symbol ratio, language ID). The Llama and Falcon papers both describe elaborate multi-stage filtering pipelines. It matters more than most people think.

Data Mix and Curriculum

Real training data is a blend: web text, books, code, scientific papers, conversation data. The data mix — the ratio of each source — shapes the model's personality. More code makes it better at reasoning. More books improve coherence. More conversation data makes it chattier. Finding the right mix is largely empirical, but a common starting point is roughly 60-70% web, 10-15% code, 5-10% books, and the rest from curated sources.

Curriculum training takes this further by changing the data mix over the course of training. You might start with broad, noisy web data to learn general language patterns, then shift toward higher-quality, domain-specific data later. Think of it like school — general education first, specialization later. Some teams also upweight harder examples as training progresses, though the evidence on whether this consistently helps is mixed.

AdamW Optimizer

Why Not Plain SGD?

Transformers are notoriously finicky to optimize. Plain SGD barely works. You need adaptive learning rates — different parameters need different step sizes because the loss landscape is wildly non-uniform. Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) maintains per-parameter running averages of gradients (first moment) and squared gradients (second moment), giving each weight its own effective learning rate. It converges much faster than SGD on transformer-shaped problems.

Decoupled Weight Decay

But there's a subtle bug in vanilla Adam. When you add L2 regularization (weight decay) to Adam, the adaptive scaling interacts with the regularization term in a way that effectively reduces the decay for parameters with large gradients. The fix is AdamW, which decouples weight decay from the gradient update. Instead of adding the weight decay to the loss (and then letting Adam scale it), AdamW applies the decay directly to the weights after the Adam step. The difference looks small in code but has a measurable impact on generalization.

# Pseudocode: Adam vs AdamW difference
# Adam with L2: gradient += weight_decay * param
# AdamW:        param -= weight_decay * lr * param  (separate step)

# PyTorch makes this easy
optimizer = torch.optim.AdamW(
    model.parameters(),
    lr=3e-4,
    betas=(0.9, 0.95),
    weight_decay=0.1
)

The typical hyperparameters for LLM training: β₁ = 0.9, β₂ = 0.95 (slightly lower than Adam's default 0.999 — helps with training stability), weight decay of 0.1, and an initial learning rate that depends on model size but often lands around 3e-4 for medium models.

Learning Rate Schedules

Warmup Phase

Here's a counterintuitive fact: if you start training with your target learning rate, the model often diverges immediately. Why? Early in training, the Adam second-moment estimates are initialized to zero. With no history, the adaptive scaling is unreliable, and a large learning rate causes wild parameter updates. Warmup fixes this by starting with a tiny learning rate and linearly increasing it over the first few thousand steps. This gives Adam's running statistics time to stabilize before you let the optimizer take big steps.

Decay Strategies

After warmup, you need to decrease the learning rate. Several strategies work. Inverse square root decay (used in the original "Attention Is All You Need" paper) decays the rate as 1/√step. It's simple and effective. Cosine annealing smoothly decreases the rate following a cosine curve down to some minimum value — this is the most popular choice for LLM pre-training today. The WSD schedule (Warmup-Stable-Decay) holds the learning rate constant after warmup for most of training, then decays sharply at the end. WSD is gaining popularity because the stable phase makes it easy to extend training if the model hasn't converged.

# Cosine annealing with warmup (simplified)
def get_lr(step, warmup_steps, total_steps, max_lr, min_lr):
    if step < warmup_steps:
        return max_lr * step / warmup_steps
    progress = (step - warmup_steps) / (total_steps - warmup_steps)
    return min_lr + 0.5 * (max_lr - min_lr) * (1 + math.cos(math.pi * progress))

Batch Size Scaling

An underappreciated trick: you can ramp up batch size during training instead of (or alongside) decaying the learning rate. Small batches early give noisy gradients that help explore the loss landscape. Large batches later give precise gradients for fine convergence. Some teams start at batch size 256 and scale up to 2048+ over the first 10-20% of training. The linear scaling rule says: if you double the batch size, double the learning rate to compensate. In practice, square root scaling (lr ∝ √batch_size) works better for large-scale runs.

Embedding Scaling and Padding Masks

The √d_model Trick

Look at any transformer implementation and you'll find this line: embeddings are multiplied by √d_model before adding positional encodings. Why? Embedding scaling is a variance control trick. Embedding vectors are initialized with small random values (roughly unit variance). Positional encodings, especially sinusoidal ones, also have values in [-1, 1]. If the embedding dimension is large (say 4096), raw embeddings are tiny relative to positional encodings — the model can't distinguish between tokens. Multiplying by √d_model scales the token embeddings up so they're in the same ballpark as the positional signals.

# In the embedding layer
x = self.token_embedding(input_ids) * math.sqrt(self.d_model)
x = x + self.positional_encoding(positions)

Padding Masks

Batched sequences rarely have the same length. Shorter sequences get padded with a special token to fill the batch tensor. But you don't want the model attending to these pad tokens — they carry no information and would corrupt the attention weights. A padding mask is a binary tensor that marks real tokens as 1 and padding tokens as 0. Before softmax in the attention layer, you set the attention scores for padded positions to -inf, which drives their softmax probability to zero. Without this, the model learns to "look at" padding, wasting capacity and degrading quality.

Training Instabilities

Loss Spikes

You're watching your loss curve at 2 AM. It's been smoothly decreasing for days. Then suddenly — spike. The loss jumps 10x and may or may not recover. Loss spikes are the bane of large-scale training. They can be caused by bad data batches (a batch of garbage text), numerical overflow in attention logits, or the optimizer hitting a sharp region of the loss landscape. Some teams simply skip the bad batch and reload from a checkpoint a few steps earlier. Others log the offending batch and investigate post-hoc.

Gradient Clipping

Gradient clipping is your primary defense against instability. Before each optimizer step, you compute the global gradient norm (the L2 norm of all gradients concatenated) and, if it exceeds a threshold, you scale all gradients down proportionally. A typical max norm is 1.0. This prevents any single bad batch from causing a catastrophically large update. It doesn't fix the root cause — but it keeps the model alive while you figure out what went wrong.

# Standard gradient clipping in PyTorch
torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)
optimizer.step()

Numerical Stability

Attention scores are dot products that can grow with the sequence length and model dimension. Without the 1/√d_k scaling, these scores become enormous, pushing softmax into saturation where gradients vanish. Even with scaling, FP16 training can overflow on long sequences. Solutions include computing attention in FP32 even when the rest of the model is in FP16, using log-sum-exp tricks for numerically stable softmax, and monitoring for NaN/Inf values in activations.

Loss Curves and Checkpointing

Reading Loss Curves

A healthy loss curve drops steeply in the first few percent of training (the model is learning basic patterns like word frequencies), then gradually levels off into a slow, steady decrease. If the curve plateaus early, your learning rate might be too low or your data might be too easy. If it's noisy and jagged, your batch size might be too small. If it diverges (shoots up and never comes back), something fundamental is broken — learning rate too high, a bug in masking, or corrupted data.

Track validation loss alongside training loss. If training loss keeps dropping but validation loss stops improving, you're overfitting — rare in pre-training (you're usually data-limited, not capacity-limited) but common in fine-tuning. Also track gradient norms over time. A sudden spike in gradient norm often precedes a loss spike by a few steps — giving you early warning.

Checkpointing Strategies

Checkpointing means saving the model weights, optimizer state, learning rate scheduler state, and random number generator states to disk at regular intervals. If your training crashes (hardware failure, NaN divergence, preemption on a shared cluster), you resume from the last checkpoint instead of starting over. For a multi-week training run, losing even a day of compute is painful.

How often? It depends on how much compute you can afford to lose. Every 1000 steps is common. Some teams checkpoint every 500 steps but only keep the last 3-5 checkpoints to save disk space (a single checkpoint for a 70B model is ~140 GB in FP16). Asynchronous checkpointing — writing to disk in a background thread while training continues — avoids stalling the GPU pipeline.

Mixed Precision Training

FP16 and BF16

Full FP32 training is wasteful. Half-precision formats use 16 bits instead of 32, halving memory and doubling throughput on modern GPUs. FP16 has high precision but a narrow dynamic range — values outside roughly [6e-8, 65504] become zero or infinity. BF16 (bfloat16) sacrifices precision for range: it matches FP32's exponent bits, so it can represent the same range of magnitudes but with less decimal accuracy. For transformers, BF16 is almost always preferred because gradient magnitudes vary wildly and you need that range more than you need decimal precision.

Loss Scaling and AMP

With FP16, tiny gradients (common in early layers of deep networks) underflow to zero. Loss scaling fixes this by multiplying the loss by a large factor before backprop (so gradients are larger and don't underflow), then dividing the gradients by the same factor before the optimizer step. Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) handles this for you — it chooses which operations run in FP16 vs FP32 (matrix multiplications in FP16, reductions and softmax in FP32) and manages loss scaling dynamically.

# PyTorch AMP training loop
scaler = torch.amp.GradScaler()
for batch in dataloader:
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    with torch.amp.autocast(device_type='cuda', dtype=torch.bfloat16):
        loss = model(batch)
    scaler.scale(loss).backward()
    scaler.unscale_(optimizer)
    torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), 1.0)
    scaler.step(optimizer)
    scaler.update()
FP8 on the Horizon

FP8 (8-bit floating point) is emerging in newest hardware like NVIDIA H100 and H200 GPUs. It promises another 2x speedup for matrix multiplications. Early results show it works for forward passes and most of backward, but accumulation steps still need higher precision. Libraries like Transformer Engine handle the format conversion automatically. Expect FP8 to become standard for large-scale training within the next hardware generation.

Activation and Gradient Checkpointing

The Memory-Compute Tradeoff

During backpropagation, you need the activations (intermediate outputs) from the forward pass to compute gradients. Normally, all activations are kept in memory. For a large transformer, this is enormous — a 7B parameter model can easily consume 60+ GB just in activations for a modest batch size. Activation checkpointing (also called gradient checkpointing) saves memory by not storing intermediate activations. Instead, during the backward pass, it recomputes them on-the-fly by re-running portions of the forward pass. You trade ~33% more compute for ~60-70% less activation memory.

Selective Checkpointing and Offloading

Selective checkpointing is smarter than all-or-nothing. You checkpoint only the activations that are expensive to store but cheap to recompute (like the output of each transformer block, but not the intermediate attention matrices). This gives you most of the memory savings with minimal recomputation cost.

Offloading goes further: move activations (or even optimizer states) to CPU RAM or NVMe storage, then stream them back to GPU when needed during the backward pass. This is slower than GPU memory but far cheaper, and with PCIe 5.0 or NVLink the bandwidth penalty is manageable for very large models. DeepSpeed ZeRO-Offload pioneered this technique.

Distributed Training for LLMs

Data Parallelism

The simplest distributed strategy: put a copy of the full model on every GPU, split the data across GPUs, and average the gradients after each step. Data parallelism (DP) scales the effective batch size linearly with the number of GPUs. The catch is that every GPU must hold the entire model — weights, gradients, and optimizer states. For a 7B model in FP32 with AdamW, that's about 7B × (4 + 4 + 8) = 112 GB per GPU. A single A100 has 80 GB. You need a smarter approach.

FSDP and ZeRO

Fully Sharded Data Parallelism (FSDP) — PyTorch's implementation of the ZeRO (Zero Redundancy Optimizer) idea — shards the model states across GPUs. Instead of every GPU holding everything, each GPU holds a slice of the weights, gradients, and optimizer states. When a layer needs its full parameters for a forward or backward computation, the shards are gathered temporarily via all-gather, used, and then discarded. This dramatically reduces per-GPU memory at the cost of communication overhead.

Gradient Accumulation and Micro-Batching

What if your target batch size doesn't fit on your GPUs even after sharding? Gradient accumulation lets you simulate a large batch by processing multiple smaller micro-batches sequentially and summing the gradients before a single optimizer step. If your micro-batch size is 4 and you accumulate over 8 steps, your effective batch size is 32 — without needing 8x the memory. This is essential for training on consumer hardware or when memory is tight.

# Gradient accumulation
accumulation_steps = 8
for i, batch in enumerate(dataloader):
    loss = model(batch) / accumulation_steps
    loss.backward()
    if (i + 1) % accumulation_steps == 0:
        torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), 1.0)
        optimizer.step()
        optimizer.zero_grad()

3D Parallelism

For the largest models (70B+), you need all three: data parallelism across groups of GPUs, tensor parallelism (splitting individual matrix multiplications across GPUs within a node), and pipeline parallelism (splitting layers across GPUs, with micro-batches flowing through the pipeline stages). This combination — 3D parallelism, pioneered by Megatron-LM — is how models like GPT-3, PaLM, and Llama-2 70B were trained. Getting the balance right between these three dimensions is an art; too much pipeline parallelism creates bubbles (idle time), too much tensor parallelism burns bandwidth on all-reduce.

⚠️ Communication Is the Bottleneck

In distributed training, GPU compute is rarely the limiting factor — communication is. All-reduce operations for gradient synchronization can dominate wall-clock time at scale. Techniques like gradient compression, overlapping communication with computation, and hierarchical all-reduce (reduce within a node first over NVLink, then across nodes over InfiniBand) are critical for maintaining hardware utilization above 40-50%. Below that, you're lighting money on fire.

Multi-Epoch vs Single-Epoch Training

The Data Wall

Large language models are data-hungry. GPT-3 trained on 300B tokens. Llama-2 used 2T tokens. At some point, you run out of fresh data. Do you loop (train multiple epochs)? Empirically, repeated data hurts — models start memorizing rather than generalizing, and benchmark performance degrades after ~4 epochs on the same data. The current consensus is that single-epoch training is ideal, and if you need more tokens, invest in finding or generating more data rather than recycling what you have. Data augmentation and synthetic data generation (using an existing model to create training data) are active areas of research to push this boundary.

Putting It All Together: A Training Recipe

Here's what a real pre-training recipe looks like. Prepare your data: deduplicate, filter for quality, tokenize, shuffle, and pack sequences to minimize padding. Initialize the model with small random weights. Set AdamW with β₁=0.9, β₂=0.95, weight decay 0.1. Use cosine annealing with 2000 steps of warmup. Start with a smaller batch size and ramp up over the first 5-10% of training. Train in BF16 with gradient clipping at 1.0. Checkpoint every 1000 steps. Use FSDP or 3D parallelism depending on your model size and cluster topology. Monitor loss curves, gradient norms, and learning rate in your experiment tracker. And pray the cluster stays up.

🔥 Hard-Won Lesson

Always validate your data pipeline end-to-end before launching a long training run. Train a tiny model (125M parameters) on your actual data for a few thousand steps. Check that loss decreases, that generated text looks reasonable, and that your checkpointing and resumption logic works. A bug in padding mask construction or tokenizer configuration will waste days of GPU-hours on a large run. Twenty minutes of validation saves twenty days of debugging.
