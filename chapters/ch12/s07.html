Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 7 subtopics
TL;DR

LLMs hallucinate, go stale, and can't see your private data. Retrieval-Augmented Generation fixes this by fetching relevant documents at query time and stuffing them into the prompt. You chunk your documents, embed them into vectors, store them in a vector database, retrieve the top-k most relevant chunks for a user query, optionally rerank them, and feed them to the LLM as context. The quality of your RAG pipeline depends far more on retrieval quality than on the LLM itself. Chunking strategy, embedding model choice, hybrid search, and reranking are where most of the engineering effort goes. Evaluate with metrics like faithfulness and context relevance — not just vibes.

Why RAG?

Here's the uncomfortable truth about large language models: they make things up. Confidently. Fluently. With perfect grammar. Ask GPT about your company's internal vacation policy and it will generate something that sounds plausible but is entirely fabricated. This is hallucination, and it's not a bug that will be fixed in the next model release — it's a fundamental property of how these models work. They are next-token prediction machines trained on static snapshots of the internet. They have no mechanism to say "I don't know."

Three problems compound this. First, the knowledge cutoff: an LLM trained in January 2024 knows nothing about events in March 2024. The world moves. The model doesn't. Second, private data: your company's documents, Confluence pages, Slack messages, and internal databases were never in the training set. The model literally cannot know about them. Third, even for facts it did see during training, the model sometimes confuses, conflates, or invents details. It's a fuzzy compression of the internet, not a database.

You could fine-tune the model on your private data, but that's expensive, slow, and needs to be repeated every time your data changes. You could expand the context window to a million tokens and dump everything in, but that's wasteful, slow, and the model's attention degrades over very long contexts (the "lost in the middle" problem). The most practical solution — the one that 90% of production LLM applications use today — is Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG).

The core idea is almost embarrassingly simple. When a user asks a question, don't just send the question to the LLM. First, search your knowledge base for the most relevant documents. Then paste those documents into the prompt alongside the question. The LLM now has the actual facts right there in its context window and can ground its answer in real information. It's like the difference between asking someone a trivia question from memory versus letting them look it up in a textbook first. RAG gives the LLM an open-book exam.

RAG Architecture

Every RAG system has two core components: a retriever that finds relevant documents and a generator (the LLM) that produces an answer conditioned on those documents. The flow looks like this: the user submits a query. The retriever converts that query into a representation (an embedding vector, a keyword query, or both), searches an index of pre-processed documents, and returns the top-k most relevant chunks. Those chunks are then formatted into a prompt — typically something like "Answer the following question based on the provided context" — and sent to the LLM along with the original question. The LLM generates an answer grounded in the retrieved context.

Think of it as a two-stage pipeline. Stage one: find the right information. Stage two: synthesize an answer from it. The crucial insight is that these stages are largely independent. You can swap your retriever from BM25 to a neural embedding model without touching the generator. You can swap your generator from GPT-4 to Claude to LLaMA without changing retrieval. This modularity is why RAG is so popular in production — it's composable and each piece can be improved independently.

The connection between the two stages is the context window. You take the retrieved chunks and literally concatenate them into the LLM's input prompt. This is sometimes called context stuffing. The art is in deciding how many chunks to include (too few and you miss relevant info, too many and you dilute the signal with noise), how to order them (models pay more attention to the beginning and end of the context), and how to instruct the model to use them (your system prompt matters enormously).

The Retrieval Bottleneck

Here's a counterintuitive fact: in most RAG systems, the LLM is not the bottleneck — retrieval is. If you retrieve the wrong documents, even GPT-4 can't save you. It will either hallucinate or say "the context doesn't contain this information." Invest 80% of your RAG optimization effort into improving retrieval quality. The generator is usually good enough out of the box.

Document Processing & Chunking Strategies

Before you can retrieve anything, you need to break your documents into pieces. Why? Because documents are long and context windows are finite. A 50-page PDF won't fit in a prompt, and even if it did, you'd be wasting tokens on irrelevant sections. Chunking is the process of splitting documents into smaller, semantically meaningful pieces that can be independently indexed and retrieved.

The simplest approach is fixed-size chunking: split the text every N characters (or tokens). Set N to 500 tokens, and every chunk is exactly 500 tokens long. It's fast and predictable, but it's dumb — it will happily split a sentence in half, or cut a code block right through a function definition. The information at chunk boundaries gets mangled.

Recursive character splitting is smarter. It tries to split on paragraph boundaries first. If a paragraph is too long, it falls back to sentence boundaries. If a sentence is still too long, it splits on word boundaries. This preserves semantic coherence at each level. LangChain's RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter is the most commonly used implementation of this idea, and for good reason — it works well with minimal tuning.

Sentence-based splitting treats each sentence as an atomic unit and groups consecutive sentences into chunks until you hit a size limit. This guarantees you never break a sentence, which matters a lot for embedding quality — a half-sentence produces a meaningless vector. Semantic chunking goes further: it computes embeddings for each sentence, then groups consecutive sentences that are semantically similar. When the embedding similarity between adjacent sentences drops below a threshold, it starts a new chunk. This produces chunks that are naturally coherent — each chunk is about one topic.

One more critical detail: chunk overlap. If chunk 1 ends at "The patient was prescribed" and chunk 2 starts with "metformin 500mg twice daily," neither chunk alone captures the full fact. Overlap fixes this. You set an overlap of, say, 50–200 tokens, so the end of each chunk repeats at the beginning of the next. This redundancy ensures that facts spanning a chunk boundary appear in at least one complete chunk. The tradeoff is more storage and slightly more retrieval noise, but it's almost always worth it.

from langchain.text_splitter import (
    RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter,
    CharacterTextSplitter,
    SentenceTransformersTokenTextSplitter,
)

document = open("company_handbook.md").read()

# 1. Fixed-size: fast but dumb
fixed_splitter = CharacterTextSplitter(
    chunk_size=500,
    chunk_overlap=50,
    separator=""  # split anywhere
)
fixed_chunks = fixed_splitter.split_text(document)

# 2. Recursive: smart boundary detection (recommended default)
recursive_splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(
    chunk_size=500,
    chunk_overlap=100,
    separators=["\n\n", "\n", ". ", " ", ""]  # try in order
)
recursive_chunks = recursive_splitter.split_text(document)

# 3. Semantic chunking: group by meaning
from langchain_experimental.text_splitter import SemanticChunker
from langchain_openai import OpenAIEmbeddings

semantic_splitter = SemanticChunker(
    OpenAIEmbeddings(),
    breakpoint_threshold_type="percentile",
    breakpoint_threshold_amount=75
)
semantic_chunks = semantic_splitter.split_text(document)

print(f"Fixed: {len(fixed_chunks)} chunks")
print(f"Recursive: {len(recursive_chunks)} chunks")
print(f"Semantic: {len(semantic_chunks)} chunks")

In practice, start with recursive character splitting at 500–1000 tokens with 100–200 token overlap. That's a sensible default. Only move to semantic chunking if your retrieval quality is suffering and you've confirmed that chunk quality is the bottleneck. Semantic chunking requires an embedding model call for every sentence, which adds latency and cost during indexing.

Retrieval: Dense, Sparse, and Hybrid

You've chunked your documents. Now you need to find the right chunks when a user asks a question. There are two fundamentally different approaches, and the best systems use both.

Sparse retrieval is the classic approach. Think keyword search. The workhorse algorithm is BM25, a descendant of TF-IDF that has been the backbone of search engines for decades. BM25 scores documents based on exact term overlap with the query, weighted by how rare each matching term is (a match on "mitochondria" is more informative than a match on "the"). It's fast, interpretable, and requires no model — just an inverted index. It excels when the user's query uses the exact same words as the document. If the user asks "What is the PTO policy?" and the document says "PTO policy," BM25 nails it.

But sparse retrieval fails on semantic similarity. If the user asks "How many vacation days do I get?" and the document talks about "PTO policy" or "annual leave entitlement," BM25 sees low word overlap and misses the match. This is the vocabulary mismatch problem, and it's the reason dense retrieval was invented.

Dense retrieval uses embedding models to convert both queries and documents into high-dimensional vectors that capture meaning, not just words. A query about "vacation days" and a chunk about "annual leave" will have similar embedding vectors because they mean the same thing. You compute cosine similarity (or dot product) between the query vector and all stored chunk vectors, and return the chunks with the highest similarity. Models like sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2, OpenAI's text-embedding-3-small, and Cohere's embed-v3 are popular choices.

Dense retrieval handles synonyms, paraphrases, and conceptual similarity beautifully. But it has its own weakness: it can miss exact matches that BM25 would catch trivially. If someone searches for a specific error code like "ERR_SSL_PROTOCOL_ERROR," a dense model might return chunks about SSL errors in general rather than the exact error code. The embedding captures the vibe, not the exact string.

The solution is hybrid search: run both BM25 and dense retrieval, then combine the results. The most common fusion strategy is Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF), which merges the two ranked lists by giving each result a score based on its rank in each list. A chunk ranked #1 in both lists gets the highest combined score. A chunk ranked #1 in one but absent from the other still makes the final list, just with a lower score. This gets you the best of both worlds — exact keyword matching and semantic understanding.

from langchain_community.retrievers import BM25Retriever
from langchain_openai import OpenAIEmbeddings
from langchain_community.vectorstores import FAISS
from langchain.retrievers import EnsembleRetriever

# Sparse retriever (BM25)
bm25_retriever = BM25Retriever.from_texts(
    chunks,
    metadatas=[{"source": f"doc_{i}"} for i in range(len(chunks))]
)
bm25_retriever.k = 5  # top 5 from BM25

# Dense retriever (FAISS + embeddings)
embeddings = OpenAIEmbeddings(model="text-embedding-3-small")
vectorstore = FAISS.from_texts(chunks, embeddings)
dense_retriever = vectorstore.as_retriever(search_kwargs={"k": 5})

# Hybrid: combine with Reciprocal Rank Fusion
hybrid_retriever = EnsembleRetriever(
    retrievers=[bm25_retriever, dense_retriever],
    weights=[0.4, 0.6]  # slightly favor dense
)

results = hybrid_retriever.invoke("How many vacation days do new employees get?")
for doc in results:
    print(doc.page_content[:100])
Embedding Model Choice Matters More Than You Think

The MTEB leaderboard ranks embedding models on retrieval benchmarks. The gap between the best and worst models is enormous — 15–20% in recall. Don't default to the cheapest model. Test at least two or three on a sample of your actual queries. A better embedding model often gives more lift than any amount of prompt engineering on the generator side.

Vector Stores

Once you've computed embeddings for all your chunks, you need somewhere to store them and query them efficiently. A vector store (or vector database) is a database optimized for similarity search over high-dimensional vectors. You insert vectors, and at query time you ask "give me the k nearest neighbors to this query vector." The challenge is speed: brute-force comparison against millions of vectors is too slow, so vector stores use approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) algorithms to trade a tiny bit of accuracy for massive speedups.

The two most common ANN algorithms are HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) and IVF (Inverted File Index). HNSW builds a multi-layer graph where each vector is connected to its neighbors. Query time walks the graph from a random entry point, greedily moving to closer neighbors. It's fast and accurate but uses a lot of memory (the graph is stored in RAM). IVF partitions the vector space into clusters, then at query time only searches the clusters closest to the query vector. It uses less memory but requires a training step to build the clusters, and accuracy depends heavily on the number of clusters probed at query time.

The landscape of vector store options is crowded. Here's an honest comparison:

Vector Store Type Best For Indexing Scale Cost
FAISS Library (local) Prototyping, offline batch IVF, HNSW, PQ Millions Free
Chroma Local / embedded Quick prototyping, small apps HNSW Hundreds of thousands Free
Pinecone Managed cloud Production, zero-ops teams Proprietary Billions $$
Weaviate Self-hosted / cloud Hybrid search, multimodal HNSW Billions $ (self) / $$ (cloud)
Qdrant Self-hosted / cloud Filtering + vector search HNSW Billions $ (self) / $$ (cloud)
Milvus Self-hosted / cloud Massive scale, GPU search IVF, HNSW, DiskANN Billions+ $ (self) / $$ (cloud)

For prototyping and small applications (under a million chunks), FAISS or Chroma are the right choice. They run locally, have no infrastructure overhead, and integrate with LangChain in a few lines. For production at scale, Pinecone is the easiest managed option — you don't manage servers, indexes, or replication. Weaviate and Qdrant offer more control and are good choices if you want to self-host for data privacy reasons. Milvus is the heavy artillery — it supports GPU-accelerated search and can handle billions of vectors, but it's complex to operate.

# FAISS: local, fast, great for prototyping
import faiss
import numpy as np
from langchain_openai import OpenAIEmbeddings
from langchain_community.vectorstores import FAISS as LangFAISS

embeddings = OpenAIEmbeddings(model="text-embedding-3-small")

# Create from chunks
vectorstore = LangFAISS.from_texts(
    texts=chunks,
    embedding=embeddings,
    metadatas=[{"source": f"doc_{i}", "page": i // 10} for i in range(len(chunks))]
)

# Save to disk and reload (persistence!)
vectorstore.save_local("faiss_index")
loaded_store = LangFAISS.load_local("faiss_index", embeddings)

# Chroma: embedded database with filtering
from langchain_community.vectorstores import Chroma

chroma_store = Chroma.from_texts(
    texts=chunks,
    embedding=embeddings,
    metadatas=[{"source": f"doc_{i}", "department": "engineering"} for i in range(len(chunks))],
    persist_directory="./chroma_db"
)

# Retrieval with metadata filtering
results = chroma_store.similarity_search(
    "deployment checklist",
    k=5,
    filter={"department": "engineering"}  # only search engineering docs
)

Reranking

Initial retrieval (whether sparse, dense, or hybrid) returns a rough set of candidates. The problem is that embedding similarity is a blunt instrument. A chunk might be topically related but not actually answer the question. Or two chunks might score similarly despite one being far more relevant. Reranking is a second-stage scoring step that re-orders the retrieved chunks using a more powerful (and more expensive) model.

The key difference is in how the scoring works. Embedding-based retrieval computes query and document vectors independently — the query encoder never "sees" the document text, and vice versa. This is called a bi-encoder architecture. It's efficient (you precompute document embeddings once) but limited. A cross-encoder reranker feeds the query and each candidate document together into a single transformer model, which can attend across both texts simultaneously. This joint attention lets the model understand fine-grained relevance — whether a specific sentence in the document actually answers the specific question — at the cost of being much slower (you can't precompute anything).

In practice, you retrieve 20–50 candidates with the fast bi-encoder, then rerank them with the slow cross-encoder, and keep the top 3–5. This two-stage approach gives you the speed of embedding search with the accuracy of cross-attention. Popular reranker options include Cohere Rerank (managed API, very accurate), cross-encoder/ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2 (open-source, runs locally), and ColBERT (a hybrid approach that precomputes token-level embeddings for late interaction, giving cross-encoder-like quality at bi-encoder-like speed).

from langchain.retrievers import ContextualCompressionRetriever
from langchain_cohere import CohereRerank
from langchain_openai import ChatOpenAI

# Step 1: Initial retrieval (get 20 candidates)
base_retriever = vectorstore.as_retriever(search_kwargs={"k": 20})

# Step 2: Rerank down to top 5
reranker = CohereRerank(
    model="rerank-english-v3.0",
    top_n=5  # keep only top 5 after reranking
)

# Wrap into a compression retriever
reranking_retriever = ContextualCompressionRetriever(
    base_compressor=reranker,
    base_retriever=base_retriever
)

# Now retrieval automatically reranks
results = reranking_retriever.invoke("What's the process for requesting PTO?")
for i, doc in enumerate(results):
    print(f"Rank {i+1}: {doc.page_content[:100]}...")

Reranking is one of the highest-leverage improvements you can make to a RAG pipeline. In benchmarks, adding a cross-encoder reranker typically improves answer quality by 10–20% compared to raw embedding retrieval. The latency cost is modest — reranking 20 short chunks takes 50–200ms with a local model. If you're building a production RAG system and you're not reranking, you're leaving quality on the table.

Complete RAG Pipeline

Let's put everything together into a full end-to-end pipeline: load documents, chunk them, embed and store, retrieve with hybrid search, rerank, and generate an answer. This is the code you'd actually write for a production-grade RAG system.

from langchain_community.document_loaders import DirectoryLoader, TextLoader
from langchain.text_splitter import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter
from langchain_openai import OpenAIEmbeddings, ChatOpenAI
from langchain_community.vectorstores import FAISS
from langchain_community.retrievers import BM25Retriever
from langchain.retrievers import EnsembleRetriever, ContextualCompressionRetriever
from langchain_cohere import CohereRerank
from langchain_core.prompts import ChatPromptTemplate
from langchain_core.output_parsers import StrOutputParser
from langchain_core.runnables import RunnablePassthrough

# ── 1. Load documents ──────────────────────────────────────
loader = DirectoryLoader(
    "./company_docs/",
    glob="**/*.md",
    loader_cls=TextLoader
)
documents = loader.load()
print(f"Loaded {len(documents)} documents")

# ── 2. Chunk ────────────────────────────────────────────────
splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(
    chunk_size=800,
    chunk_overlap=150,
    separators=["\n\n", "\n", ". ", " ", ""]
)
chunks = splitter.split_documents(documents)
print(f"Created {len(chunks)} chunks")

# ── 3. Embed and store ─────────────────────────────────────
embeddings = OpenAIEmbeddings(model="text-embedding-3-small")
vectorstore = FAISS.from_documents(chunks, embeddings)
vectorstore.save_local("rag_index")

# ── 4. Build hybrid retriever ──────────────────────────────
dense_retriever = vectorstore.as_retriever(search_kwargs={"k": 20})
bm25_retriever = BM25Retriever.from_documents(chunks)
bm25_retriever.k = 20

hybrid_retriever = EnsembleRetriever(
    retrievers=[bm25_retriever, dense_retriever],
    weights=[0.4, 0.6]
)

# ── 5. Add reranking ───────────────────────────────────────
reranker = CohereRerank(model="rerank-english-v3.0", top_n=5)
final_retriever = ContextualCompressionRetriever(
    base_compressor=reranker,
    base_retriever=hybrid_retriever
)

# ── 6. Build generation chain ──────────────────────────────
llm = ChatOpenAI(model="gpt-4o", temperature=0)

prompt = ChatPromptTemplate.from_template("""
Answer the question based ONLY on the following context.
If the context doesn't contain the answer, say "I don't have enough information to answer this."

Context:
{context}

Question: {question}

Answer:""")

def format_docs(docs):
    return "\n\n---\n\n".join(doc.page_content for doc in docs)

rag_chain = (
    {"context": final_retriever | format_docs, "question": RunnablePassthrough()}
    | prompt
    | llm
    | StrOutputParser()
)

# ── 7. Query ────────────────────────────────────────────────
answer = rag_chain.invoke("What is the process for requesting time off?")
print(answer)

Notice how each step is modular. You can swap FAISS for Chroma by changing one line. You can swap Cohere Rerank for a local cross-encoder. You can replace GPT-4o with any other LLM. This composability is the whole point — RAG pipelines are built from interchangeable components, and improving any single component improves the whole system.

RAG Evaluation

You've built a RAG pipeline. How do you know it's working well? "The answers look good" is not a measurement. You need systematic evaluation, and this is where most teams drop the ball. RAG evaluation is harder than traditional ML evaluation because you're evaluating two things at once: did the retriever find the right documents, and did the generator produce a correct answer from those documents? Failures in either stage look the same to the user — a wrong answer — but the fixes are completely different.

The RAGAS framework (Retrieval Augmented Generation Assessment) defines four core metrics that decompose RAG quality into interpretable components. Faithfulness measures whether the generated answer is actually supported by the retrieved context. An answer that's factually correct but not grounded in the provided documents scores low on faithfulness — the model is using its parametric memory instead of the context, which means it might hallucinate on harder questions. Answer relevance measures whether the generated answer actually addresses the question. A faithful but off-topic answer scores low here.

On the retrieval side, context precision measures whether the retrieved chunks are actually relevant to the question (are you retrieving signal or noise?). Context recall measures whether the retrieved chunks contain all the information needed to answer the question completely. Low context precision means your retriever is bringing back junk. Low context recall means it's missing important chunks.

Metric Evaluates Low Score Means Fix
Faithfulness Generator Answer contradicts or goes beyond context Better prompt, lower temperature, stronger model
Answer Relevance Generator Answer doesn't address the question Improve prompt template, check context quality
Context Precision Retriever Retrieved chunks are mostly irrelevant Better embeddings, reranking, metadata filtering
Context Recall Retriever Missing important information from knowledge base Better chunking, more overlap, hybrid search

The diagnostic power comes from combining these metrics. If faithfulness is high but answer relevance is low, your generator is faithfully reporting irrelevant context — fix retrieval. If context recall is high but faithfulness is low, the right information is being retrieved but the generator is ignoring it or distorting it — fix the prompt or switch to a more instruction-following model. If context precision and recall are both low, your retrieval is fundamentally broken — re-examine your chunking, embedding model, or hybrid search configuration.

The "Vibes-Based" Evaluation Trap

Don't evaluate RAG by manually reading 10 answers and saying "looks good." Build an evaluation set of at least 50–100 question-answer pairs covering your most important use cases. Run RAGAS metrics automatically on every pipeline change. Teams that skip this inevitably ship regressions — a chunking change that improves one category of questions while silently breaking another. Evaluation is not optional. It's infrastructure.

Advanced RAG

The basic retrieve-then-generate pipeline works remarkably well, but it has limitations. What if the answer requires reasoning across multiple documents? What if the retrieved context is irrelevant or misleading? What if the question needs to be decomposed into sub-questions first? Advanced RAG techniques address these limitations, and they're where the field is moving rapidly.

Graph RAG combines vector retrieval with knowledge graphs. Instead of treating chunks as independent pieces of text, you extract entities and relationships from your documents and build a graph: "Alice works at Acme Corp," "Acme Corp is headquartered in Seattle," "Seattle is in Washington state." When a user asks "What state does Alice's company operate in?", simple vector search might not surface the answer because no single chunk contains this chain of facts. But a graph traversal from Alice → Acme Corp → Seattle → Washington state connects the dots. Microsoft's GraphRAG implementation extracts community summaries from the graph, enabling both local (specific fact) and global (thematic summary) retrieval.

Agentic RAG wraps the RAG pipeline in an agent loop. Instead of always retrieving and always generating, an LLM agent decides when to retrieve, what query to use, whether to retrieve again with a refined query, and when it has enough information to answer. This is powerful for complex questions that require iterative refinement. The user asks "Compare our Q3 and Q4 revenue," and the agent issues two separate retrieval calls — one for Q3, one for Q4 — then synthesizes. A basic RAG pipeline would mush both quarters into one query and probably retrieve noise.

Self-RAG introduces self-reflection into the pipeline. After the initial generation, the model evaluates its own output: "Is this answer supported by the retrieved context? Is the retrieved context relevant to the question? Do I need to retrieve more?" Based on these self-assessments, it can trigger additional retrieval rounds or revise its answer. The key innovation is that these reflection steps are trained into the model itself (via special tokens), not handled by an external orchestrator. The model learns when to trust its retrieved context and when to be skeptical.

Corrective RAG (CRAG) takes a different angle. It adds an explicit evaluation step between retrieval and generation. A lightweight classifier scores each retrieved chunk as "correct," "ambiguous," or "incorrect." Correct chunks pass through to the generator. Ambiguous chunks get refined through a knowledge strip step that extracts only the relevant sentences. Incorrect chunks trigger a web search fallback — if your knowledge base doesn't have the answer, go find it on the internet. This makes the system robust to retrieval failures rather than blindly trusting whatever comes back from the vector store.

Multi-modal RAG extends retrieval beyond text. Your knowledge base might include images (architecture diagrams, charts, product photos), tables, and PDFs with complex layouts. Multi-modal RAG systems use vision-language models to embed images alongside text, enabling queries like "show me the system architecture for the payment service" to retrieve both the relevant text description and the associated diagram. Tools like ColPali embed entire document page images directly, bypassing the need for OCR or text extraction.

Production Considerations

Building a RAG prototype takes a weekend. Running it in production takes real engineering. Here are the things that will bite you when you move from demo to deployment.

Metadata filtering is essential. In a real system, you don't search the entire knowledge base for every query. A question about the engineering team's deployment process should only search engineering documents, not HR policies or sales playbooks. Attach metadata (department, document type, date, author, access level) to every chunk during indexing, and filter on it at query time. Most vector stores support pre-filtering (filter before search) or post-filtering (filter after search). Pre-filtering is faster and produces more relevant results, but not all stores support it efficiently.

Incremental indexing matters because your data changes. Documents get updated, new ones get added, old ones get deleted. You don't want to re-embed your entire knowledge base every time someone edits a page. Design your pipeline to detect changes (via timestamps, hashes, or a CDC — change data capture — stream), chunk and embed only the changed documents, and upsert them into the vector store. Most vector stores support upsert operations that insert or update vectors by ID. Make sure each chunk has a stable, deterministic ID derived from its source document and position.

Latency is a user experience issue. A RAG pipeline adds retrieval time on top of LLM generation time. Dense retrieval against an in-memory FAISS index takes 5–20ms. A managed vector store API call takes 50–200ms. Reranking adds another 50–300ms. LLM generation takes 500–3000ms depending on the model and output length. Your total latency is the sum of these, and users start getting impatient after about 2 seconds. Profile each stage, set latency budgets, and optimize accordingly. Caching frequent queries can cut latency dramatically — if 20% of questions are the same, cache those answers.

Cost monitoring is non-negotiable. Every API call costs money: embedding calls for indexing and query time, LLM calls for generation, reranker calls for reranking. At scale, the costs add up. A system processing 10,000 queries per day with GPT-4o and Cohere Rerank can easily cost $500–2000/month. Track cost per query, broken down by component. You might find that 80% of your cost is the LLM — in which case, a smaller model with good retrieval might save money with minimal quality loss.

A/B testing is how you make confident improvements. Change one thing at a time — a new embedding model, a different chunk size, a new reranker — and measure the impact on your RAGAS metrics and user satisfaction. Don't make five changes at once and hope it gets better. In a production setting, route a percentage of traffic to the new pipeline and compare metrics side by side. This is standard practice for search systems and applies equally to RAG.

Honest Tradeoffs

RAG isn't magic. It can't answer questions that aren't in your knowledge base. It struggles with complex multi-hop reasoning unless you use advanced techniques. It adds latency and cost compared to a bare LLM call. And it creates a new failure mode: if retrieval breaks, everything breaks — silently. Your LLM will confidently generate answers from garbage context. Build monitoring, build evaluation, build alerting. Treat your RAG pipeline like any other production system, not like a science experiment.

The most common mistake teams make is over-engineering from day one. Start with the simplest possible pipeline: recursive chunking, a single dense retriever, no reranker, and a good LLM. Measure with RAGAS. Identify your bottleneck. Add hybrid search if you're seeing vocabulary mismatch issues. Add reranking if retrieval precision is the problem. Add metadata filtering if you're drowning in irrelevant results. Each component adds complexity, and complexity has a maintenance cost. Add it only when you have evidence that it helps.

RAG is the most practical bridge between what LLMs know and what your users need. It's not glamorous — it's plumbing. But like all good plumbing, when it's done right, everything just works. Your users get accurate, grounded, up-to-date answers. Your LLM stays honest. And you sleep at night knowing it's not making things up.
