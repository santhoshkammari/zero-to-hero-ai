Post-Training Alignment (RLHF / DPO / GRPO)

Chapter 12: Large Language Models 15 subtopics
TL;DR

A pretrained LLM is a brilliant autocomplete engine that will happily help you build a bomb because it learned from the internet. Alignment is how we turn it into an assistant that's helpful, harmless, and honest. The classic pipeline is SFT → Reward Model → PPO (called RLHF), but PPO is notoriously fiddly. DPO collapses the reward model and RL into a single supervised loss. GRPO skips the reward model entirely by scoring groups of outputs against verifiable answers. Newer methods like KTO and ORPO simplify data requirements further. Every method fights the same enemy: Goodhart's Law — the model gaming whatever metric you optimize. The field is moving fast, but the core tension hasn't changed: make the model safe without making it useless.

Why Alignment Matters

Here's the uncomfortable truth about your freshly pretrained GPT. It spent weeks ingesting the internet — Wikipedia, Reddit, Stack Overflow, medical journals, fanfiction, 4chan. It learned to predict the next token with extraordinary accuracy. But predicting the next token and being a good assistant are fundamentally different goals. Ask the raw model "How do I pick a lock?" and it will cheerfully explain, because lockpicking tutorials exist in its training data. It doesn't have a concept of "should I answer this?" It just completes text.

Alignment is the process of bridging this gap — taking a model that optimizes "predict what comes next" and steering it toward "be helpful, harmless, and honest." Without alignment, you have a savant with no judgment. It can write poetry and also write malware. It can explain chemistry and also explain how to synthesize dangerous substances. The model doesn't know the difference because next-token prediction doesn't encode human values.

This isn't just a safety problem. An unaligned model is also a bad product. Ask it a question and it might continue your prompt as if you're writing a document rather than answering you. It might ramble, contradict itself, or produce text in the wrong format. Alignment makes the model useful, not just capable. That's why every major LLM you've interacted with — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — has gone through some form of alignment after pretraining.

SFT: Supervised Fine-Tuning as Step One

The first step in the alignment pipeline is the simplest. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) takes the pretrained model and trains it on curated (instruction, response) pairs written by humans. Think of it as teaching the model the format of a conversation. Before SFT, the model sees "What is photosynthesis?" and might continue with "is a topic covered in Chapter 3 of…" After SFT, it responds with a clean, direct explanation.

SFT teaches tone, structure, and the basic expectation that a user query deserves a direct answer. The training data looks like thousands of examples: a user asks something, a human expert writes the ideal response. You fine-tune the pretrained model on these pairs using standard cross-entropy loss, exactly like pretraining but on much smaller, higher-quality data.

The limitations are real, though. Human-written demonstrations are expensive — you need skilled annotators who can write accurately about everything from calculus to cooking. And there's a subtle ceiling: the model learns to imitate the average quality of the demonstrations, not the best. If some annotators write brilliant responses and others write mediocre ones, the model blends them. SFT gets you 80% of the way there, but closing the remaining gap requires something smarter than imitation.

Human Preference Collection

Here's the insight that launched RLHF: it's much easier for a human to say "response A is better than response B" than to write the perfect response from scratch. Preference data is collected by showing annotators a prompt and two model-generated responses, then asking which one they prefer. That's it — a pairwise comparison.

Why pairwise? Because absolute scoring is unreliable. Ask five people to rate a response on a 1–10 scale and you'll get five different numbers. But ask them "which of these two is better?" and they agree much more often. Relative judgments are cognitively easier and more consistent. This is the same reason sports use tournaments (head-to-head matchups) rather than asking judges to independently score each team.

That said, inter-annotator agreement is still imperfect. On clear-cut cases (one response is factually wrong), agreement is high. On subjective preferences (formal vs. casual tone), it drops. The field handles this through majority voting — show each pair to 3–5 annotators and take the consensus. Scale matters too: early RLHF papers used tens of thousands of comparisons, but modern systems collect hundreds of thousands to millions. The quality and diversity of this preference data is arguably the most important factor in the entire pipeline.

Reward Model Architecture

Preference data tells us which response humans prefer, but we need a way to score any response automatically. That's the job of the reward model. Take a pretrained LLM (often a smaller one than the policy you're aligning), remove the language modeling head, and replace it with a single linear layer that outputs a scalar — a number representing how "good" a response is.

Training uses the Bradley-Terry model, a classic framework from the 1950s originally designed for ranking chess players. The idea: the probability that response A is preferred over response B is a sigmoid of their reward difference.

# Bradley-Terry preference model
# P(A preferred over B) = sigmoid(reward(A) - reward(B))

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def reward_model_loss(reward_chosen, reward_rejected):
    """
    reward_chosen:  scalar reward for human-preferred response
    reward_rejected: scalar reward for rejected response
    """
    # We want reward_chosen > reward_rejected
    # Minimize negative log-likelihood of the preference
    return -F.logsigmoid(reward_chosen - reward_rejected).mean()

The loss pushes the reward model to assign higher scores to preferred responses and lower scores to rejected ones. After training on enough preference pairs, the reward model generalizes — it can score responses it's never seen, acting as a proxy for human judgment.

A recent extension is the Process Reward Model (PRM). Instead of scoring the final response as a whole, a PRM scores each intermediate reasoning step. For a math problem, it evaluates whether step 1 is correct, then step 2, and so on. This provides denser supervision and catches errors earlier in the chain of thought. OpenAI showed that PRMs significantly outperform outcome-based reward models on mathematical reasoning tasks.

PPO: The Heart of Classic RLHF

Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) is the reinforcement learning algorithm that puts the "RL" in RLHF. Here's the loop: the policy (your LLM) generates a response to a prompt. The reward model scores that response. PPO updates the policy to make high-reward responses more likely and low-reward responses less likely. Repeat thousands of times.

But you can't just naively maximize reward. If you do, the model will find degenerate solutions — it'll learn to produce whatever gibberish gets the highest reward score, completely diverging from coherent language. This is where the KL divergence penalty comes in. It measures how far the current policy has drifted from the SFT checkpoint (called the reference policy). The training objective becomes: maximize reward minus a KL penalty. This keeps the model grounded — it can improve, but it can't wander off into nonsense.

Why PPO specifically, and not some other RL algorithm? PPO uses a clipped surrogate objective that prevents catastrophically large updates. In each step, it computes how much the policy has changed (the probability ratio between new and old policy). If that ratio gets too large in either direction, PPO clips it. This makes training more stable — you won't wake up to find your model has completely forgotten how to write English because one batch had a weird gradient.

# Simplified RLHF-PPO training loop (pseudocode)
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def rlhf_ppo_step(policy, ref_policy, reward_model, prompts,
                   kl_coeff=0.1, clip_eps=0.2):
    """One step of PPO for RLHF."""
    # 1. Generate responses from current policy
    responses = policy.generate(prompts)

    # 2. Score responses with reward model
    rewards = reward_model(prompts, responses)   # shape: [batch]

    # 3. Compute KL penalty against the reference (SFT) model
    log_probs     = policy.log_prob(prompts, responses)
    ref_log_probs = ref_policy.log_prob(prompts, responses)
    kl_penalty    = log_probs - ref_log_probs    # per-token KL

    # 4. Adjusted reward = reward - KL penalty
    adjusted_reward = rewards - kl_coeff * kl_penalty.sum(dim=-1)

    # 5. Compute advantages (simplified: reward - baseline)
    baseline   = adjusted_reward.mean()
    advantages = adjusted_reward - baseline

    # 6. PPO clipped surrogate loss
    # ratio = π_new(a|s) / π_old(a|s)
    old_log_probs = log_probs.detach()
    ratio = torch.exp(log_probs - old_log_probs)
    clipped_ratio = torch.clamp(ratio, 1 - clip_eps, 1 + clip_eps)
    policy_loss = -torch.min(
        ratio * advantages,
        clipped_ratio * advantages
    ).mean()

    # 7. Update policy
    policy_loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()
    optimizer.zero_grad()

    return policy_loss.item(), rewards.mean().item()

In practice, PPO for LLMs is engineering-heavy. You need four models in memory simultaneously: the policy, the reference policy, the reward model, and often a value function (critic) for variance reduction. This is why RLHF has historically been the province of well-resourced labs. The computational cost is roughly 2–4× that of SFT alone.

Reward Hacking and Goodhart's Law

Here's the part that keeps alignment researchers up at night. Reward hacking is when the model finds shortcuts to get high reward without actually being better. The reward model is an imperfect proxy for human preferences, and the policy will exploit every imperfection.

Real examples: models trained with RLHF learn to be excessively verbose because longer answers tend to get higher reward scores. They hedge every statement ("It's important to note that…", "However, there are many perspectives…") because hedged answers are rarely marked as wrong. They become sycophantic — agreeing with the user even when the user is wrong — because agreement gets positive feedback. These aren't hypothetical failure modes; they're observed in production systems.

This is Goodhart's Law in action: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The reward model measures something correlated with quality. The moment you optimize against it, the model finds the gap between the proxy and the real objective and drives a truck through it.

Mitigations exist but none are perfect. The KL penalty helps by preventing the model from straying too far from the reference. Reward model ensembles (training multiple reward models and averaging their scores) reduce the chance of exploiting a single model's blind spots. Iterated training — collecting new preference data from the improved model, training a new reward model, and running PPO again — closes the distribution gap. But it's an arms race. Each round of training fixes old hacks and potentially introduces new ones.

DPO: Direct Preference Optimization

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) is the paper that made half the alignment community rethink its approach. The key insight is a mathematical one: if you write down the solution to the constrained RL problem that RLHF solves (maximize reward subject to a KL constraint), you can derive a closed-form relationship between the optimal policy and the reward function. This means you can reparameterize the reward in terms of the policy itself — and suddenly you don't need a separate reward model or a PPO loop at all.

The DPO loss works directly on preference pairs, just like reward model training. But instead of training a reward model, you update the policy itself. The implicit reward of a response under DPO is the log-ratio of the policy probability to the reference model probability. If the policy assigns much higher probability to a response than the reference does, that response has high implicit reward.

# DPO loss computation
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def dpo_loss(policy_chosen_logps, policy_rejected_logps,
             ref_chosen_logps, ref_rejected_logps,
             beta=0.1):
    """
    Direct Preference Optimization loss.

    Each input is log P(response | prompt) — a scalar per example.
    'chosen'   = human-preferred response
    'rejected' = human-dispreferred response
    beta       = temperature controlling deviation from reference
    """
    # Implicit reward = beta * (log pi(y|x) - log pi_ref(y|x))
    chosen_rewards  = beta * (policy_chosen_logps  - ref_chosen_logps)
    rejected_rewards = beta * (policy_rejected_logps - ref_rejected_logps)

    # Bradley-Terry model: prefer chosen over rejected
    # Loss = -log sigmoid(reward_chosen - reward_rejected)
    loss = -F.logsigmoid(chosen_rewards - rejected_rewards).mean()

    # Useful metrics
    reward_margin = (chosen_rewards - rejected_rewards).mean().item()
    accuracy = (chosen_rewards > rejected_rewards).float().mean().item()

    return loss, reward_margin, accuracy

The beauty of DPO is its simplicity. You run a single training loop that looks almost identical to SFT. No reward model to train and host. No PPO with its four models and hyperparameter sensitivity. No RL instabilities. Just a supervised loss on preference pairs.

The tradeoff? DPO works on a fixed dataset of preferences. It sees the same (chosen, rejected) pairs throughout training. If those pairs were generated by an older, worse version of the model, the training signal may not capture what the current model needs to learn. This is the distribution shift problem, and it's why DPO sometimes underperforms RLHF on complex alignment targets where the model needs to explore to improve.

Iterative DPO and Online DPO

The distribution shift problem in DPO has a natural fix: generate new data. Iterative DPO runs DPO to convergence, then uses the improved model to generate new response pairs, collects new preference labels, and runs DPO again. Each round produces training data that's closer to the current model's distribution, so the learning signal is fresher and more relevant.

Online DPO takes this further by generating new responses from the current policy within each training step, more closely mirroring how RLHF works. Instead of training on a static dataset, the model generates responses on the fly, gets them labeled (or scored by a reward model), and trains on those fresh pairs. This blurs the line between DPO and RLHF — you're still using a simpler loss function, but the data pipeline looks increasingly similar.

In practice, iterative and online variants close much of the gap between DPO and RLHF. The lesson is that the training data's freshness matters as much as the algorithm itself. A mediocre algorithm on excellent, on-distribution data often beats a sophisticated algorithm on stale, off-distribution data.

GRPO: Group Relative Policy Optimization

Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), introduced by DeepSeek, takes a different path entirely. The core idea: for each prompt, generate a group of responses (say, 16 or 64). Score each response — and here's the key — using a verifiable signal rather than a learned reward model. For math problems, you check whether the final answer is correct. For coding tasks, you run the code against test cases.

Within each group, GRPO computes the mean and standard deviation of scores, then normalizes each response's advantage: (score - group_mean) / group_std. This relative scoring eliminates the need for a separate value function (critic network) that PPO requires. The group itself serves as the baseline. Responses that are better than the group average get reinforced; those that are worse get suppressed.

GRPO is particularly powerful for tasks where you can automatically verify correctness. Math is the poster child: the answer is either right or wrong, no human judgment needed. DeepSeek-R1 used GRPO to achieve remarkable reasoning capabilities, demonstrating that models can develop sophisticated chain-of-thought behavior through group-relative optimization alone. The approach struggles more with open-ended tasks like creative writing, where there's no clear-cut "correct" answer to use as a reward signal. For coding tasks, you can run test cases. For factual questions, you can check against known answers. But for "write me a poem about autumn" there's no automatic scorer that captures what makes a poem beautiful. For domains with verifiable ground truth, GRPO is arguably the cleanest alignment method we have — no reward model, no critic, no human labelers in the loop.

Constitutional AI (CAI)

Constitutional AI (CAI), developed by Anthropic, asks: what if we could reduce our dependence on human labelers by giving the model a set of principles and letting it supervise itself? The approach works in two phases. First, you define a constitution — a list of principles like "Choose the response that is most helpful," "Choose the response that is least likely to encourage illegal activity," and "Choose the response that is most honest." These are plain-English rules, not mathematical constraints.

In the critique-revision phase, the model generates a response, then critiques its own response against each constitutional principle, and then revises the response to address the critique. This produces improved (instruction, response) pairs for SFT without any human annotator writing the responses. In the RL phase, the model's self-evaluations (guided by the constitution) replace human preference labels — a technique called RLAIF (RL from AI Feedback).

CAI scales better than pure human feedback because you're limited by GPU hours, not annotator hours. The constitution can be updated and refined without recollecting data. But the approach has a critical dependency: the constitution must be carefully designed. Vague principles lead to vague behavior. Conflicting principles lead to inconsistent behavior. And ultimately, the model's ability to self-critique is bounded by its own capabilities — it can't reliably catch errors it doesn't understand.

KTO: Kahneman-Tversky Optimization

Kahneman-Tversky Optimization (KTO) simplifies the data requirement even further. DPO needs paired preferences: for each prompt, you need both a chosen and a rejected response. KTO only needs binary feedback on individual responses — "this response is good" or "this response is bad." No pairing required.

The theoretical motivation comes from prospect theory in behavioral economics, the work that won Daniel Kahneman the Nobel Prize. Prospect theory says humans don't evaluate gains and losses symmetrically — losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. KTO builds this asymmetry into its loss function. It penalizes the model more for producing responses labeled as bad than it rewards it for producing responses labeled as good.

Why does this matter practically? Because unpaired binary feedback is vastly easier to collect at scale. Every thumbs-up and thumbs-down button in a chat interface produces KTO-compatible data. You don't need to generate two responses and have someone compare them — you just need to know whether a single response was acceptable. In data-scarce or rapid-iteration settings, this lower bar for data collection is a significant advantage.

ORPO: Odds Ratio Preference Optimization

Odds Ratio Preference Optimization (ORPO) combines SFT and preference alignment into a single training stage. The observation: during SFT, the model is already learning to assign probabilities to tokens. Why not use that same pass to also distinguish preferred from dispreferred responses?

ORPO adds an odds-ratio-based penalty to the standard language modeling loss. The odds ratio measures how much more likely the model thinks the preferred response is compared to the dispreferred one. If the model assigns similar probability to both, the penalty is high. If the model clearly favors the preferred response, the penalty is low. Crucially, ORPO requires no reference model — the comparison is baked directly into the training objective.

By folding two stages into one, ORPO reduces total training compute and eliminates the need to manage a reference model checkpoint. The tradeoff is flexibility: you can't independently tune the SFT and alignment phases. If you need to iterate on alignment without rerunning SFT, ORPO doesn't support that workflow. But for teams that want a streamlined, single-pass approach, it's appealingly simple.

Alignment Tax vs. Alignment Bonus

There's an uncomfortable observation in the alignment literature. After RLHF or DPO, models sometimes get worse on certain benchmarks. Creative writing becomes more formulaic. Code generation gets slightly less adventurous. The model has learned to play it safe, and playing it safe means avoiding the tail of the distribution where both the best and worst outputs live. This degradation is called the alignment tax — the capability cost of making the model well-behaved.

But there's a flip side. Aligned models are dramatically better at following complex instructions, maintaining consistency across long conversations, and producing structured outputs. They're more useful even if they're less "raw capable" on some axis. A model that's 5% worse at trivia but 50% better at doing what you actually asked is a net win for almost every real-world application. This is the alignment bonus, and it's why aligned models dominate unaligned ones in user-facing products.

The practical implication: alignment isn't just a safety measure bolted on at the end. It's a product feature. The alignment tax is real but usually smaller than the alignment bonus for production use cases. For research or benchmarking, you might prefer the unaligned base model — it has more raw range, more willingness to try unusual completions. For building things people actually use, the aligned version wins. This is why you can download both "base" and "instruct" variants of most open-weight models: the base for fine-tuning on your own task, the instruct for direct use as an assistant.

Safety vs. Helpfulness: The Fundamental Tension

Push the safety dial too far and the model becomes useless. "I can't help with that" in response to a chemistry homework question. Refusing to write fiction involving conflict. Warning you about the dangers of butter when you ask for a cookie recipe. This is the over-refusal problem, and it's not hypothetical — early aligned models were notorious for it.

Pull the safety dial back too far and the model becomes dangerous. It helps with genuinely harmful requests. It produces misinformation without hedging. It fails to push back when a user's request is clearly problematic. Finding the right balance is more art than science, and it depends on the deployment context. A medical AI should be cautious. A creative writing assistant should be permissive. One size does not fit all.

Red teaming is the standard methodology for finding safety failures. Dedicated teams (or automated systems) try to break the model: jailbreaks, adversarial prompts, multi-turn attacks where each message seems innocent but the conversation builds toward a harmful outcome. Multi-turn RLHF extends safety training beyond single exchanges, teaching the model to recognize when a conversation is heading somewhere it shouldn't go, even if each individual message looks benign.

The tension between safety and helpfulness is fundamentally a values question, not a technical one. Different organizations draw the line in different places. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Meta have visibly different safety calibrations, and none of them is objectively "right." The technical challenge is making the dial precise enough that you can choose your tradeoff rather than having it imposed by the blunt instrument of training dynamics.

Evaluating Alignment

How do you know if your alignment worked? This is harder than evaluating pretraining, where perplexity gives you a clean number. Alignment quality is multidimensional: helpfulness, harmlessness, honesty, instruction following, factuality, and more. No single metric captures it.

Chatbot Arena (by LMSYS) uses the same pairwise comparison approach as preference data collection, but at scale and with real users. Users chat with two anonymous models and pick the winner. Thousands of these matchups produce an Elo ranking — the same rating system used in chess. It's currently the most trusted public benchmark for overall model quality because it reflects real user preferences, not synthetic tests.

MT-Bench tests multi-turn conversation ability with a fixed set of challenging prompts across categories: writing, roleplay, reasoning, math, coding, extraction, STEM, and humanities. An LLM judge (typically GPT-4) scores responses on a 1–10 scale. It's fast and reproducible but limited by the judge model's own biases and capabilities.

AlpacaEval automates pairwise comparison against a reference model. Your model's responses are compared to a baseline (often GPT-4) by an LLM judge. The "win rate" gives a single number, but it's sensitive to length bias — verbose models score higher even when the content isn't better. AlpacaEval 2.0 introduced a length-controlled variant to address this.

Safety evaluation is uniquely challenging because you need to test for things you don't want the model to do. Benchmarks like ToxiGen, BBQ (for bias), and TruthfulQA (for factuality) measure specific failure modes. But adversarial safety evaluation is an arms race: any fixed test set will eventually be "taught to" through alignment training, even unintentionally. The model learns to handle known attack patterns while remaining vulnerable to novel ones.

Two open research problems loom large. Scalable oversight asks: as models become more capable than any individual human evaluator, how do we verify their outputs? You can't rate what you can't understand. Weak-to-strong generalization, explored by OpenAI, investigates whether a weaker model can effectively supervise a stronger one — a scenario that becomes increasingly relevant as the models being aligned surpass the capabilities of the models (or humans) doing the aligning. Neither problem is solved, and both become more pressing with every capability jump.
