Hierarchical Bayesian Models

Chapter 14: Probabilistic & Bayesian ML 5 subtopics
TL;DR

Hierarchical Bayesian Models let you share statistical strength across related groups without assuming they are identical. If you have data from 50 hospitals, 200 schools, or 30 ad campaigns, a hierarchical model learns both within-group and between-group patterns simultaneously. This section covers the hierarchical structure, why partial pooling beats both "one model per group" and "one model for all," multilevel models, hierarchical linear regression, and the trade-off between empirical Bayes shortcuts and full Bayesian inference.

Hierarchical Structure and Hyperpriors

Here is a problem you will hit constantly: you have data from multiple groups, and you need to estimate something for each group. Maybe you are estimating click-through rates for 200 different ad creatives. Some ads have 10,000 impressions — plenty of data. Others launched yesterday and have 50 impressions. How do you handle the small-data ads?

Option 1: Estimate each ad's CTR independently. The 50-impression ad's estimate will be wildly noisy — maybe it got lucky and shows 8% CTR when the true rate is 2%. This is the no-pooling approach. Each group is treated as if the others do not exist.

Option 2: Pool all ads together and estimate a single CTR. This is the complete-pooling approach. It ignores real differences between ads. A genuinely great ad gets dragged down to the average.

Option 3: A hierarchical Bayesian model that recognizes ads are related (they come from the same company, target similar audiences) but not identical. Each ad has its own parameter, but those parameters are drawn from a shared distribution. The shared distribution acts as a hyperprior — a prior on the prior.

# Hierarchical structure for ad click-through rates:
#
# Level 2 (hyperprior):  μ ~ Normal(0, 10)       ← global mean CTR
#                         σ ~ HalfNormal(5)       ← how much CTRs vary across ads
#
# Level 1 (group prior): θ_i ~ Normal(μ, σ)      ← each ad's "true" CTR
#                         for i = 1, ..., 200
#
# Data level:            clicks_i ~ Binomial(impressions_i, θ_i)
#
# The magic: μ and σ are learned from ALL the data,
# then each θ_i is informed by both its own data AND the group.
# Small-data ads get pulled toward the global mean.
# Large-data ads stay close to their own estimate.

The parameters μ and σ at the top level are called hyperparameters (not to be confused with ML hyperparameters like learning rate). Their priors are hyperpriors. This layered structure — data depends on group parameters, group parameters depend on hyperparameters, hyperparameters depend on hyperpriors — is what makes it hierarchical.

You can stack more levels. Students nested within classrooms nested within schools nested within districts. Each level adds parameters that capture variation at that scale. The model learns how much variation exists at each level from the data itself.

Partial Pooling and Shrinkage

Partial pooling is the key mechanism that makes hierarchical models work. Group estimates are "shrunk" toward the global mean, with the amount of shrinkage depending on two things: how much data the group has, and how variable the groups are overall.

Think of it as a weighted average:

# For group i with n_i observations and group mean y_bar_i:
#
# Partial pooling estimate ≈ w_i * y_bar_i + (1 - w_i) * global_mean
#
# where w_i = n_i / (n_i + κ)
#       κ = within-group variance / between-group variance
#
# If n_i is large → w_i ≈ 1 → trust the group's own data
# If n_i is small → w_i ≈ 0 → shrink toward global mean
# If between-group variance is large → κ small → less shrinkage
# If between-group variance is small → κ large → more shrinkage
#
# This is the James-Stein effect: shrinkage improves TOTAL estimation
# error across all groups simultaneously, even if individual group
# estimates are biased.

This is not a hack. It is provably optimal in a precise mathematical sense. The James-Stein estimator (1961) showed that when you estimate three or more means simultaneously, the shrinkage estimator dominates the individual estimates in total squared error. Hierarchical Bayesian models generalize this result with a probabilistic framework that also gives you uncertainty estimates.

A concrete example makes this tangible. Suppose you are estimating batting averages for baseball players after the first month of the season. A player who went 4-for-10 has a .400 batting average — clearly unsustainable. A player who went 1-for-10 has a .100 average — also unrealistic. Partial pooling pulls both toward the league average (~.260), with more pull for the 10-at-bat players than for someone with 200 at-bats. By the end of the season, the partial pooling estimates made in April are closer to final averages than the raw early-season numbers.

💡 Key Insight

Partial pooling gives you the best of both worlds: you never overfit to small-sample groups (because they are regularized toward the mean), and you never underfit large-sample groups (because they are allowed to deviate). The model learns the right amount of pooling from the data — you do not have to specify it manually.

Multilevel Models

Multilevel models (also called mixed-effects models in frequentist statistics) are hierarchical models where the groups have a natural nesting or crossing structure. "Nesting" means groups live inside other groups: students within classrooms within schools. "Crossing" means groups overlap: students take multiple subjects from multiple teachers.

In a two-level model, Level 1 describes variation within groups (how students in the same classroom differ), and Level 2 describes variation between groups (how classrooms differ). Each level has its own regression equation.

import pymc as pm
import numpy as np

# Example: student test scores nested within schools
# y_ij = score of student j in school i
# Level 1: y_ij = α_i + β_i * x_ij + ε_ij    (within school)
# Level 2: α_i ~ Normal(μ_α, σ_α)              (school intercepts)
#          β_i ~ Normal(μ_β, σ_β)              (school slopes)

n_schools = 30
n_students_per_school = np.random.randint(15, 60, size=n_schools)
school_idx = np.concatenate([np.full(n, i) for i, n in enumerate(n_students_per_school)])
n_total = len(school_idx)
ses = np.random.normal(0, 1, n_total)  # Socioeconomic status (predictor)

with pm.Model() as multilevel_model:
    # Hyperpriors (Level 2)
    mu_alpha = pm.Normal("mu_alpha", mu=50, sigma=20)
    sigma_alpha = pm.HalfNormal("sigma_alpha", sigma=15)
    mu_beta = pm.Normal("mu_beta", mu=0, sigma=10)
    sigma_beta = pm.HalfNormal("sigma_beta", sigma=5)

    # School-level parameters (varying intercepts and slopes)
    alpha = pm.Normal("alpha", mu=mu_alpha, sigma=sigma_alpha, shape=n_schools)
    beta = pm.Normal("beta", mu=mu_beta, sigma=sigma_beta, shape=n_schools)

    # Student-level noise
    sigma_y = pm.HalfNormal("sigma_y", sigma=10)

    # Expected score for each student
    mu_y = alpha[school_idx] + beta[school_idx] * ses

    # Likelihood

The model above has varying intercepts (each school has a different baseline score α_i) and varying slopes (the effect of socioeconomic status β_i differs by school). The hyperpriors μ_α, σ_α, μ_β, σ_β learn the overall average intercept/slope and how much these vary across schools.

Why bother with this structure instead of throwing everything into one big regression with school dummy variables? Three reasons. First, partial pooling gives better estimates for small schools. Second, you get estimates of between-school variation (σ_α, σ_β) as a first-class parameter with full uncertainty. Third, you can make predictions for a new school that was not in the training data by drawing from the group-level distribution.

Hierarchical Linear Models

Hierarchical linear models (HLMs) are the special case where the relationships at each level are linear. They are the workhorse of applied Bayesian statistics in social science, education, epidemiology, and A/B testing. The general form:

# Level 1 (within group):
#   y_ij = α_i + β_i * x_ij + ε_ij,    ε_ij ~ Normal(0, σ²)
#
# Level 2 (between groups):
#   α_i = γ_00 + γ_01 * z_i + u_0i,    u_0i ~ Normal(0, τ_00)
#   β_i = γ_10 + γ_11 * z_i + u_1i,    u_1i ~ Normal(0, τ_11)
#
# x_ij = level-1 predictor (e.g., student SES)
# z_i  = level-2 predictor (e.g., school funding)
# γ's  = fixed effects (overall intercept and slopes)
# u's  = random effects (group-level deviations)
# τ's  = variance components (how much groups differ)
#
# Combined ("mixed model" form):
# y_ij = γ_00 + γ_01*z_i + γ_10*x_ij + γ_11*z_i*x_ij + u_0i + u_1i*x_ij + ε_ij
#        ╰───────────── fixed effects ──────────────╯   ╰── random effects ──╯

The terms γ_00, γ_01, γ_10, γ_11 are fixed effects — the average intercept and slopes across all groups and their dependence on group-level predictors. The terms u_0i, u_1i are random effects — each group's deviation from the average. The τ values capture how much groups vary.

A practical example: you run an A/B test across 50 countries. The treatment effect might vary by country (cultural differences, market maturity). An HLM lets each country have its own treatment effect β_i while learning the global average effect γ_10 and the variation σ_β. Countries with small sample sizes borrow strength from the global estimate. You can also include country-level predictors (GDP, internet penetration) to explain some of the between-country variation.

import pymc as pm
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

# A/B test across 50 countries
# y = revenue per user, x = treatment (0 or 1), z = GDP per capita
n_countries = 50

with pm.Model() as ab_test_hlm:
    # Hyperpriors
    gamma_00 = pm.Normal("gamma_00", mu=10, sigma=5)    # global intercept
    gamma_01 = pm.Normal("gamma_01", mu=0, sigma=1)     # effect of GDP on baseline
    gamma_10 = pm.Normal("gamma_10", mu=0, sigma=2)     # global treatment effect

The cross-level interaction γ_11 (GDP moderating treatment effect) is one of the most powerful features of hierarchical models. It lets you answer questions like "does the treatment work better in wealthier countries?" with principled uncertainty estimates. This is hard to do well with non-hierarchical approaches.

Empirical Bayes vs Full Bayesian Hierarchical

There are two philosophies for fitting hierarchical models, and the difference matters in practice.

Empirical Bayes (EB) takes a shortcut: estimate the hyperparameters (μ, σ) by maximum likelihood, then use those point estimates as fixed priors for the group-level parameters. It is a two-step procedure: first optimize the hyperparameters, then compute group-level posteriors conditional on those estimates.

Full Bayesian inference puts priors on the hyperparameters too and jointly estimates everything — hyperparameters and group parameters — using MCMC or variational inference. The hyperparameters get their own posterior distributions, and all the uncertainty propagates correctly.

# Empirical Bayes (EB):
# 1. Estimate μ̂, σ̂ by maximizing marginal likelihood p(data | μ, σ)
# 2. Plug in: compute p(θ_i | data, μ̂, σ̂) for each group
#
# Pros: Fast (no MCMC). Easy to implement. Scales well.
# Cons: Underestimates uncertainty (treats μ̂, σ̂ as known).
#        Can overfit the hyperparameters with few groups.
#
# Full Bayesian:
# 1. Place hyperpriors on μ, σ
# 2. Jointly sample p(μ, σ, θ_1, ..., θ_K | data) via MCMC
#
# Pros: Proper uncertainty propagation. Hyperparameter uncertainty included.
# Cons: Computationally expensive. MCMC convergence can be tricky.
#        Requires choosing hyperpriors.
#
# When to use which:
# - Many groups (>50): EB and Full Bayes give similar results. EB is faster.
# - Few groups (<10): Full Bayes is strongly preferred — EB overfits σ̂.
# - Need calibrated uncertainty: Full Bayes. EB underestimates.
# - Production system, needs speed: EB with careful validation.

The practical difference is often smaller than you might expect when the number of groups is large. With 200 hospitals, the hyperparameter estimates are well-constrained and EB gives almost identical group-level estimates to full Bayes. But with 5 hospitals, the uncertainty in the hyperparameters is substantial, and ignoring it (as EB does) leads to overconfident group estimates.

A common middle ground: use EB for initial exploration and model building (fast iteration), then switch to full Bayes for final inference when you need reliable uncertainty intervals. Libraries like PyMC, Stan, and NumPyro make full Bayesian inference straightforward enough that the computational cost is the main reason to prefer EB.

⚠️ Warning

Empirical Bayes can produce paradoxical results when the number of groups is very small. With 3 groups, the between-group variance estimate σ̂ can collapse to zero, eliminating all shrinkage or forcing complete pooling. Full Bayesian inference with a proper hyperprior on σ avoids this by allowing uncertainty in the variance component. If you have fewer than ~10 groups, invest the compute in full Bayes.

Hierarchical models are everywhere once you learn to recognize them. Any time you have repeated measurements, grouped data, or nested structure — patients within hospitals, users within countries, items within categories, time points within subjects — a hierarchical model is the principled way to share information across groups while respecting their differences.
