TL;DR

Generative models go beyond GANs, VAEs, and diffusion. Bigger constellation: paint photographs in Van Gogh style, generate images one pixel at time, model data as electric fields. No need to implement them today. When they show up in papers, interviews, and rabbit holes, you know what they are and why they matter.

Neural Style Transfer

First time I saw photo rendered in style of Starry Night, I assumed elaborate Photoshop filter. Wrong. Gatys et al. (2015) showed deceptively clever trick. No generative model. Uses pre-trained image classifier. Surprise part.

Setup: take content image (city skyline photo) and style image (Van Gogh painting). Goal: new image keeps city structure but looks like Van Gogh painted it. Two goals: preserve what image contains, transform how it looks.

Insight: CNN like VGG-19 already separates these concerns across layers. Early layers capture textures and patterns — brushstrokes, color palettes, repeating motifs. Deep layers capture high-level structure — "building here, sky there." Style transfer exploits separation.

Process: optimize image from scratch (random noise or content-image copy) to minimize two losses. Content loss measures difference between generated image's deep-layer activations and content image's — keeps buildings, skyline, layout. Style loss measures difference in texture statistics between generated image and style image. Those statistics come from Gram matrix — matrix recording how much every pair of feature channels correlates across image. If Van Gogh painting has swirling blue-yellow textures, Gram matrix captures correlation pattern, and style loss pushes generated image toward same correlations.

Total loss: α × content_loss + β × style_loss. Raise β, get wild painterly effects. Raise α, photo stays almost unchanged. Balance point makes art.

Original approach was slow — minutes per image, because gradient descent runs on pixels themselves. Johnson et al. (2016) fixed this with fast style transfer: train feedforward network to produce stylized image in one forward pass. Once trained for particular style, it runs in real time. Most phone style-transfer apps work this way.

Key limitation: style transfer works well for textures and color palettes, but struggles with structural style. It can make photo look painted, but not like Picasso cubist rearrangement of face. Gram matrix captures statistical texture patterns, not spatial rearrangements. Problem is harder. Limitation helped motivate more powerful generative approaches.

Deepfakes and Detection

Deepfakes happen when generative models meet human faces with malicious — or at least mischievous — intent. Term started with specific Reddit user who used autoencoders to swap faces in videos. Now catch-all for AI-generated face manipulation.

Core face-swapping technique is straightforward. Train two autoencoders that share encoder but use different decoders — one for face A, one for face B. Shared encoder learns common "face representation" space. At inference time, feed face A through shared encoder, then decode with face B's decoder. Result: face A's expressions and pose, face B's identity. Blending is imperfect, so post-processing handles color correction, edge smoothing, and alignment.

GAN-based deepfakes pushed further. Models like StyleGAN generate fully synthetic faces or interpolate between real identities with unnerving smoothness. Realism got so good humans could not reliably distinguish real from synthetic. Then detection arms race started.

Deepfake detection is its own subfield. Hard problem. Main approaches: CNN- and Transformer-based detectors trained on real/fake media to spot pixel-level artifacts — compression inconsistencies, unnatural skin textures, weird eye reflections. Temporal analysis checks frame-to-frame inconsistencies: overly regular blinking, head movement-body mismatch, jittery facial landmarks. Biological signal analysis looks for subtle skin-color changes from blood flow (photoplethysmography). Real faces pulse with heartbeat. Synthetic faces do not. Audio-visual mismatch detectors compare lip movements and spoken words, catching cases where face and voice came from different models.

Uncomfortable truth: detection plays catch-up. Each new generation model leaves fewer artifacts, and detectors trained on one generation often fail on newer ones. Field is shifting toward provenance-based solutions — cryptographic watermarks embedded at capture time — instead of post hoc forgery detection. Long-run winner still unclear.

Adversarial Examples for Generative Models

Most adversarial-example discussion targets classifiers — tiny invisible perturbations turn panda into "gibbon." Generative models have adversarial vulnerabilities too, maybe more concerning because outputs are things people see and trust.

Concept transfers directly. For classifier, adversarial example makes wrong label. For generative model, adversarial example is input — or latent-space manipulation, or conditioning-signal perturbation — that produces wildly wrong or harmful output. Example: text-to-image system where carefully optimized prompt embedding — numerical vector, not natural-language prompt — yields image far from what human expects.

With diffusion models, researchers showed imperceptible perturbations to initial noise can steer generation toward specific target images. Model thinks it is doing normal denoising, but chosen starting point funnels it toward predetermined destination. Like rigged marble run — marble follows physics each step, track sends it where you want.

Defensive angle exists too. Adversarial purification flips script — use diffusion denoising to remove adversarial perturbations before passing images to classifier. Idea: denoising destroys crafted perturbation while preserving real content. Works surprisingly well. Signal: denoising process implicitly knows what "natural" looks like and pushes images back toward data manifold.

Open question: why some adversarial attacks transfer between generative models and some do not. Transferability remains active, unsettled area.

Score Matching and Langevin Dynamics

If you worked through diffusion section, you already met score matching without naming it. Here math underneath diffusion shows up.

Core generative-modeling problem: learn probability distribution p(x) from data. Trouble: computing p(x) needs normalizing constant — integral of unnormalized density over all possible x so probabilities sum to one. For high-dimensional data like images, integral is intractable. Cannot compute it.

Score matching (Hyvärinen, 2005) sidesteps this by learning score function — ∇ₓ log p(x), gradient of log-density with respect to data. Key insight: taking gradient of log p(x) removes normalizing constant. Constant's gradient is zero. So neural network s_θ(x) can approximate ∇ₓ log p(x) without ever computing p(x) itself.

Once you have score function, you can sample with Langevin dynamics — iterative process starting from random noise and walking toward high-probability regions by following score. Each step nudges sample toward higher log-probability plus small random perturbation for diversity. Like marble rolling downhill on negative-log-probability landscape, with little jiggling so it does not stick in nearest valley.

Song and Ermon (2019) made this practical by training score networks at multiple noise levels — whole family, one per noise scale. Start with heavily corrupted data, then progressively denoise using right score function at each level. Sounds like diffusion because it is. Denoising score matching and DDPM (Ho et al., 2020) turned out mathematically equivalent — different starting points, same destination.

Bridge: energy-based models define energy function. Score is gradient of negative energy. Score matching learns gradient. Langevin dynamics uses it for sampling. Diffusion models do this across multiple noise scales. One story, different angles.

Poisson Flow Generative Models

Most generative models borrow math from probability theory or thermodynamics. Poisson Flow Generative Models (PFGM), introduced in 2022, borrow from electrostatics. Idea makes you wonder why nobody tried it sooner.

Analogy: every training data point is tiny electric charge on flat surface. Charges create electric field above surface. Drop test particle from far away and let it follow field lines; it moves toward regions where charges are dense — high data density. That is generative process. Start from random noise, follow field, reach realistic sample.

Formally, PFGM embeds data in (d+1)-dimensional space and solves Poisson equation — core electrostatics equation — to compute vector field guiding generation. Neural network learns vector field, and generation integrates along it from random starting point on spherical shell down to data hyperplane.

Practical appeal: PFGM can reach high-quality samples with fewer integration steps than vanilla diffusion, because field lines give more direct path from noise to data than diffusion's stochastic back-and-forth. Theoretical appeal: different math lens on same problem. Things hard in diffusion frame may be natural in Poisson frame, and vice versa.

Approach is still young. I have not seen large-scale production deployment yet. But physics-inspired framing is elegant enough to keep on radar. Marble analogy from score matching still works — now hill is shaped by Coulomb's law instead of thermodynamic noise.

Neural ODEs for Generation

In standard neural network, each layer is discrete step: transform hidden state, pass forward. Neural ODEs (Chen et al., 2018) replace step sequence with continuous transformation described by ordinary differential equation. Instead of h₁ = f(h₀), h₂ = f(h₁), ..., define dh/dt = f(h(t), t, θ) and let numerical ODE solver evolve hidden state from time 0 to time T. Network depth becomes continuous — no layers, just smooth trajectory through state space.

For generation, this becomes Continuous Normalizing Flows (CNFs). Traditional normalizing flows need each layer invertible and must compute Jacobian determinant at each step — expensive and architecture-constraining. CNFs avoid this. ODE-defined transformation is automatically invertible if you run ODE backward, and log-density change over time depends only on Jacobian trace, not full determinant. Trace is much cheaper and loosens architecture constraints.

Trade-off: ODE solvers add computational cost, and function-evaluation count depends on trajectory complexity. Simple transformations are fast; complex ones need many solver steps. Memory story is better — adjoint method gives gradients with constant memory regardless of solver-step count, because intermediate states need not be stored.

Neural ODEs connect beautifully to score-based models. Song et al. (2021) showed diffusion can be written as stochastic differential equation (SDE), and reverse process — generation — can be cast as either SDE or probability flow ODE. ODE version produces same samples as SDE version but without stochastic noise, swapping randomness for deterministic trajectory. That probability flow ODE is continuous normalizing flow. Ideas converge.

Discrete Diffusion for Text

One nagging issue: diffusion adds Gaussian noise, then learns reversal. That makes sense for continuous data like images — pixel values are real numbers, and small noise keeps them real. Text is different. Words are discrete. You cannot add little noise to word "cat" and land between "cat" and "car." No "between" in discrete space.

Discrete diffusion models solve this by replacing Gaussian noise with discrete corruption processes. Instead of adding noise, they mask or randomly substitute tokens. Forward process gradually replaces more tokens with [MASK] or random vocabulary tokens, and reverse process learns to reconstruct original tokens from corrupted sequence.

Sounds like BERT masked language modeling because it is — but with principled probabilistic footing. D3PM (Discrete Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models) formalized this in 2021, defining discrete transition matrices that play same role as Gaussian noise schedule in continuous diffusion. MDLM (Masked Diffusion Language Models, 2024) simplified framework sharply. Training loss becomes weighted mixture of masked-language-modeling losses at different corruption levels. Weighting comes from diffusion math, but training loop looks familiar.

Practical result: MDLM reaches perplexity competitive with autoregressive language models — first for diffusion-based text generation. It also gets capability autoregressive models lack: fill tokens in any order, not only left-to-right. Useful for text infilling, constrained generation, and editing, where middle changes but beginning and end stay fixed.

Limitation: text generation from scratch still usually needs many denoising steps (10–1000), while autoregressive models generate each token in one step. Semi-autoregressive variants help — generate a few tokens in parallel, then refine — but speed gap remains. For most practical text generation, autoregressive models still win on throughput. Discrete diffusion still opens doors autoregressive models cannot.

Autoregressive Image Models: PixelCNN to VQGAN

Before diffusion took over, another image-generation lineage asked simple question: what if images were generated like text — one piece at time, each piece conditioned on everything before it?

PixelCNN (van den Oord et al., 2016) took this literally. It models probability of each pixel conditioned on all earlier pixels, using masked convolutions to preserve causality — prediction for pixel (i, j) can see only pixels above and left. Model scans image like book: left to right, top to bottom. At each position it predicts color distribution, samples, then moves on.

Results were sharp and diverse — no mode collapse. Generation speed was brutal. For 256×256 image with 3 color channels, you need about 200,000 sequential predictions. Each depends on previous ones, so no parallelization. Training was parallel via teacher forcing. Generation was painfully serial.

VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2017) changed game with one trick: do not model pixels directly. Train autoencoder with discrete bottleneck — codebook of learned vectors. Encoder maps image patches to nearest codebook entry, producing grid of discrete codes much smaller than original image. Decoder reconstructs image from codes. Then train autoregressive model like PixelCNN over codes, not pixels. Code grid might be 32×32 instead of 256×256 — 64× fewer sequential predictions.

VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021) pushed further by adding adversarial training to autoencoder. GAN loss forces decoder to make texture-realistic images, not blurry reconstructions. Codebook learns richer entries. Autoregressive prior also upgrades from PixelCNN to Transformer, which handles long-range dependencies across code grid better. This powered DALL·E 1 — tokenize images into visual "words," then generate those words with Transformer, treating image generation as sequence modeling.

Evolution is clean: PixelCNN proved autoregressive image generation works but is slow. VQ-VAE compressed problem into smaller discrete space. VQGAN made that space high-fidelity with adversarial training and paired it with Transformers. Diffusion later beat these on raw image quality, but VQ-VAE/VQGAN tokenization survived — many multimodal models still encode images as discrete tokens living in same "language" as text.

The Creative AI Tools Ecosystem

Everything in this chapter — GANs, VAEs, diffusion models, autoregressive generation — has converged into fast-moving ecosystem of creative tools reshaping how people make things. Knowing landscape matters, not because you must use every tool, but because generative-model interviews increasingly end with "...and how is this used in practice?"

For image generation, major players are Midjourney (high aesthetic quality, stylized outputs), DALL·E (OpenAI, integrated with ChatGPT), and Stable Diffusion (open-source backbone of open ecosystem). Stable Diffusion's open weights spawned fine-tuned models, LoRA adapters, ControlNet conditioning, and community-driven work on Civitai and Hugging Face. Distinction matters: Midjourney and DALL·E are walled gardens; Stable Diffusion is infrastructure you can run, modify, and build on.

For video generation, Runway (Gen-2, Gen-4) leads with creative professionals, offering AI video generation, inpainting, and motion editing. Pika and Sora (OpenAI) are newer entrants pushing toward longer, more coherent video generation. Architectures vary — diffusion in pixel space, diffusion in latent space, autoregressive methods — but user experience converges on "describe what you want, get video."

For audio and music, Suno and Udio generate full songs from text descriptions. Most use two-stage architecture: compress audio into discrete tokens with neural codec like Meta's EnCodec, then generate tokens with language model or diffusion model. ElevenLabs leads voice synthesis — voice cloning, emotionally expressive speech, real-time dubbing across languages.

Emerging pattern is multimodal pipeline: sketch concept in Midjourney, animate it in Runway, add voiceover with ElevenLabs, score it with Suno. Work that once needed studio, team, and months can now be prototyped by one person in one afternoon. Not hyperbole. Actual workflow for solo creators and small studios today.

Most interesting part of ecosystem is not any single tool. Gap between "understands generative models" and "ships creative products with them" has nearly vanished. Five years ago, using GAN meant writing training loops. Today models are infrastructure. Value shifted to knowing which model to use, how to control it, and when outputs need human judgment. Different expertise. Industry is hiring for that.
