Modern Breakthroughs in Generative AI

New model every week — DALL·E something, Midjourney v-something, another Stable Diffusion release. Easy to scroll past and think “know gist.” Problem: I couldn’t explain why one model made photorealistic faces, another nailed oil paintings, how text prompt became video, or how flat image became 3D object. Underneath, same few core ideas repeat. Worth unpacking.

The Text-to-Image Revolution

Start with thought experiment. Imagine one-person creative studio hired to make short animated film — characters, environments, soundtrack, whole thing. Five years ago, this needed team of dozens and budget in hundreds of thousands. Today, tools for each piece exist, powered by generative AI. We build understanding of whole landscape through this studio — one capability at time.

First need: still images. Concept art, backgrounds, character designs. Text-to-image revolution started whole wave in earnest.

The Three Families

By late 2022, three systems could generate photorealistic images from text: DALL·E (OpenAI), Midjourney (independent research lab), and Stable Diffusion (Stability AI / open source community). Outside view looked same — type words, get pictures. Underneath, architectures differed in important ways. Knowing those differences separates tool use from tool understanding.

DALL·E's lineage shows field in miniature. DALL·E 1 (January 2021) used discrete VAE plus autoregressive transformer — it generated tokens for image patches one at time, conditioned on text. Slow. Limited resolution. Still novel. DALL·E 2 (April 2022) switched to diffusion guided by CLIP embeddings — unCLIP. Encode text with CLIP text encoder, map embedding into CLIP image embedding space, then use diffusion to generate image conditioned on that image embedding. Intermediate CLIP embedding served as bridge between language and pixels. DALL·E 3 (September 2023) pushed further: language model similar to GPT-4 rewrote and expanded user prompts before diffusion backbone, which by then was transformer-based. Result: much better prompt adherence. Model generates what you asked for, including spatial relationships and quantities earlier systems botched.

DALL·E 3 lesson felt almost anticlimactic at first: biggest gain came from chatbot rewriting prompt before generation. Then test makes point fast. Prompt “a blue cube on top of a red sphere, with a green cylinder behind both” in DALL·E 2 versus DALL·E 3. Difference stark. Language model adds not creativity but comprehension.

Midjourney stays black box. Architecture is proprietary and undisclosed. What we know: diffusion-based, optimized hard for aesthetic quality, likely using custom training-data curation and loss functions that favor visual appeal over strict prompt fidelity. Midjourney images have distinctive “look” — dreamy, cinematic lighting, rich color palettes. Not accident. Design choice baked into training pipeline. For creative studio, Midjourney acts like art director with strong opinions: output is beautiful, exact match to mental image takes learning its language.

Stable Diffusion is open-source family. Its evolution reveals technical trajectory because changes are visible. Stable Diffusion 1.5 (2022) used U-Net in latent space — latent diffusion from Rombach et al., 2022 — plus CLIP text encoder and VAE that compressed images into smaller latent representation. Worked surprisingly well for size. Limitation: text encoder. CLIP was not designed for generation, so complex prompts often turned incoherent.

SDXL (2023) scaled up U-Net and added second, larger text encoder (OpenCLIP ViT-G) beside original CLIP model. Bigger model. Better text understanding. Higher resolution. Still U-Net. Then Stable Diffusion 3 (2024) replaced U-Net with Multimodal Diffusion Transformer (MMDiT). Pivotal shift. Instead of separate text and image pathways joined by cross-attention, MMDiT feeds both into one transformer — text tokens and image-latent tokens attend to each other in every layer. It also switched from DDPM noise schedule to flow matching, where noise-to-data path is straight line instead of curved stochastic trajectory. Straighter path means fewer generation steps and cleaner math.

FLUX (by Black Forest Labs, founded by key Stable Diffusion researchers) pushed this further in late 2024. Same core ideas — flow matching, DiT backbone — with refined architecture and larger-scale training. FLUX.1-dev became go-to open model for many practitioners.

Pattern across all three families: early systems used patchwork stacks — VAE + CLIP + U-Net + separate text encoders. Field converged toward unified transformer systems that process text and images together. Unified pipeline means freer information flow between parts. Result: more coherent output.

The Limitation

Text-to-image is remarkable. Creative-studio problem: model gives result, you take it. If character's left hand looks wrong or background has one bad element, you cannot reach in and fix it. You can re-roll generation. You cannot edit. Next need: editing.

Image Editing — Inpainting, Outpainting, and Img2Img

Studio has beautiful concept art. Problem: protagonist's eyes look off, background needs extension to left for wider composition. Starting over wastes good work. Need edit.

Diffusion denoising fits editing naturally. Core insight: generation starts from pure noise and denoises to clean image. Editing starts from mostly clean image, replaces specific regions with noise, then lets model fill only those regions.

Inpainting: Filling What's Missing

Inpainting fills masked (erased) regions of image. Input: image, binary mask showing region to regenerate, optional text prompt describing what belongs there.

Simplest inference-time method: at each denoising step, keep unmasked pixels at original values and update only masked region. Model sees surrounding context and blends new content into it. Like tearing hole in painting and asking artist to fill gap — surrounding brushstrokes guide result.

More advanced method — used by Stable Diffusion's dedicated inpainting model — trains denoiser with extra input channels. Standard model takes 4-channel latent input from VAE latent space. Inpainting model takes 9 channels: 4 for noisy latent, 4 for masked-image latent, 1 for mask. Model learns “fill this region” during training, not as inference-time hack. Result: more coherent edits.

Outpainting: Expanding the Canvas

Outpainting is inpainting's sibling. Instead of filling middle hole, it expands image past original borders. Put original on larger canvas, mark new blank areas as masked, run inpainting. Model generates scenery, patterns, or whatever fits edge context.

Studio use: practical. Generate portrait-oriented character design, then need landscape version for background plate. Outpainting extends scene without regenerating parts already good.

Img2Img: Controlled Transformation

Image-to-image (img2img) works differently. No specific mask. Take whole source image, encode into latent space, add controlled noise with strength parameter, then denoise while conditioning on new text prompt. Low strength (say, 0.3) preserves most structure — colors, composition, rough shapes — and makes subtle changes. High strength (0.8+) treats original more like rough sketch and regenerates aggressively.

Think of strength as dial between “touch up” and “reimagine.” At 0.0, original image returns unchanged. At 1.0, new generation ignores input entirely. Middle range gives useful alchemy: pencil sketch becomes photorealistic scene, daytime photo becomes moody night shot, rough 3D render becomes concept art.

Strength still trips people. More art than science. Right value depends on image, model, and prompt in ways clean formulas do not capture. Same pattern across generative AI: powerful knobs, intuition required.

ControlNet: Precise Spatial Control

Inpainting, outpainting, and img2img give broad control. Sometimes broad control is not enough. Maybe image must follow specific edge map, match human pose, or respect depth map from 3D scene.

ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) solved this cleanly. Take pre-trained diffusion model, clone encoder, feed control signal — Canny edge map, depth map, OpenPose skeleton, segmentation mask — into clone. Clone outputs are injected into original model through zero convolution layers, convolutional layers initialized with all weights at zero.

Zero initialization matters. At training start, zero convolutions output nothing, so ControlNet leaves base model unchanged. During training, model learns exact amount of spatial guidance to inject. Base-model weights stay frozen whole time. Generation quality stays intact.

For creative studio, ControlNet changes workflow. Pose character with stick-figure skeleton, model fills details. Use rough 3D blockout as depth map, get rendered environment. Visual intent goes straight into generation.

The Limitation

Still-image generation and editing now have strong control. Film needs motion. Independent frame generation fails — frames drift, flicker, feel uncanny. Need models that understand time.

From Still to Motion — Video Generation

Jump from single image to video is jump from photography to cinema. Goal is not 30 images per second. Goal is coherent story across time. Ball thrown in frame 1 must arc correctly through frame 30. Person turning head must keep same face. Lighting cannot shift randomly between frames.

This is temporal coherence. Hard problem. Image model learns statistics of natural scenes. Video model must learn scene appearance, scene change over time, and some physics behind that change. Distribution is not just images anymore. It is physically plausible spacetime volumes.

Reason temporal coherence hurts more than spatial coherence: image has strong local pixel correlations — blue sky pixel predicts nearby blue sky pixels. Video correlations span space and time, and temporal ones are longer-range and more complex. Hand reaching for cup in frame 1 may not grasp it until frame 60. Model must maintain plausible motion across whole interval. Dependency spans seconds, not pixels.

How Video Diffusion Works

Most video generation models extend image diffusion in three ways. First: temporal attention. Start with image diffusion model, add attention layers across frames at same spatial position. Interleave spatial attention within frame with temporal attention across frames. Result: sharp frames, better consistency over time.

Second approach treats video as 3D volume. Instead of 2D image patches, extract spacetime patches — cubes spanning width, height, and time. Transformer processes sequence of spacetime tokens. Sora (OpenAI, 2024) popularized this. A 10-second video becomes long sequence of 3D patches, and DiT (Diffusion Transformer) learns spatial, temporal, and cross-frame relationships between them.

Third approach is hybrid: generate few keyframes with image diffusion, then interpolate between them with separate model. Modular setup. Strong image model handles keyframes. Lighter model handles interpolation. Limitation: interpolation model still must invent plausible motion between keyframes.

The Major Players

Sora showed that scaling spacetime DiT can produce very coherent videos — up to minute long — with what looks like emergent physics understanding. Water flows. Reflections behave. Objects have weight. Model natively handles variable-length, variable-resolution video by compressing raw video with visual encoder into spacetime patches, applying huge DiT, then decoding back. OpenAI has released it slowly for safety reasons. That signals realism level.

Runway Gen-3 (and Gen-4) is maybe most production-ready tool. Cinematic quality. Strong camera control. Smooth motion. Clips run 10–16 seconds. Gen-4 added “world consistency” — characters, lighting, and objects stay coherent across multiple generated shots. For creative studio, this is workhorse: describe scene, maybe add reference image, get usable footage.

Kling (Kuaishou) pushes duration frontier. It can generate 1–2 minute clips with strong temporal coherence, keeping character identity and scene consistency much longer than most competitors. It also adds “virtual director” tools — storyboard interfaces for camera angles, pacing, and shot transitions.

Stable Video Diffusion and CogVideoX mark open-source frontier. Quality trails best closed systems. Access is open, and community improves them fast. For creative studio, they are R&D sandbox — less polished, more modifiable.

What's Still Hard

Honest state: video generation in 2025 is about where image generation stood in mid-2022. Short clips can look stunning, but failures are predictable. Hands gain or lose fingers. Objects teleport. Physics falls apart after about 10 seconds. Compute cost is huge — one-minute Sora-level video needs orders of magnitude more compute than single image.

Evaluation also remains hard. No consensus metric for video quality. Fréchet Video Distance (FVD) measures statistical similarity to real video distributions but misses temporal artifacts humans spot instantly — sixth finger for one frame, shadow jumping between frames. Humans know good video on sight. Automatic metrics still lag.

Escaping Flatland — 3D Generation

Creative studio has another problem. Concept art and short clips exist. Film still needs 3D assets — characters viewable from any angle, environments virtual camera can fly through. Traditional 3D modeling is labor-intensive. One detailed character can take weeks. Question: can generative AI help?

Core challenge drove some of smartest recent AI research. Diffusion models learned what objects look like from many angles because training data contained billions of images from billions of viewpoints. But output is still flat 2D image. Question: can that 2D understanding produce 3D objects, even without 3D training data?

Neural Radiance Fields — The 3D Foundation

Before generating 3D content, need neural-network-friendly 3D representation. Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF), introduced by Mildenhall et al. in 2020, gave exactly that. A NeRF is neural network that takes 3D position (x, y, z) and viewing direction (two angles) as input, then outputs color and density at that point. To render image from any camera angle, trace rays through scene, query NeRF at many points along each ray, then composite results.

Traditional 3D stores mesh of triangles. NeRF stores function: “given point in space and viewing direction, here is color.” Benefit: NeRF can represent complex scenes — reflections, translucency, fine detail — because neural network can learn arbitrary functions. Cost: speed. Rendering needs hundreds of network evaluations per pixel, per ray. One frame can take seconds. Too slow for real time.

3D Gaussian Splatting — The Speed Revolution

Mid-2023, 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al.) upended field. Approach differs from NeRF. Instead of continuous function, scene becomes cloud of millions of tiny 3D Gaussians — ellipsoidal blobs with position, size, orientation, color, and opacity. Rendering projects each Gaussian onto 2D image plane and blends them. No ray marching. No per-pixel network evaluations.

Result: real-time rendering at 60+ frames per second on consumer GPUs. Visual quality matches high-end NeRF methods, but rendering is hundreds of times faster.

Gaussian splatting sounds crude at first: millions of colored blobs? Yet demos make point clear. With enough Gaussians, model approximates almost any visual appearance. Each Gaussian acts like tiny soft brushstroke in 3D space. Final “painting” emerges from aggregate. Splatting itself — projecting Gaussians onto 2D plane — is GPU-friendly and well understood. Pipeline becomes extremely fast.

Studio analogy: NeRF is oil painting — slow, meticulous, beautiful if patient. Gaussian splatting is mosaic — many discrete pieces that form cohesive image and can be rearranged in real time.

Score Distillation — The Bridge from 2D to 3D

Now clever bridge. DreamFusion (Poole et al., 2022) introduced Score Distillation Sampling (SDS), letting 2D diffusion model guide 3D creation without training on 3D data.

Process: start with random 3D representation — NeRF or set of Gaussians. Render from random camera angle to get 2D image. Ask pre-trained diffusion model, “Does this rendering look like [text prompt]?” Noise prediction tells you how rendered image should change to better match prompt. Backpropagate that signal through renderer into 3D representation, nudging it from that viewpoint. Pick new random camera angle. Repeat.

After thousands of iterations across hundreds of viewpoints, 3D representation converges toward object that matches text prompt from every angle. Diffusion model is never fine-tuned. It acts as frozen critic — like art director who cannot paint but judges with extreme taste. 3D representation learns to satisfy critic from all directions at once.

Brilliant idea. Early results had telltale flaw: Janus problem. Diffusion model judges each viewpoint independently, so it tends to put recognizable features on every side — dog with face on front and back, for example. Easier to paste face everywhere than build consistent 3D structure that shows dog's face from front and dog's back from behind.

Beyond SDS

Zero-1-to-3 took different path. No SDS optimization. It fine-tuned diffusion model to generate novel views of object from single input image. Show chair from front, model generates plausible side, back, and top views. Then reconstruct 3D geometry from generated multi-view images with standard 3D reconstruction. No iterative optimization loop. No Janus problem, because model learns view consistency during training.

More recent direct 3D generation models train on real 3D datasets. Point-E and Shap-E (OpenAI) generate 3D point clouds and implicit 3D representations directly from text prompts. Faster than SDS-based methods and no 2D-to-3D optimization step. Tradeoff: they need large 3D datasets, much harder to curate than 2D image datasets.

Practical choice depends on workflow. SDS is slow but works with any text prompt and any pre-trained image model. Zero-1-to-3 is fast when reference image exists. Direct 3D models are fastest but still limited in diversity and detail. Field is moving toward large 3D-native models. 2D-to-3D distillation still matters — practical technique, and conceptual bridge that showed community what was possible.

The Sound of AI — Audio and Music Generation

Studio now has images, video clips, and 3D assets. Missing piece: soundtrack — music, sound effects, maybe narration. Here generative AI takes surprising turn. Most successful audio-generation approach does not look like diffusion. It looks like language modeling.

That sounds surprising at first. Images and video are continuous — smooth color and motion — so diffusion fits naturally. Audio is different. Raw audio is sequence of 16,000 to 48,000 samples per second. Diffusion on raw waveforms is possible but extremely expensive because sequences are huge.

The Tokenization Insight

Breakthrough came from neural audio codecs. Models like Google's SoundStream (2021) and Meta's EnCodec (2022) compress raw audio into discrete tokens — vocabulary of sound fragments, similar to tokenizer splitting text into subword units. A 10-second clip at 24kHz has 240,000 raw samples. After EnCodec compression, it may become about 750 discrete tokens from vocabulary around 1,024.

This reframes audio generation. Instead of generating 240,000 continuous values — intractable for most architectures — generate 750 discrete tokens from fixed vocabulary. Generating token sequences from vocabulary is exactly what language models do.

Best part of this approach: it collapses two different-looking problems — language generation and audio generation — into one framework. Codec acts as translator: continuous audio in, discrete tokens out. Transformer generates new token sequence. Codec decoder turns tokens back into continuous audio. Only codec “knows” audio. Generator is generic sequence model. Elegant.

AudioLM — Language Modeling for Sound

AudioLM (Google, 2022) first showed this approach could reach high quality. It uses SoundStream codec tokens and generates them autoregressively — predict next audio token from all previous tokens, like GPT predicts next word token. Model is hierarchical: first generate “semantic tokens” for high-level content — words spoken, melody played — then generate “acoustic tokens” for fine details — voice timbre, instrument texture.

Results were striking. Given few seconds of speech, AudioLM could continue same voice with coherent words and natural prosody. Given piano piece, it could continue melody in musically plausible way. No one explicitly taught grammar or music theory. Statistical patterns in audio tokens were enough.

MusicGen — Text-Conditioned Music

MusicGen (Meta, 2023) added text conditioning. Architecture: encode music with EnCodec into discrete tokens, then train transformer to generate token sequences conditioned on text description (“upbeat electronic dance music with a heavy bass line”). Transformer uses clever delay pattern for multiple parallel codec-token streams. Instead of generating all codebook levels at once or strictly one after another, it offsets them by one timestep each, letting one transformer handle multi-stream generation efficiently.

Output quality is high enough that short MusicGen clips often fool casual listeners into thinking human composed them. For creative studio, this is background-score generator — describe mood, get music.

Bark and Beyond

Bark (Suno, open-sourced in 2023) pushed text-to-audio in broader direction. It generates speech in multiple languages, plus laughter, sighs, music, and sound effects — all from text prompts, including annotations like [laughs] or [music]. Architecture uses cascade of three transformer models: text to semantic tokens, semantic tokens to coarse acoustic tokens, coarse to fine acoustic tokens. Each stage adds detail.

Broader audio-generation landscape also includes diffusion-based systems. Stable Audio (Stability AI) uses latent diffusion for music generation, treating spectrograms like Stable Diffusion treats images. Udio and Suno combine multiple techniques in commercial music-generation services. Field has not converged on one dominant approach the way image generation converged on diffusion. That keeps research active and somewhat unpredictable.

Why codec-plus-language-model works so well for audio is still interesting. Best intuition: neural codec compression is not “lossy” in naive sense. It reorganizes information into form where sequence-level patterns are easier to learn. Codec does not throw away emotion in voice or groove in beat. It distills them into sequence transformer can model.

Multimodal Generation — Putting It All Together

Studio now has tools for images, precise edits, video, 3D assets, and audio. These began as separate research threads. Frontier work is weaving them together.

Multimodal generation means one system that understands and produces multiple modalities — text, images, audio, video, 3D. Joint training can produce richer internal representations than single-modality training. Model that has seen millions of videos with audio can learn links between visual events and sound — bouncing ball and accompanying “thud” — that vision-only model never learns.

Architectural trend points one way: tokenize everything into shared vocabulary. Text is already tokens. Images become patches. Audio becomes codec tokens. Video becomes spacetime patch tokens. 3D objects become multi-view image tokens. Feed all into one large transformer. Model learns cross-modal relationships from data — sounds with images, motions with camera angles, 3D structures with 2D appearances.

Unification is not complete yet. As of 2025, most production systems remain single-modality specialists. Research prototypes are increasingly multimodal, and direction is clear. Endgame for creative studio is one model that takes script and outputs film — visuals, motion, sound, dialogue. Not there yet. Probably not close. Pieces are on table.

The Creative AI Ecosystem and What It Means

We spent whole journey building technical understanding. Now need to look up from engineering and ask what these tools mean.

Creative studio — one-person film operation using AI tools — is no longer thought experiment. People do this now. Tools exist. Tools work. Improvement rate is fast enough to unsettle even practitioners. That discomfort deserves attention.

The Copyright Question

Every model we've discussed was trained on data created by humans. Stable Diffusion was trained on billions of images scraped from the internet — photographs, illustrations, paintings, designs. MusicGen was trained on licensed music. The legal question of whether training on copyrighted material constitutes fair use is being litigated in courts worldwide as of 2025, with no clear resolution.

The practical reality is thornier still. These models can generate images "in the style of" specific living artists. They can generate music that sounds like specific genres or performers. They can mimic voices with a few seconds of sample audio. Whether this constitutes copying, transformative use, or something entirely new that our legal frameworks weren't designed for — I genuinely don't know, and I'm skeptical of anyone who claims to.

Deepfakes and Trust

Video generation that can produce realistic footage of events that never happened poses obvious risks. The detection-versus-generation arms race is already underway. The EU AI Act (2024) requires labeling of AI-generated content. The C2PA standard for content provenance is being adopted by major platforms. Whether these measures will be sufficient is an open question — the history of digital watermarking does not inspire unbounded optimism.

Creative Disruption

The film industry is already being reshaped. The 2023 Hollywood writers' and actors' strikes were partly about AI's role in creative production. Concept artists, illustrators, voice actors, and musicians are all watching these developments with a mixture of anxiety and adaptation. Some are integrating AI tools into their workflows and finding that it amplifies their productivity. Others are watching their freelance markets contract.

I don't have a neat conclusion for this section, because the situation doesn't have one. What I can say is this: understanding the technology — how these models actually work, what they can and can't do, where their outputs come from — is the prerequisite for having informed opinions about any of these questions. And that's what we've been building.
