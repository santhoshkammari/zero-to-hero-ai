Self-Supervised and Representation Learning

Chapter 6: Unsupervised Learning 10 subtopics
TL;DR

Self-supervised learning lets models create their own labels from raw data — no human annotator needed. The core trick is to hide part of the input and train the model to predict it, or to pull together different views of the same example while pushing apart different examples. This chapter traces the arc from Word2Vec embeddings through contrastive methods like SimCLR, MoCo, and BYOL, all the way to redundancy-reduction ideas like Barlow Twins. Along the way we cover classical pretext tasks, deep clustering, and how to visualize what these representations actually capture using UMAP.

Why Self-Supervised Learning?

Labeling data is expensive — can machines label themselves? Think about it: ImageNet took years and millions of dollars to annotate 14 million images. GPT-3 was trained on hundreds of billions of words from the internet, none of which came with hand-written labels. Somewhere between supervised learning (every example has a label) and unsupervised learning (no signal at all), there's a sweet spot where the data itself provides the supervision signal. That's self-supervised learning.

The idea is simple in spirit. You take unlabeled data, create a task where the answer is already embedded in the data, and train a model on that task. The model doesn't care about solving your made-up task — what matters is the representations it learns along the way. Those representations transfer to real downstream tasks with far less labeled data. It's like learning to draw by sketching still lifes: you don't care about the fruit bowl, you care about the hand-eye coordination you develop.

Contrastive Learning

Contrastive learning is the engine behind many self-supervised methods. The core principle: push representations of similar things closer together and pull representations of dissimilar things farther apart in an embedding space.

Say you have a photo of a cat. You create two augmented views of it — maybe one is cropped and the other is color-jittered. These two views form a positive pair: they should have similar embeddings because they show the same content. Every other image in your batch forms a negative pair with your cat: their embeddings should be far apart. The model learns not by classifying, but by arranging a geometry where meaning lives in distance.

This idea is old — the triplet loss used in face recognition does exactly this. What changed recently is scale: researchers discovered that with enough negatives, the right augmentations, and large enough models, contrastive learning produces representations that rival supervised pretraining on ImageNet. No labels required.

Word Embeddings: Word2Vec and GloVe

Before contrastive learning took over computer vision, the NLP world had already discovered self-supervised magic. Word2Vec (2013) showed that you could learn powerful word representations from raw text using a beautifully simple idea: words that appear in similar contexts should have similar embeddings.

CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)

The CBOW model looks at the surrounding context words and predicts the middle word. Given "the cat ___ on the mat", the model tries to predict "sat." The hidden layer weights, once training is done, become your word vectors. It's fast and works well for frequent words.

Skip-gram

Skip-gram flips the problem: given the center word, predict the context words. Given "sat," predict "the," "cat," "on," "the," "mat." This sounds harder, and it is — but it works better for rare words and small datasets because each training example generates multiple predictions. In practice, both models use negative sampling to avoid computing a softmax over the entire vocabulary (which can be hundreds of thousands of words). Instead of updating all word vectors on each step, you update only a small random sample of negative examples.

from gensim.models import Word2Vec

# Train a Skip-gram model on tokenized sentences
sentences = [["the", "cat", "sat", "on", "the", "mat"],
             ["the", "dog", "lay", "on", "the", "rug"]]

model = Word2Vec(sentences, vector_size=100, window=5,
                 min_count=1, sg=1)  # sg=1 → Skip-gram

# Words with similar contexts get similar vectors
print(model.wv.most_similar("cat"))
# → [('dog', 0.92), ('sat', 0.87), ...]

GloVe

GloVe (Global Vectors, 2014) takes a different approach. Instead of learning from local context windows, it factorizes the global word-word co-occurrence matrix. If "ice" and "steam" both co-occur frequently with "water" but have different co-occurrence patterns with "solid" and "gas," GloVe captures this in the vector arithmetic. The famous result: king - man + woman ≈ queen. GloVe works by minimizing the difference between the dot product of two word vectors and the log of their co-occurrence count, weighted so rare co-occurrences don't dominate.

Key Idea: Word2Vec and GloVe are self-supervised because the labels come free from the data itself — the surrounding words are the supervision signal. This same principle (hide something, predict it) powers modern foundation models like BERT and GPT.

Pretext Tasks

Before contrastive learning dominated, researchers invented clever pretext tasks — artificial puzzles where the answer is already in the data. You don't care about solving the puzzle; you care about the features the network learns while solving it.

Rotation Prediction

Take an image, rotate it by 0°, 90°, 180°, or 270°, and train a CNN to predict the rotation angle. To guess the rotation, the network must understand object shapes, gravity, and scene composition. That understanding transfers well to real tasks like object detection.

Jigsaw Puzzles

Chop an image into a 3×3 grid, shuffle the patches, and train the network to predict the correct arrangement. The network must learn about spatial relationships, object parts, and scene layout — all useful features for downstream vision tasks.

Inpainting

Mask out a rectangular region of an image and train the model to fill it in. This forces the network to understand textures, object boundaries, and semantic context. The model learns that if it sees grass on three sides, the missing region probably isn't sky.

Colorization

Convert a color image to grayscale and train the model to predict the original colors. This is a surprisingly effective pretext task — to colorize correctly, the model needs to recognize objects (grass is green, sky is blue, fire hydrants are red). It builds semantic features without a single label.

The weakness of pretext tasks is that they're hand-designed, and the optimal features for solving the pretext task don't always align with what you need downstream. Contrastive methods mostly replaced them because they learn more general features with less task-specific bias.

Feature Learning

Feature learning (or representation learning) is the broader goal that all these methods serve. Instead of hand-engineering features like HOG descriptors or SIFT keypoints, we want the network to discover useful features on its own. The hypothesis: a good representation should capture the factors of variation that matter for many tasks while discarding noise.

In practice, we evaluate learned features by the linear probe protocol. You freeze the pretrained network, slap a linear classifier on top of its representations, and measure accuracy on a downstream task. If a linear model can separate classes using your features, those features have captured something genuinely meaningful. The gap between the linear probe score and full fine-tuning tells you how much additional information the downstream task needs beyond what the representation already captures.

Good representations tend to be disentangled — different dimensions capture different factors (pose, color, shape). They're also invariant to things that don't matter (lighting changes, small translations) while being equivariant to things that do (large rotations that change the class label). Self-supervised methods learn these properties implicitly through their training objectives.

SimCLR: Simple Contrastive Learning of Representations

SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) took contrastive learning and stripped it to its essentials. The recipe is deceptively simple: take a batch of images, create two augmented views of each, push views of the same image together, and push views of different images apart.

How It Works

For a batch of N images, SimCLR creates 2N augmented views. For each image i, its two views are the positive pair. The remaining 2(N−1) views in the batch are negatives. The encoder (a ResNet) produces a representation h, then a small MLP projection head maps it to z. The NT-Xent (Normalized Temperature-scaled Cross-Entropy) loss computes cosine similarity between all pairs and applies a softmax with temperature τ:

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def nt_xent_loss(z1, z2, temperature=0.5):
    """NT-Xent loss for a batch of projected representations."""
    batch_size = z1.shape[0]
    z = torch.cat([z1, z2], dim=0)                    # (2N, D)
    z = F.normalize(z, dim=1)

    # Cosine similarity matrix (2N × 2N)
    sim = z @ z.T / temperature

    # Mask out self-similarity
    mask = torch.eye(2 * batch_size, dtype=torch.bool)
    sim.masked_fill_(mask, -1e9)

    # Positive pairs: (i, i+N) and (i+N, i)
    labels = torch.cat([
        torch.arange(batch_size, 2 * batch_size),
        torch.arange(0, batch_size)
    ])

    return F.cross_entropy(sim, labels)

What Made SimCLR Work

Three discoveries were critical. First, strong augmentations — especially the combination of random cropping and color jittering — matter more than architectural choices. Second, the projection head is essential: representations at the encoder output (h) transfer better than the projection output (z), because the projection head discards information useful for the pretext task but not for downstream tasks. Third, large batch sizes (4096 or 8192) provide enough negatives to make the contrastive objective informative.

Key Insight: SimCLR's reliance on large batches was its Achilles' heel. You need 128 TPU cores to train it properly. That limitation motivated the next wave of methods.

MoCo: Momentum Contrast

MoCo (He et al., 2020) asked: what if we didn't need a huge batch to get enough negatives? The answer was a queue and a momentum encoder.

SimCLR's problem is that negatives come from the current batch. Bigger batch = more negatives = better gradients. But big batches need big GPUs. MoCo decouples the number of negatives from the batch size by maintaining a queue of recent embeddings. Each new batch enqueues its embeddings and dequeues the oldest ones. Now you can have 65,536 negatives while training with a batch size of 256.

The Momentum Encoder

Here's the subtle part. If both the query encoder and the key encoder update with gradients every step, the keys in the queue come from wildly different versions of the model. That inconsistency ruins training. MoCo's fix: the key encoder updates slowly via an exponential moving average of the query encoder's weights:

# θ_k = momentum encoder weights, θ_q = query encoder weights
momentum = 0.999

for param_q, param_k in zip(query_encoder.parameters(),
                             key_encoder.parameters()):
    param_k.data = momentum * param_k.data + (1 - momentum) * param_q.data

With a momentum of 0.999, the key encoder evolves smoothly. Keys from 100 steps ago were produced by a model that's still very similar to the current one. This consistency makes the queue work. MoCo v2 later adopted SimCLR's stronger augmentations and projection head, combining the best of both worlds.

Key Insight: MoCo's momentum encoder solved the "stale keys" problem. It was the first method to show that self-supervised learning could match supervised ImageNet features — on a single 8-GPU machine.

BYOL: Bootstrap Your Own Latent

BYOL (Grill et al., 2020) dropped a bombshell: you don't need negatives at all. No negative pairs. No large batches. No queue. Just positive pairs — and it works better.

This seems like it shouldn't work. Without negatives, what stops the model from collapsing to a constant representation? If every input maps to the same vector, every pair has perfect similarity. Loss is zero. Training is done. The model has learned nothing.

Why It Doesn't Collapse

BYOL uses two networks: an online network and a target network. The online network has an extra predictor head on top that the target network lacks. The online network tries to predict the target network's output for a different view of the same image. The target network is updated with a momentum EMA (like MoCo), not gradients.

The asymmetry is what prevents collapse. The predictor head creates an information bottleneck — the online network must learn something meaningful to predict the target's output through this bottleneck. The stop-gradient on the target network means the system can't take the easy path of making both sides output the same constant. Recent theoretical work suggests that the batch normalization layers also help prevent collapse by maintaining variance in the representations.

# Simplified BYOL training step
# online_net has: encoder + projector + predictor
# target_net has: encoder + projector (EMA-updated)

view1, view2 = augment(image), augment(image)

# Online predictions
pred1 = online_net(view1)   # through encoder + projector + predictor
pred2 = online_net(view2)

# Target projections (no gradient)
with torch.no_grad():
    target1 = target_net(view1)  # through encoder + projector only
    target2 = target_net(view2)

# Symmetrized loss: predict each target from the other view
loss = cosine_loss(pred1, target2) + cosine_loss(pred2, target1)
loss.backward()

# Update target via EMA
update_momentum(online_net, target_net, momentum=0.996)

BYOL's result was stunning: it outperformed SimCLR and MoCo on ImageNet without needing any negatives, and it was more robust to smaller batch sizes. It showed that contrastive negatives are sufficient but not necessary — what matters is the asymmetry and the slow-moving target.

Barlow Twins: Redundancy Reduction

Barlow Twins (Zbontar et al., 2021) took yet another angle, inspired by neuroscience. Named after the neuroscientist Horace Barlow, who proposed that the goal of sensory processing is to produce non-redundant representations.

The idea: compute the cross-correlation matrix between the embeddings of two augmented views across a batch. Ideally, this matrix should be the identity matrix. The diagonal should be all ones (each feature in view₁ correlates perfectly with the same feature in view₂ — they capture the same invariant content). The off-diagonal should be all zeros (different features capture different information — no redundancy).

def barlow_twins_loss(z1, z2, lambd=0.005):
    """Barlow Twins loss: make cross-correlation approach identity."""
    # Normalize along batch dimension
    z1 = (z1 - z1.mean(dim=0)) / z1.std(dim=0)
    z2 = (z2 - z2.mean(dim=0)) / z2.std(dim=0)

    batch_size = z1.shape[0]
    # Cross-correlation matrix (D × D)
    c = (z1.T @ z2) / batch_size

    # Loss: push diagonal toward 1, off-diagonal toward 0
    on_diag = ((1 - c.diagonal()) ** 2).sum()
    off_diag = (c.flatten()[1:].view(c.shape[0]-1, c.shape[1]+1)[:,:-1] ** 2).sum()

    return on_diag + lambd * off_diag

Barlow Twins is elegant because it avoids negative pairs, doesn't need a momentum encoder, and has a clear theoretical motivation. The λ hyperparameter trades off between invariance (diagonal = 1) and reducing redundancy (off-diagonal = 0). In practice it's competitive with BYOL and easier to implement.

The Self-Supervised Progression: SimCLR showed contrastive learning works at scale but needs huge batches → MoCo decoupled negatives from batch size using a queue → BYOL proved negatives aren't needed at all → Barlow Twins simplified everything to a correlation objective. Each method solved a specific limitation of its predecessor.

UMAP for Visualizing Transformer Embeddings

Once you've learned representations, you want to see what they capture. Raw embeddings live in high-dimensional space (768 dimensions for BERT, 2048 for a ResNet). UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection) compresses these into 2D or 3D for visualization while preserving both local neighborhood structure and some global topology.

UMAP works by constructing a fuzzy topological graph in the high-dimensional space — connecting each point to its nearest neighbors with edge weights that decay with distance — and then finding a low-dimensional layout that preserves this graph structure. Compared to t-SNE, UMAP better preserves global relationships (cluster-to-cluster distances are more meaningful), runs faster on large datasets, and has a stronger mathematical foundation in algebraic topology.

import umap
from transformers import AutoModel, AutoTokenizer
import torch

# Extract embeddings from a pretrained transformer
tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")
model = AutoModel.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased")

texts = ["machine learning is great", "deep learning rocks",
         "the cat sat on the mat", "dogs love belly rubs"]

with torch.no_grad():
    inputs = tokenizer(texts, return_tensors="pt", padding=True)
    embeddings = model(**inputs).last_hidden_state[:, 0, :]  # [CLS] token

# Project to 2D with UMAP
reducer = umap.UMAP(n_components=2, n_neighbors=15, min_dist=0.1)
coords = reducer.fit_transform(embeddings.numpy())

# coords[:,0] and coords[:,1] are your 2D plot coordinates
# Similar sentences cluster together

In practice, UMAP plots of transformer embeddings reveal fascinating structure: semantically similar sentences form tight clusters, different languages with shared meaning overlap in multilingual models, and fine-tuning visibly reorganizes the embedding landscape to separate task-relevant categories. These visualizations are invaluable for debugging — if your positive pairs don't cluster in UMAP space, your self-supervised objective isn't working.

Deep Clustering

Self-supervised learning and clustering share a goal: discover structure without labels. Deep clustering methods combine the two by jointly learning representations and cluster assignments.

DEC (Deep Embedded Clustering)

DEC (Xie et al., 2016) starts with a pretrained autoencoder. It initializes cluster centers in the bottleneck space using k-means, then iteratively refines both the encoder and the cluster centers. The trick is a soft assignment based on Student's t-distribution — each point gets a probability distribution over clusters, and the loss (KL divergence) pushes these soft assignments toward sharper, more confident targets. DEC alternates between computing target distributions from current assignments and updating the network to match those targets.

IDEC (Improved DEC)

IDEC extends DEC by keeping the autoencoder reconstruction loss during clustering. DEC's weakness is that it can distort the embedding space to satisfy cluster assignments at the expense of data fidelity. IDEC adds a reconstruction term that says "cluster the data, but don't forget what the data looks like." This simple addition consistently improves results.

DeepCluster

DeepCluster (Caron et al., 2018) is beautifully direct. It alternates between two steps: (1) run k-means on the current network's features to get pseudo-labels, and (2) train the network to predict those pseudo-labels using standard cross-entropy. The pseudo-labels are noisy, but they improve each round as the features improve. A key challenge is avoiding trivial solutions where all points get assigned to one cluster — DeepCluster handles this by uniformly sampling from clusters during training so empty clusters don't snowball.

Deep clustering methods work best when the data has genuinely distinct groups. For more continuous or hierarchical structure, representation learning methods (SimCLR, BYOL) followed by separate clustering tend to be more flexible. The lesson: match your method to your data's actual structure.

Putting It All Together

Self-supervised learning is not one technique — it's a philosophy: extract supervision from the data itself. Word2Vec predicts context words. Pretext tasks predict rotations and colors. Contrastive methods align augmented views. BYOL bootstraps a target from itself. Barlow Twins decorrelate features. All of them produce representations that transfer to downstream tasks with minimal labeled data.

The practical takeaway is clear. If you have lots of unlabeled data and little labeled data — which describes most real-world problems — pretrain with self-supervised methods first, then fine-tune with your labels. This is exactly what powers modern foundation models: BERT pretrains by predicting masked words, GPT pretrains by predicting the next token, CLIP aligns images and text descriptions. Self-supervised learning isn't an alternative to supervised learning. It's the foundation that makes supervised learning work with less data, less cost, and more generalization.
