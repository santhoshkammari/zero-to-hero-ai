Dimensionality Reduction

Chapter 6: Unsupervised Learning 8 subtopics
TL;DR

Your data has 500 features — most are redundant or noisy. Dimensionality reduction finds a smaller set of variables that keeps the essential signal. PCA rotates your coordinate system so the first few axes capture maximum variance. Kernel PCA handles curved structures. t-SNE and UMAP project high-dimensional data into 2D maps you can actually look at. LDA uses class labels to find the most discriminating directions. ICA separates mixed signals into independent sources. Autoencoders learn nonlinear compressions with neural networks. Each method trades off fidelity, speed, and interpretability differently — knowing which to reach for depends on whether you're exploring, visualizing, or feeding features into a downstream model.

Why Reduce Dimensions?

Your data has 500 features — most are redundant or noisy. Maybe 300 of those columns are slight variations of each other: "total income" and "annual salary" and "monthly pay × 12" all carry roughly the same information. Another 150 are noise that crept in from sensors, user-input errors, or features someone thought might matter but don't. The remaining 50 carry the real signal. Dimensionality reduction is the art of finding those 50 (or 10, or 2) directions that matter and throwing away the rest.

Why bother? Three concrete reasons. First, speed. Training a model on 50 features is drastically faster than training on 500 — think factor of 10 or more for distance-based algorithms like k-NN. Second, overfitting. With 500 features and 1,000 rows, your model has plenty of room to memorize noise. Cut down to 50 features and it's forced to learn real patterns. Third, visualization. Humans can see 2D and maybe fake 3D. If you want to actually look at your data — spot clusters, find outliers, check if classes separate — you need to project it onto a flat surface. That's what dimensionality reduction gives you.

There's also a deeper mathematical reason called the curse of dimensionality. In high-dimensional space, data points spread out so thin that distances stop being meaningful. Every point looks roughly equally far from every other point. Algorithms that rely on distance — clustering, nearest neighbors, kernel methods — start to break. Reducing dimensions concentrates your data back into a region where distances carry information again.

Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

Principal Component Analysis is the workhorse of dimensionality reduction. It's been around since 1901 (Karl Pearson) and it's still the first thing you should try. The idea is beautifully simple: find the directions in your data that have the most variance, then project onto those directions.

The Intuition

Imagine a cloud of data points in 3D space. The cloud isn't a perfect sphere — it's elongated, like a cigar. Most of the spread is along the long axis. If you had to describe each point with just one number, you'd use its position along that long axis. That axis is the first principal component. The second principal component is the direction of maximum remaining variance, perpendicular to the first. And so on.

PCA is a rotation. It doesn't warp or stretch your data — it just finds a new coordinate system where the first axis captures the most variance, the second captures the next most, and each subsequent axis captures less and less. Then you drop the later axes (the ones with tiny variance) because they're mostly noise.

Covariance Matrix and Eigendecomposition

Here's the mechanics. Start with your data matrix X of shape (n_samples, n_features). First, center it: subtract the mean of each feature so everything is centered at zero. Then compute the covariance matrix C = (1/(n-1)) Xᵀ X. This is a p×p matrix where entry C[i,j] tells you how much feature i and feature j vary together. If C[i,j] is large and positive, when feature i goes up, feature j tends to go up too. If it's near zero, they're unrelated.

The principal components are the eigenvectors of this covariance matrix, and the amount of variance each captures is its corresponding eigenvalue. Eigendecomposition factors C = VΛVᵀ, where V is a matrix whose columns are the eigenvectors (the new axes) and Λ is a diagonal matrix of eigenvalues (how much variance each axis captures). Sort by eigenvalue descending, keep the top k eigenvectors, and you've got your k principal components.

SVD: The Practical Path

In practice, nobody computes the covariance matrix explicitly. For large datasets it's expensive (p×p can be huge) and numerically unstable. Instead, we use Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) directly on the centered data matrix. SVD factors X = UΣVᵀ, where the columns of V are exactly the principal component directions and the singular values in Σ relate to the eigenvalues by λᵢ = σᵢ²/(n-1). This is what scikit-learn does under the hood.

SVD is faster and more numerically stable, especially when n_features > n_samples. The Randomized PCA variant goes even further: it uses randomized linear algebra to approximate the top k singular vectors without computing the full decomposition. For a million-feature dataset where you only need 100 components, this can be orders of magnitude faster.

Explained Variance and the Scree Plot

How many components do you keep? The explained variance ratio for component k is its eigenvalue divided by the sum of all eigenvalues. If the first component explains 40% of variance, the second 25%, the third 15%, and the rest trail off, then three components capture 80% of the total information. That might be enough.

A scree plot graphs the explained variance of each component in order. You're looking for an "elbow" — the point where the curve flattens out. Components before the elbow carry signal; components after it carry noise. The Kaiser criterion is a simpler rule: keep components with eigenvalues greater than 1 (when working with the correlation matrix). The logic is that a component should explain at least as much variance as a single original feature to be worth keeping.

import numpy as np
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.datasets import load_digits

# Load handwritten digits: 64 features (8×8 pixel images)
X, y = load_digits(return_X_y=True)
print(f"Original shape: {X.shape}")        # (1797, 64)

# Always scale before PCA
X_scaled = StandardScaler().fit_transform(X)

# Fit PCA keeping all components to inspect variance
pca_full = PCA().fit(X_scaled)
cumvar = np.cumsum(pca_full.explained_variance_ratio_)
n_95 = np.argmax(cumvar >= 0.95) + 1
print(f"Components for 95% variance: {n_95}")  # ~28

# Now fit with chosen number of components
pca = PCA(n_components=n_95)
X_reduced = pca.fit_transform(X_scaled)
print(f"Reduced shape: {X_reduced.shape}")  # (1797, 28)

# Reconstruction error
X_reconstructed = pca.inverse_transform(X_reduced)
mse = np.mean((X_scaled - X_reconstructed) ** 2)
print(f"Reconstruction MSE: {mse:.4f}")

PCA Variants for Special Cases

Incremental PCA processes data in mini-batches. If your dataset doesn't fit in memory — say 10 million rows — you feed it chunk by chunk and the algorithm updates its estimate of the components. The result closely approximates full PCA without requiring all data in RAM at once.

Sparse PCA adds an L1 penalty so that each component is a linear combination of only a few original features rather than all of them. This makes components interpretable: instead of "0.03×feature_1 + 0.02×feature_2 + ... + 0.01×feature_500," you get "0.8×feature_12 + 0.6×feature_47." You can name those components. The tradeoff is that the components are no longer orthogonal and capture slightly less variance.

Probabilistic PCA frames PCA as a generative model: each data point is generated from a low-dimensional latent variable plus Gaussian noise. This gives you a proper likelihood function, so you can do model selection (compare different numbers of components using BIC), handle missing data, and even generate new samples. It's PCA with a probabilistic hat on.

PCA ≈ Linear Autoencoder

Here's a fact that connects PCA to neural networks: a single-layer autoencoder with linear activations and a mean squared error loss learns exactly the same subspace as PCA. The encoder learns a projection down to k dimensions, the decoder learns a projection back up, and the reconstruction loss pushes them to capture maximum variance. Add nonlinear activations and you get something strictly more powerful — but the linear case and PCA are mathematically equivalent. This makes PCA the theoretical baseline for all learned compressions.

Kernel PCA

Standard PCA finds linear directions. But what if your data lives on a curved surface? Imagine data arranged in a Swiss roll: in 3D it's a flat sheet rolled up. PCA will find three straight axes, none of which "unroll" the sheet. You need a method that can find nonlinear structure.

Kernel PCA applies the kernel trick: instead of computing the covariance matrix in the original feature space, it computes it in a high-dimensional (possibly infinite-dimensional) feature space induced by a kernel function. The kernel function K(xᵢ, xⱼ) computes the inner product between transformed versions of xᵢ and xⱼ without ever explicitly computing the transformation. Common choices are the RBF (Gaussian) kernel for smooth curved manifolds and the polynomial kernel for polynomial relationships.

The practical catch is the pre-image problem. With standard PCA, you can easily go from reduced space back to the original space (inverse_transform). With Kernel PCA, the reduced coordinates live in the kernel feature space, and there's no exact way to map them back to the original input space. Approximate pre-image methods exist but they're iterative and imperfect. This makes Kernel PCA better for analysis and visualization than for creating features you plan to reconstruct.

Kernel selection matters. RBF has a bandwidth parameter γ — too small and everything looks similar (useless), too large and each point is an island (overfitting). Cross-validation or reconstruction error on downstream tasks guides the choice. In practice, if your data isn't clearly nonlinear, stick with regular PCA. Kernel PCA is the scalpel for when the linear approach visibly fails.

t-SNE: Seeing High-Dimensional Structure

t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) is a visualization algorithm. Say that again: visualization. It produces beautiful 2D maps where clusters leap off the screen. But it's not suitable for creating features for downstream models. It distorts distances, the axes are meaningless, and running it twice on the same data gives different pictures. With those caveats understood, it's incredibly useful for exploration.

How It Works

Step one: in the original high-dimensional space, compute pairwise similarities using a Gaussian kernel. For each point, compute the probability that it would pick each other point as a neighbor (closer points get higher probability). This creates a probability distribution over pairs in high-D.

Step two: place all points randomly in 2D. Compute pairwise similarities there too, but using a Student's t-distribution with one degree of freedom instead of a Gaussian. This is the key innovation.

Step three: minimize the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the high-D distribution and the low-D distribution using gradient descent. KL divergence measures how different the two distributions are. The optimization moves points around in 2D until nearby points in high-D are also nearby in 2D.

Why the t-Distribution? The Crowding Problem

The crowding problem is why you can't just use Gaussians in both spaces. In high dimensions, a single point can have many equidistant neighbors (think of the corners of a 100-dimensional hypercube). But in 2D, space is limited — you can only pack so many points around a center before they start overlapping with other clusters. The t-distribution has heavier tails than the Gaussian, which means moderately distant points in high-D are mapped to much more distant points in low-D. This creates the breathing room needed to pull clusters apart.

Perplexity

The perplexity parameter (typically 5–50) controls how many effective neighbors each point considers. Low perplexity means each point only cares about its closest few neighbors — you'll see tight, possibly fragmented clusters. High perplexity means each point considers a wider neighborhood — the map shows broader, more global structure. There's no single right answer. Best practice: run t-SNE at several perplexity values (5, 30, 50) and see which tells the most coherent story. If a cluster appears at all perplexity values, it's probably real.

Practical Details

The Barnes-Hut approximation reduces t-SNE's complexity from O(n²) to O(n log n) by approximating interactions between distant points, making it feasible for datasets up to ~100k points. For larger datasets, use UMAP instead.

Critical rules for interpreting t-SNE: cluster sizes are meaningless (the algorithm can expand or shrink clusters arbitrarily). Distances between clusters are meaningless (two well-separated clusters might be very similar in high-D). Only the existence and relative composition of clusters is reliable information. And never use t-SNE coordinates as features for a classifier. The embedding is optimized for visual appearance, not predictive utility.

from sklearn.manifold import TSNE
from sklearn.datasets import load_digits
import numpy as np

X, y = load_digits(return_X_y=True)

# t-SNE to 2D — note: perplexity and random_state matter
tsne = TSNE(
    n_components=2,
    perplexity=30,        # try 5, 30, 50 and compare
    learning_rate='auto',
    init='pca',           # PCA init for reproducibility
    random_state=42
)
X_2d = tsne.fit_transform(X)
print(f"Embedded shape: {X_2d.shape}")  # (1797, 2)

# Each row of X_2d is now a 2D coordinate for visualization
# Plot with: plt.scatter(X_2d[:, 0], X_2d[:, 1], c=y, cmap='tab10', s=5)

UMAP: The Modern Alternative

Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) emerged in 2018 and quickly became the go-to alternative to t-SNE. It produces similar-quality visualizations but is dramatically faster, scales to millions of points, preserves more global structure, and — unlike t-SNE — the embeddings can be used for downstream tasks.

Topological Foundation

UMAP rests on ideas from topology and category theory. Don't let that intimidate you — the practical intuition is straightforward. UMAP assumes your high-dimensional data lies on a manifold (a curved surface) and tries to find a low-dimensional representation that preserves the manifold's structure.

It models the data as a fuzzy simplicial set — think of it as a weighted graph where each point is connected to its neighbors. The weight of each edge represents how "connected" two points are. In high-D, UMAP builds this graph using the n_neighbors nearest neighbors for each point. In low-D, it builds another graph. Then it optimizes the low-D layout so the two graphs match, using cross-entropy as the cost function rather than t-SNE's KL divergence.

Key Parameters

n_neighbors is UMAP's equivalent of perplexity. Small values (5–15) emphasize local structure — you'll see very fine-grained clusters. Large values (50–200) capture more global structure — you'll see how clusters relate to each other. This parameter is the single most important knob.

min_dist controls how tightly UMAP is allowed to pack points together. A min_dist of 0.0 creates very dense clusters with clear separation. A min_dist of 0.5 spreads points out, preserving more of the continuous structure. For cluster identification, use small min_dist. For understanding the topology of your data, use larger values.

UMAP vs t-SNE

UMAP preserves more global structure. In t-SNE, the distances between clusters are arbitrary. In UMAP, if cluster A is far from cluster B in high-D, they'll tend to be far apart in the embedding too. UMAP is also deterministic given the same random seed and runs in O(n) time for the graph construction plus O(n) for the optimization (vs O(n log n) for Barnes-Hut t-SNE). For a million points, UMAP finishes in minutes while t-SNE would take hours.

UMAP also supports supervised mode: you can pass labels to the algorithm, and it will adjust the embedding so that labeled points of the same class are pulled closer together. This is useful for semi-supervised exploration — you have labels for some points and want to see if the unlabeled points cluster similarly.

# pip install umap-learn
import umap
from sklearn.datasets import load_digits
import numpy as np

X, y = load_digits(return_X_y=True)

# Standard UMAP for visualization
reducer = umap.UMAP(
    n_neighbors=15,
    min_dist=0.1,
    n_components=2,
    random_state=42
)
X_umap = reducer.fit_transform(X)
print(f"UMAP shape: {X_umap.shape}")  # (1797, 2)

# Supervised UMAP — uses labels to guide the embedding
reducer_sup = umap.UMAP(
    n_neighbors=15,
    min_dist=0.1,
    n_components=2,
    random_state=42
)
X_umap_sup = reducer_sup.fit_transform(X, y=y)

# UMAP for feature engineering (higher dims, use in pipeline)
reducer_feat = umap.UMAP(n_components=10, random_state=42)
X_features = reducer_feat.fit_transform(X)
print(f"Feature shape: {X_features.shape}")  # (1797, 10)

Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA)

Every method so far has been unsupervised — it ignores class labels. Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) flips this on its head: it's a supervised dimensionality reduction technique that uses labels to find the most discriminating directions.

Fisher's Criterion

The goal is to find a projection that maximizes the separation between classes while minimizing the spread within each class. Formally, LDA maximizes Fisher's criterion: the ratio of between-class scatter to within-class scatter.

Between-class scatter S_B measures how far apart the class means are from the overall mean. Within-class scatter S_W measures how spread out the points are within each class. LDA finds the directions w that maximize wᵀ S_B w / wᵀ S_W w. This is a generalized eigenvalue problem: S_B w = λ S_W w.

Practical Limits

With C classes, LDA gives you at most C−1 components. For a binary classification problem, that's just one dimension — a single number that maximally separates the two classes. For 10-class digit recognition, you get at most 9 LDA components. That's a hard cap, regardless of how many original features you have.

LDA assumes each class is Gaussian with equal covariance matrices. When these assumptions hold, LDA is optimal. When they don't — when classes have very different shapes or the boundaries are curved — LDA can underperform. It's still a solid baseline for classification-oriented dimensionality reduction, especially when you want interpretable directions that separate classes.

Independent Component Analysis (ICA)

Independent Component Analysis (ICA) solves a different problem than PCA. PCA finds uncorrelated directions. ICA finds statistically independent sources. The difference is subtle but important: uncorrelated means the covariance is zero. Independent means knowing one source tells you absolutely nothing about any other source. Independence is a much stronger condition.

The Cocktail Party Problem

The classic motivation: you're at a cocktail party with three people talking simultaneously. Three microphones at different positions in the room each record a different mix of the three voices. You hear mic1 = 0.7×voice_A + 0.2×voice_B + 0.1×voice_C, and similar weighted mixes on the other two mics. ICA takes the three mixed signals and recovers the original three voices. This is blind source separation — "blind" because you don't know the mixing coefficients in advance.

How It Works

ICA's key assumption is that the original sources are non-Gaussian. The Central Limit Theorem tells us that a mix of independent signals tends toward Gaussian. So ICA works backward: it finds the directions that maximize non-Gaussianity, as measured by kurtosis or negentropy. The most non-Gaussian direction recovers an original source.

The FastICA algorithm is the standard implementation. It uses a fixed-point iteration scheme that's much faster than gradient descent. You pre-whiten the data (make it uncorrelated with unit variance using PCA), then iteratively find the directions of maximum non-Gaussianity, deflating the data after each source is found.

ICA is used heavily in EEG/MEG analysis (separating brain signals from eye-movement artifacts), audio processing, and financial data analysis (finding independent market factors). It's not typically used for general-purpose dimensionality reduction because it requires the number of sources to equal the number of observed signals, and the sources must be non-Gaussian.

Autoencoders: Learned Compression

An autoencoder is a neural network trained to reconstruct its own input. That sounds useless — why would you predict what you already have? The trick is the bottleneck.

Encoder-Decoder Architecture

The network has two halves. The encoder maps the input x (say, 784 pixels for an MNIST digit) down to a low-dimensional bottleneck layer z (say, 32 neurons). The decoder maps z back up to the original dimensionality to produce a reconstruction x̂. The training loss is the reconstruction error: typically mean squared error ||x − x̂||² or binary cross-entropy for pixel values.

Because the bottleneck is smaller than the input, the network can't just memorize. It's forced to learn a compressed representation that captures the most important features. The bottleneck activations z are your dimensionality-reduced features.

Variants

Denoising autoencoders intentionally corrupt the input (adding noise, zeroing out random pixels) and train the network to reconstruct the clean original. This forces the network to learn robust features rather than copying pixel values through. The bottleneck learns to capture the underlying structure, not the noise.

Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) add a probabilistic spin. Instead of encoding to a fixed vector z, the encoder outputs the mean and variance of a Gaussian distribution. You sample z from that distribution. The loss adds a KL divergence term that keeps the latent distribution close to a standard normal. The payoff: you can sample new z values from the normal distribution and decode them to generate entirely new data points. VAEs are generative models disguised as dimensionality reducers.

Sparse autoencoders add a penalty that encourages most neurons in the bottleneck to be inactive for any given input. This means each data point is represented by only a few active features — like a sparse code. Contractive autoencoders add a penalty on the Jacobian of the encoder, making the learned representation insensitive to small changes in the input. Both variants learn more robust features than a plain autoencoder.

The honest tradeoff with autoencoders: they can capture nonlinear structure that PCA misses, but they require choosing an architecture, training with gradient descent (which is noisy and slow), and they're black boxes. PCA gives you an eigenvalue you can interpret. An autoencoder gives you 32 numbers you can't. Use autoencoders when linear methods visibly fail and you have enough data to train the network properly.

Other Methods Worth Knowing

The landscape of dimensionality reduction is vast. Here are the remaining methods you'll encounter, with honest assessments of when to use each.

Multidimensional Scaling (MDS)

MDS takes a distance matrix (pairwise distances between all points) and finds a low-dimensional embedding that preserves those distances as faithfully as possible. Classical MDS is equivalent to PCA when the distances are Euclidean. The value of MDS is that it works with any distance matrix — you can compute custom domain-specific distances and embed based on those.

Isomap

Isomap extends MDS by using geodesic distances instead of straight-line distances. It builds a k-nearest-neighbor graph, computes shortest paths through the graph (approximating distances along the manifold surface), then applies MDS to those geodesic distances. This unrolls the Swiss roll that PCA and MDS cannot. The weakness: it's sensitive to the neighborhood graph. If the graph has a "short circuit" — a connection that cuts across the manifold rather than following its surface — the embedding will be distorted.

Locally Linear Embedding (LLE)

LLE takes a different approach. For each point, it finds the weights that best reconstruct that point as a linear combination of its neighbors. Then it finds low-dimensional coordinates that preserve those same reconstruction weights. The result captures local linear structure without computing pairwise distances. LLE handles manifolds with varying density better than Isomap but struggles with holes in the manifold.

Factor Analysis

Factor Analysis is PCA's probabilistic cousin. It models observed variables as linear combinations of latent factors plus per-variable noise with different variances for each variable. Unlike PCA (which treats all residual variance the same), Factor Analysis separates shared variance (captured by the factors) from unique variance (noise specific to each variable). It's popular in psychology and social sciences for identifying latent constructs like "intelligence" or "extraversion" from test batteries.

Non-Negative Matrix Factorization (NMF)

NMF decomposes a non-negative matrix X into two non-negative matrices: X ≈ WH. The non-negativity constraint makes the parts additive — each component represents a "part" that is present to some degree. For face images, NMF learns parts like "nose," "left eye," "mouth." For text (term-frequency matrices), NMF discovers topics. The constraint makes NMF components much more interpretable than PCA components, which can have negative values and represent subtractive combinations.

Random Projections

Random Projections are the laziest method — and sometimes the smartest. The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma guarantees that projecting n points to O(log n / ε²) dimensions using a random matrix preserves all pairwise distances within a factor of (1±ε). No eigendecomposition, no optimization, no parameters — just multiply by a random matrix. For very high-dimensional, very large datasets where you need a quick-and-dirty reduction before applying a more expensive algorithm, random projections are unbeatable.

Laplacian Eigenmaps

Laplacian Eigenmaps build a graph where edges connect nearby points, weight those edges by similarity (Gaussian kernel), compute the graph Laplacian, and find the eigenvectors corresponding to the smallest non-zero eigenvalues. These eigenvectors give a smooth low-dimensional embedding that respects the manifold geometry. Laplacian Eigenmaps are the foundation for spectral clustering and connect dimensionality reduction to graph theory.

Choosing a Method: A Decision Framework

Key Idea — Matching Method to Purpose

For feature engineering before a model: Start with PCA. If class labels exist, try LDA. If nonlinear structure is evident, try a UMAP embedding with more than 2 components. Autoencoders for complex data like images or sequences.

For visualization: UMAP first (faster, preserves global structure). t-SNE if you want a second opinion. Always try multiple hyperparameter settings. Never trust a single plot.

For interpretability: Sparse PCA or NMF. The components will be sparse and nameable.

For signal processing: ICA when you believe independent sources are mixed together.

For speed on massive data: Random Projections → PCA → UMAP. Avoid Isomap, LLE, Kernel PCA on more than ~50k points.

One more piece of practical advice: dimensionality reduction is often a preprocessing step, not a final answer. The right evaluation is downstream performance. Run PCA, train your classifier, measure accuracy. Run UMAP, train your classifier, measure accuracy. The method that improves your downstream metric wins, regardless of how elegant its mathematics is.
