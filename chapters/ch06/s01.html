Clustering

Chapter 6: Unsupervised Learning 6 subtopics
TL;DR

Clustering finds natural groups in data when you have no labels. k-Means partitions data into k spherical clusters by iterating centroid assignments — fast and simple, but you must choose k and it struggles with non-spherical shapes. DBSCAN discovers arbitrarily-shaped clusters by chasing density, handling noise automatically. Hierarchical clustering builds a merge tree (dendrogram) that lets you explore groupings at every resolution. Spectral clustering rewires the problem as a graph cut, unlocking non-convex clusters. Gaussian Mixture Models assign soft, probabilistic memberships via the EM algorithm. No single method wins everywhere — evaluation metrics like silhouette score, ARI, and NMI help you compare.

You Have 10,000 Customers and No Labels

Imagine this. Your company has 10,000 customers. You know their purchase history, browsing patterns, how often they return, and what they spend. Marketing asks: "What are the natural customer segments?" Nobody has labeled these customers as "bargain hunters" or "loyal high-spenders." There are no categories in the database. You have features, but no target column.

This is the core problem of clustering: discovering groups in data without being told what the groups are. In supervised learning, someone hands you labeled examples and says "learn this mapping." In clustering, you stare at a cloud of points and ask the data itself, "are there natural groupings here?" The answer depends entirely on what you mean by "natural" — and different clustering algorithms encode different definitions of what a group is.

We'll start with the simplest and most popular approach, then progressively remove its assumptions until we arrive at methods that handle nearly anything.

k-Means Clustering

The Idea: Partition by Nearest Centroid

The intuition behind k-Means is almost childishly simple. Pick k points as initial "centers" (called centroids). Assign every data point to the nearest centroid. Recompute each centroid as the average of all points assigned to it. Repeat until nothing changes. That's it. The algorithm converges because each step either reduces total error or leaves it unchanged, and there are finitely many possible assignments.

Lloyd's Algorithm

The standard implementation is called Lloyd's algorithm, and it has exactly two alternating steps:

Assignment step: For each data point xᵢ, find the centroid μⱼ that minimizes the squared Euclidean distance ‖xᵢ − μⱼ‖². Assign xᵢ to cluster j.

Update step: For each cluster j, recompute μⱼ as the mean of all points currently assigned to it.

# Lloyd's algorithm — the core loop
# 1. Assign each point to closest centroid
# 2. Recompute centroids as cluster means
# 3. Repeat until assignments stabilize

assignments = assign_to_nearest(X, centroids)
new_centroids = compute_means(X, assignments)
# Continue until convergence

Convergence is guaranteed because the objective — the within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS), also called inertia — decreases monotonically:

WCSS = Σⱼ Σᵢ∈Cⱼ ‖xᵢ - μⱼ‖²

Each assignment step reduces WCSS (or keeps it the same) because you're moving each point to its closest centroid. Each update step reduces WCSS because the mean minimizes squared distances within a group. Since WCSS is bounded below by zero and decreases every iteration, the algorithm must converge. In practice, it usually converges in 10–30 iterations.

The Random Initialization Problem

Here's the catch. Lloyd's algorithm converges to a local minimum, not necessarily the global one. Where you start matters enormously. Pick bad initial centroids and the algorithm gets stuck in a terrible partition where one cluster is enormous and another has three points.

The naive fix is to run k-Means multiple times with different random seeds and keep the run with the lowest WCSS. Scikit-learn does this by default (n_init=10). But there's a smarter approach.

k-Means++ Initialization

k-Means++ is an initialization scheme that spreads the initial centroids apart. The first centroid is chosen uniformly at random from the data. Each subsequent centroid is chosen with probability proportional to D(x)² — the squared distance from each point to its nearest existing centroid. Points that are far from all current centroids are more likely to be picked next. This is called D² weighting.

Why does this work? It ensures the initial centroids aren't all clumped in one region. The theoretical guarantee is strong: k-Means++ produces an initial clustering that is O(log k) competitive with the optimal solution. In practice, it often gives results close to the best possible even before a single Lloyd iteration runs. Scikit-learn uses k-Means++ by default.

Mini-Batch k-Means

Standard k-Means scans every data point on every iteration. With a million points, that's slow. Mini-Batch k-Means randomly samples a small batch (say 1,000 points) per iteration and updates centroids using only that batch. The result is noisier per step but dramatically faster overall. The final clustering is typically within 1–2% of the full k-Means solution at a fraction of the cost. Use it when your dataset is too large for the standard version.

Choosing k: Elbow, Silhouette, and Gap Statistic

k-Means requires you to specify the number of clusters upfront. This is the most common complaint. Three methods help:

The elbow method: plot WCSS against k for k = 1, 2, 3, ... The curve always decreases (more clusters means less intra-cluster distance), but at some point the improvement flattens. The "elbow" in the plot is your candidate k. The problem? Real data often has no clear elbow. The curve just bends gradually.

The silhouette score: for each point, measure how similar it is to its own cluster (cohesion, a) versus the nearest other cluster (separation, b). The silhouette coefficient is (b − a) / max(a, b), ranging from −1 (wrong cluster) to +1 (perfectly clustered). Average this over all points for a given k. Pick the k with the highest average silhouette score.

The gap statistic: compare the WCSS of your clustering to the WCSS you'd expect from a uniformly random dataset with no cluster structure. The "gap" measures how much better your clustering is than random. The k where this gap is largest (accounting for standard error) is the best choice. It's more principled than the elbow but computationally expensive because it requires generating reference datasets.

import numpy as np
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans, MiniBatchKMeans
from sklearn.metrics import silhouette_score
from sklearn.datasets import make_blobs

# Generate sample data: 3 natural clusters
X, y_true = make_blobs(n_samples=1000, centers=3,
                        cluster_std=1.0, random_state=42)

# --- Standard k-Means with k-Means++ ---
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=3, init='k-means++', n_init=10,
                random_state=42)
labels = kmeans.fit_predict(X)
print(f"Inertia (WCSS): {kmeans.inertia_:.1f}")
print(f"Silhouette score: {silhouette_score(X, labels):.3f}")

# --- Mini-Batch k-Means for large datasets ---
mb_kmeans = MiniBatchKMeans(n_clusters=3, batch_size=100,
                            random_state=42)
mb_labels = mb_kmeans.fit_predict(X)
print(f"Mini-Batch inertia: {mb_kmeans.inertia_:.1f}")

# --- Elbow method: try k = 2 to 8 ---
inertias = []
sil_scores = []
for k in range(2, 9):
    km = KMeans(n_clusters=k, n_init=10, random_state=42)
    km.fit(X)
    inertias.append(km.inertia_)
    sil_scores.append(silhouette_score(X, km.labels_))
    print(f"k={k}: inertia={km.inertia_:.0f}, "
          f"silhouette={silhouette_score(X, km.labels_):.3f}")
# Look for the elbow in inertias and peak in sil_scores

Limitations of k-Means

k-Means assumes clusters are spherical, roughly equal-sized, and equally dense. These assumptions are baked into the objective: minimizing squared Euclidean distance naturally produces Voronoi cells — convex regions with straight boundaries. If your clusters are elongated, crescent-shaped, or wildly different in size, k-Means will force them into round blobs and give you garbage.

It's also sensitive to outliers. A single extreme point can drag a centroid far from where it should be. And it only works with numerical features — you can't compute the mean of categorical values like "red," "blue," "green."

K-Modes and K-Prototypes

For categorical data, K-Modes replaces the mean with the mode (most frequent value) and uses a simple matching dissimilarity instead of Euclidean distance. For mixed data — some columns numerical, some categorical — K-Prototypes combines both approaches, using means for numeric features and modes for categorical ones. These are essential when your customer data has columns like "subscription tier" or "preferred category" alongside spending amounts.

DBSCAN: Density-Based Clustering

Why Density?

What if your clusters aren't blobs? Think of two interlocking half-moons, a ring surrounding a dense core, or any shape where "closeness to a center" is the wrong definition of membership. You need a different idea: clusters are dense regions separated by sparse regions. That's the insight behind DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise).

Core Points, Border Points, and Noise

DBSCAN classifies every point into one of three roles based on two parameters: eps (ε, the neighborhood radius) and MinPts (the minimum number of points within that radius).

A core point has at least MinPts neighbors within distance eps. It's in the thick of a cluster. A border point is within eps of a core point but doesn't have MinPts neighbors itself — it's on the edge of a cluster. A noise point (outlier) is neither core nor border — it's isolated, belonging to no cluster.

The algorithm works by picking an unvisited core point, expanding its neighborhood recursively (any core neighbor's neighbors are also reachable), and marking everything reachable as one cluster. Then it picks another unvisited core point and repeats. Border points get assigned to whatever cluster their nearest core point belongs to. Noise points are left unlabeled.

Choosing eps and MinPts

The k-distance graph helps choose eps. For each point, compute the distance to its k-th nearest neighbor (where k = MinPts). Sort these distances and plot them. You'll see a curve that rises sharply at some point — the "elbow." Points below the elbow are in dense regions; points above are in sparse regions or noise. Set eps at the elbow. For MinPts, a common rule of thumb is MinPts ≥ dimensionality + 1, often starting with MinPts = 5 for 2D data.

Why DBSCAN Shines

DBSCAN discovers clusters of arbitrary shape. It doesn't need you to specify the number of clusters. And it has a built-in concept of noise — outliers are automatically identified, not forced into the nearest cluster like k-Means does. For spatial data, geographic clustering, or any problem with irregular cluster shapes, DBSCAN is often the first thing to try.

Honest Tradeoffs

DBSCAN struggles with datasets where different regions have vastly different densities. A single eps can't capture both a tight urban cluster and a spread-out rural cluster. It also doesn't work well in very high-dimensional spaces because distances become meaningless (the curse of dimensionality). And it can be sensitive to the choice of eps and MinPts.

from sklearn.cluster import DBSCAN
from sklearn.datasets import make_moons
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.metrics import silhouette_score

# Two interlocking half-moons — k-Means fails here
X_moons, y_moons = make_moons(n_samples=500, noise=0.08,
                               random_state=42)
X_scaled = StandardScaler().fit_transform(X_moons)

# --- DBSCAN ---
db = DBSCAN(eps=0.3, min_samples=5)
db_labels = db.fit_predict(X_scaled)

n_clusters = len(set(db_labels)) - (1 if -1 in db_labels else 0)
n_noise = list(db_labels).count(-1)
print(f"Clusters found: {n_clusters}")
print(f"Noise points: {n_noise}")
# Only compute silhouette if we found more than 1 cluster
if n_clusters > 1:
    mask = db_labels != -1  # Exclude noise
    print(f"Silhouette: {silhouette_score(X_scaled[mask], db_labels[mask]):.3f}")

# --- Compare: k-Means on the same moons data ---
km_moons = KMeans(n_clusters=2, random_state=42)
km_labels = km_moons.fit_predict(X_scaled)
print(f"k-Means silhouette on moons: "
      f"{silhouette_score(X_scaled, km_labels):.3f}")
# k-Means splits the moons vertically — wrong!

OPTICS and HDBSCAN

OPTICS (Ordering Points To Identify the Clustering Structure) extends DBSCAN by computing a reachability distance for each point, producing a reachability plot that reveals cluster structure at multiple density levels. You don't need to fix eps — you analyze the plot afterwards.

HDBSCAN (Hierarchical DBSCAN) takes this further. It runs DBSCAN across all possible eps values, builds a hierarchy of clusters, and uses a stability measure to pick the most persistent clusters. It handles varying densities gracefully, automatically determines the number of clusters, and is the go-to density-based method in practice. If you're choosing between DBSCAN and HDBSCAN for a real project, choose HDBSCAN — it's strictly more flexible.

Hierarchical Clustering

Building a Tree of Merges

Hierarchical clustering doesn't give you a single partition. It gives you a tree — a nested hierarchy of clusters that you can cut at any level to get as many or as few groups as you want. Think of it like a company org chart, but for data points.

There are two approaches. Agglomerative (bottom-up) starts with each point as its own cluster and repeatedly merges the two closest clusters. Divisive (top-down) starts with everything in one cluster and repeatedly splits. Agglomerative is far more common because it's simpler to implement and faster in practice.

Agglomerative Linkage Methods

The critical question is: when you have two candidate clusters to merge, how do you measure the "distance" between them? This choice is called the linkage criterion, and it dramatically affects the result.

Single linkage: the distance between two clusters is the minimum distance between any pair of points, one from each cluster. This produces elongated, chain-like clusters. It's good at finding non-convex shapes but is very sensitive to noise — a single bridge point between two groups merges them prematurely. This is called the chaining effect.

Complete linkage: use the maximum distance between any pair of points. This produces compact, roughly equal-sized clusters. It's the opposite of single linkage — resistant to chaining but forces spherical shapes.

Average linkage (UPGMA): use the average distance between all pairs of points across the two clusters. A compromise between single and complete. Robust in practice.

Ward's method: merge the two clusters that result in the smallest increase in total within-cluster variance (WCSS). This is conceptually similar to k-Means — it produces compact, spherical clusters and is the most commonly used linkage for general-purpose clustering.

Divisive Methods

DIANA (DIvisive ANAlysis) starts with all data in one cluster and iteratively splits the most heterogeneous cluster. It selects the cluster with the largest diameter, finds the most dissimilar point, and builds a "splinter group" around it. Bisecting k-Means takes a simpler approach: at each step, pick the largest (or highest-WCSS) cluster and run k-Means with k=2 on it. Repeat until you have the desired number of clusters. Bisecting k-Means is faster than agglomerative methods on large datasets and often produces better-balanced clusters.

The Dendrogram

The merge history is visualized as a dendrogram — a tree diagram where the y-axis shows the distance at which each merge happened. Tall vertical bars represent merges between distant clusters; short bars represent merges between nearby points. You choose the number of clusters by drawing a horizontal line across the dendrogram and counting how many vertical lines it crosses.

The cophenetic correlation measures how faithfully the dendrogram preserves the original pairwise distances. A value close to 1 means the tree is a good representation of the actual distance structure. If it's low, the hierarchical representation is distorting your data, and you should try a different linkage or a different method entirely.

from sklearn.cluster import AgglomerativeClustering
from sklearn.datasets import make_blobs
from scipy.cluster.hierarchy import dendrogram, linkage
from sklearn.metrics import silhouette_score
import numpy as np

X, y_true = make_blobs(n_samples=200, centers=4,
                        cluster_std=0.8, random_state=42)

# --- Agglomerative clustering with Ward linkage ---
agg = AgglomerativeClustering(n_clusters=4, linkage='ward')
agg_labels = agg.fit_predict(X)
print(f"Ward linkage silhouette: "
      f"{silhouette_score(X, agg_labels):.3f}")

# --- Compare linkage methods ---
for link in ['ward', 'complete', 'average', 'single']:
    model = AgglomerativeClustering(n_clusters=4, linkage=link)
    labels = model.fit_predict(X)
    score = silhouette_score(X, labels)
    print(f"  {link:10s} → silhouette = {score:.3f}")

# --- Dendrogram using scipy ---
Z = linkage(X, method='ward')
# To plot: dendrogram(Z, truncate_mode='level', p=4)
# Cut at different levels to explore cluster counts
print(f"
Linkage matrix shape: {Z.shape}")
print(f"Last 3 merges (distance):")
for row in Z[-3:]:
    print(f"  Merged clusters of size {int(row[3])}, "
          f"distance = {row[2]:.2f}")

Scalable Variants: BIRCH and CURE

BIRCH (Balanced Iterative Reducing and Clustering using Hierarchies) is designed for very large datasets. It builds a compact tree structure called a CF-tree (Clustering Feature tree) in a single pass over the data, summarizing each sub-cluster with just three numbers: the count, the linear sum, and the squared sum of its points. This lets it handle millions of points without loading everything into memory. After building the CF-tree, you apply a standard clustering algorithm to the leaf summaries.

CURE (Clustering Using REpresentatives) represents each cluster with a fixed number of scattered representative points (not just a centroid), then shrinks those representatives toward the cluster center by a fraction. This lets it handle non-spherical and unequal-sized clusters much better than centroid-based methods, while still being more scalable than naive agglomerative clustering.

Spectral Clustering

From Points to Graphs

Here's a different way to think about clustering. Instead of measuring distances between points and centroids, build a graph. Each data point is a node. Connect nearby points with edges weighted by their similarity. Now the clustering problem becomes a graph-cutting problem: partition the graph into groups so that edges within groups have high weight (similar points stay together) and edges between groups have low weight (dissimilar points are separated).

This is spectral clustering. The word "spectral" comes from the spectrum (eigenvalues) of a matrix derived from the graph.

How It Works

Step 1: Build the affinity matrix W. For each pair of points, compute a similarity — often a Gaussian (RBF) kernel: W_ij = exp(−‖xᵢ − xⱼ‖² / 2σ²). Points that are close get high similarity; distant points get near-zero similarity.

Step 2: Compute the graph Laplacian. The unnormalized Laplacian is L = D − W, where D is a diagonal matrix with Dᵢᵢ = Σⱼ Wᵢⱼ (the degree of each node). The normalized variant, used in practice, is L_sym = D^{−1/2} L D^{−1/2}.

Step 3: Compute the eigendecomposition of L. Take the k smallest eigenvalues and their corresponding eigenvectors. Stack these k eigenvectors as columns to form an n × k matrix. Each row of this matrix is a new, low-dimensional representation of the corresponding data point — a spectral embedding.

Step 4: Run k-Means on the embedded rows. In this new space, even non-convex clusters become well-separated blobs that k-Means can handle cleanly.

Normalized Cut (Ncut)

The mathematical foundation is the normalized cut criterion. A naive graph cut minimizes the total edge weight between partitions, but this tends to cut off isolated nodes. Ncut normalizes by the total edge weight within each partition, encouraging balanced groups. The relaxed solution to the Ncut problem is exactly the eigenvectors of the normalized Laplacian. That's the deep reason spectral clustering works — it's solving an optimization problem that balances compactness and separation.

When to Use Spectral Clustering

Spectral clustering excels when clusters are non-convex — concentric circles, interleaving spirals, or any shape where "distance to center" is meaningless. The cost is computational: computing the full affinity matrix is O(n²) in space and the eigendecomposition is expensive. For datasets beyond about 10,000 points, you'll need approximations (like Nyström methods or sparse affinity matrices). For small-to-medium datasets with complex geometry, spectral clustering is often the best choice.

Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs)

Soft Assignments: Probability Instead of Hard Labels

k-Means makes a hard decision: every point belongs to exactly one cluster. But what about a point sitting right between two groups? k-Means assigns it to one and ignores the ambiguity. Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) fix this by assigning each point a probability of belonging to each cluster. A point might be 70% cluster A and 30% cluster B. These are called soft assignments.

A GMM models the data as being generated from a mixture of k Gaussian (normal) distributions. Each Gaussian component has its own mean μₖ, covariance matrix Σₖ, and a mixing weight πₖ (the prior probability that a random point came from component k). The overall density is:

p(x) = Σₖ πₖ · N(x | μₖ, Σₖ)

where N(x | μ, Σ) is the multivariate Gaussian density
and Σₖ πₖ = 1 (mixing weights sum to one)

EM Algorithm for GMMs

We can't directly optimize the log-likelihood of a mixture model — it contains a sum inside a log, which doesn't simplify. The Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm solves this by alternating between two steps:

E-step (Expectation): Compute the responsibilities — the posterior probability that each point came from each component, given current parameters. For point xᵢ and component k:

r_ik = πₖ · N(xᵢ | μₖ, Σₖ) / Σⱼ πⱼ · N(xᵢ | μⱼ, Σⱼ)

This is just Bayes' theorem. If component k explains point xᵢ well (high likelihood) and has a large mixing weight, it gets a high responsibility.

M-step (Maximization): Update the parameters using the responsibilities as soft counts. The new mean of component k is the responsibility-weighted average of all points. The new covariance is the responsibility-weighted covariance. The new mixing weight is the average responsibility for that component.

EM for GMMs is k-Means' more sophisticated cousin. In fact, if you force all covariances to be σ²I (spherical, equal) and take responsibilities to be hard (0 or 1), EM reduces exactly to Lloyd's algorithm. k-Means is a special case of GMMs.

Covariance Types

The shape of each cluster is controlled by the covariance matrix. Scikit-learn offers four options:

Full: each component has its own unconstrained covariance matrix. Clusters can be ellipsoids of any orientation and size. Most flexible, but requires the most data to estimate well (O(d²) parameters per component).

Tied: all components share the same covariance matrix. The clusters have the same shape and orientation but different centers. Useful when you believe the groups have similar spread.

Diagonal: each component's covariance is diagonal — features are assumed independent within each cluster. Clusters are axis-aligned ellipsoids. Far fewer parameters than "full."

Spherical: each component's covariance is σₖ²I — a single variance parameter per component. Clusters are spheres (like k-Means but with different sizes). The most constrained.

Model Selection: BIC and AIC

How many components should your GMM have? Unlike k-Means where you eyeball an elbow, GMMs have a principled approach. The Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) and Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) both balance model fit against complexity:

BIC = -2 · log L + p · log(n)
AIC = -2 · log L + 2p

where L = maximized likelihood, p = number of parameters, n = number of data points

BIC penalizes complexity more heavily than AIC (especially with large n), so it tends to select simpler models. Fit GMMs with k = 1, 2, ..., 10 and pick the k with the lowest BIC. This is far more reliable than the elbow method.

Bayesian GMM

A Bayesian GMM (also called a Dirichlet Process Gaussian Mixture) places a Dirichlet process prior on the mixing weights. In practice, you set a large upper bound on the number of components, and the model automatically shrinks the unnecessary ones to near-zero weight. It effectively learns the number of components from the data. The trade-off: it's slower and introduces hyperparameters (the concentration parameter), but it removes the need for explicit model selection via BIC.

from sklearn.mixture import GaussianMixture, BayesianGaussianMixture
from sklearn.datasets import make_blobs
from sklearn.metrics import silhouette_score
import numpy as np

X, y_true = make_blobs(n_samples=600, centers=3,
                        cluster_std=[1.0, 1.5, 0.5],
                        random_state=42)

# --- Standard GMM ---
gmm = GaussianMixture(n_components=3, covariance_type='full',
                       random_state=42)
gmm.fit(X)
gmm_labels = gmm.predict(X)
gmm_probs = gmm.predict_proba(X)  # Soft assignments!

print(f"GMM converged: {gmm.converged_}")
print(f"Log-likelihood: {gmm.score(X):.2f}")
print(f"BIC: {gmm.bic(X):.1f}")
print(f"Silhouette: {silhouette_score(X, gmm_labels):.3f}")
print(f"
Sample soft assignments (first 3 points):")
for i in range(3):
    probs = ', '.join(f'{p:.2f}' for p in gmm_probs[i])
    print(f"  Point {i}: [{probs}] → cluster {gmm_labels[i]}")

# --- Model selection via BIC ---
print("
BIC for different k:")
for k in range(1, 8):
    gm = GaussianMixture(n_components=k, random_state=42)
    gm.fit(X)
    print(f"  k={k}: BIC={gm.bic(X):.1f}, AIC={gm.aic(X):.1f}")

# --- Bayesian GMM: automatic component selection ---
bgmm = BayesianGaussianMixture(n_components=10,  # Upper bound
                                 random_state=42)
bgmm.fit(X)
# Effective number of components (weights > 0.01)
active = np.sum(bgmm.weights_ > 0.01)
print(f"
Bayesian GMM: {active} active components "
      f"(out of 10 max)")
print(f"Weights: {np.round(bgmm.weights_, 3)}")

Cluster Evaluation Metrics

The Evaluation Problem

How do you know your clustering is good? In supervised learning, you compare predictions to labels. In clustering, there usually are no labels. This creates a fundamental tension: you need to evaluate without ground truth. Metrics split into two categories: internal metrics (computed from the data alone) and external metrics (computed against known labels, when you have them for validation).

Internal Metrics: No Labels Needed

The silhouette score is the most widely used internal metric. For each point i, compute a(i) = average distance to other points in the same cluster, and b(i) = average distance to points in the nearest other cluster. The silhouette coefficient is:

s(i) = (b(i) - a(i)) / max(a(i), b(i))

Range: -1 to +1
  +1: point is far from neighboring clusters (good)
   0: point is on the boundary between clusters
  -1: point is probably in the wrong cluster

Average over all points for an overall score. Above 0.5 is reasonable; above 0.7 is strong structure.

The Davies-Bouldin Index measures the average "worst-case" similarity between each cluster and its most similar neighbor. For each cluster i, find the cluster j that maximizes (within-scatter_i + within-scatter_j) / between-center_distance_ij. Average these over all clusters. Lower is better. A value of 0 means clusters are infinitely separated. It's fast to compute but biased toward convex clusters.

The Calinski-Harabasz Index (also called the Variance Ratio Criterion) is the ratio of between-cluster dispersion to within-cluster dispersion, adjusted for degrees of freedom. Higher is better. It essentially asks: are the cluster centers far apart relative to how spread out each cluster is? It's fast and works well for convex, well-separated clusters.

External Metrics: When You Have Ground Truth

Sometimes you do have labels — in benchmarks, validation sets, or when clustering known categories to test your method. External metrics compare your clustering assignments to these labels.

The Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) counts pairs of points. Two points can be in the same cluster and the same true class (true positive), in the same cluster but different classes (false positive), and so on. The Rand Index is the fraction of pairs correctly classified. The "adjusted" version corrects for chance — a random clustering gets an ARI of 0, a perfect match gets 1. Negative values mean worse than random.

The Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) measures the mutual information between the clustering and the true labels, normalized to fall between 0 and 1. It answers: how much does knowing the cluster assignment reduce your uncertainty about the true label? NMI = 1 means the clustering perfectly reconstructs the true labels (up to permutation). NMI = 0 means the clustering is independent of the true labels.

Homogeneity, Completeness, and V-Measure decompose the evaluation further. Homogeneity asks: does each cluster contain only members of a single class? Completeness asks: are all members of a class assigned to the same cluster? V-Measure is their harmonic mean — the clustering equivalent of the F1 score.

Internal vs. External: A Practical Guide

Use internal metrics when choosing k, tuning hyperparameters, or comparing algorithms on unlabeled data. Use external metrics when validating against known structure or benchmarking. Be cautious: internal metrics are biased toward the assumptions of the clustering algorithm. Silhouette favors compact, well-separated clusters — exactly what k-Means produces. It will happily give k-Means a higher silhouette score than DBSCAN on data where DBSCAN found the correct non-convex clusters. Always visualize in addition to computing scores.

Stability-based evaluation is an underappreciated technique. Cluster your data multiple times with different random subsets or perturbations. If the same clusters keep appearing, they're probably real. If the clustering changes dramatically with small perturbations, the structure is fragile and your "clusters" might be artifacts. This is especially useful when internal metrics are ambiguous.

Choosing the Right Algorithm: A Decision Framework

Start with k-Means when you have a rough idea of k and expect roughly spherical clusters. It's fast, scalable, and easy to explain. Switch to DBSCAN or HDBSCAN when you don't know the number of clusters, suspect non-convex shapes, or have noise/outliers. Use hierarchical clustering when you want to explore the data at multiple resolutions or need an interpretable tree structure. Try spectral clustering when you know the clusters are non-convex and the dataset is small enough (under ~10k points). Choose GMMs when you want soft assignments, when clusters have different shapes or sizes, or when you need a generative model. In practice, run two or three methods, compare their results visually and via metrics, and pick the one that makes the most domain sense.

No clustering algorithm is universally best. Each one encodes a different definition of "cluster." k-Means says a cluster is a set of points closest to a shared center. DBSCAN says it's a dense region. Hierarchical methods say it's whatever the merge tree reveals. GMMs say it's a Gaussian distribution. The right algorithm depends on what kind of structure you expect — and the best way to find out is to try multiple approaches and see which one tells a story that makes sense for your data.
