Association Rules

Chapter 6: Unsupervised Learning 3 subtopics
TL;DR

Association rule mining discovers relationships between items in transactional data — "customers who buy X also buy Y." Support measures how often an itemset appears, confidence measures conditional probability, and lift tells you whether the co-occurrence is meaningful or just a coincidence. The Apriori algorithm uses the anti-monotone property to efficiently prune the search space. FP-Growth avoids candidate generation entirely by compressing transactions into a tree. These techniques power market basket analysis, recommendation systems, and web usage mining.

Customers Who Buy Diapers Also Buy Beer

There's a famous (possibly apocryphal) story from the early days of data mining. A retail chain analyzed millions of point-of-sale transactions and discovered that customers who bought diapers on Friday evenings also tended to buy beer. The explanation? New fathers, sent to the store for diapers, grabbed a six-pack while they were at it. The store moved the beer display closer to the diapers, and sales of both went up.

Whether or not that specific story is true, the technique behind it is very real. Association rule mining finds patterns like "if a customer buys item A, they're likely to also buy item B" from transactional data. It doesn't need labels. It doesn't need a target variable. It just looks at what items appear together and quantifies how strong the relationship is.

This is different from clustering (which groups similar items or customers) and different from classification (which predicts a label). Association rules answer a fundamentally different question: given that something happened, what else is likely to happen alongside it?

Key Concepts

Itemsets and Transactions

The data is a collection of transactions. Each transaction is a set of items. In a grocery store, a transaction is a receipt: {bread, milk, butter}. In web mining, a transaction might be the set of pages a user visited in one session: {homepage, products, checkout}. The complete set of possible items is the item universe. Your goal is to find subsets that appear together suspiciously often.

An itemset is any subset of items: {bread}, {bread, milk}, {bread, milk, eggs}. A k-itemset contains k items. The number of possible itemsets explodes combinatorially — if you have 1,000 products, there are 2¹⁰⁰⁰ possible subsets. You can't check them all. That's where clever algorithms come in.

Support

Support measures how frequently an itemset appears in the dataset. If you have 10,000 transactions and {bread, milk} appears in 800 of them, the support is 800/10,000 = 0.08, or 8%. Support answers: "how common is this combination?" Low-support itemsets are rare — maybe interesting, maybe noise. High-support itemsets are common patterns.

Support({bread, milk}) = Count(transactions containing both bread AND milk)
                         ÷ Total number of transactions

You set a minimum support threshold to filter out rare combinations. If min_support = 0.01, you ignore any itemset appearing in fewer than 1% of transactions. This drastically reduces the search space — most item combinations are rare.

Confidence

Confidence measures the conditional probability of the consequent given the antecedent. For the rule {bread} → {milk}, confidence is the fraction of bread-buying transactions that also include milk.

Confidence({bread} → {milk}) = Support({bread, milk})
                                ÷ Support({bread})

If 40% of transactions contain bread, and 8% contain both bread and milk, then confidence = 0.08 / 0.40 = 0.20. Twenty percent of bread buyers also buy milk. Confidence looks like a useful metric — but it has a fatal flaw. If 25% of all customers buy milk regardless, then bread buyers are actually less likely to buy milk than average. Confidence doesn't account for the baseline popularity of the consequent.

Lift

This is where lift saves you. Lift measures whether the antecedent and consequent co-occur more often than you'd expect if they were independent.

Lift({bread} → {milk}) = Confidence({bread} → {milk})
                          ÷ Support({milk})

Lift = 1 means the items are independent — knowing someone bought bread tells you nothing about whether they'll buy milk. Lift > 1 means positive association — bread buyers are more likely to buy milk than random customers. Lift < 1 means negative association — bread buyers are actually less likely to buy milk. In our example, lift = 0.20 / 0.25 = 0.80 — bread and milk are negatively associated, despite what the raw confidence suggested.

Lift is symmetric: the lift of {bread} → {milk} equals the lift of {milk} → {bread}. This makes sense — the degree of association between two items doesn't depend on which one you condition on.

Conviction

Conviction measures the degree to which the antecedent and consequent are associated, with a focus on how often the rule would be wrong if the items were independent. It's defined as:

Conviction({A} → {B}) = (1 - Support(B)) / (1 - Confidence(A → B))

A conviction of 1.0 means independence. Higher values mean stronger association. Unlike lift, conviction is asymmetric — the direction of the rule matters. Conviction of ∞ means the rule is never violated (the consequent always appears when the antecedent does). It's less commonly used than lift but gives a more nuanced view of rule strength, especially for rules with high confidence.

Algorithms

Apriori Algorithm

Apriori was the first practical algorithm for association rule mining, published by Agrawal and Srikant in 1994. It solves the combinatorial explosion problem with one elegant observation: the anti-monotone property (also called the downward closure property).

The property states: if an itemset is infrequent, then all of its supersets are also infrequent. If {bread, milk} appears in only 0.5% of transactions (below your minimum support), then {bread, milk, eggs} must appear in ≤ 0.5% too — because every transaction containing all three items also contains the first two. You don't need to check {bread, milk, eggs} at all.

Apriori works level by level. First, count all individual items and keep those above minimum support. These are frequent 1-itemsets. Then, generate candidate 2-itemsets by combining pairs of frequent 1-itemsets. Scan the data to count their support. Keep those above the threshold. Generate candidate 3-itemsets from frequent 2-itemsets. Repeat until no more frequent itemsets are found.

The pruning is aggressive. If 1,000 products exist but only 100 are individually frequent, the candidate 2-itemsets drop from ~500,000 to ~5,000. Each level eliminates more candidates, making the search tractable.

import pandas as pd
from mlxtend.preprocessing import TransactionEncoder
from mlxtend.frequent_patterns import apriori, association_rules

# Sample transactions
transactions = [
    ['bread', 'milk', 'eggs'],
    ['bread', 'butter', 'eggs'],
    ['milk', 'butter'],
    ['bread', 'milk', 'butter', 'eggs'],
    ['bread', 'milk'],
    ['butter', 'eggs'],
    ['milk', 'bread', 'butter'],
    ['bread', 'eggs'],
    ['milk', 'eggs', 'butter'],
    ['bread', 'milk', 'eggs', 'butter'],
]

# Encode transactions into a boolean DataFrame
te = TransactionEncoder()
te_array = te.fit(transactions).transform(transactions)
df = pd.DataFrame(te_array, columns=te.columns_)

# Find frequent itemsets with min support of 30%
frequent_itemsets = apriori(df, min_support=0.3, use_colnames=True)
print("Frequent itemsets:")
print(frequent_itemsets.sort_values('support', ascending=False))

# Generate rules with min confidence of 60%
rules = association_rules(frequent_itemsets,
                          metric="confidence",
                          min_threshold=0.6)
rules_sorted = rules.sort_values('lift', ascending=False)
print("
Top rules by lift:")
print(rules_sorted[['antecedents', 'consequents',
                     'support', 'confidence', 'lift']].head(10))

The main limitation of Apriori is performance. It requires multiple passes over the data — one per itemset level. For large transaction databases with long transactions, this gets slow. Each pass involves candidate generation, which creates potentially large intermediate sets in memory. That's why FP-Growth was invented.

FP-Growth

FP-Growth (Frequent Pattern Growth) eliminates candidate generation entirely. It compresses the transaction database into a compact data structure called an FP-tree, then mines frequent patterns directly from the tree.

Building the FP-tree requires two passes over the data. First pass: count item frequencies and discard infrequent items. Sort the remaining items by frequency. Second pass: insert each transaction into the tree, with items ordered by frequency. Transactions that share a common prefix share the same nodes in the tree, which provides enormous compression. A database of millions of transactions might compress into a tree that fits in memory.

Mining works by constructing conditional pattern bases — projections of the tree for each item — and recursively finding frequent patterns within those projections. No candidate generation, no repeated database scans. For dense datasets with many frequent items, FP-Growth can be orders of magnitude faster than Apriori.

The tradeoff: FP-Growth uses more memory for the tree structure. For very sparse datasets (where most items are infrequent), Apriori's pruning is so effective that it may actually be faster. In practice, FP-Growth is the default choice for modern implementations.

Eclat (Vertical Data Format)

Eclat (Equivalence Class Transformation) takes yet another approach. Instead of storing data horizontally (each row is a transaction listing its items), it stores data vertically: for each item, list the transaction IDs that contain it. This is called a tidset (transaction ID set).

To find the support of {bread, milk}, just intersect the tidsets of bread and milk. The size of the intersection is the support count. No database scans needed — just set intersections, which are fast on modern hardware. Eclat uses depth-first search through the itemset lattice, which uses less memory than Apriori's breadth-first approach.

Eclat is simple to implement and fast for small to medium datasets. For very large datasets where tidsets don't fit in memory, it becomes impractical. It's a good teaching algorithm and works well in practice when the data fits in RAM.

Applications

Market Basket Analysis

The original and most famous application. Retailers analyze point-of-sale data to discover which products are frequently purchased together. The results drive store layout (put associated products near each other), promotions (buy bread, get 20% off butter), and inventory management (if bread is selling fast, stock more butter too).

In practice, the raw rules need domain filtering. Rules like {salt} → {pepper} are obvious and unactionable. You want surprising, actionable rules — combinations that aren't obvious but have high lift. A good analyst filters by lift > 1.5, minimum support > 0.01, and then applies business judgment.

Recommendation Systems

Association rules power simple but effective recommendation engines. "Customers who bought this item also bought..." is literally a set of association rules filtered by the current item in the cart. The recommendations are the consequents of rules where the antecedent matches items the customer has already selected.

This is cruder than collaborative filtering or matrix factorization, but it's interpretable, fast at serving time (precompute the rules, look them up at query time), and requires no user profiles — just transaction data. For e-commerce with many anonymous visitors, this can be more practical than user-based methods.

Web Usage Mining

Replace "items" with "web pages" and "transactions" with "user sessions." Association rules reveal navigation patterns: users who visit the FAQ page also tend to visit the contact page (maybe the FAQ isn't answering their question). Users who read three blog posts tend to visit the pricing page (content marketing is working). These patterns inform site redesign, content strategy, and conversion optimization.

⚡ Apriori vs. FP-Growth: When to Use Which

Apriori: Simpler to understand and implement. Works well for sparse datasets with high minimum support thresholds. Good for teaching and small-scale analysis.

FP-Growth: Faster for dense datasets and low support thresholds. No candidate generation means less memory pressure from intermediate candidates. The default choice for production systems.

Eclat: Fast set intersections on vertical data. Good middle ground when data fits in memory. Often overlooked but effective.
