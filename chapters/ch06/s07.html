Topic Modeling

Chapter 6: Unsupervised Learning 6 subtopics
TL;DR

Topic modeling automatically discovers the hidden themes running through a collection of documents. Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is the classic approach — it models each document as a mixture of topics, and each topic as a mixture of words, using Dirichlet priors to keep things sparse. NMF offers a simpler, linear-algebra alternative. Modern approaches like BERTopic replace bag-of-words with transformer embeddings and clustering, producing sharper topics with less preprocessing. Evaluation is tricky: perplexity measures statistical fit, coherence measures human interpretability, and the two often disagree. The right method depends on your corpus size, whether you need interpretability, and how much preprocessing you're willing to do.

Why Topic Modeling?

You have 100,000 documents — what are they about? You could read them all, but that would take years. You could search for keywords, but you'd need to know the keywords in advance, and real themes don't reduce to single words. "Machine learning" might appear in a document about healthcare, finance, or art. The word alone doesn't tell you what the document is really about.

Topic modeling solves this by automatically discovering the recurring themes — called topics — in a collection of documents. Each topic is a distribution over words. A topic about sports might place high probability on "game," "player," "score," and "team." A topic about cooking might favor "recipe," "heat," "flour," and "butter." The algorithm figures out these clusters of co-occurring words without any labels or supervision. You just hand it the raw text.

The output is two things. First, for each topic, you get a ranked list of words — so you can look at the top 10 words and say, "ah, this topic is about politics." Second, for each document, you get a mixture of topics — so a newspaper article might be 60% politics, 30% economics, and 10% international relations. That mixture is the document's fingerprint, and it's useful for search, clustering, recommendation, and trend analysis.

Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA)

The Generative Story

LDA is a generative model — it tells a story about how your documents were "written" by an imaginary random process. Nobody believes documents are actually created this way, but the story defines a probability distribution, and fitting that distribution to real data recovers useful topics.

Here's the story. Imagine you're about to write a document. Before you type a single word, you first decide the mix of topics this document will cover. Maybe 40% machine learning, 35% statistics, 25% programming. This mixture is drawn from a Dirichlet distribution — a distribution over distributions that favors sparse mixtures (most documents are about a few topics, not all of them).

Now you write each word one at a time. For each word position, you first pick a topic according to your document's mixture. Say you picked "machine learning." Then you pick a word from that topic's word distribution — maybe "gradient" or "neural" or "loss." You repeat this for every word in the document. That's it. The whole model.

More formally, for a corpus of M documents with K topics:

# LDA Generative Process (pseudocode)
# 1. For each topic k = 1..K:
#      Draw word distribution β_k ~ Dirichlet(η)
#
# 2. For each document d = 1..M:
#      a. Draw topic mixture  θ_d ~ Dirichlet(α)
#      b. For each word position n = 1..N_d:
#           i.  Draw topic assignment  z_dn ~ Categorical(θ_d)
#           ii. Draw word              w_dn ~ Categorical(β_{z_dn})

θ (Doc-Topic) and β (Topic-Word) Distributions

Two matrices hold everything that matters. θ (theta) is the document-topic matrix: each row is a document, each column is a topic, and the entries are probabilities that sum to 1 across topics. If θ[d][k] = 0.4, then 40% of document d's words come from topic k. β (beta) is the topic-word matrix: each row is a topic, each column is a word in the vocabulary, and the entries are probabilities that sum to 1 across words. If β[k]["neural"] = 0.05, then 5% of the words generated by topic k are "neural."

These two matrices together let you do everything. Want to know what a document is about? Look at its row in θ. Want to know what a topic means? Look at its row in β and sort by probability. Want to find similar documents? Compare their θ vectors using cosine similarity or KL divergence.

Dirichlet Priors

The Dirichlet distribution is the secret ingredient that makes LDA work sensibly. It's a distribution over probability vectors — vectors whose entries are non-negative and sum to 1. The concentration parameter α controls sparsity. When α is small (say 0.1), the Dirichlet produces sparse vectors — most entries are near zero, with one or two entries dominating. This encodes a reasonable prior: most documents are about a small number of topics. When α is large (say 10), the Dirichlet produces uniform-ish vectors — every topic gets roughly equal weight. Similarly, the parameter η controls sparsity of the topic-word distributions. Small η means each topic uses a focused vocabulary.

Key Idea — Why Dirichlet?

The Dirichlet distribution is the conjugate prior of the Categorical distribution. This means that when you observe categorical data (word choices) and update your beliefs about the parameters (topic mixtures), the posterior is still a Dirichlet. This mathematical convenience makes inference tractable. Without conjugacy, updating beliefs after each observation would require intractable integrals.

Plate Notation

Probabilistic models get messy when you write out every variable for every document and every word. Plate notation is a compact diagram that uses rectangles (plates) to denote repetition. A plate labeled M means "repeat everything inside for each of the M documents."

Inference: Variational Inference and Gibbs Sampling

The generative story tells us how to generate documents. But we have real documents and need to go backwards — infer the hidden topics. This is posterior inference: given the observed words w, find the posterior distribution over the latent variables θ, z, and β. The exact posterior is intractable (it requires summing over all possible topic assignments for every word in every document), so we use approximations.

Variational Inference (VI) turns inference into optimization. You posit a simpler, factored distribution q(θ, z, β) and minimize the KL divergence between q and the true posterior. This is equivalent to maximizing a quantity called the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO). The original LDA paper by Blei, Ng, and Jordan (2003) used this approach. It's fast and scales well to large corpora, especially in the online variant (Online LDA) that processes documents in mini-batches.

Collapsed Gibbs Sampling is the other main approach. It integrates out θ and β analytically (thanks to Dirichlet-Categorical conjugacy) and samples only the topic assignments z. For each word in the corpus, it asks: given all the other topic assignments, what topic should this word belong to? It cycles through every word, resampling its topic, and after enough iterations the samples approximate the posterior. Gibbs is simpler to implement and often produces better topics than VI, but it's slower because it's inherently sequential.

Choosing K (Number of Topics)

LDA requires you to specify K, the number of topics, in advance. There's no free lunch here. Too few topics and each one becomes an incoherent grab-bag. Too many and you get redundant, overlapping themes. Practical approaches: run LDA for K = 5, 10, 15, 20, 30, 50 and compute coherence scores (see Evaluation below) for each. Plot coherence vs. K and look for an elbow or plateau. Also examine the topics qualitatively — are the top words interpretable? Do any two topics look like duplicates? Ultimately, the "right" K depends on your use case. A broad survey might need 10 topics. A detailed analysis of legal documents might need 100.

LDA in Practice: A Complete Example

Let's build a topic model from scratch. We'll use gensim, which implements Online LDA with efficient streaming and provides built-in coherence metrics.

import gensim
from gensim import corpora
from gensim.models import LdaModel
from gensim.models.coherencemodel import CoherenceModel
import re

# --- Sample corpus (in practice, load your 100K documents here) ---
documents = [
    "Neural networks learn representations from data using backpropagation",
    "Gradient descent optimizes the loss function by updating weights",
    "The stock market rally was driven by strong earnings reports",
    "Investors are watching inflation data and interest rate decisions",
    "The team scored three goals in the second half of the match",
    "The coach praised the defensive strategy after the victory",
    "Deep learning models require large datasets and GPU compute",
    "Transfer learning lets you fine-tune pretrained models on small data",
    "Bond yields rose as the central bank signaled tighter policy",
    "The midfielder received a red card for a dangerous tackle",
]

# --- Preprocessing ---
def preprocess(text):
    text = text.lower()
    tokens = re.findall(r'\b[a-z]{3,}\b', text)  # words >= 3 chars
    stopwords = {'the', 'and', 'was', 'are', 'for', 'from', 'you', 'that',
                 'this', 'with', 'lets', 'using', 'after', 'by'}
    return [t for t in tokens if t not in stopwords]

texts = [preprocess(doc) for doc in documents]

# --- Build dictionary and bag-of-words corpus ---
dictionary = corpora.Dictionary(texts)
dictionary.filter_extremes(no_below=1, no_above=0.8)
corpus = [dictionary.doc2bow(text) for text in texts]

# --- Train LDA ---
lda = LdaModel(
    corpus=corpus,
    id2word=dictionary,
    num_topics=3,         # K = 3 topics
    random_state=42,
    passes=15,            # full passes over corpus
    alpha='auto',         # learn alpha from data
    eta='auto',           # learn eta from data
    per_word_topics=True,
)

# --- Inspect topics ---
for idx, topic in lda.print_topics(num_words=6):
    print(f"Topic {idx}: {topic}")

# --- Evaluate coherence ---
coherence = CoherenceModel(
    model=lda, texts=texts, dictionary=dictionary, coherence='c_v'
)
print(f"\nCoherence (C_v): {coherence.get_coherence():.3f}")

# --- Infer topic mixture for a new document ---
new_doc = "The goalkeeper saved a penalty in the last minute"
bow = dictionary.doc2bow(preprocess(new_doc))
topic_dist = lda.get_document_topics(bow)
print(f"\nNew doc topics: {topic_dist}")

With real data you'd have thousands of documents and you'd sweep over different K values. But the API stays the same. The alpha='auto' and eta='auto' settings let gensim learn the Dirichlet hyperparameters from data, which usually beats hand-tuning.

Evaluation

Perplexity

Perplexity measures how well the model predicts held-out words. Lower is better. Formally, it's the exponential of the negative average log-likelihood per word. A model with perplexity 100 is as uncertain as a uniform distribution over 100 words. Perplexity is easy to compute and was the original evaluation metric for LDA. The problem? Perplexity often disagrees with human judgments of topic quality. A model can achieve excellent perplexity by spreading probability mass cleverly while producing topics that are incoherent gibberish to a human reader. Chang et al. (2009) demonstrated this convincingly in "Reading Tea Leaves."

Topic Coherence

Coherence scores measure whether the top words in a topic actually co-occur in real text — which correlates much better with human judgments. Several variants exist:

C_v combines normalized pointwise mutual information (NPMI) with cosine similarity over sliding windows. It's the most commonly reported metric and correlates well with human ratings. NPMI measures whether two words co-occur more than expected by chance, normalized to [-1, 1]. UCI uses pointwise mutual information with an external reference corpus (like Wikipedia). Higher coherence means the top words in a topic genuinely belong together. A typical workflow: train models with K = 5 to 50, compute C_v for each, and pick the K that maximizes coherence.

Human Evaluation

Numbers don't tell the whole story. The gold standard is showing topics to domain experts and asking: "Can you label this topic in two or three words? Do these words belong together?" If your experts can't name the topic, the model hasn't found a useful theme — regardless of what the coherence score says. Word intrusion tests are a more rigorous version: insert a random word into a topic's top words and ask humans to spot the intruder. If they can, the topic is coherent. If they can't, it isn't.

Key Idea — Perplexity vs. Coherence

Optimizing perplexity and optimizing coherence can lead to different models. Perplexity rewards statistical fit — the model assigns high probability to held-out words. Coherence rewards interpretability — the top words in a topic make sense together. In practice, always evaluate coherence. Report perplexity if you like, but don't select your model based on it alone.

NMF for Topic Modeling

Non-Negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) offers a completely different route to topics. No probability, no Bayesian inference, no generative story. Just linear algebra.

You start with a term-document matrix V of shape (words × documents), where each entry is a TF-IDF weight. NMF factors this into two non-negative matrices: W (words × topics) and H (topics × documents), such that V ≈ W × H. The columns of W are your topics (ranked word lists), and the columns of H are your document-topic mixtures. The non-negativity constraint is what makes the results interpretable — you can't subtract words from a topic, only add them, so the decomposition is naturally additive and parts-based.

from sklearn.decomposition import NMF
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer

documents = [
    "Neural networks learn representations from data using backpropagation",
    "Gradient descent optimizes the loss function by updating weights",
    "The stock market rally was driven by strong earnings reports",
    "Investors are watching inflation data and interest rate decisions",
    "The team scored three goals in the second half of the match",
    "The coach praised the defensive strategy after the victory",
]

vectorizer = TfidfVectorizer(stop_words='english', max_features=500)
tfidf = vectorizer.fit_transform(documents)
feature_names = vectorizer.get_feature_names_out()

nmf = NMF(n_components=3, random_state=42, max_iter=400)
W = nmf.fit_transform(tfidf)   # document-topic matrix
H = nmf.components_             # topic-word matrix

for i, topic in enumerate(H):
    top_words = [feature_names[j] for j in topic.argsort()[-6:][::-1]]
    print(f"Topic {i}: {', '.join(top_words)}")

When to prefer NMF over LDA? NMF is faster (just matrix factorization, no iterative sampling), deterministic (same input → same output), and tends to produce crisper, less overlapping topics on short texts. LDA is better when you need a proper probabilistic model — for instance, if you want to compute the probability that a new document belongs to each topic, or if your downstream pipeline needs uncertainty estimates. For exploratory analysis on a reasonably-sized corpus, NMF is often the pragmatic first choice.

Text Preprocessing for Topic Modeling

Topic models are only as good as the text you feed them. Garbage in, garbage out — but here, "garbage" has a specific meaning: words that don't carry topical signal.

Tokenization splits text into words. Lowercasing ensures "Python" and "python" are treated as the same word. Stop word removal drops high-frequency words like "the," "is," and "of" that appear in every document and carry no topical information. Lemmatization reduces words to their base form: "running" → "run," "better" → "good." This shrinks the vocabulary and merges words that mean the same thing.

Beyond the basics: remove numbers (unless they carry meaning in your domain), remove very short words (1-2 characters), and filter by document frequency. Words that appear in only one document are too rare to indicate a theme. Words that appear in 80%+ of documents are too common. The gensim Dictionary.filter_extremes() call in the example above does exactly this.

One underappreciated step: bigrams and trigrams. Individual words lose context. "New" and "York" are meaningless alone; "new_york" is a meaningful token. Gensim's Phrases detector finds statistically significant multi-word expressions and joins them. For domain-specific corpora (medical, legal, technical), this can dramatically improve topic quality.

A warning: with neural topic models like BERTopic, heavy preprocessing is often counterproductive. Transformer embeddings already capture semantics, and stripping stop words or lemmatizing can actually degrade the embeddings. Know which kind of model you're feeding before you preprocess.

Extensions and Modern Approaches

Dynamic Topic Models

Dynamic Topic Models (DTM) extend LDA to capture how topics evolve over time. Instead of a single β for each topic, you have a sequence β₁, β₂, ..., β_T, one for each time slice. The word distribution for each topic drifts from one time period to the next via a Gaussian random walk. This lets you ask questions like: "How has the 'artificial intelligence' topic changed from 2010 to 2024?" You'd see "SVM" and "kernel" fade while "transformer" and "attention" rise.

Correlated Topic Models

Standard LDA assumes topics are independent — the presence of one topic in a document tells you nothing about whether another topic is present. That's unrealistic. A document about "machine learning" is more likely to also discuss "statistics" than "cooking." Correlated Topic Models (CTM) replace the Dirichlet prior on θ with a logistic-normal distribution, which can capture correlations between topics. The tradeoff: inference is harder because the logistic-normal isn't conjugate to the Categorical.

Hierarchical LDA

Hierarchical LDA (hLDA) discovers not just topics but a tree of topics — broad themes at the top and specific sub-themes at the leaves. It uses a nested Chinese Restaurant Process to learn both the tree structure and the topic assignments. This is powerful for exploring large, structured corpora (e.g., scientific papers where "biology" splits into "genomics," "proteomics," and "ecology"), but inference is expensive and the tree structure can be hard to interpret.

Neural Topic Models

Classical LDA relies on bag-of-words representations and hand-crafted inference. Neural topic models replace both with neural networks. ProdLDA uses a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) where the encoder maps a document's bag-of-words to a latent topic distribution, and the decoder reconstructs the word counts. The key insight is using a Laplace approximation to the Dirichlet prior via a softmax over a Gaussian, which lets you backpropagate through the sampling step. The result: sharper topics than standard VI-based LDA, and the ability to plug in any neural architecture you like.

Embedded Topic Models (ETM) go further by combining word embeddings with the topic model. Each topic is a point in the same embedding space as the words, and a word's probability under a topic is determined by the similarity between the word embedding and the topic embedding. This means the model can handle words it's never seen before (if their embeddings are close to known words) and produces more semantically coherent topics.

BERTopic

BERTopic takes a radically different approach. Instead of modeling word co-occurrence, it operates on document embeddings from a pretrained transformer. The pipeline: (1) embed each document with a sentence transformer, (2) reduce dimensionality with UMAP, (3) cluster the reduced embeddings with HDBSCAN, (4) extract topic representations using class-based TF-IDF (c-TF-IDF). No Dirichlet priors, no generative model, no bag-of-words. The result is topics that capture semantic meaning, not just word overlap.

from bertopic import BERTopic
from sklearn.datasets import fetch_20newsgroups

# Load real data
docs = fetch_20newsgroups(subset='all', remove=('headers', 'footers', 'quotes'))['data']
docs = [d for d in docs if len(d.strip()) > 50]  # filter empties

# BERTopic with sensible defaults
topic_model = BERTopic(
    language="english",
    min_topic_size=30,       # minimum docs per topic
    nr_topics="auto",        # auto-merge similar topics
    verbose=True,
)
topics, probs = topic_model.fit_transform(docs)

# Inspect top topics
print(topic_model.get_topic_info().head(10))

# Top words for topic 0
print("\nTopic 0:", topic_model.get_topic(0))

# Visualize topic similarity (opens interactive plot)
# topic_model.visualize_topics()

BERTopic's advantage: it requires almost no text preprocessing (the transformer handles it), it produces very interpretable topics, and it supports dynamic topics, hierarchical topics, and guided/seeded topics out of the box. The disadvantage: it needs a GPU for the embedding step on large corpora, and because it relies on clustering rather than a probabilistic model, you don't get proper probability distributions over topics for each document — just soft assignments based on cluster distances.

Applications

Document Clustering and Organization

The most direct application. Given a large corpus — support tickets, research papers, news articles — topic modeling assigns each document a topic mixture. You can group documents by their dominant topic, build browsable topic hierarchies, or create automatic tags. Libraries often use topic models to organize their digital collections. Customer support teams use them to discover the most common complaint categories without manually reading thousands of tickets.

Recommendation Systems

If two documents share similar topic distributions (similar θ vectors), they're probably about similar things. This is content-based recommendation. A news app can recommend articles that match the topic profile of what you've already read. A research tool can suggest related papers. The topic vector is a compact, interpretable representation that's easy to store and compare — much cheaper than comparing raw text or full embeddings.

Trend Analysis

Combine topic modeling with timestamps and you get trend analysis. Track how the proportion of each topic changes over time in a corpus of news articles, academic papers, or social media posts. You'll see topics like "COVID-19" spike in early 2020 and topics like "cryptocurrency" surge in late 2021. Dynamic topic models make this even richer by showing how the vocabulary within a topic shifts — the "AI" topic in 2015 talked about "convolutional networks" while the same topic in 2023 talks about "large language models."

Key Idea — Choosing Your Approach

Start with BERTopic if you have a GPU and want quick, interpretable results with minimal preprocessing. Use LDA if you need a principled probabilistic model, especially for downstream tasks that require proper topic distributions. Use NMF for a fast, deterministic baseline — especially on short texts or when you want easily reproducible results. For all three, always inspect topics manually. No metric replaces a human looking at the top words and asking: "Does this make sense?"
