Self-Supervised & Representation Learning

Chapter 6: Unsupervised Learning How modern AI learns without labels

The Labeled Data Bottleneck

Here's the dirty secret of supervised learning: it's incredibly expensive to get labels. ImageNet took years and millions of dollars to annotate 14 million images. Medical imaging? You need board-certified radiologists at $400/hour. Legal documents? Domain experts who charge even more. Meanwhile, the internet produces roughly 2.5 quintillion bytes of unlabeled data every day.

Self-supervised learning flips the script. Instead of begging humans for labels, you design tasks where the data labels itself. Hide a word, predict it. Rotate an image, guess the angle. The model doesn't care about your made-up puzzle — what matters are the representations it learns along the way. This is exactly how BERT and GPT are pretrained, and it's the reason modern AI works as well as it does.

Pretext Tasks: Labels for Free

A pretext task is any task where you can generate the supervision signal automatically from the data itself. You're not trying to solve the task — you're trying to force the model to learn useful features in the process. Think of it like learning to cook by reading recipes: you don't care about memorizing the recipe, you care about the knife skills and flavor intuitions you develop.

The Greatest Hits

Pretext TaskDomainWhat It Does
Masked Language ModelingNLPHide 15% of tokens, predict them (BERT)
Next Token PredictionNLPGiven all previous tokens, predict the next one (GPT)
Rotation PredictionVisionRotate image by 0°/90°/180°/270°, classify the angle
Jigsaw PuzzlesVisionShuffle 3×3 image patches, predict the permutation
ColorizationVisionConvert to grayscale, predict original colors
Masked AutoencodersVisionMask 75% of image patches, reconstruct them (MAE)

The pattern is always the same: corrupt or hide part of the input, then train the model to recover it. To recover masked words, BERT must understand grammar, semantics, and world knowledge. To predict the next token, GPT must model everything from syntax to reasoning. The pretext task is the excuse; the representation is the prize.

Why pretext tasks got eclipsed: They're hand-designed, and the features optimal for "guess the rotation" don't always transfer well to "detect tumors." Contrastive methods mostly replaced them in vision because they learn more general features. But in NLP, pretext tasks (masked LM, next-token prediction) still reign supreme — they just happen to be really good pretext tasks.

Word2Vec & GloVe: The OG Self-Supervised Methods

Before anyone used the phrase "self-supervised learning," Word2Vec (2013) was already doing it. The skip-gram model takes a center word and predicts its context words — that's literally a pretext task. The learned word vectors turned out to capture remarkable semantic structure: king - man + woman ≈ queen.

GloVe (2014) achieved similar results by factorizing the global word co-occurrence matrix instead of using local context windows. Both methods proved the same point: if you force a model to predict structure in raw text, it learns representations that encode meaning. This was the proof of concept that made BERT and GPT conceptually possible.

from gensim.models import Word2Vec

sentences = [["the", "cat", "sat", "on", "the", "mat"],
             ["the", "dog", "lay", "on", "the", "rug"]]

# sg=1 → skip-gram (the original pretext task)
model = Word2Vec(sentences, vector_size=100, window=5, min_count=1, sg=1)
print(model.wv.most_similar("cat"))
# → [('dog', 0.92), ...]  — similar contexts → similar vectors

Contrastive Learning: The Big Idea

Contrastive learning asks a deceptively simple question: can we learn representations just by knowing what's similar and what's different? Take a photo of a cat. Create two augmented views — one cropped, one color-jittered. These form a positive pair (same content, different appearance). Every other image in your batch forms negative pairs. Train the model to pull positives together and push negatives apart in embedding space.

The loss function that makes this work is InfoNCE — essentially a softmax over cosine similarities. For a positive pair (i, j) in a batch of N images (2N augmented views):

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def info_nce_loss(z_i, z_j, temperature=0.07):
    """InfoNCE loss — the workhorse of contrastive learning."""
    batch_size = z_i.shape[0]
    z = torch.cat([z_i, z_j], dim=0)          # (2N, D)
    z = F.normalize(z, dim=1)

    sim = z @ z.T / temperature                # (2N, 2N) cosine sim matrix

    # mask out self-similarity (diagonal)
    mask = torch.eye(2 * batch_size, dtype=torch.bool)
    sim.masked_fill_(mask, -1e9)

    # positive pairs: (i, i+N) and (i+N, i)
    labels = torch.cat([torch.arange(batch_size, 2 * batch_size),
                        torch.arange(0, batch_size)])

    return F.cross_entropy(sim, labels)

The temperature τ controls how hard the model concentrates on difficult negatives. Lower temperature = sharper distribution = the model works harder to distinguish similar-but-different examples.

SimCLR: Simple but Hungry

SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) distilled contrastive learning to its essence: augment → encode → project → contrast. Three discoveries made it work. First, strong augmentations (random crop + color jitter) matter more than architecture choices. Second, a projection head (small MLP) on top of the encoder is critical — you train on the projected features but use the encoder features for downstream tasks. Third, you need large batch sizes (4096+) to get enough negatives.

That third point was SimCLR's Achilles' heel. You need 128 TPU cores to train it properly. That limitation sparked the next wave of innovation.

MoCo: Decoupling Negatives from Batch Size

MoCo (He et al., 2020) asked: why should the number of negatives be limited by your batch size? Its solution: maintain a queue of recent embeddings (65,536 negatives!) and use a momentum encoder that updates slowly via exponential moving average. The momentum encoder ensures keys in the queue stay consistent — they all come from nearly the same model version.

# MoCo's momentum update — the key trick
momentum = 0.999
for p_q, p_k in zip(query_encoder.parameters(), key_encoder.parameters()):
    p_k.data = momentum * p_k.data + (1 - momentum) * p_q.data

MoCo was the first method to match supervised ImageNet features — on a single 8-GPU machine. MoCo v2 later borrowed SimCLR's augmentations and projection head, combining the best of both.

Non-Contrastive Methods: No Negatives Required

Here's where things get surprising. What if you don't need negative pairs at all?

BYOL: Bootstrap Your Own Latent

BYOL (Grill et al., 2020) dropped the negatives and outperformed SimCLR and MoCo. It uses two networks: an online network (encoder + projector + predictor) and a target network (encoder + projector only, updated via momentum EMA). The online network predicts the target's output for a different augmented view of the same image.

Why doesn't it collapse to a constant? Without negatives, what stops every input from mapping to the same vector (zero loss, nothing learned)? The answer is architectural asymmetry. The extra predictor head in the online network, combined with the stop-gradient on the target network, creates an information bottleneck that makes the trivial solution unreachable. The slow-moving target acts like a stable reference point that the online network chases.

# BYOL training step (simplified)
view1, view2 = augment(image), augment(image)

pred1, pred2 = online_net(view1), online_net(view2)  # encoder + projector + predictor

with torch.no_grad():
    targ1, targ2 = target_net(view1), target_net(view2)  # encoder + projector only

loss = cosine_loss(pred1, targ2) + cosine_loss(pred2, targ1)
loss.backward()
ema_update(online_net, target_net, momentum=0.996)

Barlow Twins: Redundancy Reduction

Barlow Twins (Zbontar et al., 2021) takes an entirely different angle, inspired by neuroscientist Horace Barlow's hypothesis that sensory neurons minimize redundancy. Compute the cross-correlation matrix between embeddings of two views across a batch. The goal: make it the identity matrix. Diagonal = 1 means each feature captures the same invariant content across views. Off-diagonal = 0 means different features capture different information.

def barlow_twins_loss(z1, z2, lambd=0.005):
    """Push cross-correlation toward the identity matrix."""
    z1 = (z1 - z1.mean(0)) / z1.std(0)
    z2 = (z2 - z2.mean(0)) / z2.std(0)
    c = (z1.T @ z2) / z1.shape[0]  # cross-correlation (D × D)

    on_diag = ((1 - c.diagonal()) ** 2).sum()
    off_diag = (c[~torch.eye(c.shape[0], dtype=bool)] ** 2).sum()
    return on_diag + lambd * off_diag

No negatives. No momentum encoder. No asymmetric architecture. Just a clean correlation objective. It's competitive with BYOL and easier to reason about.

The self-supervised progression: SimCLR proved contrastive learning works at scale (needs huge batches) → MoCo decoupled negatives from batch size (queue + momentum) → BYOL proved negatives aren't needed at all (asymmetry prevents collapse) → Barlow Twins simplified to a pure correlation objective. Each solved a specific limitation of its predecessor.

Representation Learning: The Actual Goal

Everything above is in service of one goal: learning representations that transfer. You don't care about predicting masked words or distinguishing augmented cats. You care about getting a feature extractor that works well on tasks you actually care about — sentiment analysis, object detection, medical diagnosis — with minimal labeled data.

Frozen Features vs. Fine-Tuning

Once you have a pretrained encoder, there are two ways to use it:

StrategyWhat You DoWhen to Use
Linear probe (frozen) Freeze encoder, train a linear classifier on top Evaluating representation quality; very small labeled sets
Fine-tuning Unfreeze encoder, train everything end-to-end with a small learning rate Maximum downstream performance; moderate labeled data

The linear probe is the gold standard for measuring how good your representations are. If a linear model can separate classes using only frozen features, those features have captured something genuinely meaningful. The gap between linear probe accuracy and fine-tuning accuracy tells you how much task-specific adaptation the representation still needs.

Good representations tend to be invariant to things that don't matter (lighting, background, camera angle) and sensitive to things that do (object identity, scene type, semantic content). Self-supervised methods learn these properties implicitly through their training objectives — augmentation invariance in contrastive learning, context prediction in language models.

The Big Picture: Pretrain → Fine-Tune

Self-supervised pretraining followed by supervised fine-tuning is now the dominant paradigm in modern AI. This isn't a niche technique — it's how the most capable systems in the world are built.

MethodKey IdeaDomainWhen to Use
Word2Vec / GloVe Predict context words / factorize co-occurrence NLP Lightweight word embeddings, limited compute
BERT Masked language modeling (bidirectional) NLP Understanding tasks: classification, NER, QA
GPT Next token prediction (autoregressive) NLP Generation tasks: text, code, dialogue
SimCLR Contrastive learning with large batches Vision When you have massive compute (TPU pods)
MoCo Momentum encoder + queue of negatives Vision Contrastive learning on a realistic GPU budget
BYOL No negatives — asymmetric architecture Vision Small batches, robust to augmentation choices
Barlow Twins Redundancy reduction via cross-correlation Vision Simplicity; no momentum, no negatives, no asymmetry
DINO / DINOv2 Self-distillation with no labels Vision State-of-the-art vision features; emergent segmentation
MAE Masked autoencoding (75% patches masked) Vision Efficient pretraining; BERT-style for images

In NLP, the pretrain → fine-tune pipeline has been dominant since 2018. In vision, DINO and MAE showed that self-supervised ViTs produce features that rival or beat supervised pretraining — with emergent properties like object segmentation appearing without any segmentation labels. The walls between "unsupervised" and "supervised" are dissolving. Self-supervised learning isn't an alternative to supervised learning. It's the foundation that makes supervised learning work with less data, less cost, and more generalization.
