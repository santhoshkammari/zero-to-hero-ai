Expectation-Maximization (EM) Algorithm

Chapter 6: Unsupervised Learning 5 subtopics
TL;DR

The EM algorithm is a general recipe for fitting models with hidden (latent) variables. It alternates between two steps: the E-step computes soft assignments — "how likely is each hidden explanation given our current parameters?" — and the M-step re-estimates parameters as if those soft assignments were ground truth. Each iteration is guaranteed to increase (or at least not decrease) the data likelihood, and the pair of steps repeats until convergence. EM powers Gaussian Mixture Models, Hidden Markov Models, missing-data imputation, and many more — essentially any situation where if you knew the hidden variables, estimation would be easy.

Why EM Exists: The Hidden-Variable Problem

Some data is hidden — how do you learn from incomplete observations? That question sits at the heart of a huge class of machine learning problems. You record customer purchases, but you don't know which customer segment generated each transaction. You measure gene expression levels, but you don't know which regulatory pathway is active. You observe words in documents, but you don't know which latent topic produced each word. In every case, the thing you want to estimate (parameters) is tangled up with something you can't observe (latent variables).

If the hidden variables were revealed, life would be easy. Suppose someone told you "this customer belongs to segment A" — then you'd just compute the average behavior of segment-A customers and you'd have your parameter estimate. But nobody tells you. You have to figure out both the segments and the segment parameters simultaneously, and that circular dependency is what makes the problem hard.

The Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, introduced by Dempster, Laird, and Rubin in 1977, breaks this chicken-and-egg cycle with an elegant trick: guess the hidden variables, then optimize parameters, then re-guess, repeat. It's not a single model — it's a meta-algorithm, a general framework you can plug into any latent-variable model. And it works remarkably well.

A Concrete Example: The Coin-Flip Mixture

Before we touch any math, let's build intuition with a toy problem. Imagine you have two coins — Coin A and Coin B — with unknown biases. The true biases happen to be θ_A = 0.6 (60% heads) and θ_B = 0.5 (50% heads), but you don't know these values. You run 5 trials. In each trial, someone secretly picks one of the two coins (50/50 chance), flips it 10 times, and hands you only the count of heads. You never see which coin was used.

Here's what you observe across the 5 trials: [7, 4, 6, 5, 8] heads out of 10. Your goal: estimate θ_A and θ_B from this data alone.

Initialization

You have to start somewhere, so you guess: θ_A = 0.55 and θ_B = 0.45. These are wrong, but EM will fix them iteratively.

E-Step: Compute Responsibilities

For each trial, you ask: "Given my current parameter guesses, how likely is it that Coin A produced this outcome vs. Coin B?" This is a direct application of Bayes' theorem. For trial 1 (7 heads out of 10):

The likelihood of 7 heads from Coin A with θ_A = 0.55 is proportional to 0.55^7 × 0.45^3 ≈ 0.01172. From Coin B with θ_B = 0.45 it's 0.45^7 × 0.55^3 ≈ 0.00277. Since we assumed equal prior probability of picking either coin, the responsibility of Coin A for trial 1 is:

r_A1 = 0.01172 / (0.01172 + 0.00277) ≈ 0.809

So we're about 81% confident Coin A generated trial 1. That makes sense — 7/10 heads is more consistent with the higher-bias coin. Doing this for all 5 trials gives us a table of soft assignments — not hard "Coin A" or "Coin B" labels, but fractional weights. Trial 4 (5 heads) might split nearly 50/50, while trial 5 (8 heads) will lean heavily toward Coin A.

M-Step: Update Parameters

Now treat those responsibilities as fractional counts. To re-estimate θ_A, take a weighted average of the observed heads, where each trial is weighted by its responsibility toward Coin A:

θ_A_new = (r_A1 × 7 + r_A2 × 4 + r_A3 × 6 + r_A4 × 5 + r_A5 × 8) / (r_A1 × 10 + r_A2 × 10 + r_A3 × 10 + r_A4 × 10 + r_A5 × 10)

Trials with high Coin-A responsibility contribute more to the Coin-A estimate. Trials that look like Coin-B outcomes get down-weighted. Similarly for θ_B, using r_Bi = 1 - r_Ai. After this step, you have new parameter estimates that are closer to the truth.

Iterate

You feed the updated θ_A and θ_B back into the E-step, recompute responsibilities with the better parameters, then do another M-step. Each cycle nudges the estimates closer. After 10–20 iterations, the values stabilize near θ_A ≈ 0.6 and θ_B ≈ 0.5 — the true values.

import numpy as np
from scipy.special import comb

def em_coin_mixture(observations, n_flips=10, n_iter=20):
    # Initialize guesses
    theta_a, theta_b = 0.55, 0.45

    for i in range(n_iter):
        # E-step: compute responsibilities
        resp_a = []
        for heads in observations:
            like_a = (theta_a ** heads) * ((1 - theta_a) ** (n_flips - heads))
            like_b = (theta_b ** heads) * ((1 - theta_b) ** (n_flips - heads))
            r = like_a / (like_a + like_b)
            resp_a.append(r)
        resp_a = np.array(resp_a)

        # M-step: weighted MLE
        theta_a = np.sum(resp_a * observations) / np.sum(resp_a * n_flips)
        theta_b = np.sum((1 - resp_a) * observations) / np.sum((1 - resp_a) * n_flips)

        print(f"Iter {i+1:2d}: θ_A={theta_a:.4f}, θ_B={theta_b:.4f}")

    return theta_a, theta_b

obs = np.array([7, 4, 6, 5, 8])
em_coin_mixture(obs)
The EM intuition: If you knew the hidden labels, parameter estimation would be trivial (just compute averages per group). If you knew the parameters, inferring the labels would be trivial (just apply Bayes' rule). EM alternates between these two "easy" steps, bootstrapping its way to a solution.

The General Framework

Latent Variables and Incomplete Data

In the general setting, you observe data X and there exist hidden variables Z that you don't observe. Together, (X, Z) form the complete data; X alone is the incomplete data. Your model has parameters θ, and you want to maximize the incomplete-data log-likelihood:

log p(X | θ) = log Σ_Z p(X, Z | θ)

That sum (or integral, for continuous Z) inside the logarithm is the problem. It couples everything together and usually has no closed-form solution. You can't just take a derivative and set it to zero.

The Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO)

EM sidesteps the intractable sum by working with a lower bound on the log-likelihood instead. For any distribution q(Z) over the latent variables, Jensen's inequality gives us:

log p(X | θ) ≥ Σ_Z q(Z) log [p(X, Z | θ) / q(Z)]

The right-hand side is called the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO). Think of it as a floor under the true log-likelihood. The gap between the ELBO and the true log-likelihood is exactly the KL divergence between q(Z) and the true posterior p(Z | X, θ). When q(Z) = p(Z | X, θ), the KL divergence is zero and the bound is tight — the ELBO equals the log-likelihood.

Why Jensen's Inequality Matters Here

Jensen's inequality says that for a concave function f (and log is concave), f(E[X]) ≥ E[f(X)]. Applied to our problem, it lets us push the log inside the sum over Z, turning an intractable optimization into a tractable one. The price is that we optimize a lower bound rather than the true objective — but the EM two-step trick makes the bound tight at each E-step, so we still make genuine progress on the real objective.

E-Step: Inferring the Hidden World

The E-step (Expectation step) computes the posterior distribution over the latent variables given the current parameter estimates θ^(t):

q(Z) = p(Z | X, θ^(t))

This choice of q makes the ELBO tight (the KL gap is zero). In practice, this means computing responsibilities — the probability that each data point was generated by each latent component. For the coin example, that was the r_Ai values. For a Gaussian Mixture Model, it's the probability that each data point belongs to each Gaussian cluster.

Expected Sufficient Statistics

Instead of working with the full posterior, you often only need its expected sufficient statistics. Sufficient statistics are the minimal summaries of the data that capture all the information the parameters need. For a Gaussian, that's the sum of values and the sum of squared values. The E-step computes these statistics weighted by the responsibilities. This is computationally efficient because you never have to enumerate all possible configurations of Z — you just compute weighted sums.

Posterior and Responsibilities

The word responsibility is standard terminology in the EM literature. For data point x_n and component k, the responsibility is:

r_nk = π_k · p(x_n | θ_k) / Σ_j π_j · p(x_n | θ_j)

where π_k is the mixing weight (prior probability of component k) and p(x_n | θ_k) is the likelihood of the data point under component k. This is soft assignment: every data point partially belongs to every component, proportional to how well that component explains it.

M-Step: Updating Parameters

The M-step (Maximization step) finds the parameters that maximize the expected complete-data log-likelihood:

θ^(t+1) = argmax_θ Σ_Z q(Z) log p(X, Z | θ)

In words: pretend the responsibilities from the E-step are the truth, then fit your model parameters accordingly. Because the complete-data likelihood p(X, Z | θ) usually factors cleanly (that was the whole point of introducing latent variables), this maximization often has a closed-form solution.

Parameter Update Rules

For the coin-flip mixture, the M-step update was a weighted average. For Gaussian Mixture Models, you get similarly clean formulas. The new mean of component k is:

μ_k = Σ_n r_nk · x_n / Σ_n r_nk

The new covariance is a responsibility-weighted scatter matrix, and the new mixing weight is π_k = (1/N) Σ_n r_nk — just the average responsibility for component k across all data points. Each formula is intuitive: it's the same as the ordinary estimate, but with fractional counts instead of hard assignments.

When Closed-Form Solutions Don't Exist

Not every model gives you a clean M-step. When the maximization has no closed form, you can run a few steps of gradient ascent or Newton's method inside the M-step. This variant is called Generalized EM (GEM) — instead of fully maximizing, you just need to increase the expected complete-data log-likelihood. The monotonic convergence guarantee still holds as long as each M-step doesn't make things worse.

Properties and Convergence

Monotonic Likelihood Increase

This is the big theoretical guarantee. Each full EM iteration (E-step + M-step) is guaranteed to increase the data log-likelihood log p(X | θ), or at worst leave it unchanged. The proof is elegant: the E-step makes the ELBO tight (equal to the log-likelihood), and the M-step increases the ELBO. Since the ELBO was tight before the M-step, the new ELBO (and hence the new log-likelihood) must be at least as large as the old log-likelihood. No learning rate to tune. No risk of the objective going backwards.

Convergence to a Local Maximum

The monotonic increase guarantee, combined with the fact that the log-likelihood is bounded above, means EM must converge. But here's the catch: it converges to a local maximum (or saddle point), not necessarily the global maximum. The likelihood surface for mixture models is typically full of local optima, especially when components overlap or when the number of components is large. EM will find a good solution, but not necessarily the best solution.

Sensitivity to Initialization

Because EM finds local optima, the starting point matters — a lot. Two different initializations can converge to very different solutions with very different likelihoods. The standard mitigation is multiple restarts: run EM several times with different random initializations and keep the result with the highest final likelihood. Another common trick is to initialize with the output of a faster algorithm. For GMMs, running k-means first and using its cluster assignments as the initial responsibilities works well in practice.

EM Generalizes k-Means

Here's a satisfying connection. k-Means is a special case of EM — specifically, it's EM applied to a Gaussian Mixture Model where every Gaussian has the same fixed, tiny covariance (approaching an identity matrix scaled by a small constant). In the limit, the soft responsibilities become hard assignments (each point belongs to exactly one cluster), and the M-step just computes cluster centroids. This variant is sometimes called hard EM. Standard EM with actual responsibilities is soft EM. The softness is what gives EM its power: partial assignments let the algorithm hedge its bets and explore the space more smoothly.

Local optima in practice: For well-separated clusters, most initializations converge to the same (global) solution. The initialization sensitivity becomes a real problem when clusters overlap significantly or when the model is misspecified (e.g., fitting 5 Gaussians to data generated from 3). Always compare the final log-likelihood across multiple runs.

Applications

Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs)

This is the most common application of EM and the one most people encounter first. A GMM models the data as a weighted sum of K Gaussian distributions, each with its own mean and covariance. The latent variable for each data point is which Gaussian generated it. The E-step computes responsibilities (soft cluster memberships), and the M-step updates the means, covariances, and mixing weights. GMMs are the backbone of many density estimation and clustering tasks, from speech recognition to anomaly detection.

from sklearn.mixture import GaussianMixture
import numpy as np

# Generate synthetic data from 3 clusters
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
X = np.vstack([
    rng.normal(loc=[0, 0], scale=0.8, size=(100, 2)),
    rng.normal(loc=[4, 4], scale=1.0, size=(100, 2)),
    rng.normal(loc=[8, 1], scale=0.6, size=(100, 2)),
])

# Fit a GMM with EM (sklearn handles E/M steps internally)
gmm = GaussianMixture(n_components=3, random_state=42)
gmm.fit(X)

# Soft assignments (responsibilities)
responsibilities = gmm.predict_proba(X)  # shape: (300, 3)

# Hard assignments
labels = gmm.predict(X)

print(f"Converged: {gmm.converged_}, Iterations: {gmm.n_iter_}")
print(f"Learned means:\n{gmm.means_.round(2)}")

Hidden Markov Models and Baum-Welch

In a Hidden Markov Model (HMM), you observe a sequence of emissions (words, sensor readings, stock prices), and the hidden variable is the sequence of latent states that produced them. EM for HMMs is called the Baum-Welch algorithm. The E-step uses the forward-backward algorithm to compute the expected state occupancies and transition counts. The M-step updates the transition probabilities and emission parameters. This is how speech recognition systems were trained for decades, and it remains central to computational biology (gene finding, protein structure prediction).

Missing Data Imputation

EM has a natural interpretation for missing data problems. Think of the missing values as latent variables. The E-step computes the expected values of the missing entries given the observed data and current parameters. The M-step re-estimates the model parameters (e.g., means and covariances of a multivariate Gaussian) using the completed data. This is more principled than ad-hoc imputation strategies like filling in column means, because it accounts for the uncertainty in the missing values and uses the full correlation structure of the data.

Factor Analysis

Factor analysis posits that high-dimensional observed data is generated from a smaller number of latent factors plus noise. The latent factors are continuous (unlike the discrete cluster labels in GMMs), but EM still applies. The E-step infers the posterior distribution over the latent factors for each data point, and the M-step updates the factor loading matrix and noise variances. Factor analysis is a workhorse in psychometrics, finance (factor models of asset returns), and genomics.

Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA)

In LDA, each document is modeled as a mixture of topics, and each topic is a distribution over words. The latent variables are the per-word topic assignments. The standard inference method isn't vanilla EM — the posterior over topics is intractable, so people use variational EM (which approximates the E-step using variational inference) or collapsed Gibbs sampling. But the conceptual framework is still EM: infer hidden topics, then re-estimate topic-word distributions. LDA powers topic modeling in text analysis, recommendation systems, and beyond.

Practical Considerations and Tradeoffs

Convergence Speed

EM is simple and reliable, but it can be slow. Near convergence, the likelihood improves by smaller and smaller amounts — EM exhibits linear convergence, meaning it takes a fixed fraction off the remaining error each iteration. Gradient-based methods can achieve quadratic convergence (Newton's method), which is much faster near the optimum. In practice, people often run EM for the first several iterations to get into a good region and then switch to a direct optimizer for the final polish.

Choosing K

EM assumes you know the number of components K. It doesn't tell you how many clusters exist. In practice, you run EM for different values of K and compare using model selection criteria: the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) penalizes complexity to prevent overfitting, while the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) provides a slightly different tradeoff. Alternatively, Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Dirichlet Process Mixture Model learn K from the data directly.

Singularities

A nasty practical issue with GMMs: if a Gaussian component collapses onto a single data point, its variance goes to zero and the likelihood goes to infinity. This is a singularity in the likelihood surface, and EM will happily walk right into it. The standard fix is to add a small regularization term to the covariances (a floor on the diagonal elements) or to restart any component that becomes degenerate.

Variants

Several variants address EM's limitations. Variational EM approximates the E-step when the true posterior is intractable (as in LDA). Monte Carlo EM samples from the posterior instead of computing it exactly. Incremental EM processes data points one at a time for scalability. Online EM uses stochastic updates for streaming data. Each variant trades off exactness for computational feasibility, but they all preserve the core E-step/M-step structure.

When to reach for EM: Use EM when your model has a clean latent-variable structure and the complete-data likelihood is easy to work with. If the E-step posterior is intractable, consider variational inference. If the M-step has no closed form, gradient-based optimization might be simpler. EM shines when both steps have analytical solutions — that's where you get simplicity, stability, and guaranteed progress for free.

Summary

The EM algorithm turns an impossible joint optimization over parameters and hidden variables into two alternating easy steps. The E-step infers the hidden variables; the M-step fits the parameters. Each cycle provably improves the likelihood, and the framework generalizes across a remarkable range of models — from clustering with GMMs to sequence modeling with HMMs to topic discovery with LDA. The core tradeoffs are real: local optima, slow convergence, and sensitivity to initialization. But with multiple restarts and sensible initialization, EM remains one of the most practical and widely-used algorithms in machine learning. If you see hidden variables, think EM first.
