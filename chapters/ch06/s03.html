Anomaly Detection

Chapter 6: Unsupervised Learning 10 subtopics
TL;DR

Anomaly detection finds the rare, suspicious, or broken data points that don't behave like the rest. Statistical methods flag values that are too many standard deviations from the mean. Density-based approaches catch points in sparse neighborhoods. Isolation Forest isolates outliers with random splits in linear time. One-Class SVM draws a tight boundary around normal data. Autoencoders learn to reconstruct normal patterns and flag anything they can't rebuild. Choosing the right method depends on your data shape, the anomaly type, and whether you have labels — which you almost never do.

Why Anomaly Detection Matters

Imagine you work at a bank. Every day, ten million credit card transactions flow through your system. Roughly 9,997,000 of them are legitimate. Maybe 3,000 are fraudulent. Your job is to catch the 3,000 without blocking the 9,997,000. That's anomaly detection in a nutshell — finding the needles in a haystack so large that most people forget there are needles at all.

This problem shows up everywhere. A factory sensor that suddenly reads 300°C when it's been reading 150°C for months. A network server that starts sending packets at 3 AM to an IP address it has never contacted before. A patient's lab result that looks nothing like their historical readings. In every case, you're looking for data that doesn't fit the pattern — and the pattern is defined by the overwhelming majority of normal behavior.

The challenge is subtle. You can't just train a binary classifier the way you would for spam detection. In spam, you have millions of examples of both spam and not-spam. In fraud detection, you might have ten million normal transactions and fifty confirmed frauds. That's a 200,000:1 class ratio. A model that always predicts "normal" achieves 99.9995% accuracy and catches zero frauds. Accuracy is useless here. You need specialized techniques.

Anomaly Types: Point, Contextual, and Collective

Not all anomalies look the same, and recognizing the type helps you choose the right method.

A point anomaly is a single data point that's globally weird. A credit card charge of $50,000 when the average is $85. A server response time of 45 seconds when the norm is 200 milliseconds. You can spot these by looking at the value alone, without any additional context.

A contextual anomaly (also called a conditional anomaly) is normal in one context but abnormal in another. A temperature of 35°C is fine in July but alarming in January. A $500 purchase is normal at a jewelry store but strange at a gas station. The value itself isn't extreme — the context makes it suspicious. These are harder to catch because you need to model context, not just the value.

A collective anomaly is a group of data points that are individually normal but together form an abnormal pattern. Each heartbeat in an ECG looks fine, but the sequence of intervals spells arrhythmia. Each network packet is legitimate, but the sequence of 10,000 packets in a minute signals a denial-of-service attack. You need sequential or structural awareness to detect these.

Taxonomy: Supervised, Semi-Supervised, and Unsupervised

How you approach anomaly detection depends on what labels you have.

Supervised anomaly detection uses labeled examples of both normal and anomalous data. In theory this sounds great — train a classifier, done. In practice it's rare because anomalies are scarce, expensive to label, and constantly evolving. Fraudsters change tactics. New types of equipment failure appear. Your labeled dataset from last year might not cover this year's anomalies.

Semi-supervised anomaly detection trains only on normal data — no anomaly labels needed. The model learns what "normal" looks like, then flags anything that deviates. This is the most practical sweet spot. You usually have plenty of normal data (yesterday's non-fraudulent transactions), and the model can catch novel anomalies it's never seen because it's not memorizing specific fraud patterns — it's modeling normalcy.

Unsupervised anomaly detection uses no labels at all. It assumes anomalies are rare and different from the bulk of the data. This is the most general approach and the most common in practice, because labeling is expensive and anomalies are, by definition, rare.

Statistical Methods

Z-Score / Standard Deviation

The simplest anomaly detector is one you probably already know. Compute the mean μ and standard deviation σ of your data. For any new point x, calculate the Z-score: z = (x − μ) / σ. If |z| > 3, the point is more than three standard deviations from the mean — a classic threshold for "unusual." In a normal distribution, only 0.27% of points fall outside ±3σ.

The Z-score is fast, interpretable, and works well when your data is roughly Gaussian and univariate. It falls apart when the distribution is skewed, heavy-tailed, or multivariate. A salary of $500,000 might have a Z-score of 4 in a company, but that doesn't account for the fact that executive salaries follow a very different distribution than entry-level salaries.

Grubbs' Test

Grubbs' test is a formal statistical hypothesis test for outliers. It assumes the data comes from a normal distribution and tests whether the most extreme value is significantly far from the rest. The test statistic is G = max|xᵢ − x̄| / s, where s is the standard deviation. You compare G against a critical value from a t-distribution. If it exceeds the threshold, you reject the null hypothesis that there are no outliers.

Grubbs' test is principled but limited. It tests one outlier at a time. If you have multiple outliers, they can pull the mean and standard deviation toward themselves, masking each other — this is called the masking effect. You'd need to apply it iteratively, removing one outlier at a time, which gets messy.

Mahalanobis Distance

When your data has multiple features, Euclidean distance misleads you. Imagine a dataset where height (in centimeters) ranges from 150–190 and weight (in kilograms) ranges from 50–100. A point at (180, 90) might look close to the center in Euclidean space but be extreme when you account for the fact that height and weight are correlated.

The Mahalanobis distance fixes this by incorporating the covariance matrix. It measures how many standard deviations away a point is from the center of the distribution, accounting for correlations between features: D = √((x − μ)ᵀ Σ⁻¹ (x − μ)). Points with large Mahalanobis distances are anomalous even if no single feature looks extreme. The catch: you need to estimate the covariance matrix, which is unreliable when you have more features than data points or when the normal data itself contains outliers.

Gaussian Distribution Fitting

Fit a multivariate Gaussian to your training data (estimate μ and Σ), then compute the probability density for each new point. Points with very low probability — below some threshold ε — are flagged as anomalies. This is the foundation of many production anomaly detection systems because it's fast at inference time (just a matrix multiplication) and provides a natural anomaly score (the probability itself).

The limitation is obvious: real data is rarely Gaussian. Transaction amounts follow power laws. Sensor readings might be bimodal. You can use mixture models (GMMs) to handle multimodal data, but then you're choosing the number of components, and you're back to hyperparameter tuning with no labels to validate against.

Density-Based Methods

Local Outlier Factor (LOF)

Statistical methods assume a global distribution. But what if your normal data has clusters of different densities? A point might be sparse compared to a dense cluster nearby but perfectly normal in the context of a sparse cluster far away. The Local Outlier Factor (LOF) handles this by comparing each point's local density to the local density of its neighbors.

Here's how it works. For each point, find its k nearest neighbors. Compute the local reachability density — roughly, how tightly packed the neighborhood is. Then compare that density to the densities of the neighbors themselves. If a point's neighborhood is much less dense than its neighbors' neighborhoods, its LOF score is high — it's in a sparse patch next to a dense region, which is suspicious.

LOF scores near 1.0 mean the point has similar density to its neighbors (normal). Scores significantly above 1.0 indicate anomalies. The beauty of LOF is that it adapts to local structure. A point that's far from a dense cluster gets flagged, but an equally isolated point in a region where everything is spread out does not.

DBSCAN-Based (Noise Points as Anomalies)

DBSCAN, which we covered in the clustering section, naturally identifies noise points — data that doesn't belong to any cluster because it doesn't have enough neighbors within the ε radius. You can repurpose this as an anomaly detector: run DBSCAN on your data and treat every noise point (labeled −1) as an anomaly.

This works surprisingly well when your normal data forms clear clusters. The tricky part is setting ε and min_samples. Too tight, and you flag normal points as noise. Too loose, and you absorb anomalies into clusters. There's no principled way to tune these without labels, which is a recurring theme in unsupervised anomaly detection.

Kernel Density Estimation (KDE)

Kernel Density Estimation builds a smooth probability density function from your data without assuming any particular distribution shape. Place a kernel (usually Gaussian) on each data point, sum them up, and normalize. The result is a smooth surface that's high where data is dense and low where it's sparse. Points with very low estimated density are anomalies.

KDE is non-parametric and flexible, but it scales poorly to high dimensions. In 2D or 3D, it works beautifully. In 100 dimensions, the kernels spread so thin that everything looks equally sparse — the curse of dimensionality strikes again. Use it for low-dimensional anomaly detection or after dimensionality reduction.

Isolation Forest

Isolation Forest is one of the most elegant ideas in anomaly detection. Instead of modeling what normal looks like and then measuring how far a point deviates, it directly measures how easy a point is to isolate from the rest.

Random Partitioning

The intuition is simple. Pick a random feature. Pick a random split value between the minimum and maximum of that feature. This divides the data in two. Repeat recursively on each half, like building a random decision tree, until every point is alone in its own partition.

Here's the key insight: anomalies are few and different. If a point is far from the rest, a random split is likely to separate it early. Normal points, surrounded by similar points, take many splits to isolate. The number of splits needed to isolate a point — its path length — becomes the anomaly score.

Anomaly Score and Path Length

Build many random trees (a forest). For each data point, average the path length across all trees. Short average path length → anomaly. Long average path length → normal. The score is normalized so that it falls between 0 and 1, with values close to 1 indicating anomalies.

No density estimation. No distance calculations. No assumptions about the data distribution. The algorithm runs in linear time O(n), making it practical for millions of data points. It also handles high-dimensional data gracefully because each split only considers one feature at a time.

Practical Implementation

import numpy as np
from sklearn.ensemble import IsolationForest
from sklearn.datasets import make_blobs

# Generate normal data with a few injected anomalies
np.random.seed(42)
X_normal, _ = make_blobs(n_samples=500, centers=1,
                          cluster_std=1.0, random_state=42)
X_anomalies = np.random.uniform(low=-6, high=6, size=(20, 2))
X = np.vstack([X_normal, X_anomalies])
true_labels = np.array([1]*500 + [-1]*20)  # 1=normal, -1=anomaly

# Fit Isolation Forest
iso_forest = IsolationForest(
    n_estimators=200,       # number of trees
    contamination=0.05,     # expected fraction of anomalies
    random_state=42
)
predictions = iso_forest.fit_predict(X)
scores = iso_forest.decision_function(X)  # lower = more anomalous

# Check results
n_detected = (predictions == -1).sum()
print(f"Detected {n_detected} anomalies out of {len(X)} points")
print(f"Score range: {scores.min():.3f} to {scores.max():.3f}")

# How many of the true anomalies did we catch?
true_anomaly_mask = true_labels == -1
detected_correctly = (predictions[true_anomaly_mask] == -1).sum()
print(f"Caught {detected_correctly}/{true_anomaly_mask.sum()} true anomalies")

The contamination parameter is the one knob you have to set. It tells the model what fraction of the training data you expect to be anomalous. If you set it too high, you'll flag normal points. Too low, and anomalies slip through. In practice, domain knowledge or a rough estimate (e.g., "fraud is about 0.1% of transactions") guides this choice.

Local Outlier Factor in Practice

from sklearn.neighbors import LocalOutlierFactor

# Using the same data from above
lof = LocalOutlierFactor(
    n_neighbors=20,         # size of local neighborhood
    contamination=0.05,     # expected anomaly fraction
    novelty=False           # True for prediction on new data
)
lof_predictions = lof.fit_predict(X)
lof_scores = lof.negative_outlier_factor_  # more negative = more anomalous

n_detected_lof = (lof_predictions == -1).sum()
print(f"LOF detected {n_detected_lof} anomalies")

detected_correctly_lof = (lof_predictions[true_anomaly_mask] == -1).sum()
print(f"LOF caught {detected_correctly_lof}/{true_anomaly_mask.sum()} true anomalies")

# Compare scores for normal vs anomaly points
print(f"Mean LOF score (normal): {lof_scores[true_labels==1].mean():.3f}")
print(f"Mean LOF score (anomalies): {lof_scores[true_labels==-1].mean():.3f}")

Notice the n_neighbors parameter. Too small and the density estimates are noisy. Too large and you lose local sensitivity — the whole point of LOF. A value between 10 and 50 works for most datasets. Set novelty=True if you want to train on clean data and then score new incoming points.

One-Class SVM

One-Class SVM takes a different approach. Instead of measuring density or isolation, it learns a decision boundary that wraps tightly around the normal data. Think of it as inflating a balloon around your normal data points in feature space. Anything outside the balloon is anomalous.

Learning the Boundary of Normal Data

Technically, One-Class SVM maps the data into a high-dimensional feature space using a kernel function (usually RBF) and then finds the hyperplane that separates the data from the origin with maximum margin. Points on the origin side of the hyperplane are anomalies.

Nu Parameter (Outlier Fraction)

The nu parameter (ν) sets an upper bound on the fraction of training points that the model is allowed to classify as anomalies, and a lower bound on the fraction of support vectors. If you set ν = 0.05, the model will allow up to 5% of training points to fall outside the boundary. This is similar to the contamination parameter in Isolation Forest — it encodes your prior belief about how much of the data is anomalous.

One-Class SVM works well for small to medium datasets with clear boundaries between normal and abnormal regions. It's expensive for large datasets (SVM training is O(n²) to O(n³) in the number of samples) and sensitive to the kernel choice and bandwidth parameter γ. In high-dimensional spaces with messy boundaries, Isolation Forest is usually the better default.

Autoencoder-Based Anomaly Detection

If you have access to deep learning (covered in later chapters), autoencoders offer a powerful semi-supervised approach. An autoencoder is a neural network that learns to compress data into a lower-dimensional representation and then reconstruct it. You train it exclusively on normal data.

High Reconstruction Error as Anomaly Signal

After training, feed a new data point through the autoencoder and measure the reconstruction error — how different the output is from the input. Normal points reconstruct well because the autoencoder has learned their patterns. Anomalous points reconstruct poorly because the autoencoder has never seen anything like them. High reconstruction error → anomaly.

Training on Normal Data Only

This is the semi-supervised setup. You curate a training set of "known normal" data (which is usually easy — last month's transactions minus confirmed fraud), train the autoencoder to minimize reconstruction error on that set, and then set a threshold. Any new point with reconstruction error above the threshold gets flagged.

Threshold Selection

The threshold is the hard part. Too low, and you flag normal points (false positives that annoy customers whose cards get blocked). Too high, and you miss anomalies (false negatives that cost the bank money). In practice, you plot the distribution of reconstruction errors on a validation set, pick a percentile (say, 99th), and iterate based on business feedback. There's no universal rule — the right threshold depends on the cost of false positives versus false negatives in your specific domain.

Evaluation: How Do You Know It's Working?

Evaluating anomaly detectors is harder than evaluating classifiers because the class distribution is so extreme.

Precision at k

Precision@k asks: if I examine the top k most suspicious points (ranked by anomaly score), what fraction are actually anomalous? This mirrors real operations. A fraud analyst can review maybe 200 alerts per day. If your model ranks anomalies so that 180 of the top 200 are real, that's Precision@200 = 0.9 — excellent. If only 20 are real, the analyst wastes 90% of their time on false alarms.

ROC-AUC for Anomaly Scoring

ROC-AUC evaluates the ranking quality across all thresholds. It answers: if I pick a random anomaly and a random normal point, what's the probability that the model assigns a higher anomaly score to the anomaly? AUC = 1.0 means perfect separation. AUC = 0.5 means random guessing. For anomaly detection, AUC is often more informative than accuracy because it doesn't depend on the threshold you choose.

Class Imbalance Challenges

Standard metrics lie under extreme imbalance. Accuracy is meaningless (always predicting "normal" gets 99.99%). Even F1-score can be misleading. Precision-Recall AUC (PR-AUC) is often better than ROC-AUC when the positive class (anomalies) is very rare, because it focuses on how well the model performs among its positive predictions rather than across the entire dataset.

In practice, evaluation often comes down to domain-specific metrics: dollars of fraud caught, number of system outages prevented, or reduction in false alarm rate. These matter more than any single number.

Time Series Anomaly Detection

Time series adds a wrinkle: order matters. A stock price of $50 is normal or anomalous depending on whether it was $48 yesterday or $200 yesterday. Anomalies in time series can be sudden spikes, gradual drifts, or changes in the pattern of variation itself.

Common approaches include sliding window statistics (flag if the current value deviates from the rolling mean by more than 3 rolling standard deviations), ARIMA residual analysis (fit a time series model and flag large residuals), and Prophet or STL decomposition (separate the signal into trend, seasonal, and residual components, then flag large residuals). For more complex patterns, LSTM autoencoders learn the temporal structure and flag sequences with high reconstruction error.

The unique challenge of time series is seasonality. A spike every Monday morning isn't an anomaly — it's the weekly pattern. Your detector must learn what's periodic and what's genuinely unusual, which requires enough historical data to observe multiple cycles.

Concept Drift in Anomaly Detection Systems

Concept drift is what happens when the definition of "normal" changes over time. In January, customers buy winter coats. In July, they buy swimsuits. A fraud model trained on January data might flag every swimsuit purchase in July as suspicious. The underlying data distribution has shifted, and your model hasn't kept up.

There are two types of drift. Gradual drift is a slow shift — customer spending patterns change over years. Sudden drift is an abrupt change — a new product launches, a pandemic hits, or a new fraud technique appears. Both degrade your model's performance over time.

The practical solution is retraining on recent data. Maintain a sliding window of the last N days of data, retrain your anomaly detector periodically (daily or weekly), and monitor the false positive rate. If it starts climbing, the model's concept of "normal" has drifted away from reality. Some teams use adaptive methods that continuously update the model, but periodic retraining is simpler and works well in most production settings.

⚡ Choosing Your Method: A Quick Guide

Small data, few features, roughly Gaussian: Z-score or Mahalanobis distance. Simple, fast, interpretable.

Mixed-density clusters: Local Outlier Factor. It adapts to local structure.

Large data, many features, no assumptions: Isolation Forest. Linear time, handles high dimensions, great default.

Small data, clear boundary: One-Class SVM. Powerful but expensive.

Complex patterns, deep learning available: Autoencoder. Learns subtle normal patterns.

Time series: Start with rolling statistics, graduate to LSTM autoencoders if needed.
