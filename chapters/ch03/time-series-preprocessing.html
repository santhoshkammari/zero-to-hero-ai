Privacy and Compliance

Chapter 3: Data Fundamentals 6 subtopics
TL;DR

Privacy in machine learning is not just a legal checkbox — it shapes trust, fairness, and the long-term viability of your models. Regulations like GDPR, CCPA, and HIPAA impose real constraints on how you collect, store, and use data, including the thorny problem of "unlearning" data from trained models. Anonymization techniques (k-anonymity, l-diversity, t-closeness) offer some protection but fail more often than you'd expect. Differential privacy gives you a mathematically rigorous guarantee by adding calibrated noise, at the cost of model utility. Federated learning keeps raw data on-device, reducing exposure. Synthetic data generation can sidestep privacy concerns entirely — if you can verify the generator didn't memorize the originals. Tying it all together requires a data governance framework: clear ownership, access controls, audit trails, and retention policies that your team actually follows.

Why Privacy Matters in ML

Uncomfortable truth: most machine learning systems are giant functions memorizing patterns from data — some data belongs to real people. If training set contains medical records, browsing histories, or financial transactions, model has, in compressed form, "seen" all of it. Reason to take privacy seriously. Before lawyer shows up.

Three reasons beyond legality. First, trust. Users share data expecting responsible handling. One breach or one headline about model leaking personal information, trust evaporates. Rebuilding trust costs more than getting privacy right from start.

Second, fairness. Privacy failures hit people unevenly. Minority groups and vulnerable populations take disproportionate harm from data leaks and re-identification attacks. Model trained on poorly anonymized health data does more than violate privacy — it can reinforce existing inequalities if exposure leads to discrimination.

Third, model risk. If you must delete someone's data after model is trained — and regulations increasingly say you must — what happens to model? Can you prove it no longer "remembers" that data? If not, deployed model becomes legal liability. Privacy not bolt-on feature. Constraint shaping entire pipeline.

GDPR, CCPA, and HIPAA Implications

Three regulations show up constantly in ML work. Each cares about different things.

General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) applies to anyone processing data of EU residents. Core principles: lawful basis for processing, data minimization (collect only what you need), purpose limitation (use only for stated purpose), and — part keeping ML engineers up at night — right to erasure. Article 17 says individuals can request deletion of personal data. If that data trained model, deleting it from database is not enough. Model weights may still encode information about that individual. Result: whole subfield called machine unlearning. More on that shortly.

California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) gives California residents right to know what data is collected about them, right to delete it, and right to opt out of its sale. For ML teams, "sale" has broad definition — sharing data with third-party model provider could qualify. CCPA is less prescriptive than GDPR about technical measures, but penalties for non-compliance are real.

HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) governs protected health information (PHI) in United States. If you're building ML models on patient data, HIPAA requires "minimum necessary" standard — access only PHI you need — and mandates specific safeguards for storage, transmission, and access. De-identification under HIPAA uses either "Safe Harbor" method (remove 18 specific identifiers) or "Expert Determination" method (statistician certifies re-identification risk is very small).

Practical impact on ML pipelines is significant. Data minimization means asking "do I need this feature?" before putting it in training set. Collecting everything "just in case" is sloppy engineering and compliance violation. Purpose limitation means model trained for fraud detection cannot be repurposed for marketing targeting without new consent. Right to erasure means tracking data lineage: which records went into which training runs, and what models came out.

Anonymization Techniques

Intuition behind anonymization: strip identifying information so record cannot be traced back to person. Reality: much harder than it sounds.

Pseudonymization replaces direct identifiers (names, emails) with tokens or codes. Lookup table maps tokens back to identities. This is not anonymization — reversible. GDPR treats pseudonymized data as personal data. It reduces risk, but does not remove compliance burden.

Anonymization, by contrast, is irreversible. Done properly, data is no longer personal data under GDPR. Hard part: doing it properly.

k-anonymity requires every record in dataset be indistinguishable from at least k−1 other records with respect to certain quasi-identifiers (like age, zip code, gender). If k=5, any combination of quasi-identifier values must appear in at least 5 rows.

# Before k-anonymity (k=2): each row is unique on (Age, Zip)
#   Age  Zip    Disease
#   29   10001  Flu
#   31   10001  Diabetes
#   29   10002  HIV
#   32   10002  Flu

# After generalization for k=2:
#   Age    Zip    Disease
#   29-31  1000*  Flu
#   29-31  1000*  Diabetes
#   29-32  1000*  HIV
#   29-32  1000*  Flu
# Now each quasi-identifier group has at least 2 records.

Problem: k-anonymity does not protect sensitive attribute. If all 5 people in group have same disease, knowing someone is in that group reveals diagnosis. This is homogeneity attack.

l-diversity fixes this by requiring each equivalence class have at least l "well-represented" values for sensitive attribute. If l=3, each group of quasi-identifier-matching records must contain at least 3 distinct diseases. This blocks homogeneity attack but stays vulnerable if sensitive-value distribution in group differs sharply from overall population.

t-closeness addresses this by requiring distribution of sensitive attribute in any equivalence class stay within distance t of attribute's overall distribution. Strongest of three. Also hardest to achieve without destroying data utility.

Honest tradeoff: real-world anonymization failures are common. In 2006, AOL released "anonymized" search logs; researchers re-identified users within days. Netflix's "anonymized" movie ratings were cross-referenced with public IMDb reviews to identify individuals. Lesson: quasi-identifiers are everywhere, and more auxiliary data makes re-identification easier. Anonymization reduces risk. Not guarantee.

Differential Privacy

If anonymization gives best-effort promise, differential privacy (DP) gives mathematical one. Core idea: output of analysis should stay roughly same whether any single individual's data is included or not.

Formally, randomized mechanism M satisfies ε-differential privacy if for any two datasets D and D′ differing in exactly one record, and for any set of possible outputs S:

P[M(D) ∈ S] ≤ e^ε × P[M(D′) ∈ S]

Parameter ε (epsilon) controls privacy budget. Smaller ε means stronger privacy but noisier results. In practice, ε values between 1 and 10 are common; ε below 1 means strong privacy; ε above 10 gives weak guarantees.

How to achieve this? Add calibrated noise. Laplace mechanism adds noise drawn from Laplace distribution scaled to query's sensitivity (how much one person's data can change output). Gaussian mechanism uses Gaussian noise and provides (ε, δ)-differential privacy, slightly relaxed variant where guarantee holds with probability 1−δ.

For ML specifically, key technique is DP-SGD (Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent). Idea: during training, clip each per-example gradient to bound its influence, then add Gaussian noise to aggregated gradient before updating weights.

# DP-SGD pseudocode
for each minibatch B:
    for each example x_i in B:
        g_i = compute_gradient(model, x_i)
        # Clip individual gradient to bound sensitivity
        g_i = g_i * min(1, C / ||g_i||)

    # Aggregate and add noise
    g_noisy = (1/|B|) * (sum(g_i) + N(0, σ²C²I))

    # Update model
    model.weights -= lr * g_noisy

# Privacy accounting: track cumulative ε across all steps
# Tools like Opacus (PyTorch) or TF Privacy handle this.

Tradeoff is real and unavoidable. More privacy (smaller ε) means more noise, which means lower model accuracy. In practice, DP-SGD models often lose 2–10% accuracy compared to non-private training, depending on task and dataset size. Larger datasets help because noise is amortized across more examples. Small datasets suffer more.

Common pitfall: people apply differential privacy to training step but forget hyperparameter tuning. Every validation-set evaluation and adjustment consumes privacy budget. Proper privacy accounting must cover entire pipeline, not only final training run.

Federated Learning (Data Aspects)

Federated learning flips usual ML paradigm. Instead of bringing data to model, you bring model to data. Each participant (phone, hospital, bank branch) trains local copy of model on its own data and sends only model updates — gradients or weight deltas — to central server, which aggregates them.

Privacy benefit is obvious: raw data never leaves device. Central server never sees individual patient's records or user's typing history. Google uses this for next-word prediction on Android keyboards; hospitals use it for collaborative diagnostic models without sharing patient data across institutions.

Standard aggregation protocol is Federated Averaging (FedAvg): each client runs several steps of SGD locally, then server averages resulting weight updates, weighted by each client's dataset size. More sophisticated protocols exist — secure aggregation uses cryptographic techniques so server can compute sum of updates without seeing any individual update.

But federated learning alone is not privacy guarantee. Model updates can leak information. Gradient inversion attacks show training images can be reconstructed from shared gradients, especially in early training rounds with small batch sizes. Production federated systems combine FL with differential privacy (adding noise to local updates before sharing) and secure aggregation for this reason.

Communication efficiency is practical concern. Sending full model updates over mobile networks is expensive. Techniques like gradient compression (sending only top-k largest gradient values), quantization (reducing precision from 32-bit to 8-bit or lower), and federated distillation (sharing predictions instead of gradients) reduce bandwidth. Each brings privacy and accuracy implications — compression, for instance, can accidentally leak more information about outlier data points.

Synthetic Data for Privacy

Appealing idea: instead of anonymizing real data, generate fake data with same statistical properties. Train model on synthetic version. No real person's data in training set, so no privacy violation. Right?

Mostly right — with important caveats.

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and diffusion models can produce synthetic tabular data, images, or time series closely matching distribution of real data. For tabular data, models like CTGAN learn joint distribution of features and generate new rows preserving correlations, marginal distributions, and complex multi-column dependencies.

# Using CTGAN (sdv library) to generate synthetic tabular data
from sdv.single_table import CTGANSynthesizer
from sdv.metadata import Metadata

metadata = Metadata.detect_from_dataframe(real_data)
synthesizer = CTGANSynthesizer(metadata, epochs=500)
synthesizer.fit(real_data)
synthetic_data = synthesizer.sample(num_rows=10000)

# Evaluate quality
from sdmetrics.reports.single_table import QualityReport
report = QualityReport()
report.generate(real_data, synthetic_data, metadata)
print(report.get_score())  # 0.0 to 1.0

Evaluating synthetic data quality matters. You need to check that synthetic data preserves statistical properties you care about (column distributions, pairwise correlations, downstream ML model performance) while not preserving individual-level details. Common metrics: column-wise distribution similarity, pairwise correlation comparison, and "train on synthetic, test on real" (TSTR) accuracy.

Big risk: memorization. If generative model overfits to training data, "synthetic" samples may be near-copies of real records. That defeats whole purpose. Test this by checking nearest-neighbor distances between synthetic and real records — if any synthetic record is suspiciously close to real one, you have memorization problem. Combining synthetic generation with differential privacy guarantees (DP-GANs) is gold standard, but it further reduces data quality.

Data Governance Frameworks

All techniques above are useless if organization lacks operational structure to enforce them. Data governance is boring, essential work of defining who owns data, who can access it, and what happens to it over time.

Data ownership means every dataset has named owner — not "data team" but specific person or role responsible for quality, access, and lifecycle. When incident occurs, ownership determines who gets paged. When deletion request arrives, ownership determines who acts.

Access controls should follow principle of least privilege. ML engineer building recommendation model does not need access to raw PII — they need access to feature-engineered, de-identified datasets. Role-based access control (RBAC) is minimum; attribute-based access control (ABAC) is better for complex environments where access depends on context (time of day, project, data sensitivity level).

Audit trails record every access, transformation, and model training run. When regulator asks "who accessed this patient's data and what did they do with it?", you need answer within hours, not weeks. Immutable audit logs — append-only, tamper-evident — are essential. Tools like Apache Atlas, Amundsen, or cloud-native solutions (AWS Lake Formation, GCP Data Catalog) can help, but tool matters less than discipline of using it consistently.

Data retention policies define how long data is kept and when it's deleted. GDPR's storage limitation principle says you should not keep personal data longer than necessary for its purpose. In ML terms, this means deciding: how long keep training data? What about model trained on it? What about cached predictions? Retention policy should cover entire data lifecycle from ingestion through model deployment through retirement.

Practical implementation often starts with data classification system: tag every dataset with sensitivity level (public, internal, confidential, restricted). Tie access controls and retention policies to these levels. Automate enforcement where possible — do not rely on people remembering to delete data after 90 days. Schedule it. Log it. Verify it.

Hardest part is not technology. It is getting engineers, product managers, and legal teams to agree on policies and follow them. Governance framework existing only in wiki page is worse than no framework at all — it creates false sense of security. Make governance part of CI/CD pipeline. Automated checks flagging PII in training data, alerting on unusual access patterns, and enforcing retention schedules do more than any policy document.

Privacy and compliance in ML is not solved. Regulations evolve. Attacks get more sophisticated. Protection techniques improve but always involve tradeoffs. What you can do today: build pipeline with privacy as first-class design constraint — not afterthought. Track data lineage. Minimize what you collect. Add formal privacy guarantees where you can. Build governance structures keeping everything accountable over time.
