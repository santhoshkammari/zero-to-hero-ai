Nice to Know

Chapter 13: ML Systems & Production Section 9 of 9

These are topics you'll encounter in the wild — in job postings, conference talks, or that one Slack thread where someone drops a term and everyone nods like they know what it means. You don't need to master any of them right now. Recognize them, know roughly what they solve, and look them up when the need actually hits.

The Topics

Federated Learning

Training ML models across multiple devices or organizations without centralizing the data. Each participant trains locally and shares only model updates (gradients/weights) with a coordinating server. The classic algorithm is FedAvg — average the weights, repeat. The hard parts are non-IID data across clients, communication overhead, and new privacy attack surfaces (gradient inversion can reconstruct training data from updates). Differential privacy and secure aggregation help, but add complexity.

When you need it: Healthcare (hospitals can't share patient data), mobile keyboards (Google's Gboard), cross-org fraud detection, or anywhere regulation/competition prevents data pooling.

On-Device ML

Running inference directly on phones, edge devices, or microcontrollers — no cloud round-trip. The main frameworks: TensorFlow Lite (widest hardware support, works everywhere), Core ML (Apple ecosystem, exploits the Neural Engine), ONNX Runtime Mobile (framework-agnostic), and PyTorch Mobile / ExecuTorch (stays in the PyTorch ecosystem). Models get quantized to INT8, pruned, and sometimes distilled to fit in kilobytes-to-megabytes of memory.

When you need it: Offline capability, latency-critical apps (AR, camera effects, voice), privacy-sensitive on-device processing, or when you want to stop paying cloud GPU bills for a million users.

Model Cards and Documentation Standards

A model card is a standardized document that ships with a model — what it was trained on, what it's good at, where it fails, known biases, intended use cases, and evaluation metrics broken down by demographic group. Think of it as a nutrition label for ML models. Google introduced the format in 2019; Hugging Face made it mainstream by baking model cards into every repo on the Hub.

When you need it: Regulated industries (finance, healthcare), open-source model releases, internal model registries, or any time someone asks "should we trust this model?" and you need a real answer.

ML Security

Models are attack surfaces. Adversarial attacks craft tiny input perturbations that fool classifiers (a stop sign with a few stickers reads as "speed limit 45"). Data poisoning corrupts training data to insert backdoors. Model stealing reconstructs your model by querying your API thousands of times. Prompt injection is the LLM-era flavor — tricking models into ignoring instructions. Defenses exist (adversarial training, input validation, rate limiting, output filtering) but it's an arms race.

When you need it: Any public-facing ML system. If users can send inputs to your model or see its outputs, someone will try to break it.

Green AI / Sustainable ML

Training GPT-4-scale models emits as much CO₂ as dozens of transatlantic flights. Green AI is about being intentional: choosing efficient architectures, using carbon-aware scheduling (train when the grid is running on renewables), picking data center regions with low carbon intensity, and reporting compute costs alongside accuracy. Tools like CodeCarbon and ML CO2 Impact track emissions during training runs.

When you need it: Large-scale training jobs, ESG reporting requirements, grant applications that ask about environmental impact, or when your conscience catches up with your GPU bill.

AutoML in Production

Automated machine learning — tools that handle feature engineering, model selection, hyperparameter tuning, and sometimes even deployment. Google AutoML, H2O AutoML, Auto-sklearn, and FLAML are the big names. They're not magic — they search a predefined space of models and configs, often finding good-enough solutions faster than a human would. The output is a regular model you can inspect and deploy normally.

When you need it: Rapid prototyping, baseline creation, teams without deep ML expertise, or when you have 50 modeling tasks and can't hand-tune each one.

Data Flywheel

The virtuous cycle where your product generates data, that data improves your model, the better model improves your product, and the better product generates more data. Tesla's autopilot is the textbook example — every mile driven by every car feeds back into training. The key engineering challenge is closing the loop: logging the right signals in production, identifying high-value examples (edge cases, user corrections), and feeding them back into retraining pipelines without manual intervention.

When you need it: Any system with user interaction data — search engines, recommendation systems, content moderation, or any product where users implicitly label data through their behavior.

Feature Flags for ML

The same concept from software engineering, applied to models. Instead of deploying a new model to everyone at once, you roll it out to 1% of traffic, then 5%, then 20%, watching metrics at each step. Tools like LaunchDarkly, Unleash, or even a simple config flag let you instantly roll back if the new model tanks. You can also use them for A/B testing models, running shadow deployments (new model scores requests but doesn't serve results), or canary releases.

When you need it: High-stakes production systems where a bad model deployment costs real money or trust. Also useful for managing multiple model versions across different user segments.

ML Testing Pyramid

Borrowed from software engineering's test pyramid. At the base: unit tests — does your feature transform produce the right output? Does your loss function compute correctly? In the middle: integration tests — does the full pipeline from raw data to prediction work end-to-end? Does the model load and serve correctly? At the top: system tests — does model quality meet minimum thresholds? Do predictions satisfy fairness constraints? Does the model behave correctly on known edge cases?

When you need it: Every production ML system. Seriously. The reason ML systems are fragile isn't that testing is hard — it's that almost nobody does it. Start with unit tests on your data transforms and a smoke test that the model loads and predicts. You'll catch 80% of production bugs.

The Point of This Section

None of these topics require deep expertise on day one. But knowing they exist — and roughly when they matter — means you won't be blindsided when they come up in a design review, a job interview, or a production incident. Bookmark the ones relevant to your domain and dig in when the time comes.

What You Should Now Be Able To Do

  • Explain federated learning and on-device ML at a high level, and know when each is the right architectural choice
  • Recognize model cards, ML security concerns, and Green AI as real engineering considerations — not just buzzwords
  • Describe what a data flywheel is and why it's a competitive moat
  • Articulate why ML testing and feature flags matter in production, even if your team hasn't adopted them yet
