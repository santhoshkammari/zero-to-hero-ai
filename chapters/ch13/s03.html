Training Data Management

Chapter 13: ML Systems & Production 4 subtopics
TL;DR

Getting enough high-quality labeled data is the bottleneck in most ML projects. This section covers the four strategies that production teams actually use: manual labeling platforms and when to invest in them, active learning to label smarter instead of more, weak supervision with Snorkel to generate noisy labels programmatically, and data augmentation to squeeze more out of every sample you have.

Data Labeling Platforms

Why Labeling Is Hard

Supervised learning needs labeled examples. That sounds simple until you try to do it at scale. You need 10,000 labeled images to train a decent object detector. Each image takes 30 seconds to label. That is 83 hours of human work — and that is before you account for quality control, disagreements between labelers, and the fact that your labeling guidelines will change three times as you discover edge cases.

This is why data labeling platforms exist. They provide tools for distributing labeling work to teams of annotators, managing task queues, enforcing quality standards, and tracking progress. The major options include Label Studio (open source), Labelbox, Scale AI, Amazon SageMaker Ground Truth, and Prodigy.

Building a Labeling Workflow

A good labeling workflow has five components. First, clear labeling guidelines — a document that tells annotators exactly how to label each case, with examples of common edge cases. "Is this image of a hot dog or a sandwich?" seems obvious until someone submits a bratwurst in a bun. Second, a pilot round where a small team labels a batch and you measure inter-annotator agreement. If two annotators disagree 30% of the time, your guidelines are ambiguous. Third, quality control — embed known-answer questions in the task stream so you can catch annotators who are clicking randomly. Fourth, adjudication — when annotators disagree, a senior reviewer makes the final call. Fifth, versioning — track which guidelines were used for which batch, so you can retrain when guidelines change.

Measuring Label Quality

Inter-annotator agreement is the most important metric for labeling quality. If two independent annotators agree 95% of the time, your labels are reliable. If they agree 60% of the time, your model is learning from noise. Cohen's kappa and Krippendorff's alpha are standard measures that account for chance agreement — a kappa of 0.8 or higher is generally considered strong agreement.

When agreement is low, the problem is almost always the guidelines, not the annotators. Make the guidelines more specific, add examples, and re-pilot.

Active Learning for Labeling

The Core Idea

Labeling everything is expensive. But not all examples are equally useful. A model learns very little from an image that is obviously a cat — it already knows that. It learns a lot from an image it is uncertain about — the blurry photo that could be a cat or a small dog. Active learning selects the most informative examples for labeling, so you get more model improvement per label dollar spent.

The process is iterative. Train a model on your current labeled set. Use the model to score unlabeled examples. Select the examples the model is most uncertain about. Send those to annotators. Add the new labels to your training set. Retrain. Repeat.

import numpy as np
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression

def active_learning_loop(labeled_X, labeled_y, unlabeled_X, budget=100):
    """Select the most uncertain examples for labeling."""
    model = LogisticRegression()
    model.fit(labeled_X, labeled_y)

    # Get predicted probabilities for unlabeled data
    probs = model.predict_proba(unlabeled_X)

    # Uncertainty sampling: select examples closest to the decision boundary
    # For binary classification, most uncertain = probability closest to 0.5
    uncertainty = 1 - np.max(probs, axis=1)

    # Select top-k most uncertain examples
    selected_indices = np.argsort(uncertainty)[-budget:]

    return selected_indices  # Send these to annotators

Acquisition Strategies

There are several ways to decide which examples to label next. Uncertainty sampling picks examples where the model is least confident — this is the simplest and most common approach. Query-by-committee trains multiple models and picks examples where they disagree most. Expected model change picks examples that would cause the largest update to model parameters if labeled. Diversity sampling ensures selected examples are spread across the feature space, not clustered in one region.

In practice, uncertainty sampling works well for most problems. The more sophisticated strategies help when your unlabeled data has large redundant clusters — diversity sampling prevents you from labeling 100 near-identical examples.

Pitfalls

Active learning has a cold-start problem. If your initial model is terrible (because you have very few labels), it selects poorly. Start with a small random sample (a few hundred examples) to bootstrap the model before switching to active selection. Also, active learning creates a biased training set — the model only sees examples it was uncertain about, which can miss entire categories it was confidently wrong about. Periodically add random samples to counteract this bias.

Weak Supervision with Snorkel

The Core Idea

What if you could write simple rules to generate labels instead of labeling by hand? The labels would be noisy — each rule gets some examples wrong — but if you have many rules and combine them cleverly, the aggregate signal can be surprisingly strong. This is weak supervision.

Snorkel is the framework that popularized this approach. You write labeling functions — Python functions that take a data point and return a label or abstain. Each function encodes a heuristic, a pattern, or a rule of thumb. Snorkel then uses a generative model to estimate the accuracy of each labeling function and combine their votes into probabilistic labels.

from snorkel.labeling import labeling_function, PandasLFApplier, LFAnalysis
from snorkel.labeling.model import LabelModel

SPAM = 1
NOT_SPAM = 0
ABSTAIN = -1

@labeling_function()
def lf_contains_free(x):
    """Emails containing 'free' are likely spam."""
    return SPAM if "free" in x.text.lower() else ABSTAIN

@labeling_function()
def lf_short_email(x):
    """Very short emails are usually not spam."""
    return NOT_SPAM if len(x.text.split()) < 10 else ABSTAIN

@labeling_function()
def lf_known_sender(x):
    """Emails from contacts are not spam."""
    return NOT_SPAM if x.sender in known_contacts else ABSTAIN

@labeling_function()
def lf_all_caps_subject(x):
    """Subjects in ALL CAPS are likely spam."""
    return SPAM if x.subject == x.subject.upper() and len(x.subject) > 5 else ABSTAIN

@labeling_function()
def lf_contains_unsubscribe(x):
    """Marketing emails with unsubscribe links."""
    return SPAM if "unsubscribe" in x.text.lower() else ABSTAIN

# Apply labeling functions to unlabeled data
lfs = [lf_contains_free, lf_short_email, lf_known_sender,
       lf_all_caps_subject, lf_contains_unsubscribe]
applier = PandasLFApplier(lfs=lfs)
L_train = applier.apply(df=train_df)

# Check coverage and overlap
print(LFAnalysis(L=L_train, lfs=lfs).lf_summary())

# Train the label model to combine noisy votes
label_model = LabelModel(cardinality=2, verbose=True)
label_model.fit(L_train=L_train, n_epochs=500, lr=0.001)

# Get probabilistic labels
prob_labels = label_model.predict_proba(L=L_train)

How Snorkel Combines Noisy Labels

The magic of Snorkel is the label model. It learns the accuracy and correlation structure of your labeling functions without any ground-truth labels. If labeling function A and labeling function B always agree, and labeling function C often disagrees with both, the label model learns that A and B are probably more accurate than C. It then produces a weighted vote that accounts for each function's reliability.

The resulting labels are probabilistic — instead of a hard 0 or 1, you get a probability like 0.87. You can train your downstream model using these soft labels (noise-aware training) or threshold them into hard labels.

When to Use Weak Supervision

Weak supervision works best when domain experts can articulate patterns that distinguish classes, even if those patterns are imperfect. It is particularly valuable when you have a lot of unlabeled data but labeling is expensive — medical records, legal documents, specialized industrial applications. It does not replace manual labeling entirely. You still need a small manually-labeled validation set to evaluate how well your labeling functions (and the final model) actually perform.

Data Augmentation at Scale

The Core Idea

Data augmentation creates new training examples by applying transformations to existing ones. Flip an image horizontally, and you have a "new" training example that teaches the model that objects can face either direction. The label stays the same — a flipped cat is still a cat. Augmentation is essentially free labeled data.

Image Augmentation

Computer vision has the richest augmentation toolkit. Standard transforms include random cropping, flipping, rotation, color jitter (brightness, contrast, saturation), and scaling. More advanced techniques include Cutout (randomly masking rectangular regions), Mixup (blending two images and their labels), and CutMix (pasting a patch from one image onto another).

import albumentations as A
from albumentations.pytorch import ToTensorV2

# Production augmentation pipeline
train_transform = A.Compose([
    A.RandomResizedCrop(height=224, width=224, scale=(0.8, 1.0)),
    A.HorizontalFlip(p=0.5),
    A.ColorJitter(brightness=0.2, contrast=0.2, saturation=0.2, hue=0.1, p=0.8),
    A.GaussianBlur(blur_limit=(3, 7), p=0.3),
    A.CoarseDropout(max_holes=8, max_height=32, max_width=32, p=0.5),  # Cutout
    A.Normalize(mean=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406], std=[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]),
    ToTensorV2(),
])

# AutoAugment / RandAugment: learned augmentation policies
# RandAugment applies N random transforms at magnitude M
rand_augment = A.Compose([
    A.RandAugment(num_ops=2, magnitude=9),  # simple and effective
    A.Normalize(mean=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406], std=[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]),
    ToTensorV2(),
])

RandAugment simplified augmentation tuning dramatically. Instead of choosing which transforms to apply and at what probability, you set just two numbers: N (how many transforms to apply) and M (how strong they are). This works surprisingly well and has become the default for many vision tasks.

Text Augmentation

Text is trickier because small changes can alter meaning. "The bank approved the loan" and "The bank rejected the loan" differ by one word but have opposite meanings. Safe text augmentations include back-translation (translate to another language and back — "the cat sat on the mat" → "le chat était assis sur le tapis" → "the cat was sitting on the rug"), synonym replacement, random insertion of related words, and random deletion of non-essential words.

For NLP, paraphrasing with large language models has become a powerful augmentation strategy. You prompt an LLM: "Rephrase the following sentence while preserving its meaning: [original]". The outputs are diverse and natural-sounding, though you should verify that labels are preserved.

Audio Augmentation

For speech and audio models, standard augmentations include SpecAugment (masking random frequency bands and time steps in the spectrogram), time stretching (making audio faster or slower without changing pitch), pitch shifting, and adding background noise. SpecAugment is simple and remarkably effective — it was key to Google's state-of-the-art speech recognition results.

Pitfalls

Augmentation can hurt if the transformations are unrealistic or label-destroying. Rotating a digit "6" by 180 degrees makes it a "9" — the label is now wrong. Aggressive color jitter on medical images can remove the very patterns the model needs to detect. Always validate that your augmentation policy improves validation performance. And never augment the validation or test set — those must reflect real data.
