Probabilistic Models & Inference

Graphical models compress exponentially large joint probability tables by encoding direct variable dependencies. Judea Pearl formalized them in 1980s; they became dominant framework for reasoning under uncertainty. Today backbone of causal inference, medical diagnosis, probabilistic programming, and latent-variable structure inside VAEs.

The Problem with Giant Probability Tables

Start concrete. Build diagnostic system for small clinic. Patients might have three conditions: cold, flu, or allergies. Doctor observes four symptoms: fever, cough, runny nose, and fatigue. Each variable binary — present or absent.

Brute-force representation: joint probability table over every disease-symptom combination. Seven binary variables means 27 = 128 combinations, each with its own probability. Sum-to-one constraint leaves 127 numbers.

Manageable now. Clinic grows? Add ten diseases and twenty symptoms. Now 30 binary variables and 230 − 1 ≈ one billion entries. Real hospital system with hundreds of variables? Table bigger than atom count in observable universe.

Fundamental problem: joint probability distributions grow exponentially. Need compression. Key insight: most variables do not directly interact with most others. Fever depends on flu, not hospital parking-lot fullness. Write down which variables directly influence which others, and giant table factors into many small tables.

That is graphical models. Map — literal picture — of which variables talk to which. Have map, math becomes tractable.

Drawing the Story: Bayesian Networks

Bayesian network = directed acyclic graph — DAG — where each node is random variable and each arrow points from cause to effect. “Directed” means arrows have direction. “Acyclic” means you cannot follow arrows in a circle back to start. Family tree for variables: parents influence children, children do not influence parents.

Back to clinic. Cold can cause cough and runny nose. Flu can cause fever, cough, and fatigue. Allergies can cause runny nose and fatigue. Draw graph:

  Cold          Flu         Allergies
   │ \          / | \          / |
   │  \        /  |  \        /  |
   │   ↓      ↓   ↓   ↓     ↓   ↓
   │  Cough  Fever │  Fatigue  Runny Nose
   │    ↑          │    ↑        ↑
   └────┘          └────┘        │
                                 │
   (Cold also causes Cough)   (Allergies also cause Runny Nose)

Power of picture: full joint distribution over all seven variables factors into product of small conditional probability tables, one per node:

P(Cold, Flu, Allergies, Fever, Cough, Runny, Fatigue)

  = P(Cold) × P(Flu) × P(Allergies)
    × P(Fever | Flu)
    × P(Cough | Cold, Flu)
    × P(Runny | Cold, Allergies)
    × P(Fatigue | Flu, Allergies)

Each node’s probability depends only on its parents in graph. Factoring rule for Bayesian networks. Instead of 127 numbers for full joint table, we need: 1 number for P(Cold), 1 for P(Flu), 1 for P(Allergies), 1 for P(Fever | Flu), 3 for P(Cough | Cold, Flu), 3 for P(Runny | Cold, Allergies), and 3 for P(Fatigue | Flu, Allergies). That’s 13 numbers, not 127. Savings grow exponentially as more variables are added.

Why is throwing away joint probabilities okay? Conditional independence. Drawing arrows this way asserts, for example, that once you know flu status, fever is independent of allergy status. Modeling assumption. Must be roughly right. When right, compression is dramatic.

Each small table — P(Fever | Flu), P(Cough | Cold, Flu), and so on — is a conditional probability table, or CPT. Each CPT lists probability of each outcome for every parent combination. Node with no parents (like Cold, Flu, Allergies) has simpler table: single probability.

When There’s No Arrow Direction: Markov Random Fields

Bayesian networks work when causal story exists — diseases cause symptoms, rain causes wet grass, burglar causes alarm. Sometimes relationships are symmetric. Neighboring pixels in image tend to have similar colors, but neither pixel “causes” other. Amino acids near each other in protein interact, but interaction has no direction.

For these situations, use a Markov random field (MRF), also called undirected graphical model. Edges have no arrows. Instead of conditional probability tables, MRFs use potential functions — also called factors — that score how “compatible” neighboring variable assignments are. High score means values fit together. Low score means they don’t.

Joint distribution in an MRF looks like this:

P(X₁, ..., Xₙ) = (1/Z) × ∏ ψ_c(X_c)
                            cliques c

Each ψc is potential function over a clique — group of variables all connected to each other. Z is partition function, normalizing constant that makes probabilities sum to one.

Trouble starts at Z. To compute Z, sum product of all potentials over every possible configuration of every variable. For n binary variables, that’s 2n terms. Exact probabilities in an MRF are generally intractable — reason we need approximate inference later.

Difference: Bayesian network tells generative story: disease happens, then symptoms appear. You can simulate data by following arrows forward. MRF describes correlations: variables tend to agree, without saying who caused whom. Bayesian networks fit diagnosis and causal reasoning. MRFs fit images, spatial data, and physics. Ising model from statistical mechanics — granddaddy of undirected models — is an MRF.

Plate Notation: Handling Repetition Without Losing Your Mind

Back to clinic. Suppose we see not one patient but thousand. Each patient has own diseases and symptoms, but all share same disease prevalence rates and same symptom-disease relationships. Drawing thousand copies of same graph would be absurd.

Plate notation solves this with rectangle. Draw variables for one patient, wrap them in box (the “plate”), write “N = 1000” in corner. Everything inside plate repeats N times. Everything outside plate is shared across repetitions.

   θ  ← disease prevalence (shared across all patients)
   │
   ↓
 ┌──────────────────────┐
 │  Disease_i            │
 │     │                 │  N = 1000
 │     ↓                 │
 │  Symptoms_i           │
 └──────────────────────┘

Parameter θ sits outside plate — one copy, shared by everyone. Each patient’s disease and symptoms sit inside — own independent copy. This is how you represent model with thousand data points using diagram size of napkin sketch.

Plates can nest. Clinic with multiple departments? Outer plate for departments, inner plate for patients within each department. Notation scales to arbitrarily complex hierarchical models — exactly what probabilistic programming languages like PyMC and Stan are built to handle.

Rule: if variable is specific to each data point, it goes inside plate. If it’s shared across data points (parameter you’re trying to learn), it goes outside. Shaded nodes mean observed data. Unshaded nodes mean latent (hidden) variables. That’s entire visual vocabulary.

D-Separation: Reading Independence Off the Graph

Here graphical models become powerful, not merely pretty. Given graph, you can determine which variables are conditionally independent of which others by looking at picture. No math required. Algorithm is d-separation.

Three fundamental connection patterns. Everything else builds from these three. Walk through clinic examples.

The Chain. Flu → Fever → HospitalVisit. Flu causes fever, fever causes hospital visit. Without other information, learning flu status tells you something about hospital visit. Once fever is known, learning flu gives no additional information about hospital visit. Fever “screens off” flu from hospital visit. Observing middle variable blocks information flow.

The Fork. Cough ← Cold → RunnyNose. Cold is common cause of both symptoms. If you see cough, you might guess runny nose too, because both suggest cold. If you already know cold is present, cough tells you nothing new about runny nose. Known common cause makes effects independent.

The Collider. Common trap. Cold → Cough ← Flu. Both cold and flu can cause cough. Normally, having cold tells you nothing about flu — they’re independent. Observe cough. Then learn patient doesn’t have cold. That increases belief in flu. Observing common effect creates dependency between causes that was not there before. This is explaining away.

Collider intuition: when you see effect with multiple possible causes, ruling out one cause makes other causes more likely. Reason conditioning on collider, or any descendant of collider, can introduce bias in statistical analyses — critical insight for causal inference.

Full d-separation algorithm checks whether every path between two variables is “blocked” by observed variables, using these three patterns. If every path is blocked, variables are conditionally independent given observations. If even one path is unblocked, they’re dependent.

Asking Questions: Exact Inference

Graphical model is map. Inference uses map to answer questions. Common question: given observed evidence (patient has fever and cough), what is probability of each unobserved variable (do they have flu)?

For small or nicely structured graphs, we can answer exactly.

Variable elimination works by summing out (marginalizing) one variable at a time. Simple idea: if you don’t care about variable, sum over all its possible values. Trace tiny clinic example.

Say we want P(Flu | Cough=yes). We need to eliminate (sum out) Cold, Allergies, Fever, RunnyNose, and Fatigue. Pick elimination order. For each variable, multiply all factors that mention it, sum over its values, and produce new, smaller factor. When unwanted variables are gone, normalize what’s left.

Catch: elimination order matters enormously. Bad order can produce exponentially large intermediate factors. Finding optimal elimination order is NP-hard. In practice, heuristics like “eliminate variable that creates smallest new factor” work well enough.

Belief propagation takes different approach: message passing. Each node sends “messages” to neighbors summarizing beliefs. On trees — graphs with no loops — this is exact. Messages flow leaves to root, then root back to leaves. Two passes, and every node gets its marginal probability. This is sum-product algorithm: each node talks only to neighbors, and whole system converges in linear time.

On graphs with loops, belief propagation is not exact anymore. But “loopy belief propagation” — same message-passing rules on loopy graph — often works surprisingly well. No convergence guarantee. Sometimes oscillates or diverges. In practice, for error-correcting codes (turbo codes, LDPC codes) and image segmentation, loopy BP has been wildly successful.

There’s also junction tree algorithm, which transforms any graph into tree of cliques and then runs exact belief propagation on that tree. General-purpose exact inference algorithm. Cost is exponential in treewidth of graph — measure of how “tree-like” graph is. For chain-shaped or tree-shaped graphs, treewidth is small and inference is fast. For densely connected graphs like grid-shaped MRFs over images, treewidth is too large for practical use.

Fundamental tension. Exact inference is beautiful when it works. For most interesting models, graph is too big, too loopy, or too densely connected. Need approximations.

When Exact Answers Are Too Expensive: The Approximation Fork

Clinic model with seven variables? Exact inference is instant. Hospital network with few hundred variables? Intractable. Continuous latent variables in a VAE or Gaussian process? Exact inference is not even on table.

Field has split into two philosophies for decades. Understanding both is core skill in probabilistic ML.

Sampling philosophy: cannot write posterior in closed form, but can draw samples from it. Draw enough samples, estimate any quantity you care about — mean, variance, probability parameter is positive, anything. This family is Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC).

Optimization philosophy: forget true posterior. Pick family of simple distributions and find member closest to true posterior. Turn inference problem into optimization problem. This family is variational inference (VI).

Mountain analogy. Sampling sends thousand hikers with altimeters across mountain and averages readings. Slow but accurate. Optimization fits smooth surface to satellite images. Fast but misses craggy details.

Walk through each family. Start with sampling.

The Sampling Path: MCMC

MCMC idea: construct Markov chain — sequence of random states where each state depends only on previous one — whose long-run behavior (its stationary distribution) is exactly posterior we want to sample from. Run chain long enough, and collected samples follow p(θ|D), true posterior.

“Monte Carlo” means random sampling to estimate quantities. “Markov chain” describes mechanism generating those samples. Together, MCMC means: generate random samples by running carefully designed chain of random steps.

Make this concrete. Back to clinic, but now with continuous parameter: estimate θ, true prevalence of flu in patient population, given 12 flu cases out of 100 patients.

Metropolis-Hastings: The Random Walk

Metropolis-Hastings is foundational MCMC method, published in 1953 — older than most of computer science. Here is mechanism.

Imagine standing on landscape where height at each point is posterior probability. Goal: spend more time in high regions (likely parameter values) and less time in low regions (unlikely values). You cannot see whole landscape at once. You can only measure height under your feet.

Algorithm: stand at point θ. Propose random step — small random distance in random direction. That gives candidate point θ′. Then check: is proposed point higher or lower than current point?

If θ′ is higher (more probable), move there. Always. If θ′ is lower, you might still move there, with probability proportional to how much lower it is. Slightly lower? Probably accept. Dramatically lower? Almost certainly reject and stay put.

Acceptance probability: α = min(1, p(θ′|D) / p(θ|D)). Key part: this ratio cancels normalizing constant. You never compute intractable denominator in Bayes’ theorem. Ratio of unnormalized posteriors is enough.

import numpy as np

def metropolis_hastings(log_target, initial, n_samples, proposal_std):
    """MH sampler: the random walk through posterior space."""
    samples = np.zeros(n_samples)
    current = initial
    current_lp = log_target(current)
    accepted = 0

    for i in range(n_samples):
        # Propose: take a random step
        proposed = current + np.random.normal(0, proposal_std)
        proposed_lp = log_target(proposed)

        # Accept/reject: compare heights
        # (Working in log space for numerical stability)
        if np.log(np.random.uniform()) < (proposed_lp - current_lp):
            current = proposed
            current_lp = proposed_lp
            accepted += 1

        samples[i] = current

    print(f"Acceptance rate: {accepted / n_samples:.1%}")
    return samples

Entire algorithm. Propose, compare, accept or reject, repeat. After enough iterations, histogram of collected samples approximates posterior distribution.

Problem: “enough iterations” can be very large. In one dimension, random walk explores space reasonably fast. In ten dimensions, sluggish. In hundred dimensions, nearly useless. Random walk takes tiny steps, and each step changes parameter value only a little. In high-dimensional spaces, chance of proposing point in high-probability region becomes vanishingly small.

Need smarter ways to move through space.

Gibbs Sampling: One Variable at a Time

One idea: instead of proposing all parameters at once, update them one at time. For each parameter θi, sample new value from conditional distribution p(θi | everything else). If these conditionals have nice closed forms — common in conjugate models — every proposal is automatically accepted. No rejection. No wasted steps.

This is Gibbs sampling. For decades, Bayesian-computation workhorse. Software packages like BUGS and JAGS were built around it. Latent Dirichlet Allocation (topic model) became practical through Gibbs sampling.

Weakness shows up when variables are strongly correlated. Imagine posterior has long, narrow diagonal ridge — like tilted ellipse. Gibbs can move only horizontally or vertically, one variable at time. It takes many tiny steps to crawl along diagonal. Like walking diagonally across city with only north-south/east-west streets. You get there. Lots of zigzagging.

For models with many strongly correlated parameters — most interesting models — we need method that takes big, informed steps along posterior contours.

Hamiltonian Monte Carlo: Physics to the Rescue

This method made modern Bayesian inference work on real problems. Physics connection is key.

Key insight: replace random walk with physical system. Negative log-posterior becomes landscape — specifically, potential energy surface. High-probability regions are valleys (low potential energy); low-probability regions are peaks (high potential energy). Put ball on surface and give random kick. Ball rolls, gaining speed in valleys and slowing on hills, following Hamiltonian mechanics.

Ball needs momentum — velocity in parameter space. Each HMC step does two things: randomly sample new momentum (random kick), then simulate ball rolling along surface for fixed number of steps. Final position becomes proposed sample. Because ball follows posterior gradient — rolling downhill into high-probability regions — proposals tend to be far away and in high-probability areas.

The physics:
  Position θ     ↔  parameter values
  Potential energy U(θ)  ↔  -log p(θ|D)
  Momentum p     ↔  auxiliary variable (sampled fresh each step)
  Total energy H(θ,p) = U(θ) + K(p)  ↔  potential + kinetic

Hamilton's equations:
  dθ/dt = p           (position changes according to momentum)
  dp/dt = -∇U(θ)      (momentum changes according to gradient)
         = ∇ log p(θ|D)   ← this is the crucial ingredient

Gradient — slope of posterior surface — makes HMC dramatically more efficient than random-walk methods. In 100 dimensions, random walk does not know which direction to go. HMC follows gradient, rolling along posterior contours like marble in bowl.

But HMC has two hyperparameters to tune: step size (how finely we simulate physics) and number of leapfrog steps (how long ball rolls before stopping). Get these wrong, and HMC can be worse than random walk.

NUTS — No-U-Turn Sampler — removes this tuning burden. It runs simulation forward and backward in time, builds tree of possible trajectories, and stops when trajectory starts doubling back on itself (“U-turn”). NUTS makes Stan and PyMC practical for working scientists. HMC without hand-tuning. Works well across wide range of models.

Important requirement: HMC needs gradient of log-posterior. Model must be differentiable. Reason modern probabilistic programming languages are built on automatic differentiation engines — PyMC on Aesara/PyTensor, NumPyro on JAX, Stan on its own AD system. They compute gradient for you, so you write model and sampler handles physics.

Formal reason physics simulation preserves right distribution: Hamiltonian dynamics are volume-preserving (Liouville’s theorem) and time-reversible, which guarantees detailed balance. Mental image that helps: ball rolling on posterior surface.

The Optimization Path: Variational Inference

MCMC gives true posterior — eventually. But “eventually” might mean hours or days of sampling. If you have millions of data points, need answer in milliseconds, or want end-to-end training of deep generative model with gradient descent, sampling is too slow.

Variational inference takes different route. Instead of sampling true posterior, we approximate it. Choose family of simple, tractable distributions — maybe Gaussians, maybe something else — and find member closest to true posterior. Inference problem becomes optimization problem, and optimization runs fast.

Earlier mountain analogy. MCMC sends hikers through every ridge and valley. VI takes satellite photo and fits smooth surface matching data. Faster, but can smooth away craggy bits.

“Closest” is measured by KL divergence, which quantifies how different one probability distribution is from another. Goal: minimize KL(q || p) — divergence from simple approximation q(θ) to true posterior p(θ|D). But computing this KL divergence requires p(θ|D), exact thing we can’t compute. We seem stuck.

ELBO solves this.

The ELBO: The Quantity That Makes It All Work

Mathematical identity saves us. Log-evidence — log p(D), total probability of observed data — decomposes as:

log p(D) = ELBO(q) + KL(q || p)

Left side is fixed constant (it doesn’t depend on q). KL divergence on right is always non-negative. So ELBO is always less than or equal to log p(D) — lower bound on log-evidence. ELBO stands for Evidence Lower BOund.

Trick: since log p(D) is constant, maximizing ELBO is exactly equivalent to minimizing KL(q || p). We can’t compute KL divergence directly, but we can maximize ELBO and get same target.

ELBO decomposes into two terms we can compute:

ELBO(q) = E_q[log p(D|θ)]  −  KL(q(θ) || p(θ))
           ─────────────────    ──────────────────
           "How well does θ     "How far is q from
            explain the data?"   the prior?"

First term rewards q for concentrating on parameter values that explain data well. Second term penalizes q for straying too far from prior. Same fit-versus-regularization tradeoff seen across machine learning, written in language of probability.

Main insight is not algebra. We wanted impossible objective — minimize KL to true posterior. We found surrogate objective — ELBO — that achieves same thing and that we can compute. That’s whole move.

Mean-Field: The Crudest Possible Approximation

Need family of distributions for q. Biggest simplification is mean-field approximation: assume all latent variables are independent. Variational distribution factors as:

q(θ₁, θ₂, ..., θₙ) = q₁(θ₁) × q₂(θ₂) × ... × qₙ(θₙ)

Each variable gets its own distribution, optimized independently. Optimization algorithm — coordinate ascent variational inference (CAVI) — cycles through variables one at a time, updating each qi while others stay fixed, until convergence.

This is crude. The true posterior might have strong correlations between parameters — maybe θ1 and θ2 are tightly coupled, so knowing one tells you a lot about the other. Mean-field ignores this entirely. It treats them as independent, which typically underestimates the posterior uncertainty. The marginal distributions might be centered in the right place, but they’re too narrow and too confident.

There’s a deeper problem. KL(q || p) is mode-seeking. When the true posterior has multiple peaks (modes), q tends to collapse onto a single peak and pretend the others don’t exist. It finds one good explanation and ignores alternatives. For a multimodal posterior — common in mixture models, multi-layer neural networks, and other models with symmetries — mean-field VI gives a misleadingly simple picture of the uncertainty.

Despite these limitations, mean-field is fast, GPU-friendly, and often gives reasonable point estimates. For many applications — especially when you need a quick answer and the uncertainty isn’t critical — it’s good enough. The key is knowing what it gets wrong.

Modern approaches improve on mean-field by using richer variational families. Full-rank Gaussian approximations capture correlations. Normalizing flows can represent complex, multimodal distributions. Each step toward a richer family gives a tighter ELBO bound and a better approximation, at the cost of more computation.

Amortized Inference: Teaching a Neural Network to Do Inference

Standard VI optimizes variational parameters separately for each data point. If you have a million images and each one has its own latent variable z, that’s a million separate optimization problems. Slow.

Amortized inference solves this by training a neural network — the encoder or recognition network — to predict the variational parameters directly from the data. Instead of optimizing q(z|x) for each x individually, you learn a function that maps any x to the parameters of q(z|x) in a single forward pass.

This is exactly what a Variational Autoencoder does. The VAE encoder takes an image x and outputs the mean μ(x) and variance σ²(x) of a Gaussian q(z|x). The decoder takes a sample from that Gaussian and reconstructs the image. The training loss is the negative ELBO:

VAE loss = -ELBO
         = reconstruction error  +  KL(q(z|x) || p(z))
           ──────────────────────    ──────────────────
           "How good is the          "How far is the
            reconstruction?"          posterior from the prior?"

Every time you train a VAE, you’re doing amortized variational inference. The encoder has learned to perform approximate inference in a single forward pass — no iterative optimization at test time. The “amortization” is that the cost of learning to do inference is spread across the entire training set, and once learned, inference for a new data point is nearly free.

There’s one technical detail that makes this trainable: the reparameterization trick. We can’t backpropagate through a sampling operation (sampling z from q(z|x) is non-differentiable). The trick is to rewrite the sampling as: z = μ(x) + σ(x) × ε, where ε is random noise sampled from a standard Gaussian N(0,1). Now ε is independent of the network parameters, and we can backpropagate through μ(x) and σ(x). The randomness is factored out. The gradient flows through.

Choosing Your Path: When to Use Which

After all of this, the practical question: which method should you actually use?

Here is how I think about it. Start with the data size. If you have millions of data points and need results in minutes, VI is almost certainly the right choice. MCMC can’t process a million-row dataset without specialized tricks (stochastic MCMC exists but is still an active research area). For datasets with hundreds to tens of thousands of observations, both approaches are viable.

Next, ask how much you trust the uncertainty estimates. If you’re building a recommendation system and the uncertainty is nice-to-have, VI is fine. If you’re making medical decisions, or if your scientific paper will be judged on the faithfulness of its posterior estimates, use MCMC (specifically NUTS). Reviewers in Bayesian statistics will expect convergence diagnostics: R̂ < 1.01, effective sample sizes above 400, no divergent transitions, and trace plots that look like hairy caterpillars.

For deep generative models (VAEs, normalizing flows, diffusion models with latent variables), amortized VI is the standard. These models have too many latent variables and too much data for MCMC to be feasible. The entire training loop is built around optimizing the ELBO.

A pattern I’ve found useful in practice: start with VI to prototype quickly. Get the model structure right, check that the results are sensible. Then, for the final analysis, validate with MCMC on a representative subset. If the VI and MCMC posteriors agree, you can trust the VI results on the full dataset. If they disagree, the MCMC results are the ones to trust.

My favorite thing about this whole landscape is that MCMC and VI aren’t really competitors — they’re complementary tools for different regimes. The field spent years arguing about which was better. The answer, like most things in engineering, is: it depends.
