Collection of advanced Bayesian and probabilistic concepts at edge of mainstream ML practice. Some — like Bayesian deep learning and conformal prediction — now show up in production systems. Others — like information geometry and PAC-Bayes bounds — mostly live in research papers but shape how we think about learning. Senior practitioner should recognize all of them.

Bayesian Deep Learning

Uncomfortable truth about standard neural networks: give number with no signal whether number is trustworthy. Ask classifier trained on cats and dogs about toaster, it confidently says “dog, 92%.” No mechanism to say “I have absolutely no idea what this is.”

Imagine building diagnostic model for small rural clinic. Model reads chest X-rays and flags potential tuberculosis cases. Trained on data from large urban hospitals. When patient arrives with unusual presentation model never saw, you do not want confident wrong answer. You want model to raise hand and say “this one needs a human.” Promise of Bayesian deep learning: neural networks that carry uncertainty through every prediction.

Core idea: learn distribution over weights instead of single fixed value. Average predictions across plausible weight configurations. Prediction spread shows confidence.

Core challenge: ResNet-50 has about 25 million weights. Full probability distribution over each weight is astronomically expensive. Nobody does that in practice. People use clever approximations. Three became field workhorses.

MC Dropout

Back to clinic. You already trained standard network with dropout — technique where random neurons zero out during training to prevent overfitting. Normally dropout turns off at test time. MC Dropout says: don’t.

Keep dropout on. Run same X-ray through network 50 times. Each forward pass drops different neurons, so you get 50 slightly different predictions. If all 50 agree — “tuberculosis, tuberculosis, tuberculosis” — model confident. If scattered — “tuberculosis, pneumonia, normal, tuberculosis, normal” — model uncertain. Mean of 50 predictions is estimate. Standard deviation is uncertainty.

Gal and Ghahramani showed in 2016 this is mathematically equivalent to specific form of variational inference. Fancy way to say: not hack — theoretical justification exists for dropout-as-uncertainty. Prediction spread approximates proper Bayesian posterior, at least roughly.

Beauty of MC Dropout: nearly free. Dropout already in network. Forward pass already familiar. Only cost: run many times instead of once. For clinic, X-ray might take 2 seconds instead of 0.04 seconds. For life-or-death diagnosis, bargain.

Limitation: uncertainty quality depends heavily on dropout rate and placement. Too little dropout and every forward pass gives same answer — uncertainty estimate flatlines. Approximation also becomes overly confident on some out-of-distribution inputs. Good first step, not final word.

Bayes by Backprop

If MC Dropout is “bolt it on after training” approach, Bayes by Backprop is “bake it in from start” approach. Blundell et al. (2015) replaced each fixed weight with Gaussian distribution parameterized by mean and standard deviation. During each forward pass, you sample concrete weight from that Gaussian using reparameterization trick — way to express random sampling so gradients can flow through it during backpropagation.

Loss function changes too. Instead of minimizing prediction error alone, minimize prediction error plus KL divergence term measuring how far learned weight distributions moved from prior, usually simple Gaussian. Variational inference applied directly to neural network weights.

For clinic model, Bayes by Backprop gives richer, more principled uncertainty estimates than MC Dropout. Tradeoff is real: training takes roughly twice as long, you learn two parameters per weight instead of one, optimization landscape is harder, hyperparameters increase. In practice, most teams try MC Dropout first and move to Bayes by Backprop only when MC Dropout uncertainty is not good enough.

Laplace Approximation

Laplace approximation is “I already trained network, can I add uncertainty without retraining?” answer. Answer: yes.

Idea: train network normal way, reach single weight set — MAP estimate, peak of posterior landscape. Now stand on peak and inspect terrain curvature around it. Sharp narrow peak means small weight changes swing loss hard — model sensitive, uncertainty low in steep directions. Flat terrain means predictions barely change when you move — model uncertain about those weight values.

Mathematically, fit Gaussian centered at trained weights. Shape comes from Hessian — matrix of second derivatives of loss at those weights. Hessian captures curvature. Large second derivative means sharp peak, low uncertainty. Small second derivative means flat peak, high uncertainty. Inverse Hessian becomes covariance matrix of Gaussian posterior approximation.

For 25-million-parameter network, full Hessian is impossible — 25-million-by-25-million matrix. People use approximations: diagonal approximation (treat weights as independent — fast, crude), Kronecker-factored approximation (KFAC — captures some same-layer correlations), or low-rank approximation (keep most important curvature directions).

Laplace approach shines in production when retraining is expensive. laplace-torch library for PyTorch makes it almost plug-and-play: train model, wrap in Laplace object, call .fit(), get uncertainty estimates. For clinic, we can take existing X-ray model, bolt on uncertainty, deploy without touching training pipeline.

Downside: works well only when posterior is approximately Gaussian near peak. If true posterior is multimodal — several distinct good weight configurations exist — Laplace approximation misses all but one. Like describing mountain range by shape of single summit. One dominant peak, great. Three similar peaks, story missing.

Expectation Propagation

Previous sections used variational inference — workhorse of approximate Bayesian computation. VI finds simple distribution q close to true posterior p. Here “close” means minimizing KL(q || p), and that choice is mode-seeking. If true posterior has two humps and q can hold one, VI picks one hump and ignores other.

Think of odd-shaped puddle and circular tarp. KL(q || p) says: tarp must not extend beyond puddle. Result: tarp shrinks, covers one region perfectly, abandons rest. Expectation Propagation (EP), introduced by Tom Minka in 2001, flips divergence and minimizes KL(p || q), which is mass-covering. Now rule is: puddle must not extend beyond tarp. Tarp stretches to cover everything, even with loose fit.

EP breaks posterior into factors — one per data point or likelihood term — approximates each factor with simple distribution, then stitches factors back together. It iterates, refining one factor while others stay fixed. This message-passing structure fits models that factor into local terms, like Gaussian processes and graphical models.

EP powered sparse GP approximations in GPflow and sat at center of Microsoft’s Infer.NET framework. Less popular than VI or MCMC in day-to-day practice, partly because convergence is not guaranteed — iterations can oscillate. But when posterior has multiple modes and approximation must acknowledge all of them, reach for EP.

I underestimated EP for years and treated it like niche curiosity. Then I hit GP classification problem where VI uncertainty was dangerously overconfident, and EP gave much better calibrated answer. Moment of clarity: KL divergence direction is not mathematical detail. It is choice about what mistakes you accept.

Causal Inference and Pearl’s Framework

Everything in ML so far has been about patterns in data. Correlations. Associations. “Patients who take drug X tend to recover faster.” But does drug X cause recovery? Or do healthier patients choose it? This question separates statistics from science. For decades, people treated it as unanswerable from observational data alone.

Judea Pearl, Turing Award computer scientist, built formal framework for rigorous causal reasoning. His key move: represent causal relationships as directed graphs — causal diagrams or DAGs (directed acyclic graphs) — where arrow from A to B means A directly causes B. Diagrams encode not what data shows, but what we believe about world mechanics.

Pearl organized causal reasoning into three rungs. Association: what patterns exist in data? Standard ML. Intervention: what happens if I do something? Realm of experiments. Counterfactual: what would have happened if things were different? Realm of individual-level “what-if” questions.

I still mix up rungs sometimes. Concrete example helps.

The do-operator and do-calculus

Back to clinic. We observe patients who receive new drug recover at higher rates. In notation, P(recovery | drug) is high. But maybe sicker patients are less likely to get drug because doctor reserves it for mild cases. What we want is P(recovery | do(drug)) — probability of recovery if we force every patient to take drug, regardless of severity.

do-operator, written do(X = x), represents intervention: reach into system and set X to specific value, breaking arrows that normally point into X. Difference between seeing barometer drop, which predicts rain, and smashing barometer to low reading, which does not cause rain.

Do-calculus is set of three algebraic rules Pearl proved complete — if causal effect can be computed from observational data plus causal diagram, these rules get you there. Rules tell you when you can swap, insert, or remove do-operators in probability expressions. Each rule matches graphical condition on DAG: whether certain paths stay blocked when you remove or add edges.

Practical payoff is huge. In many real-world cases, randomized controlled trial is impossible or unethical. Do-calculus tells you whether causal question can be answered from observational data alone, given assumptions about causal structure. If yes, it gives exact adjustment formula.

Counterfactuals

Counterfactuals are Pearl’s third rung and most philosophically loaded. “If this specific patient had taken drug, would they have recovered?” Not population-level question. One individual. One alternate timeline.

Mechanics work in three steps. First, use observed patient data to abduct — infer unobserved variables like latent health state. Second, intervene — change treatment in model. Third, predict — run modified model forward and see what would have happened. Simulation of reality, then replay with one variable changed.

Counterfactuals matter for attribution, fairness, and legal reasoning: “was drug responsible for recovery?” “would this loan have been approved if applicant had different gender?” “would harm have occurred without defendant’s action?” They also depend hard on correct causal model. Wrong diagram, fictional counterfactual.

Instrumental Variables

Sometimes experiment is impossible and causal effect is tangled with unmeasured confounders. Instrumental variables (IVs) offer clever back door — more precisely, front door around confounder.

Instrument Z must satisfy three conditions: affect treatment X, affect outcome Y only through X, and stay independent of unmeasured confounders. Classic example: distance to hospital as instrument for whether patient receives surgery. Patients closer to hospital are more likely to get surgery, and distance itself does not directly affect health outcomes except through surgery decision.

Back at clinic, imagine regional policy change makes some clinics stock new drug and others not. Whether patient’s nearest clinic stocks drug is instrument: affects whether patient receives drug, but does not directly affect health. Using this instrument, we can estimate drug’s causal effect without randomized trial.

Finding good instruments is notoriously hard. Exclusion restriction — instrument affects outcome only through treatment — is fundamentally untestable. Need domain knowledge and honest argument. I have seen papers build careers on one clever instrumental variable, and seen published instruments destroyed by convincing counterargument about exclusion restriction violation.

Bayesian Nonparametrics

Every clustering algorithm asks same annoying question: “How many clusters?” K-means needs K. Gaussian mixture models need K. You try K=3, K=5, K=10, stare at elbow plots, compute silhouette scores, then pick number that feels right. Nagging feeling remains: data should tell you cluster count, not other way around.

Bayesian nonparametrics formalizes that feeling. “Nonparametric” does not mean “no parameters” — it means parameter count grows with data. As more data arrives, model can invent new clusters if data demands it.

The Dirichlet Process

Dirichlet Process (DP) is engine behind this. It is distribution over distributions — prior placed not on parameter value, but on entire probability distribution. Sounds abstract. Make it concrete with Chinese Restaurant Process, famous Bayesian statistics metaphor.

Imagine restaurant with infinitely many tables. First customer sits at table 1. Second customer either joins table 1, with probability proportional to current occupancy, or starts new table 2, with probability proportional to concentration parameter α. Third customer faces same choice: join table 1, join table 2, or start table 3, with probabilities weighted by occupancy and α.

Two things happen naturally. Popular tables get more popular — “rich get richer.” New tables keep appearing, but slower over time. Concentration parameter α controls balance: large α means frequent new tables and many small clusters, small α means customers pile onto existing tables and form few large clusters. Final table count is never fixed in advance. Data decides.

In practice, Dirichlet Process Mixture Model (DPMM) uses this as prior for mixture of Gaussians, or another distribution family. Result: clustering algorithm that infers cluster count alongside cluster parameters. Beautiful in theory. In practice, fitting DPMMs is computationally expensive and sensitive to α and base distribution. For most production clustering, running K-means for K=2 through K=20 and picking best model is faster and more stable. DP shines when cluster count is scientifically meaningful — how many distinct cell types exist in tissue sample? how many topics recur in corpus? — not mere hyperparameter.

Sibling called Indian Buffet Process (IBP) does for features what DP does for clusters. Instead of assigning each data point to one cluster, IBP lets each data point have any number of latent features, and total feature count grows with data. Used in latent factor models when underlying factor count is unknown.

Information Geometry

I do not claim deep intuition for Riemannian manifolds. But core idea made information geometry click for me: what does it mean for two probability distributions to be “close”?

Treat each probability distribution as point. Space of all distributions becomes landscape — statistical manifold. On flat landscape, moving one meter north changes terrain same way everywhere. Distribution landscape is not flat. Near some distributions, tiny parameter change causes huge shift in predicted probabilities. Near others, large parameter change barely matters.

Fisher information metric is natural way to measure distance on this curved landscape. It comes from Fisher information matrix, which captures how sensitive likelihood is to parameter changes. High Fisher information in one direction means data is very informative about parameter — steep landscape. Low Fisher information means data does not care much — flat landscape.

This connects directly to optimization. Standard gradient descent treats parameter space as flat — every direction gets same treatment. Natural gradient descent uses Fisher information metric to adjust step sizes based on actual curvature of distribution space. Like navigating with flat map versus topographic map. Flat map can make you take huge step in direction that barely changes predictions and tiny step in direction that matters. Natural gradient corrects this.

Update rule is elegant: instead of θ′ = θ − η ∇L, use θ′ = θ − η F−1∇L, where F is Fisher information matrix. In practice, computing F−1 for large network is expensive, so approximations like K-FAC exist. Natural gradient is theoretical ideal. Algorithms like Adam are rough approximations.

Information geometry also deepens our view of KL divergence. KL divergence between two nearby distributions is approximately half squared distance under Fisher metric. Infinitesimal version of same idea. This is why KL divergence is not symmetric — landscape curvature differs by direction.

PAC-Bayes Bounds

Learning theory keeps asking one haunting question: why do overparameterized neural networks generalize at all? Classical theory says model with more parameters than data points should memorize training set and fail on new data. Yet deep networks often have millions of parameters, train on thousands of examples, and still generalize. Classical bounds are vacuous — they predict generalization gaps above 100%, which is useless.

PAC-Bayes bounds give some of tightest non-vacuous generalization guarantees for stochastic and Bayesian learners. PAC means “Probably Approximately Correct.” Framework asks how likely learning algorithm is to produce hypothesis that stays approximately correct on new data.

Key insight is simple. Start with prior distribution P over hypotheses — belief before data. After training, you have posterior distribution Q. PAC-Bayes bound says generalization gap for model drawn from Q is bounded by training error plus complexity term proportional to KL(Q || P) divided by square root of training example count.

Read that again. Complexity penalty is not parameter count. It is distance your posterior moved from prior. If prior is reasonable and training does not push weights far away, bound stays tight — regardless of parameter count. One of few theoretical tools that starts to explain why deep learning works.

I will be honest — math can feel impenetrable first time through. But intuition is almost common sense: if you did not change your mind much to fit data, you probably did not overfit. More contortion, less trust. PAC-Bayes makes that intuition precise.

Recent work produced PAC-Bayes bounds that are numerically useful — tight enough to predict test accuracy within few percentage points for some architectures. People also use them for model selection: choose model whose PAC-Bayes bound is tightest.

Conformal Prediction

Everything so far needs assumptions. Bayesian methods assume prior. Parametric methods assume model family. Even PAC-Bayes bound assumes prior was chosen before data. Conformal prediction asks: can we get valid uncertainty quantification with almost no assumptions?

Answer: yes.

Setup is simple. Take any trained model — random forest, neural network, linear regression, whatever. Keep held-out calibration set unused in training. For each calibration example, compute nonconformity score — measure of how “strange” example is relative to model prediction. For regression, maybe absolute residual |y − ŷ|. For classification, maybe 1 minus predicted probability of true class.

Now new example arrives. You want 90% prediction interval. Sort calibration scores and take 90th percentile. That threshold defines interval. For regression: prediction ± threshold. For classification: include all classes whose predicted probability exceeds threshold.

Guarantee: if calibration data and future data come from same distribution — exchangeability assumption, weaker than independence — true label falls inside prediction set with exactly specified coverage probability. Not approximately. Not asymptotically. Exactly, in finite sample.

Back to clinic. You trained model to predict patient blood pressure. On calibration set, compute residuals. For new patient, conformal prediction says: “I predict 130 mmHg, and I guarantee that 90% of time, true value falls in [118, 142].” Guarantee holds regardless of whether model is neural network or linear regression, whether data is Gaussian or heavy-tailed, whether model is well specified or totally wrong. Only requirement: new patient comes from same distribution as calibration patients.

Extraordinary property. No other uncertainty quantification method offers this kind of distribution-free, model-agnostic guarantee.

Catch: conformal prediction says nothing about whether intervals are tight. Terrible model gives wide intervals, which is honest. Good model gives narrow ones, which is useful. Conformal prediction guarantees coverage, not informativeness. But where guarantee matters — regulatory compliance, safety-critical systems, clinical trials — airtight coverage promise is valuable.

Conformal prediction has quietly grown since about 2020, with libraries like MAPIE making it accessible in Python. Learn it once, start seeing uses everywhere.
