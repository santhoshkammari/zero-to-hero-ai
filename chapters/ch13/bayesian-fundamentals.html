Maximum likelihood gives single number — answer. Bayesian inference gives distribution — answer plus trust measure. Difference matters when decision happens under uncertainty. Build machinery from scratch.

Bayesian inference updates beliefs using new evidence. Thomas Bayes formalized it in 1760s (published posthumously). Pierre-Simon Laplace developed it independently. Today it powers spam filters, A/B testing, Gaussian processes, probabilistic programming. Core question: “Given what I’ve seen, what should I believe now?”

Two Camps of Probability

Before Bayesian inference, face old statistics fight: what does “probability” mean?

Imagine coin in hand. Frequentists say heads probability is 0.5 because ten thousand flips give about half heads. Probability is property of coin and flipping process. Long-run frequency. Coin does not care what you think.

Bayesians say probability is degree of belief. Before flip, heads and tails look equally likely. After ten flips with eight heads, belief shifts. Coin did not change. Knowledge changed. Probability lives in your head, not coin.

First read felt like philosophy, not math. “Degree of belief” sounded mushy. Useful mush. It lets you start with imperfect knowledge, add evidence as it arrives, and keep calibrated picture of what you know and do not know. Frequentist statistics cannot do that same way.

That is Bayesian promise. Now build machinery to make it work.

Flipping the Question

Tiny scenario. You run website and test new green button against current blue button. Question: if someone clicked, was button more likely green or blue?

Known facts. 30% of visitors see green button. 10% of green viewers click. 4% of blue viewers click. Someone clicked. Which button were they probably seeing?

Gut says green — higher click rate. But 70% of visitors see blue. Many more blue viewers. So which button makes more clickers?

Count it. Imagine 1000 visitors. 300 see green; 10% click: 30 clickers. 700 see blue; 4% click: 28 clickers. Total clickers: 58. Green made 30 of them. So given click, probability it came from green is 30/58, about 52%. Not slam dunk gut predicted.

No formula used. We counted. What we computed has name: Bayes’ theorem. We took hard question (“given click, which button?”) and split it into measurable pieces (“given each button, how often do people click?” and “how often does each button appear?”). Counting approach is theorem. Formula is counting, compressed.

The Four Characters

Bayes’ theorem has four moving parts. Each part has job. Understanding jobs is whole game.

P(θ | D) = P(D | θ) × P(θ) / P(D)

posterior = likelihood × prior / evidence

Meet them one at time with button example. Let θ be “click-through rate of green button” and D be “click data we observed.”

Prior, P(θ), is belief about click-through rate before experiment. Maybe past green buttons converted between 5% and 15%. That prior knowledge goes here. Think starting position on map — exact spot unknown, rough neighborhood known.

Likelihood, P(D|θ), measures how well each possible click-through rate explains observed data. If θ were 10%, how probable are observed clicks? If θ were 2%? Likelihood scores each candidate θ by how well it predicts observations. Same likelihood frequentists maximize in MLE (Maximum Likelihood Estimation). Bayesians and frequentists agree on this piece completely.

Evidence, P(D), is probability of seeing this data regardless of θ. It averages likelihood across all possible θ values, weighted by prior. Normalizing constant — makes posterior add up to one. Key point: for most practical problems, you do not need to compute it. Posterior is proportional to likelihood times prior, and that is often enough.

Posterior, P(θ|D), is payoff. Updated belief after seeing data. It combines prior knowledge with evidence from experiment. In map analogy, prior was rough location, likelihood was GPS signal, posterior is refined position — sharper than either alone.

That is it. Four characters. Rest of section makes them practical.

Our First Bayesian Update

Get concrete. Back to website. You launched new green “Sign Up” button and want true click-through rate θ. No historical data for this exact button, but past experiments say signup button click-through rates hover around 5% to 15%. So prior belief about θ centers near 10%, with some spread.

Now 20 visitors see green button. 4 click. Observed click rate is 20%. Should you believe true rate is 20%?

Probably not. Twenty visitors is tiny sample. Prior knowledge says rates like 20% are unusual for signup buttons. Bayesian update pulls estimate between prior (around 10%) and raw data (20%), weighted by how much data you have.

With only 20 visitors, prior pulls hard. Posterior might land around 12–14%. If you saw 4 clicks in 20 visits and another 15 clicks in next 100 visits (19 clicks total in 120 visits, about 16%), data would start to dominate and posterior would move toward observed rate.

Core Bayesian dynamic: posterior is compromise between prior and data. Little data, prior matters a lot. Lots of data, data overwhelms prior. Given enough evidence, Bayesians and frequentists converge on same answer. Prior is like rubber band tied to starting belief — it pulls hard when data is scarce, then stretches and loosens as evidence accumulates.

Rubber band analogy comes back. Keep it in mind.

Conjugate Priors — The Algebraic Shortcut

Problem exists. Computing posterior requires denominator integral — evidence P(D). For most real problems, integral has no closed-form solution. You need numerical methods, sampling, or approximations. Fine for concept. Headache for paper math or fast production updates.

Escape hatch exists. For certain prior-likelihood combinations, posterior has same mathematical form as prior, with updated parameters. Start with one distribution family, see data, end with same family — new numbers plugged in. No integrals needed. Algebra works.

Prior with this property is called conjugate prior for that likelihood. “Conjugate” means “paired with” — prior and likelihood form matched set that gives clean posterior.

Most important conjugate pairs look like this. Coin-flip data (Bernoulli or Binomial likelihood) pairs with Beta distribution. Normally distributed data with known variance pairs with another Normal. Event counts (Poisson likelihood) pair with Gamma distribution. Category proportions (Multinomial likelihood) pair with Dirichlet.

Same update pattern each time: take prior parameters, add something from data, get posterior parameters. No sampling. No optimization. Arithmetic.

Limitation is real. Conjugate priors exist only for certain likelihood-prior pairs. If model is neural network, or likelihood is exotic, no conjugate shortcut exists. You need heavier tools from later sections — MCMC, variational inference, and friends. But for many practical problems, conjugacy is enough.

The Beta-Binomial, Step by Step

Beta-Binomial is workhorse of Bayesian inference. It is conjugate pair you will use most in practice, and easiest pair to understand fully. Build it from nothing.

Scenario: still testing green signup button. You want θ, true probability that visitor clicks. Data is binary — each visitor either clicks (success) or does not (failure). That makes likelihood Binomial.

Beta distribution lives on interval [0, 1], exact range of probability. It has two parameters, α and β. Intuition: α counts “imaginary successes already seen,” and β counts “imaginary failures already seen.” Beta(1, 1) is flat — every θ value from 0 to 1 is equally likely. That is “I have no idea” prior. Beta(10, 10) peaks at 0.5 — you are fairly confident rate is near 50%, as if 10 heads and 10 tails were already seen. Beta(2, 8) peaks near 0.2 — belief says rate is low.

Now magic. Suppose prior is Beta(α₀, β₀) and you observe k successes in n trials. Posterior is:

Posterior = Beta(α₀ + k, β₀ + n - k)

That is it. Add successes to α. Add failures to β. Trace it with real numbers.

Start with Beta(2, 2). Gentle prior saying “rate is somewhere around 50%, but confidence is low.” Mean is α/(α+β) = 2/4 = 0.5. Think of it as 2 imaginary clicks and 2 imaginary non-clicks before experiment starts.

Day one: 10 visitors, 3 click. Update. α becomes 2 + 3 = 5. β becomes 2 + 7 = 9. Posterior is Beta(5, 9), with mean 5/14 ≈ 0.357. Raw data said 30% clicked, prior said 50%, posterior landed near 36% — compromise leaning toward data because 10 real observations beat 4 imaginary ones.

Day two: 20 more visitors, 6 click. Do not return to original prior. Use yesterday’s posterior as today’s prior. α becomes 5 + 6 = 11. β becomes 9 + 14 = 23. Posterior: Beta(11, 23), mean ≈ 0.324. With 30 real data points, prior influence is fading. Rubber band is stretching.

Day three: 50 visitors, 14 click. α = 11 + 14 = 25. β = 23 + 36 = 59. Posterior: Beta(25, 59), mean ≈ 0.298. After 80 observations, estimate settles near 30%. Original prior centered at 50% is barely detectable. Data did its job.

Notice this: posterior mean, α/(α+β), is weighted average of prior mean and data proportion. Weights are effective sample sizes — prior pseudocounts versus real observation count. As real data accumulates, prior pseudocounts become negligible. That is why Bayesian and frequentist answers converge with enough data. Not coincidence. Arithmetic.

Sequential Updating

Most elegant Bayesian property already appeared: today’s posterior becomes tomorrow’s prior. Worth pausing on why that matters.

In frequentist statistics, adding new data usually means re-analyzing everything from scratch. If data came in three batches, you combine all raw data and run test once. Worse, if you peeked after each batch — checked whether p-value was significant yet — you committed multiple testing. False positive rate inflates. Results stop being trustworthy at stated significance level.

Bayesian updating has no such problem. Posterior stays valid no matter when or how often you inspect it. Check after 10 visitors, after 50, after 200. Each time, posterior reflects all evidence seen so far. No corrections needed.

from scipy import stats

alpha, beta_param = 2, 2    # prior: Beta(2, 2)

batches = [
    (3, 7),     # day 1: 3 clicks, 7 no-clicks
    (6, 14),    # day 2: 6 clicks, 14 no-clicks
    (14, 36),   # day 3: 14 clicks, 36 no-clicks
]

print(f"Start:       Beta({alpha}, {beta_param}), "
      f"mean = {alpha/(alpha+beta_param):.3f}")

for i, (hits, misses) in enumerate(batches, 1):
    alpha += hits
    beta_param += misses
    lo = stats.beta.ppf(0.025, alpha, beta_param)
    hi = stats.beta.ppf(0.975, alpha, beta_param)
    print(f"After day {i}: Beta({alpha}, {beta_param}), "
          f"mean = {alpha/(alpha+beta_param):.3f}, "
          f"95% interval = [{lo:.3f}, {hi:.3f}]")

Run this and interval shrinks with each batch. Estimate stabilizes. Punchline: exact same posterior appears whether you process all 80 observations at once or in three batches. Math does not care about schedule. That is why Bayesian methods fit A/B testing, online learning, and stream data settings.

MAP vs. MLE — The Regularization Secret

So far, we used full posterior distributions — whole curves describing uncertainty. Ideal case. But sometimes one number is needed. Point estimate. Best guess.

Two obvious ways extract number from posterior. Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) ignores prior and asks: what parameter value makes observed data most probable? It maximizes P(D|θ). Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) asks different question: what parameter value is most probable given data and prior? It maximizes P(θ|D), which is proportional to P(D|θ) × P(θ).

Difference is prior term. This is where things get interesting.

Take log of MAP objective. You get: log P(D|θ) + log P(θ). First term is log-likelihood — same thing MLE maximizes. Second term is log-prior. If prior is Gaussian centered at zero — P(θ) ∝ exp(-θ²/2σ²) — then log P(θ) = -θ²/2σ² plus constant. That is L2 penalty on θ. MAP with Gaussian prior is exactly Ridge regression. Regularization strength λ equals 1/σ².

If prior is Laplace distribution instead — peaked at zero with heavier tails — then log P(θ) = -|θ|/b plus constant. That is L1 penalty. MAP with Laplace prior is exactly Lasso regression.

Every time you add regularization penalty to loss function, you do Bayesian MAP estimation with specific prior distribution. Bayesian move was there all along.

Full Bayesian inference — integrating over entire posterior instead of taking mode — costs more than MAP. Worth it most when data is scarce and uncertainty is main target. With large datasets, MAP and full Bayes usually agree. With small datasets, full posterior gives calibrated uncertainty MAP cannot. Posterior mean is also often better point estimate than posterior mode, especially for skewed distributions.

Credible Intervals vs. Confidence Intervals

This trips up everyone. Pause and think carefully here.

A 95% credible interval says: “Given observed data, there is 95% probability true parameter lies in this interval.” That is intuitive statement. That is what you want to say when reporting results.

A 95% confidence interval says something different: “If this entire experiment were repeated infinitely many times, and each time an interval were built with this procedure, 95% of those intervals would contain true parameter.” It says nothing about whether this particular interval contains true value. Parameter is either in interval or not — fixed number, and fixed numbers do not have probabilities in frequentist worldview.

Feel difference this way. Suppose you computed 95% confidence interval [0.23, 0.41]. Can you say “there’s a 95% chance true value is between 0.23 and 0.41”? Strictly, no. Not as frequentist. True value is fixed. Interval is random thing. In Bayesian framework, you can say it, because parameter is treated as random variable with distribution.

Irony: nearly everyone interprets confidence intervals like credible intervals. When colleague says “there’s a 95% chance true effect is in this range,” colleague is thinking Bayesian — whether known or not.

For large samples with vague priors, two intervals are nearly identical numerically. Philosophical difference shows up most with small data or strong priors — exactly when Bayesian approach earns its keep.

Choosing Priors Wisely

Prior makes people nervous. “You’re putting thumb on scale,” they say. Not wrong. But alternative — pretending no prior knowledge exists — also puts thumb on scale. Flat prior over all real numbers is not “no assumption.” It assumes parameter value 10 billion is as plausible as 0.5. Very strong statement. Usually absurd.

Practical wisdom, shaped largely by Andrew Gelman and Stan community, breaks into three tiers.

Weakly informative priors are sweet spot for most problems. They rule out absurd values without locking onto specific ones. For regression coefficients on standardized data, Normal(0, 2.5) is good default. For standard deviations, Half-Normal or Half-Cauchy centered at zero keeps parameter positive and moderate without being dogmatic. Guiding question: “Would true value outside this range shock me?” If yes, prior should make such values unlikely. If no, widen it.

Informative priors encode real domain knowledge. If previous experiments consistently show click-through rates between 2% and 15%, encode that. If physics constrains coefficient to be positive, say so. Informative priors are powerful when knowledge is real and dangerous when it is wishful thinking.

Non-informative priors (flat, Jeffreys’) try to “let data speak.” Jeffreys’ prior has elegant property — it is invariant under reparameterization, so switching from meters to feet does not change inference. For Bernoulli parameter, Jeffreys’ prior is Beta(0.5, 0.5). But truly non-informative priors can behave pathologically in high dimensions or complex models. Starting point, not destination.

No one agrees on “right” prior for given problem. Fine. Standard practice is prior predictive check: simulate data from prior and see whether predictions look reasonable. If prior implies 99% click-through rate is plausible, something is off. Adjust and repeat. Iterative process, not one-shot decision.

Bayesian Decision Theory

Everything so far gives posterior — distribution over belief. But at some point, you must do something. Ship green button or keep blue one. Treat patient or wait. Approve loan or decline it. Bayesian decision theory bridges belief to action.

Core idea: different mistakes cost different amounts. Declaring button “better” when it is worse is annoying — conversions are lost until someone notices. Telling patient they do not have cancer when they do is catastrophic. Optimal action depends not only on belief (posterior) but also on stakes (loss function).

Back to button example. Suppose green button’s posterior mean conversion rate is 12%, and blue button’s is 10%. Green looks better. But what if switching costs $5,000 in engineering time, and revenue difference is negligible unless true lift is at least 3 percentage points? Posterior tells probability that true lift exceeds 3 points. If that probability is low, optimal decision may be keeping blue button despite green’s higher point estimate.

Formally, given possible actions a, loss function L(θ, a) that measures cost of taking action a when true state is θ, and posterior P(θ|D), Bayes-optimal action minimizes expected posterior loss:

a* = argmin_a  E[L(θ, a)]
   = argmin_a  ∫ L(θ, a) × P(θ|D) dθ

When loss is symmetric (equal care about over- and under-estimating), optimal point estimate is posterior mean. When loss is absolute (care about magnitude, not direction), it is posterior median. When you want single most probable value, it is posterior mode (MAP). Different loss functions, different optimal actions — all from same posterior.

import numpy as np

# A/B test decision with asymmetric costs
# Green button posterior: Beta(25, 59), mean ≈ 0.298
# Blue button posterior: Beta(40, 360), mean ≈ 0.100

alpha_green, beta_green = 25, 59
alpha_blue, beta_blue = 40, 360

samples_green = np.random.beta(alpha_green, beta_green, 100_000)
samples_blue = np.random.beta(alpha_blue, beta_blue, 100_000)

# What fraction of the time does green beat blue?
p_green_wins = np.mean(samples_green > samples_blue)

# What's the probability the lift exceeds 3 percentage points?
lift = samples_green - samples_blue
p_meaningful_lift = np.mean(lift > 0.03)

print(f"P(green > blue) = {p_green_wins:.3f}")
print(f"P(lift > 3pp) = {p_meaningful_lift:.3f}")
print(f"Expected lift = {np.mean(lift):.3f}")
print(f"95% credible interval for lift: "
      f"[{np.percentile(lift, 2.5):.3f}, "
      f"{np.percentile(lift, 97.5):.3f}]")

This is question business needs answered. Not “is difference statistically significant?” but “what’s probability difference is big enough to justify action?” Decision question. Bayesian inference answers it directly.
