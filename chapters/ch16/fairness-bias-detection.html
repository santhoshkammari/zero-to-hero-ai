Fairness & Bias Detection

Fairness in ML looks political until you define "fair" mathematically and see experts disagree. Worse: definitions conflict. That tension drives this section.

Fairness in ML became formal research area around 2012–2016, though legal and philosophical roots go back decades. Field exploded after 2016 ProPublica investigation showed COMPAS falsely labeled Black defendants high-risk about twice as often as white defendants. Investigation and debate after it forced field to face hard fact: multiple rigorous definitions of "fair" exist, and they conflict.

The Loan Officer and the Spreadsheet

Imagine small bank: three branches, one loan officer each. Officers approve and reject applications by hand for years. Bank now wants model to automate process. You get spreadsheet with 20 recent applications: income, credit score, zip code, approval decision.

Data slice below. Ten applicants from neighborhood A. Ten from neighborhood B.

Applicant   Neighborhood   Income    Credit Score   Approved?
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Alice       A              $62,000   710            Yes
Bob         A              $58,000   680            Yes
Carol       A              $45,000   620            No
David       B              $61,000   700            No
Elena       B              $59,000   690            No
Frank       A              $70,000   740            Yes
Grace       B              $67,000   720            No
Hiro        A              $52,000   650            Yes
Irene       B              $55,000   660            No
James       B              $48,000   630            No

Pattern jumps out. David matches Alice almost exactly — $61k income, 700 credit score — but David gets denied and Alice gets approved. Grace has stronger numbers than Hiro, yet Hiro gets approved and Grace does not. Look at neighborhood column: approval rate is 70% for A, 10% for B.

Now imagine neighborhood A is mostly white and neighborhood B is mostly Black. Historical loan officers did not use race explicitly. They used "gut feeling," shaped by outcomes already shaped by decades of discriminatory lending. Spreadsheet recorded those decisions faithfully. Train model on spreadsheet, model reproduces them faithfully.

Bias. Not code bug. Not malicious actor. Machine learning system doing exactly what we asked — learn from past — when past was unfair.

We will keep returning to this bank. Example is small, but problems match criminal justice, healthcare, hiring, and other high-stakes domains where ML decides about people.

Where Bias Comes From

Bank spreadsheet shows one specific failure: biased human decisions in data. But bias enters machine learning pipeline many ways. Think of them as entry points. Fix depends on where contamination entered.

Historical bias is our bank's problem. Data records unfair world faithfully. Amazon hit this in 2018 with hiring model trained on decade of resumes. Tech hiring was mostly male in that period, so model learned "male" signaled "good candidate." It penalized resumes containing "women's" — like "women's chess club captain" — and downgraded graduates of all-women's colleges. Amazon scrapped project. Data was not technically wrong. It was accurate record of biased process.

Representation bias is about missing people. If training data is 90% one demographic, model works well there and poorly elsewhere. Joy Buolamwini showed this in 2018 Gender Shades study. Training data skewed toward lighter-skinned faces, so systems worked extremely well on light-skinned men (error rate below 1%) and badly on dark-skinned women (error rates up to 34.7%). Model was not malicious. Model was underfed.

Measurement bias is subtler and most dangerous. It happens when thing you measure is not thing you think you measure. In 2019, Obermeyer and colleagues published a Science study that shook healthcare. Widely used algorithm aimed to find patients needing extra care. It used healthcare cost as proxy for healthcare need. Sounds reasonable — sicker people cost more, yes? Problem: Black patients in America systematically have less access to healthcare, so costs stay lower even when sickness is same. Algorithm effectively said "people who spent less on healthcare must be healthier." At same risk score, Black patients were much sicker than white patients. Only 17.7% of Black patients were flagged for extra care, when 46.5% should have been.

Think thermometer reading five degrees low, but only in some rooms. You can calibrate it perfectly where it works and still get wrong measurements where it fails. Here, "rooms" are people's lives.

Aggregation bias happens when you pool data from groups that behave differently. Diabetes prediction model that ignores how HbA1c relates to risk across ethnic groups may look good on average and fail on specific populations. Aggregate looks fine. Parts do not.

Label bias happens when human labelers bring their prejudices. Content moderation models trained on human annotations have flagged African American Vernacular English as "toxic" more often than Standard American English. Model learned what annotators taught.

Sampling bias is mechanical. Data collection systematically excludes populations. Speech recognition trained mostly on podcast audio underperforms on elderly speakers, non-native speakers, and people with speech impediments. Pipeline never heard them, so pipeline never learned them.

Each entry point needs different fix. Rebalancing training data helps representation bias but not measurement bias. Reweighing samples helps historical bias but not broken labels. No single "debias" button. First step: find where contamination entered. Next: clean that point.

The First Instinct That Doesn't Work

First instinct for "fair" model: remove protected attribute. No race or gender feature, no discrimination. Problem solved.

I believed this far too long. Name is fairness through unawareness. Common first instinct in industry. Almost useless.

Go back to bank. Remove "neighborhood" column from training data. Model still sees zip code, income, and credit score. Each correlates with neighborhood because of history. These are proxy variables — features carrying signal of thing you removed, sometimes so well model barely notices protected attribute is gone.

Quick test: use planned features to predict sensitive attribute. If simple model does this well, proxies exist.

from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingClassifier

# Can we predict neighborhood from the "neutral" features?
X = df[["income", "credit_score", "zip_code_encoded"]]
y = df["neighborhood"]

proxy_model = GradientBoostingClassifier(n_estimators=100, max_depth=3)
proxy_model.fit(X, y)
proxy_accuracy = proxy_model.score(X, y)
baseline = y.value_counts(normalize=True).max()

# proxy_accuracy: 0.94, baseline: 0.50
# The "neutral" features predict neighborhood with 94% accuracy.
# Removing the column was theater.

In bank example, proxy model predicts neighborhood with 94% accuracy using only income, credit score, and zip code. Removing neighborhood column did not remove information. Information spread across remaining features, where it is harder to see and harder to audit.

Central tension. Same features carrying useful signal — income for repayment, credit score for default risk — also encode historical discrimination. You cannot separate "legitimate signal" from "discriminatory signal" by deleting one column. Signals are tangled.

So if removing attribute fails, what does "fair" mean? Deeper question than it first looks.

Demographic Parity — The Simplest Definition

Start with intuitive definition. Demographic parity says model should approve loans at same rate for every group. If 60% of neighborhood A gets approved, 60% of neighborhood B should too.

Mathematically: P(Ŷ=1 | G=a) = P(Ŷ=1 | G=b), where Ŷ is prediction and G is group. Chance of "yes" should not depend on group.

At bank, demographic parity looks like this: 100 applicants from each neighborhood. Uncorrected model approves 70 from A and 10 from B. Demographic parity forces equal rates — maybe 40 from each, maybe 50 from each, depending on threshold.

Feels right. Equal outcomes. No different treatment by group. So what breaks?

Problem: demographic parity ignores qualification. If neighborhood A truly has more high-income, high-credit-score applicants because of historical wealth accumulation, forcing equal approval rates means approving less-qualified applicants from B or rejecting more-qualified applicants from A. Bank might approve 550-credit-score applicant from B while rejecting 720-credit-score applicant from A.

Another view: courtroom. One judge says "fairness means acquitting same percentage from every racial group." Sounds appealing until it means acquitting guilty people or convicting innocent people to hit quota. Most people want judge to follow evidence, not demographics. That leads to next definition.

When Equal Outcomes Aren't Enough

If demographic parity ignores qualifications, define fairness by errors. Model makes two mistakes: false positives and false negatives. Equalized odds says both error rates should match across groups.

Formally: false positive rate (FPR) and true positive rate (TPR) should match for every group. If model catches 85% of good borrowers in neighborhood A (TPR = 0.85), it should catch 85% in neighborhood B too. If it wrongly approves 10% of bad borrowers in A (FPR = 0.10), it should wrongly approve 10% in B too.

Trace bank example. Assume confusion matrix looks like this:

Neighborhood A (100 applicants, 60 actually qualified):
  True Positives:  51   (approved, would repay)     TPR = 51/60 = 0.85
  False Negatives:  9   (rejected, would have repaid)
  False Positives:  4   (approved, will default)     FPR = 4/40 = 0.10
  True Negatives:  36   (rejected, will default)

Neighborhood B (100 applicants, 30 actually qualified):
  True Positives:  18   (approved, would repay)     TPR = 18/30 = 0.60
  False Negatives: 12   (rejected, would have repaid)
  False Positives:  2   (approved, will default)     FPR = 2/70 = 0.03
  True Negatives:  68   (rejected, will default)

TPR gap (0.85 vs 0.60) means qualified applicants from B are much less likely to be approved than equally qualified applicants from A. FPR gap (0.10 vs 0.03) means unqualified applicants from A slip through more often. Equalized odds says both gaps should be zero.

This feels like better judge: same decision quality no matter who stands in front. Mistakes happen. Mistakes should not land mostly on one group.

Equal opportunity is relaxed version: only true positive rates must match. Message: qualified people from every group should have same chance of approval. It says nothing about false positives. This often feels most natural in loan approvals or college admissions, where main harm is denying benefit to someone who deserved it.

But equalized odds has limit. It cares about error rates, not what model scores mean. That matters a lot.

Calibration — When the Score Itself Matters

Many real-world systems do not output yes/no. They output score. COMPAS gives recidivism risk scores from 1 to 10. Credit systems give three-digit numbers. Healthcare algorithms assign acuity levels. When system outputs score, score should mean same thing for every group.

Calibration says: among people with risk score 7, about 70% should actually have outcome, regardless of group. Score 7 for Black defendant should mean same thing as score 7 for white defendant. Also called predictive parity — formally, P(Y=1 | Ŷ=1, G=a) = P(Y=1 | Ŷ=1, G=b).

Courtroom analogy helps again. Calibrated judge says "when I mark someone high-risk, I am right 70% of time, no matter who they are." Hard to argue with. Number means what it says.

Now we have three reasonable definitions: demographic parity (equal outcomes), equalized odds (equal error rates), and calibration (scores mean same thing). Each captures real part of "fair." Here floor drops out.

The Impossibility Theorem

In 2016 and 2017, two independent research groups — Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan on one side, Alexandra Chouldechova on other — proved result that changed fairness conversation. When base rates differ between groups (actual outcome rates differ, like loan default rates across neighborhoods), you cannot simultaneously have:

  1. Calibration — a score of 0.7 means 70% probability for every group
  2. Equal false positive rates across groups
  3. Equal false negative rates across groups

Not "very hard." Not "no method yet." Mathematically impossible. Proven with algebra, not experiments.

First time I read this, I did not believe it. Felt like loophole had to exist. Walk through bank example and it becomes clear.

Suppose neighborhood A has 15% actual default rate and neighborhood B has 30% actual default rate. Difference comes from historical economic conditions, not anything inherent. Now suppose model is perfectly calibrated — when it says "risk = 0.3," exactly 30% of those people default in both neighborhoods.

Catch: higher base rate in B forces calibrated model to assign higher risk scores to B applicants on average. More B applicants land above any fixed threshold. Result: more B applicants who won't default get flagged high-risk (higher FPR for B). More A applicants who will default slip below threshold (higher FNR for A).

You can see it in the math. By Bayes' rule, the positive predictive value for group g is:

PPV_g = (TPR_g × base_rate_g) / (TPR_g × base_rate_g + FPR_g × (1 - base_rate_g))

If you force TPR and FPR to match across groups (equalized odds) while base rates differ, numerator and denominator change differently for each group. PPV must differ. Calibration breaks.

If you force calibration and equal FPR, false negative rates must differ. You can satisfy any two of three. Not all three. Mathematical identity, as unavoidable as 1 + 1 = 2.

This is exactly what happened in COMPAS debate. ProPublica showed Black defendants who did not reoffend were nearly twice as likely to be labeled high-risk as white defendants who did not reoffend. That is unequal false positive rates. Northpointe answered that scores were calibrated — score 7 meant same thing for every race. Both were right. Impossibility theorem says disagreement on at least one metric was guaranteed. Real question was never "is COMPAS fair?" Real question was "which fairness definition matters most when deciding whether someone stays in jail?" That is value judgment, not technical judgment.

Two more definitions matter, though they appear less in practice. Individual fairness says similar individuals should get similar predictions — but defining "similar" is whole problem, and no universal answer exists. Counterfactual fairness asks "would prediction change if this person had been different race?" — but it needs causal model of domain, and wrong causal model makes fairness guarantee meaningless.

Detecting Bias in Practice

Knowing fairness definitions is one thing. Measuring them in live system is another. Return to bank. Model is deployed. Loans get approved and rejected. How do we check fair behavior?

Core idea: compute same performance metrics — accuracy, TPR, FPR, precision — separately for each group, then compare. Big differences mean disparity. Next question: is disparity acceptable?

Microsoft's Fairlearn library makes this mechanical. MetricFrame takes any sklearn metric and breaks it out by sensitive attribute.

from fairlearn.metrics import (
    MetricFrame, selection_rate,
    false_positive_rate, false_negative_rate
)
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score, recall_score

# y_true: actual outcomes (did the person repay?)
# y_pred: model's predictions
# sensitive: neighborhood group for each applicant

mf = MetricFrame(
    metrics={
        "approval_rate": selection_rate,
        "accuracy": accuracy_score,
        "recall": recall_score,           # TPR — equal opportunity
        "fpr": false_positive_rate,       # equalized odds, part 1
        "fnr": false_negative_rate,       # equalized odds, part 2
    },
    y_true=y_true,
    y_pred=y_pred,
    sensitive_features=sensitive
)

# This gives us per-group metrics in a single table.
# mf.by_group shows the breakdown.
# mf.difference() shows the max gap for each metric.

That one object tells us whether model satisfies demographic parity (equal approval rates), equal opportunity (equal recall), and equalized odds (equal FPR and FNR) in one pass. .difference() gives maximum gap between any two groups for each metric. Gap near zero means good shape for that definition. Large gap means problem, and metric tells you which problem.

IBM's AIF360 is other major toolkit. It wraps data in its own BinaryLabelDataset structure. Heavier. Less Pythonic. Wider algorithm range. Start with Fairlearn for most production work. Reach for AIF360 when you need algorithm Fairlearn lacks — like disparate impact remover or optimized pre-processing.

The Four-Fifths Rule

Before picking fairness definition, check legal floor too. In United States, Equal Employment Opportunity Commission uses four-fifths rule (80% rule): if selection rate for any protected group is below 80% of rate for group with highest selection rate, you have prima facie evidence of disparate impact.

Two terms first. Disparate treatment means intentionally using protected attribute — model literally includes "race" feature. Illegal. Easy to spot. Disparate impact means facially neutral policy harms protected group disproportionately. Model can have zero race features and still cause disparate impact through proxies. That case is harder.

Apply four-fifths rule to bank. Neighborhood A: 70% approval rate. Neighborhood B: 10% approval rate. Ratio = 10% / 70% = 0.14. Threshold = 0.80. Bank fails by landslide.

# Four-fifths rule check
rates = mf.by_group["approval_rate"]
ratio = rates.min() / rates.max()
# ratio = 0.14 — well below 0.80
# This is prima facie evidence of disparate impact.

Four-fifths rule does not tell you which fairness definition to optimize. It tells you legal floor. Think smoke alarm. It does not extinguish fire. It tells you smoke exists.

EU AI Act, in force in 2024, takes different route. It classifies AI systems by risk tier. High-risk systems — hiring, credit, criminal justice, healthcare — must undergo conformity assessments that include bias audits. Details are still being formalized, but direction is clear: "we didn't know" is no longer acceptable defense.

Fixing It — Three Intervention Points

Suppose smoke alarm goes off. Model shows disparity. Now what? Pipeline has three intervention points. Each works differently.

Pre-processing changes data before training. Goal: fix root cause in data. Common technique is reweighing: give higher sample weights to underrepresented group-outcome combinations and lower weights to overrepresented ones. If neighborhood-B approvals are rare, give each one more weight during training so model pays more attention.

# Reweighing: the model sees the same data, but some
# examples count more than others during training.
# Weight = (expected frequency) / (observed frequency)
# for each (group, outcome) combination.

overall_approval_rate = y_train.mean()  # say, 0.40
group_b_approval_rate = y_train[sensitive == "B"].mean()  # say, 0.10

# A positive example from B is underrepresented:
weight_b_positive = overall_approval_rate / group_b_approval_rate
# weight = 0.40 / 0.10 = 4.0 — counts as four examples

# model.fit(X_train, y_train, sample_weight=weights)

Pre-processing is model-agnostic. You can use any downstream model. But it can distort data and hurt overall performance, and it only addresses bias visible in training labels.

In-processing modifies training itself. Instead of optimizing only accuracy, optimize accuracy subject to fairness constraint. Fairlearn's ExponentiatedGradient is gold standard here. It treats problem like game between learner (wants accuracy) and auditor (finds fairness violations), then iterates to equilibrium.

from fairlearn.reductions import ExponentiatedGradient, EqualizedOdds
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression

# The learner optimizes accuracy.
# The constraint enforces equalized odds.
# ExponentiatedGradient finds the best tradeoff.

mitigator = ExponentiatedGradient(
    estimator=LogisticRegression(solver="liblinear"),
    constraints=EqualizedOdds(),
    max_iter=50
)
mitigator.fit(X_train, y_train, sensitive_features=sensitive_train)
y_pred_fair = mitigator.predict(X_test)

This is most principled approach. It directly optimizes accuracy/fairness tradeoff. But it needs access to training process, so you cannot use it on model you did not build.

Post-processing adjusts predictions after model is trained. Most common technique is threshold optimization: use different decision thresholds for different groups. If model systematically underscores neighborhood B, lower threshold for B so qualified applicants in B have same approval chance as equally qualified applicants in A.

from fairlearn.postprocessing import ThresholdOptimizer

# Finds per-group thresholds that satisfy a fairness constraint
# while maximizing accuracy. No retraining needed.
postprocessor = ThresholdOptimizer(
    estimator=trained_model,
    constraints="equalized_odds",
    objective="accuracy_score",
    prefit=True
)
postprocessor.fit(X_val, y_val, sensitive_features=sensitive_val)
y_adjusted = postprocessor.predict(X_test, sensitive_features=sensitive_test)

Post-processing is most pragmatic option. It works on any model, even one you cannot retrain. But it needs sensitive attributes at prediction time, and it can feel like patch, not fix. Different thresholds for different groups are themselves disparate treatment, creating uncomfortable paradox: treat people differently by group to achieve fair outcomes.

My current intuition: in-processing works best when you build from scratch and care about specific fairness definition, pre-processing is safest when you do not control model, and post-processing is emergency lever for deployed systems where disparity is discovered and retraining cannot happen fast enough.

Intersectional Fairness

Everything so far checks one sensitive attribute at time. "Fair across neighborhoods?" "Fair across genders?" Single-axis checks can hide compounded harm.

Kimberlé Crenshaw coined intersectionality in 1989 to describe overlapping, compounding discrimination. Same idea applies to ML auditing. Model can look fair for women overall and fair for Black applicants overall, while being deeply unfair to Black women specifically. Single-axis audit says "fine." Intersectional audit says "serious problem."

Gender Shades study shows this vividly. Buolamwini and Gebru tested facial recognition systems from IBM, Microsoft, and Face++ on four subgroups: lighter-skinned males, lighter-skinned females, darker-skinned males, darker-skinned females. Error rates for lighter-skinned males were below 1%. For darker-skinned females, error rates reached 34.7%. That is 44x disparity. Gender-only audit shows moderate gap. Skin-tone-only audit shows moderate gap. Intersection reveals catastrophe.

In practice, intersectional auditing means building combined group labels — "neighborhood B × female × age over 50" — and computing metrics for each combination.

# Intersectional audit: combine sensitive attributes
intersection = (
    df[["neighborhood", "gender"]].astype(str).agg(" × ".join, axis=1)
)
mf_intersect = MetricFrame(
    metrics={"approval_rate": selection_rate, "recall": recall_score},
    y_true=y_true,
    y_pred=y_pred,
    sensitive_features=intersection
)
# mf_intersect.by_group now shows metrics for each combination:
# "A × male", "A × female", "B × male", "B × female"

Catch: intersectional subgroups get small fast. "Neighborhood B × female × age 60+" might be only 8 people in test set. Metrics on 8 people are noisy enough to be useless. Report confidence intervals, use bootstrap sampling, or admit some subgroups are too small to audit reliably with current data. Uncomfortable answer. Honest answer.

Fairness in LLMs

Everything above mostly fits classical ML with structured data — tabular inputs, binary outputs, clear protected attributes. Large language models break many of those assumptions, and nobody fully knows how to measure fairness there yet. Best to be honest about state of art.

LLMs can show bias in ways that do not map cleanly to older frameworks. Model might write more positive recommendation letters for male-sounding names. It might link certain nationalities with terrorism in story completion. It might give more confident medical advice for conditions affecting demographics overrepresented in training data.

Most structured evaluation tool is Bias Benchmark for QA (BBQ), introduced by Parrish et al. in 2022. BBQ contains over 58,000 multiple-choice questions across nine social categories — age, disability, gender identity, nationality, physical appearance, race, religion, sexual orientation, socioeconomic status. Each question appears in two contexts: ambiguous (correct answer is "unknown"; other answer reveals stereotype reliance) and disambiguated (evidence points to specific answer). If model picks stereotyped answer in ambiguous condition, that is measured bias.

Beyond benchmarks, red teaming — humans trying to elicit biased or harmful outputs through adversarial prompting — remains most practical way to find LLM bias. Anthropic and OpenAI run structured red-teaming before major releases. Problem: this does not scale, and found biases depend on what red teamers think to test.

Embedding-level bias is another path. Word embeddings encode geometric relationships reflecting societal stereotypes — "man" relates to "programmer" the way "woman" relates to "homemaker." These biases propagate through model layers. Debiasing methods exist — projecting out bias directions, counterfactual data augmentation — but they often reduce benchmark bias while subtler bias remains.

Best thing about fairness in LLMs: field is still unsettled. Beyond high-level advice like "check for stereotyped outputs," no one knows complete solution. Field moves fast, benchmarks are imperfect, models are huge and opaque, and surgical analysis like we do on logistic regression is much harder. If someone says they have "solved" LLM fairness, be skeptical.

The Cases That Changed Everything

Throughout this section, real-world cases carried argument. Put together, each teaches specific lesson you can use in your own work.

COMPAS (2016) taught that fairness is not one metric. ProPublica showed unequal false positive rates by race. Northpointe showed calibrated risk scores. Impossibility theorem showed both could not hold together with other criteria. Lesson: before deploying risk scoring system, choose fairness definition that matters most for stakeholders, document choice, and be ready to defend it. No neutral option.

Amazon Hiring (2014–2017) taught historical bias. Model trained on ten years of mostly-male hiring decisions learned that "male" signaled value. It penalized word "women's" on resumes. Amazon tried to fix it, decided bias ran too deep, and killed project. Lesson: if training data reflects biased process, model will reproduce that bias mechanically. Audit labels before training.

Obermeyer Healthcare Study (2019) taught measurement bias, especially proxy-variable danger. Using healthcare cost as proxy for healthcare need systematically harmed Black patients because access to care was lower. Only 17.7% were flagged for extra care when 46.5% should have been. Lesson: proxy variable encodes biases of system that created it. Ask what target variable actually measures.

Gender Shades (2018) taught intersectional failure. Facial recognition error rates were below 1% for light-skinned men and up to 34.7% for dark-skinned women. Single-axis audits missed it. Lesson: always audit intersections. Worst outcomes often hit groups that disappear when you average over one axis.

Each case is also story about thermometer reading five degrees low in some rooms. COMPAS thermometer read "high risk" differently by race. Amazon thermometer read "qualified" differently by gender. Healthcare thermometer read "sick" differently by access to doctor. Gender Shades thermometer read "face" differently by skin tone. Fix started same way each time: measure what thermometer actually said for each group, then compare.
