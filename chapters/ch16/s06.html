AI for Scientific Discovery

Chapter 16: Frontier Applications Section 6
TL;DR

AI is no longer confined to ranking ads and tagging photos — it is now a first-class tool in the sciences. AlphaFold cracked the 50-year protein folding problem using a clever mix of attention, multiple sequence alignments, and invariant point attention. GraphCast replaced billion-dollar weather simulations with a graph neural network that runs on a single TPU. AlphaProof proved International Math Olympiad problems by combining a language model with reinforcement learning over formal proof assistants. Across drug discovery, climate science, genomics, and mathematics, the pattern is the same: encode domain structure into the architecture, train on massive domain data, and let the model find patterns humans missed. This section tours each of these frontiers, shows how the core ML ideas from earlier chapters re-appear in scientific contexts, and gives you enough code to start building.

Why AI for Science?

Scientific discovery is slow. A new drug takes 10–15 years and $2 billion from first molecule to pharmacy shelf. Weather forecasts require solving partial differential equations on grids with billions of cells. Proving a single mathematical theorem can consume a career. The bottleneck in all these cases is search — searching through molecular space, searching through atmospheric states, searching through proof trees. And search is precisely what neural networks excel at once you give them the right inductive bias.

The traditional workflow in science looks like this: a human forms a hypothesis, designs an experiment, runs it, analyzes the results, and iterates. Each cycle takes weeks to years. What changed in the last five years is that AI models can now compress entire stages of that loop. Instead of simulating protein dynamics for months, AlphaFold predicts the 3D structure in seconds. Instead of running a numerical weather model on a supercomputer for hours, GraphCast produces a 10-day forecast in under a minute. The models are not replacing scientists — they are giving scientists a faster inner loop.

The key insight is that each scientific domain has structure — proteins are chains of amino acids that fold in 3D space, weather is a system of interacting physical quantities on a sphere, mathematical proofs are trees of logical deductions. When you bake that structure into your model architecture, you get dramatically better data efficiency and generalization. That is the theme running through every example in this section.

Drug Discovery and Molecular ML

The Protein Folding Problem

Every protein in your body is a string of amino acids (there are 20 types). That string folds into a specific 3D shape, and the shape determines what the protein does. Misfolded proteins cause Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and cystic fibrosis. If you could predict the 3D shape from the amino acid sequence alone, you would unlock the ability to design drugs that fit into protein pockets like a key into a lock.

This is the protein folding problem, and it stumped biologists for over 50 years. The search space is absurd — a protein with 100 residues has roughly 3^200 possible conformations. Experimentally determining a structure via X-ray crystallography takes months to years and costs tens of thousands of dollars per protein. By 2020, humanity had solved roughly 170,000 structures. There are over 200 million known protein sequences.

The CASP competition (Critical Assessment of protein Structure Prediction) has been the benchmark since 1994. Every two years, teams predict structures of proteins whose shapes have been solved experimentally but not yet published. Before 2018, the best methods scored around 40 on the GDT-TS metric (a measure of how close the prediction is to the real structure, where 100 is perfect). In CASP13 (2018), AlphaFold v1 scored 58. In CASP14 (2020), AlphaFold 2 scored 92.4 — effectively solving the problem for single-chain proteins.

AlphaFold Architecture: MSA, Evoformer, and IPA

AlphaFold 2's architecture has three core ideas, each worth understanding because they recur across scientific ML.

First, Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA). Evolution is a free experiment — if two positions in a protein always mutate together across species, they are probably in physical contact in the 3D structure. AlphaFold collects thousands of related sequences from protein databases and aligns them. This gives the model a rich 2D table: rows are related sequences, columns are positions in the protein. Co-evolution patterns in this table encode distance constraints.

Second, the Evoformer. This is a custom transformer block that operates on two representations simultaneously: a per-residue representation (what each amino acid "knows") and a pair representation (what each pair of amino acids knows about their relationship). The Evoformer alternates between row-wise attention (information flows along the sequence dimension of the MSA), column-wise attention (information flows across related sequences), and triangular updates on the pair representation. The triangular updates enforce a geometric consistency: if residue A is close to B, and B is close to C, then A should be reasonably close to C. This is not something a vanilla transformer would learn easily — the architecture encodes it directly.

Third, Invariant Point Attention (IPA). Once the Evoformer has built a rich pair representation, the Structure Module uses IPA to predict 3D coordinates. The challenge: the same protein looks different if you rotate or translate it in space, but the structure is the same. IPA operates in a local reference frame attached to each residue, making it SE(3)-equivariant — the output transforms correctly when the input is rotated or translated. Each residue carries a rigid-body frame (rotation + translation), and attention is computed using both the sequence features and 3D point positions projected into each residue's local frame.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class SimplifiedIPA(nn.Module):
    """
    Stripped-down Invariant Point Attention.
    Real IPA uses full rigid-body frames; here we illustrate the
    core idea of combining sequence features with 3D point features.
    """
    def __init__(self, dim=128, num_heads=8, num_points=4):
        super().__init__()
        self.num_heads = num_heads
        self.num_points = num_points
        self.head_dim = dim // num_heads

        # Standard query/key/value for sequence features
        self.to_qkv = nn.Linear(dim, 3 * dim, bias=False)

        # Point queries/keys/values: each head produces num_points 3D points
        self.to_point_qkv = nn.Linear(dim, 3 * num_heads * num_points * 3)

        self.out_proj = nn.Linear(dim + num_heads * num_points * 3, dim)

    def forward(self, s, translations):
        """
        s: (batch, num_residues, dim) - per-residue features
        translations: (batch, num_residues, 3) - residue positions in 3D
        """
        B, N, D = s.shape
        qkv = self.to_qkv(s).chunk(3, dim=-1)
        q, k, v = [x.view(B, N, self.num_heads, self.head_dim) for x in qkv]

        # Sequence-based attention scores
        attn = torch.einsum('bnhd,bmhd->bhnm', q, k)
        attn = attn / (self.head_dim ** 0.5)

        # Point-based attention: compute pairwise distances
        # between query points and key points in global frame
        diff = translations.unsqueeze(2) - translations.unsqueeze(1)  # (B, N, N, 3)
        dist_sq = (diff ** 2).sum(-1)  # (B, N, N)
        point_attn = -dist_sq.unsqueeze(1)  # (B, 1, N, N) broadcast over heads

        # Combine sequence and point attention
        attn = attn + point_attn
        attn = torch.softmax(attn, dim=-1)

        # Aggregate sequence values
        seq_out = torch.einsum('bhnm,bmhd->bnhd', attn, v)
        seq_out = seq_out.reshape(B, N, -1)

        # Aggregate point positions (simplified)
        point_out = torch.einsum('bhnm,bmc->bhnc', attn, translations)
        point_out = point_out.reshape(B, N, -1)

        return self.out_proj(torch.cat([seq_out, point_out], dim=-1))
Why Equivariance Matters

If you rotate a protein 90°, the predicted structure should also rotate 90° — not change shape. Without equivariance, the model would need to see every possible rotation during training. With it, the symmetry is built into the architecture, so you get correct rotational behavior for free. This same principle powers GNNs for molecules and physics simulators.

AlphaFold 3: Diffusion-Based Protein Prediction

AlphaFold 2 handles single-chain proteins beautifully, but biology is messier. Proteins bind to other proteins, to DNA, to RNA, to small drug molecules. AlphaFold 3 (2024) extended the system to predict the joint structure of any biomolecular complex. The key architectural shift: the Structure Module was replaced with a diffusion model.

Instead of directly predicting 3D coordinates, AF3 starts from random noise and iteratively denoises atom positions — the same process used in image generation (Stable Diffusion, DALL·E). This is a natural fit because molecular structures are continuous 3D point clouds, and diffusion models excel at generating such data. The diffusion process also provides a way to sample multiple conformations, capturing the inherent flexibility of biomolecules.

Diffusion Models for Molecular Design

The diffusion framework extends beyond structure prediction into de novo molecular design. Given a target protein pocket, you want to generate a small molecule that fits snugly inside. Models like DiffSBDD and TargetDiff frame this as conditional 3D generation: start from noise, denoise atom positions conditioned on the pocket geometry.

The training loop mirrors image diffusion. You take known drug-target complexes, add noise to the drug atom positions at various levels, and train a network to predict the noise. At inference, you start from pure noise inside the pocket and run the reverse process. The output is a 3D molecule that (hopefully) binds tightly.

Protein Language Models

MSAs are powerful but slow to compute — you need to search through massive sequence databases for every new protein. Protein language models like ESM-2 (from Meta) skip the MSA entirely. They are trained with masked language modeling (identical to BERT) on hundreds of millions of protein sequences. The learned embeddings capture evolutionary information implicitly: the model has seen so many sequences that it "knows" which mutations are plausible at each position.

ESMFold plugs ESM-2 embeddings directly into a structure prediction head and achieves near-AlphaFold accuracy on many targets — with inference 60× faster because there is no MSA search step. This is a recurring pattern in scientific ML: a large foundation model pre-trained on sequences can replace expensive domain-specific preprocessing.

Weather and Climate

Numerical Weather Prediction

For 70 years, weather forecasting has been dominated by Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) — solving the Navier-Stokes equations of fluid dynamics on a 3D grid covering Earth's atmosphere. The European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) runs the gold-standard model, IFS, on a grid with roughly 9 km horizontal resolution. Each 10-day forecast takes about an hour on a massive supercomputer. The model ingests observations from satellites, weather balloons, ships, and aircraft through a process called data assimilation.

NWP works, but it is computationally brutal. Running ensemble forecasts (many slightly different initial conditions to capture uncertainty) multiplies the cost. Climate projections over decades are even more expensive. This is where ML enters the picture.

GraphCast: GNN Weather Forecasting

DeepMind's GraphCast (2023) reframed weather forecasting as a learned graph-to-graph mapping. The key ideas:

The Earth is a sphere, and regular latitude-longitude grids have problems — grid cells bunch up near the poles. GraphCast uses an icosahedral mesh: start with an icosahedron (20 triangular faces), subdivide each face recursively, and project onto the sphere. This gives a nearly uniform mesh with ~40,000 nodes. Each node represents a patch of atmosphere, and edges connect neighboring patches.

The architecture has three stages. An encoder maps from the lat-lon input grid to the icosahedral mesh using learned interpolation. A processor applies multiple rounds of message passing on the mesh — each node aggregates information from its neighbors, updates its features, and passes them along. A decoder maps back to the lat-lon grid for the output. The entire thing is a Graph Neural Network (GNN).

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class WeatherGNNLayer(nn.Module):
    """
    Single message-passing layer for weather prediction on
    an icosahedral mesh. Each node holds atmospheric state
    variables (temperature, pressure, wind, humidity, etc.).
    """
    def __init__(self, node_dim=256, edge_dim=64):
        super().__init__()
        # Edge MLP: takes sender features, receiver features, edge attrs
        self.edge_fn = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(2 * node_dim + edge_dim, 256),
            nn.SiLU(),
            nn.Linear(256, 256),
        )
        # Node MLP: takes old node features + aggregated messages
        self.node_fn = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(node_dim + 256, 256),
            nn.SiLU(),
            nn.Linear(256, node_dim),
        )

    def forward(self, nodes, edge_index, edge_attr):
        """
        nodes:      (num_nodes, node_dim)
        edge_index: (2, num_edges) - [senders, receivers]
        edge_attr:  (num_edges, edge_dim) - e.g., distance, direction
        """
        src, dst = edge_index
        # Compute edge messages
        edge_input = torch.cat([nodes[src], nodes[dst], edge_attr], dim=-1)
        messages = self.edge_fn(edge_input)

        # Aggregate messages at each receiver node (sum)
        agg = torch.zeros_like(nodes[:, :256])
        agg.scatter_add_(0, dst.unsqueeze(-1).expand_as(messages), messages)

        # Update node features with residual connection
        node_input = torch.cat([nodes, agg], dim=-1)
        return nodes + self.node_fn(node_input)


class SimpleGraphCast(nn.Module):
    """Simplified GraphCast: encode -> process -> decode."""
    def __init__(self, input_dim=78, output_dim=78, hidden=256,
                 edge_dim=64, num_layers=8):
        super().__init__()
        self.encoder = nn.Linear(input_dim, hidden)
        self.layers = nn.ModuleList(
            [WeatherGNNLayer(hidden, edge_dim) for _ in range(num_layers)]
        )
        self.decoder = nn.Linear(hidden, output_dim)

    def forward(self, x, edge_index, edge_attr):
        h = self.encoder(x)
        for layer in self.layers:
            h = layer(h, edge_index, edge_attr)
        return self.decoder(h)  # predict next-step atmospheric state

GraphCast is trained on 39 years of ERA5 reanalysis data (the best available historical weather record). It takes two consecutive 6-hour snapshots as input and predicts the next 6-hour snapshot. For multi-day forecasts, you apply the model autoregressively — feed the prediction back as input and roll forward. A 10-day forecast requires 40 autoregressive steps. Even so, it runs in under a minute on a single TPU v4, versus hours on a supercomputer for NWP.

Autoregressive Weather Rollout

Autoregressive rollout introduces a subtle problem: errors compound. If your 6-hour prediction is off by 0.1°C, that error feeds into the next step, which is now starting from a slightly wrong state. By step 40, small biases can snowball into blurry, oversmoothed forecasts. GraphCast mitigates this by training with multi-step rollouts during training — backpropagating through 2–3 autoregressive steps so the model learns to produce outputs that are good not only for the next step but also as inputs for future steps.

Probabilistic Weather Forecasting

Ensemble forecasting is how meteorologists quantify uncertainty. Instead of one deterministic forecast, you run 50+ simulations with slightly perturbed initial conditions. If all 50 agree that it will rain tomorrow, confidence is high. If they diverge, uncertainty is high. In NWP, each ensemble member costs as much as the deterministic run — so 50 members means 50× the compute.

ML models make ensembles cheap. GenCast (DeepMind, 2024) uses a diffusion model to generate stochastic weather forecasts. Each sample from the diffusion process is one ensemble member. Because the model is fast, you can generate hundreds of members in the time NWP takes for one. This unlocks better tail-risk estimation — rare but impactful events like hurricanes and heat waves.

The Data Assimilation Gap

ML weather models are trained on reanalysis data, which itself comes from NWP data assimilation. They cannot yet ingest raw observations (satellite radiances, balloon soundings) directly. This means ML models are still downstream of traditional NWP for generating initial conditions. Closing this gap — building end-to-end learned data assimilation — is an active research frontier.

Mathematics and Formal Reasoning

Proof Assistants and Formal Verification

Mathematicians write proofs in natural language — English with symbols. These proofs can contain subtle gaps and errors. Formal verification removes that ambiguity by expressing proofs in a language that a computer can check mechanically, step by step. Tools like Lean, Coq, and Isabelle are proof assistants: you write proof steps, and the system verifies each one against the rules of logic. If the proof type-checks, it is correct. Period.

The problem is that writing formal proofs is agonizingly slow. Formalizing a single textbook theorem can take days. The ratio of informal-to-formal effort is roughly 10:1. This is where AI comes in: if a model can suggest proof steps, it dramatically accelerates the formalization process.

Reinforcement Learning for Theorem Proving

Theorem proving maps naturally onto the RL framework. The state is the current proof context (hypotheses you have, goals you need to prove). The actions are proof tactics — rewrite rules, case splits, induction, applying known lemmas. The reward is binary: did the proof close (all goals discharged)? This is a sparse-reward problem, which makes it hard, but tree search helps.

The approach pioneered by systems like GPT-f (OpenAI) and later refined by others works like this: train a language model on a corpus of existing formal proofs so it can suggest likely tactics. Then use that model as the policy in a tree search — from the current proof state, generate multiple candidate tactics, apply each one to get new proof states, and recurse. This is conceptually identical to AlphaGo's Monte Carlo Tree Search, but over proof trees instead of game trees.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class ProofPolicyNetwork(nn.Module):
    """
    Given a proof state (as a token sequence), predict
    the next tactic to apply. Used inside a tree search.
    """
    def __init__(self, vocab_size=32000, dim=512, num_layers=6, num_heads=8):
        super().__init__()
        self.embed = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, dim)
        self.pos_enc = nn.Embedding(2048, dim)
        encoder_layer = nn.TransformerEncoderLayer(
            d_model=dim, nhead=num_heads, dim_feedforward=2048,
            batch_first=True, activation='gelu'
        )
        self.transformer = nn.TransformerEncoder(encoder_layer, num_layers)
        self.tactic_head = nn.Linear(dim, vocab_size)
        self.value_head = nn.Linear(dim, 1)  # probability of proof closing

    def forward(self, proof_state_tokens):
        """
        proof_state_tokens: (batch, seq_len) - tokenized proof state
        Returns: tactic_logits, value
        """
        B, T = proof_state_tokens.shape
        pos = torch.arange(T, device=proof_state_tokens.device).unsqueeze(0)
        x = self.embed(proof_state_tokens) + self.pos_enc(pos)
        x = self.transformer(x)

        # Use last token for tactic prediction (autoregressive)
        tactic_logits = self.tactic_head(x)

        # Use mean-pooled representation for value estimate
        value = self.value_head(x.mean(dim=1))
        return tactic_logits, torch.sigmoid(value)

AlphaProof: Formal Math Theorem Proving

AlphaProof (DeepMind, 2024) proved problems from the International Mathematical Olympiad at a silver-medal level. The system combines a fine-tuned Gemini language model with AlphaZero-style reinforcement learning over the Lean proof assistant. The pipeline has three stages:

First, autoformalization: the language model translates the natural-language competition problem into a formal Lean statement. This is itself a hard translation task — mathematical language is dense and ambiguous, and the formal version must be precisely equivalent. Second, the language model proposes proof sketches. Third, an RL-trained search agent explores the proof tree, using the language model's suggestions as a prior but also discovering novel proof steps through self-play. The agent is trained on millions of synthetic problems generated by perturbing known theorems.

The result is remarkable. On IMO 2024, AlphaProof solved 4 out of 6 problems, including the hardest problem on the exam (Problem 6, which only a handful of human contestants solved). The proofs are verified — Lean checked every step. There is no possibility of hallucination when the proof assistant confirms the result.

Autoformalization

Autoformalization is the task of translating informal mathematics (textbook proofs, competition problems, research papers) into formal proof assistant code. It is one of the most promising applications of large language models in science because it can dramatically accelerate the creation of verified mathematical libraries. The gap between informal and formal math is one of the biggest barriers to formal verification adoption.

Biology and Genomics

Neuro-Symbolic AI for Biology

Pure neural networks are powerful pattern matchers, but they can be black boxes. In biology, interpretability matters — a drug regulator will not approve a therapy based on "the neural network said so." Neuro-symbolic AI combines neural networks with symbolic reasoning. The neural component learns patterns from data; the symbolic component enforces logical rules and produces human-readable explanations.

For example, in gene regulatory network inference, a neural network might learn which genes influence each other from expression data. A symbolic layer can then enforce known biological constraints: gene A cannot regulate gene B if they are in different cellular compartments and there is no known signaling pathway between them. The result is predictions that are both accurate and biologically plausible.

Evolutionary Program Search

An alternative to gradient-based training is evolutionary program search. Instead of optimizing weights with backpropagation, you maintain a population of candidate programs (small code snippets or mathematical expressions), evaluate them on a fitness function, and evolve them through mutation and crossover. Google DeepMind's FunSearch (2024) used an LLM as the mutation operator — given a parent program, the LLM suggests modifications. This discovered new mathematical results in extremal combinatorics, finding constructions that beat the best known human-designed solutions.

The approach is powerful for problems where the solution is a small program rather than a large weight matrix. The LLM provides intelligent mutations (rather than random perturbations), and the evaluator provides ground-truth fitness. There is no risk of hallucination because every candidate is executed and scored.

Zero-Shot Generalization and Foundation Models for Science

Zero-shot generalization — performing well on tasks never seen during training — is the holy grail for scientific foundation models. Models like Geneformer (for single-cell genomics) and SciBERT (for scientific text) are pre-trained on massive domain datasets and then applied to downstream tasks with minimal fine-tuning. The dream is a single foundation model for biology that can predict gene function, drug response, protein interactions, and disease mechanisms — all from the same pre-trained weights.

We are not there yet, but the trajectory is clear. Each domain is building its own foundation model: ESM for proteins, Enformer for genomics, MedPaLM for clinical text. Over time, these may merge into multimodal scientific models that reason across domains.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class GeneExpressionEncoder(nn.Module):
    """
    Simplified foundation model for single-cell gene expression.
    Treats each gene as a 'token' with expression level as the value.
    Pre-trains with masked gene modeling (like masked LM).
    """
    def __init__(self, num_genes=20000, dim=256, num_layers=4, num_heads=8):
        super().__init__()
        self.gene_embed = nn.Embedding(num_genes, dim)
        self.expr_proj = nn.Linear(1, dim)  # project expression value

        encoder_layer = nn.TransformerEncoderLayer(
            d_model=dim, nhead=num_heads, dim_feedforward=1024,
            batch_first=True, activation='gelu'
        )
        self.encoder = nn.TransformerEncoder(encoder_layer, num_layers)
        self.predict_expression = nn.Linear(dim, 1)

    def forward(self, gene_ids, expression_values, mask):
        """
        gene_ids:          (batch, num_expressed_genes) - which genes are expressed
        expression_values: (batch, num_expressed_genes, 1) - their expression levels
        mask:              (batch, num_expressed_genes) - True for masked genes
        """
        gene_emb = self.gene_embed(gene_ids)
        expr_emb = self.expr_proj(expression_values)

        # Zero out masked gene expression embeddings
        expr_emb = expr_emb * (~mask).unsqueeze(-1).float()

        x = gene_emb + expr_emb
        x = self.encoder(x)

        # Predict expression of masked genes
        return self.predict_expression(x[mask])

Emerging Frontiers

Vision-Language-Action Models

Scientific experiments require physical manipulation — pipetting liquids, adjusting instruments, handling samples. Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models take a camera image (vision), a natural language instruction ("pipette 5 μL into well A3"), and produce motor commands (action). These are essentially multimodal foundation models fine-tuned for robotic control.

RT-2 (Google, 2023) showed that a large vision-language model can be fine-tuned to output robot actions as text tokens. The model sees a camera feed, reads a task description, and generates action tokens that are decoded into joint velocities. This means a single model can handle novel instructions without retraining — "move the red beaker to the left" works even if the model never saw that exact phrasing during training. For lab automation, this is transformative.

Sim-to-Real Transfer and Domain Randomization

Training robots directly in the real world is expensive and dangerous. Sim-to-real transfer trains policies in simulation and deploys them on physical hardware. The challenge: simulators are imperfect. A robot arm that works perfectly in simulation may fail in the real world because the simulator got friction, lighting, or sensor noise wrong.

Domain randomization is the brute-force fix. During simulation training, you randomize everything: object colors, masses, friction coefficients, camera positions, lighting conditions. The policy sees such a wide distribution of environments that the real world becomes "just another sample" from the training distribution. It sounds crude, but it works surprisingly well for many manipulation tasks.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class SimToRealPolicy(nn.Module):
    """
    Policy network for robotic control with domain randomization.
    Takes observation + domain parameters, outputs actions.
    During training, domain params are randomized.
    At deployment, they can be estimated or set to defaults.
    """
    def __init__(self, obs_dim=64, domain_dim=16, action_dim=7, hidden=256):
        super().__init__()
        self.domain_encoder = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(domain_dim, hidden),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, 64),
        )
        self.policy = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(obs_dim + 64, hidden),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, action_dim),
            nn.Tanh(),  # bound actions to [-1, 1]
        )

    def forward(self, observation, domain_params):
        """
        observation:   (batch, obs_dim) - robot state + sensor readings
        domain_params: (batch, domain_dim) - friction, mass, etc.
        """
        domain_feat = self.domain_encoder(domain_params)
        x = torch.cat([observation, domain_feat], dim=-1)
        return self.policy(x)

Active Learning for Experiments

Active learning closes the loop between model and experiment. Instead of collecting a fixed dataset and training on it, the model chooses which experiments to run next. The idea: if the model is uncertain about a region of chemical space, it requests experiments there. If it is confident, it skips those. This can reduce the number of experiments needed by 5–10× compared to random exploration.

In drug discovery, this means the model might suggest: "Synthesize these 50 molecules next — they are in a part of chemical space where my predictions are uncertain, and they are likely to be active based on my current model." The chemist synthesizes them, tests them, feeds the results back, and the model updates. Each cycle narrows the search more efficiently than brute-force screening.

Retrosynthesis

Retrosynthesis is the inverse of chemical synthesis: given a target molecule, what sequence of reactions produces it from commercially available starting materials? This is a planning problem over a massive graph of possible chemical transformations. Traditional retrosynthesis tools use hand-coded reaction rules. ML-based approaches like Retro* use a policy network (trained on databases of known reactions) to suggest promising disconnection sites, combined with tree search to find complete synthetic routes.

The combination of retrosynthesis, molecular generation, and property prediction creates an end-to-end drug design pipeline: generate candidate molecules with desired properties, predict which ones can be synthesized, plan the synthetic route, and estimate the cost. Each step uses a different ML model, but they chain together into an automated discovery system.

The Validation Bottleneck

AI can propose a million drug candidates, forecast weather for the next month, and suggest proof strategies for unsolved theorems. But until those predictions are validated — in the wet lab, against real weather, or by a proof assistant — they remain hypotheses. The models that work best in practice are those embedded in tight feedback loops where predictions are quickly tested and the model learns from failures. Never trust a scientific ML model's output without domain-appropriate validation.

The Unifying Pattern

Across all these domains, the playbook is the same. Step one: identify the structural prior. Proteins are chains in 3D space — use equivariant attention. Weather lives on a sphere — use an icosahedral GNN. Proofs are trees of logical steps — use tree search with a learned policy. Step two: collect or generate a massive domain dataset. Step three: train a model that respects the domain structure. Step four: embed the model in a loop with domain validation.

The models are not magic. They are pattern matchers with the right inductive biases, trained on the right data, and deployed in the right feedback loops. What makes this era special is that we finally have the compute, the data, and the architectural ideas to make this work at the scale science demands. The next decade will see AI become as fundamental to scientific research as the microscope and the spectrometer. The question is not whether AI will transform science — it is which discoveries it will make first.

Where to Go From Here

If you want to get hands-on: for molecular ML, start with PyTorch Geometric and the OGB-LSC molecular benchmarks. For weather, explore WeatherBench 2 and the publicly available GraphCast weights. For theorem proving, install Lean 4 and try the Mathlib library. For genomics, look at the scGPT and Geneformer codebases. Each of these has active open-source communities welcoming newcomers. The best way to learn scientific ML is to pick a domain you care about and start building.
