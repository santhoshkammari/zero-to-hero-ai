Societal Impact

Chapter 16: Responsible AI and Ethics 5 subtopics
TL;DR

AI systems not neutral tools — they reshape societies, economies, individual lives in ways creators often miss. Generative AI creates deepfakes that erode trust; hiring algorithms reproduce decades of discrimination; surveillance systems threaten civil liberties at scale. Understanding societal impact not soft add-on to ML engineering — core competency, because systems you build affect millions whether you plan for it or not.

Amazon built internal resume screening tool around 2014. Idea made sense: feed system ten years of hiring data, let it learn what "good" candidate looks like, use that to rank applicants. Model worked exactly as designed. It learned patterns in data. Problem: data reflected decade of mostly male tech hiring. System penalized resumes mentioning women's colleges. It downgraded word "women's" in any context. Amazon scrapped project in 2018, but lesson hurts: AI did not malfunction. It learned exactly what data taught. Societal impact of AI not about broken code — about code working as trained on world full of historical inequities.

This section covers bigger picture. Before model architectures and loss functions, understand what happens when predictions leave Jupyter notebook and land in someone's life.

Generative AI Ethics

Generative models — GANs, diffusion models, large language models — produce text, images, audio, video increasingly indistinguishable from human-created content. Capability brings equal parts power and danger.

Deepfakes and Synthetic Media

Deepfake = synthetic media where person's likeness — face, voice, or both — gets replaced or fabricated using AI. Technology behind it, usually autoencoders or GANs trained on face-swap tasks, now sits within reach of anyone with laptop and few tutorial hours.

Real-world harms are concrete. In 2023, deepfake audio of school principal making racist remarks went viral in US — entirely fabricated by disgruntled staff member using off-the-shelf AI tools. Political deepfakes appeared in elections worldwide, from Slovakia to Bangladesh. Non-consensual intimate imagery generated with deepfake tools has become growing crisis, disproportionately targeting women.

# Detecting deepfakes: a simplified forensic approach
# Real detectors use more sophisticated architectures, but the concept is the same

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
from torchvision import models, transforms
from PIL import Image

class DeepfakeDetector(nn.Module):
    """Binary classifier: real (0) vs fake (1)
    Uses transfer learning from EfficientNet."""
    
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        self.backbone = models.efficientnet_b0(pretrained=True)
        # Replace classifier head
        in_features = self.backbone.classifier[1].in_features
        self.backbone.classifier = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Dropout(0.3),
            nn.Linear(in_features, 1),
            nn.Sigmoid()
        )
    
    def forward(self, x):
        return self.backbone(x)

# Key insight: deepfake detectors look for artifacts that
# generation models leave behind — inconsistent lighting,
# blending boundaries, frequency-domain anomalies.
# But generators keep improving, so detection is an arms race.
Arms race problem: Every improvement in deepfake detection feeds better generators. This differs from traditional security — you are not patching bug, you are in adversarial co-evolution. No detection system gives permanent safety.

Preventing Misuse of Generative Models

Model providers use several strategies. Content provenance standards like C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity) embed cryptographic metadata into generated images. Watermarking techniques insert imperceptible signals into AI-generated content. OpenAI, Google, Meta all implemented safety filters and usage policies. But none are foolproof. Open-source models run without safety filters. Fine-tuning can remove watermarks. Core tension: same capabilities that make generative AI useful — creativity, flexibility, accessibility — also make it dangerous.

Personalization vs Manipulation

Line between "this recommendation system knows what you like" and "this recommendation system is engineering your behavior" stays blurry. Recommendation algorithms on social media platforms optimize for engagement. Engagement correlates with emotional intensity. Emotional intensity correlates with outrage, fear, tribalism. System is not trying to radicalize anyone — it is trying to maximize metric. Downstream effect stays same.

This is not hypothetical. Internal documents from multiple social media companies show their own researchers flagged these dynamics years before public awareness caught up. Algorithms were not buggy. They optimized exactly what they were told to optimize. Problem was objective function, not optimizer.

AI-Generated Content and Misinformation

Large language models produce convincing misinformation at scale — not because they are "trying" to mislead, but because they generate statistically plausible text regardless of truth value. During election cycles, AI-generated fake news articles, fabricated quotes, synthetic "evidence" become real threats to informed public discourse. Cost of producing misinformation drops near zero while cost of fact-checking stays high. That asymmetry is core challenge.

Domain-Specific Ethics

AI in Healthcare Diagnosis

Medical AI presents real ethical paradox. Dermatology model trained mostly on lighter-skinned patients will underperform on darker skin — not from intent, but from unrepresentative training data. Studies show dermatology AI systems can have sharply different accuracy across skin tones. In healthcare, false negative is not abstract metric — it is missed cancer diagnosis.

At same time, AI can extend specialist-level diagnostic capability to regions with severe physician shortages. Well-calibrated retinal scan AI in rural India can detect diabetic retinopathy that otherwise goes undiagnosed until patient goes blind. Ethical calculus is not "AI good" or "AI bad" — it is "for whom, under what conditions, with what oversight?"

AI in Criminal Justice (COMPAS Case Study)

COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions) is textbook case study for good reason. ProPublica's 2016 investigation found COMPAS had significantly different false positive rates across racial groups: Black defendants were roughly twice as likely as white defendants to be incorrectly flagged as high-risk for recidivism.

Northpointe (now Equivant), company behind COMPAS, responded that system achieved equal predictive accuracy across groups — meaning among people scored as high-risk, similar proportions of Black and white defendants actually reoffended. Both claims were mathematically correct. This is not bug — it is impossibility theorem of fairness at work. You cannot satisfy all reasonable definitions of fairness at same time when base rates differ between groups. We explore this deeply in Section 2.

# Illustrating the impossibility: simulated recidivism scores
import numpy as np

np.random.seed(42)

# Simulated recidivism scores (0–10) for two groups
# Group A has a higher base rate (e.g., 40% vs 25%)
# due to systemic factors (policing patterns, poverty, etc.)
group_a_scores = np.random.normal(5.5, 2.0, 1000).clip(0, 10)
group_b_scores = np.random.normal(4.0, 2.0, 1000).clip(0, 10)

# If we set ONE threshold for "high risk"...
threshold = 5.0
flagged_a = (group_a_scores > threshold).mean()  # ~60%
flagged_b = (group_b_scores > threshold).mean()  # ~31%

print(f"Group A flagged as high-risk: {flagged_a:.1%}")
print(f"Group B flagged as high-risk: {flagged_b:.1%}")
print(f"Ratio: {flagged_a / flagged_b:.2f}x")
# Same threshold, vastly different impact.
# Adjusting the threshold to equalize rates
# will change error distributions. You can't win everywhere.

AI in Hiring (Amazon Case Study)

Amazon recruiting tool was not isolated case. HireVue's video interview analysis, scoring facial expressions and speech patterns, faced backlash for opacity and potential bias. In 2023, New York City's Local Law 144 took effect, requiring bias audits for automated employment decision tools — first law of its kind in US. Illinois and Maryland passed related legislation targeting AI in hiring. Regulatory landscape is catching up to technology, but slowly.

Autonomous Decision-Making Systems

Self-driving cars, autonomous drones, algorithmic trading — these systems make decisions in real time without human intervention. When autonomous vehicle faces unavoidable accident, who is responsible? "Trolley problem" framing is philosophically interesting but practically misleading. Real ethical challenge is more mundane: how do you validate system operating in open world with infinite edge cases? How many miles of safe driving count as "enough" testing? What accident rate is acceptable compared with human drivers?

Economic and Social Impact

AI Impact on Employment

Automation anxiety is not new — people worried about looms destroying weaving jobs in 1800s. But this wave differs. Previous automation hit mainly manual and routine cognitive tasks. Large language models and multimodal AI now perform tasks in writing, analysis, coding, design, customer service — domains once seen as automation-proof. Pace of capability improvement also exceeds historical precedents.

Evidence so far is mixed. Technology has historically created more jobs than it destroyed, but transition periods are brutal for affected workers. People who lose jobs and people who gain new ones are often not same people, in same places, with same skills. Policy question is not whether AI will create net economic value — it likely will — but whether that value will be distributed in anything resembling fairness.

Responsibility Assignment When AI Causes Harm

When human doctor misdiagnoses, liability is clear. When AI-assisted diagnosis goes wrong, who is responsible? Developer? Deploying hospital? Doctor who relied on AI's recommendation? Current legal frameworks were not designed for this. EU AI Act, which entered into force in August 2024, is first comprehensive attempt to create regulatory framework, establishing clear liability chains and documentation requirements for high-risk AI systems.

Lessons from Past AI Failures

Three cases every ML engineer should know: (1) Amazon's hiring tool, gender bias from historical data; (2) COMPAS, racial disparities in recidivism prediction showing fairness impossibility; and (3) Microsoft's Tay chatbot (2016), which learned from Twitter interactions and began producing racist and inflammatory content within 24 hours. Each failure had different root cause — biased training data, incompatible fairness definitions, adversarial user manipulation — but all shared one thread: builders did not model how system would interact with messy, biased, adversarial real world.

Lesson connecting all three: Technical performance on held-out test sets tells you almost nothing about societal impact. Model can have 95% accuracy and still cause systematic harm to specific communities. Evaluation must include who is affected and how.

AI Surveillance and Biometric Privacy

Facial Recognition Ethics and Bans

Facial recognition technology (FRT) became lightning rod for AI surveillance debates. Studies by Joy Buolamwini and Timnit Gebru, "Gender Shades," showed commercial facial recognition systems from IBM, Microsoft, Face++ had error rates up to 34% for darker-skinned women, compared with less than 1% for lighter-skinned men. This was not theoretical concern — it meant real people being wrongly identified by law enforcement.

Multiple US cities, including San Francisco, Boston, Minneapolis, banned government use of facial recognition. EU AI Act classifies real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces as prohibited practice, with narrow law enforcement exceptions. Several US states introduced biometric privacy laws, with Illinois' BIPA most aggressive and backed by private right of action that generated billions in settlements.

Biometric Data Regulations

Biometric data — fingerprints, face geometry, voice patterns, iris scans — is unique because you cannot change it after compromise. Unlike password, you cannot reset face. Reason: regulations treat biometric data as special category. GDPR classifies it as "special category" data requiring explicit consent. India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023) and Brazil's LGPD impose similar protections. Trend is clear: biometric data gets stricter treatment than general personal data.

Mass Surveillance Risks

China's social credit system and extensive surveillance camera network show extreme end of AI-enabled surveillance. But risk does not stop at authoritarian contexts. Clearview AI scraped billions of photos from social media to build facial recognition database used by law enforcement agencies worldwide — without consent from anyone pictured. Technology enables mass surveillance at previously impossible scale, shifting power balance between institutions and individuals.

Labor Market and Automation Impact

Job Displacement and Reskilling

Goldman Sachs estimated in 2023 that generative AI could affect 300 million full-time jobs globally. McKinsey's research suggests up to 30% of hours worked could be automated by 2030. Numbers stay uncertain, but direction is clear. Reskilling challenge is enormous — not everyone can "learn to code," and even coding itself is being partially automated. Effective policy responses likely include investment in education, portable benefits, transition support — but these are societal decisions, not technical ones.

Economic Inequality

AI tends to concentrate wealth. Training large models requires massive capital expenditure. Companies with most data and compute build best models, which attract more users, which generate more data. This flywheel effect means economic benefits of AI disproportionately accrue to small number of firms and shareholders. Workers whose tasks are automated bear costs. Without deliberate policy intervention, AI is likely to widen, not narrow, economic inequality.

Gig Economy and AI

Platform workers — ride-share drivers, delivery workers, content moderators — are already managed by algorithms. Algorithm sets prices, assigns work, evaluates performance, can "deactivate" workers with no explanation. These workers have little visibility into, or recourse against, algorithmic decisions that directly affect livelihoods. This is AI governance in immediate form: real people, real consequences, minimal transparency.

Core engineering responsibility: You do not need to solve inequality or fix democracy. But you do need to think beyond accuracy metrics. Ask: who is affected by this system? What happens when it is wrong? Who has recourse? If you cannot answer these questions, system is not ready for production — no matter how good ROC curve looks.
