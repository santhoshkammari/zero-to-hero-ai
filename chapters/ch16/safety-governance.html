Governance feels like somebody else's job — until client compliance team flags your model and you have no documentation, no risk assessment, no audit trail. Then you cannot answer "how does this thing make decisions, and who's responsible when it's wrong?" That question runs through everything below.

AI safety and governance = practices, frameworks, and regulations that shape how AI systems get built responsibly, deployed safely, and held accountable when things break. Scope spans technical alignment, international law, and company structure. Field exploded after 2023 because large language models spread fast and high-profile AI failures made governments pay attention.

The AI Safety Landscape

AI safety world split in two. Split shapes whole conversation. One side worries about near-term risks — harms happening now. Biased hiring algorithms. Hallucinating chatbots giving medical advice. Deepfakes hurting elections. Models leaking personal data. Real. Documented. Growing. OECD's AI Incidents Monitor tracked 233 incidents in 2024, up 56% from year before.

Other side worries about long-term risks — sometimes called existential or catastrophic risks. Question: what happens when AI systems become much better than humans at most cognitive tasks? Could system chase goals misaligned with human interests in ways humans cannot correct? Sounds like science fiction. Serious researchers at serious institutions work on this full-time.

Long-term risk view sounded alarmist to me for long time. Then I read actual arguments, not headlines. Core concern is not "robot decide kill us." Concern more subtle: AI system optimizing for badly specified objective at superhuman capability could cause enormous damage with zero malice. Small version already here — recommendation algorithm optimizing for engagement, ending up radicalizing teenagers. Scale that up. Far up.

Practical reality: both camps right. Concerns not competing. Hiring model that discriminates against women = near-term safety failure. Frontier model that can synthesize novel bioweapons = long-term safety concern. Both need governance. Difference is urgency, certainty, and available tools.

Think city building safety. Need fire codes for buildings standing today (near-term). Need earthquake engineering for big one that may hit in fifty years (long-term). Nobody sensible picks one. City needs both. Work on each helps other.

A Small Company, Three Models, and a Government Letter

Use running example. Small healthcare AI company: MedScreen. Three models in production:

Model A — diagnostic assistant helping radiologists flag possible tumors in chest X-rays. Highlights suspicious areas. Gives confidence score. Human radiologist makes final call.

Model B — website chatbot answering patient questions about appointment scheduling, insurance coverage, and general health topics. No medical diagnoses, though patients still try.

Model C — internal tool predicting which patients will likely miss appointments, so admin team can send reminders. Uses demographics, appointment history, and zip code.

One morning, MedScreen's CEO opens letter from European health authority. Message: "Under the EU AI Act, please provide documentation of your risk classification, conformity assessment, and ongoing monitoring procedures for all AI systems affecting EU patients."

CEO stares. Company has none of this. Company has Python scripts, few Jupyter notebooks, and Slack channel called #model-stuff. Story starts here. Common situation.

Risk Assessment: Figuring Out What Can Go Wrong

Cannot govern what you do not understand. First step: find what can fail. Many organizations miss here. They talk about risks as abstractions — "bias," "privacy," "safety" — instead of concrete failure modes for specific system.

Walk through risk assessment for MedScreen's three models. Ask four questions for each: Who is affected? What is worst realistic outcome? How likely? Can damage be reversed?

Model A, tumor detector. Affected: patients whose X-rays get analyzed. Worst realistic outcome: model misses tumor (false negative), patient's cancer goes undiagnosed for months. Or model flags healthy patient as suspicious (false positive), triggering unnecessary biopsies. Both happen. Both cause real harm — delayed treatment in one case, invasive procedures and psychological distress in other. Missed tumor damage is irreversible once discovered late. High-risk system.

Model B, scheduling chatbot. Affected: website visitors. Worst realistic outcome: chatbot gives wrong insurance information, patient arrives expecting coverage they do not have. Annoying. Costly. Correctable. Unless chatbot starts giving medical advice — forbidden, but patients push for it, and chatbots are bad at staying in lane. Possible scenario: someone asks "should I go to the ER?" and bot says "based on your symptoms, you're probably fine." Different risk category entirely.

Model C, appointment predictor. Affected: patients receiving or not receiving reminders. Worst realistic outcome: model learns patients from certain zip codes miss more appointments, and those zip codes correlate with race. Reminder system becomes proxy for racial profiling — patients from mostly minority neighborhoods get more aggressive reminder calls, which can feel intrusive or condescending. Nobody intended this. Model does not care about intentions.

NIST AI Risk Management Framework gives structure here. Released by US National Institute of Standards and Technology. Four functions: Govern (set policies and accountability), Map (identify failures and affected people), Measure (quantify risks with metrics and testing), and Manage (decide what to do — mitigate, accept, or avoid). Not legally binding, but government procurement contracts and court cases reference it more and more. "We followed NIST AI RMF" beats "we did our best."

City analogy still works. Risk assessment = building inspection. Walk structure before people move in. Find weak points. Decide what needs reinforcement. Do not skip because building "looks fine from outside." Risks that matter usually hide until you look carefully.

The EU AI Act: The Regulation That Changed Everything

EU AI Act entered force in August 2024. First comprehensive AI regulation in world. Like GDPR before it, Act applies to anyone whose systems affect EU residents — no matter where company sits. Startup in San Francisco building hiring tool for London company? Covered. Model trained in Singapore and deployed by API to Berlin hospital? Also covered.

First read feels overwhelming. Act is long. Legal language thick. Compliance timelines staggered in engineer-confusing way. Break it down piece by piece.

Risk Tiers

Whole Act runs on one idea: higher risk to fundamental rights and safety means stricter rules. Four tiers.

First tier: Unacceptable Risk. Uses banned outright. Social scoring by governments — assigning citizens trustworthiness score from behavior, like China's social credit system — is prohibited. So is real-time biometric identification in public spaces, with narrow law-enforcement exceptions; AI using subliminal manipulation; and systems exploiting vulnerabilities of groups like children or disabled people. Deadline to stop: February 2025. If you do this, already illegal.

Second tier: High Risk. Most compliance burden lives here. AI systems for hiring and recruitment, credit scoring, medical diagnosis, critical infrastructure management, law enforcement, immigration decisions, and education assessment fall here. Systems need risk management system, data governance procedures, technical documentation, logging and record-keeping, transparency provisions for users, human oversight mechanisms, plus accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity requirements. They also need conformity assessment — formal review proving system meets requirements — before deployment. Full compliance deadline: August 2026.

Third tier: Limited Risk. Chatbots, emotion recognition systems, and deepfake generators land here. Main requirement: transparency. Users must know they interact with AI or view AI-generated content. If chatbot does not clearly identify as non-human, violation. Same deadline: August 2026.

Fourth tier: Minimal Risk. Spam filters. AI in video games. Inventory management systems. No specific legal obligations, though EU encourages voluntary codes of conduct.

Back to MedScreen. Model A, tumor detector, is medical device — High Risk, full compliance required. Model B, patient chatbot, is Limited Risk — needs transparency disclosure. Model C, appointment predictor, is harder. If it makes predictions affecting patient access to care, and zip code correlation creates disparate impact, strong argument for High Risk. Legal counsel earns fee here.

General-Purpose AI Models

Foundation models and large language models get separate rules, outside risk tiers. All providers of General-Purpose AI (GPAI) models must provide technical documentation and comply with EU copyright law. Models trained with compute above 1025 FLOPs — GPT-4 scale and above — count as systemic risk. That triggers extra requirements: adversarial testing (red-teaming), incident reporting to EU AI Office, cybersecurity measures, and energy consumption reporting. Obligations started in August 2025.

Penalties

Numbers that wake executives up. Deploy banned AI system? Up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. Non-compliance with high-risk requirements? Up to €15 million or 3%. Give incorrect information to authorities? Up to €7.5 million or 1%. For SMEs and startups, fines cap at lower figure. Still enough to sink small company.

The Brussels Effect

Brussels Effect matters even if you never sell one product in Europe. Same movie as GDPR. Companies will not maintain two separate AI governance systems — one for Europe, one for everybody else. Engineering cost of forking compliance infrastructure is too high. So companies build to strictest standard and ship globally. EU AI Act becomes de facto global floor for AI governance, same way GDPR became global floor for data privacy.

City analogy changes here. EU published building code. Other cities watch. Some adopt whole thing. Others write own code, but use EU version as template. Buildings designed now get built to this code whether architect sits in Brussels or not.

The United States: Executive Orders and Their Afterlife

US approach to AI governance reads like political thriller. Big reversals. Fast reversals.

In October 2023, President Biden signed Executive Order 14110, most comprehensive US AI action so far. Order mandated safety testing and red-teaming for high-risk AI, required federal agencies to run risk assessments, set reporting requirements for developers of frontier models, and created whole-of-government framework for responsible AI development. Ambitious. Detailed. Lasted about fifteen months.

On January 20, 2025 — first day in office — President Trump revoked Biden's AI order. Three days later, he signed Executive Order 14179, "Removing Barriers to American Leadership in Artificial Intelligence." Frame changed completely: safety and oversight out; innovation and deregulation in. Mandatory red-teaming requirements got dropped. Agency risk monitoring got rolled back. New order told agencies to review and rescind Biden-era policies seen as barriers to AI innovation. By December 2025, another order sought federal preemption of state-level AI regulations, arguing state-law "patchwork" hurts interstate commerce.

Long-term meaning still unclear. Policy whiplash is real. But some pieces survived administration change. NIST AI Risk Management Framework remains intact — voluntary standard, not executive order, so one order cannot revoke it. Federal procurement references it more and more. Technical problems of AI safety also do not change with political winds. Hallucinating model does not care who signed latest order.

Engineer takeaway: do not build safety practices around one executive order. Build around durable standards (NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001) and your own organizational principles. Executive orders come and go. Good engineering practices compound.

The Global Patchwork

Outside EU and US, regulatory landscape is patchwork of philosophies. Each reflects country-wide relationship with technology and governance.

China has most prescriptive regime after EU, but character differs sharply. China mandates algorithm registration and pre-launch approval for AI systems. Generative AI must align with "core socialist values." Content control is tight. Penalties can reach 5–10% of revenue. Goal is not EU-style consumer protection — goal is state control over strategically important technology.

The UK went opposite direction: principles-based, decentralized, deliberately light-touch. No single AI law. Existing regulators — ICO for data, FCA for finance, CMA for competition — govern AI inside their own domains. Government publishes guidance but avoids prescriptive rules, aiming to foster innovation. Whether this stays sustainable as AI systems get stronger remains open question.

Canada sits in middle with proposed Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA), risk-based approach partly inspired by EU AI Act but tailored to Canada's legal framework. It classifies systems by risk, adds transparency and testing obligations, and layers them on top of existing privacy law.

Same tension across all these systems: how do you govern technology evolving faster than legislation? EU chose prescriptive path and accepted overregulation risk. UK chose flexible path and accepted underregulation risk. US oscillates with administration. China optimizes for state interests. None solved problem fully.

City analogy starts straining here. Not writing safety code for one city anymore. Trying to agree on building codes across countries that do not even agree on meaning of "safe." Fundamental governance challenge.

Organizational Governance: Building the Internal Machine

Regulations tell you what must happen. Organizational governance is internal machinery making it happen. Hard truth: organizations treating governance like engineering infrastructure ship faster than organizations treating it like legal checkbox.

Sounds backward. More process should mean slower, yes? Not when alternative is building model, deploying model, getting compliance letter, panicking, then spending six months documenting everything while model sits in limbo. MedScreen lacked governance infrastructure. Now company spends about three times longer getting Model A approved than if documentation pipeline existed from start.

What does organizational AI governance look like? Walk through components with MedScreen.

First, ownership and accountability. Every model needs owner — specific person, not team, not Slack channel. Model A's owner is Dr. Sarah Chen, lead radiologist who validated clinical performance. She is accountable for behavior in production. This does not mean she writes code. It means when European health authority sends letter, her name goes on response. Hard to establish in organizations where one team builds models, another deploys, third uses. Without clear ownership, accountability turns into finger-pointing.

Second, a model registry. MedScreen needs central record of every production model: what it does, who owns it, what data trained it, what limitations are known, and when last review happened. Organizational version of city building registry. Cannot inspect buildings you do not know exist.

Third, review gates. Before model reaches production, model passes defined review process. For high-risk model like Model A, process may include independent validation by somebody who did not build it, bias audit, security review, and sign-off from model owner. Lower-risk model like Model B may need lighter review. Key point: process exists and gets documented. Not every model needs same treatment.

Fourth, ongoing monitoring. Models degrade. Data drifts. World changes. Model performing beautifully at deployment may fail quietly three months later. MedScreen needs automated monitoring for Model A — tracking sensitivity and specificity on real-world cases, flagging when metrics drift beyond acceptable thresholds, and triggering human review when drift appears.

Fifth, retirement procedures. Every model needs defined end-of-life. What triggers retirement? What fallback exists? Model A retirement criteria might be: sensitivity drops below 0.90 on monthly validation, or fairness gap exceeds 5 percentage points across demographic groups. Models do not die gracefully alone. Models degrade silently, like building foundation that looks fine until floor caves in.

From Principles to Practice: Impact Assessments

Every major tech company publishes responsible AI principles. Fairness. Transparency. Accountability. Privacy. Looks good on website. Gap between principles and practice is where most organizations fail. Gap is huge.

I have seen companies with beautiful responsible AI frameworks that still could not answer basic questions about one model: Who does this affect? What happens when model is wrong? Did anyone check whether performance differs across demographic groups?

Algorithmic impact assessment (AIA) bridges this gap. Structured process. Run before deployment and again later. Force concrete thinking about specific system's effects on specific people. Canada's government adopted early, requiring AIAs for federal AI systems.

Walk through impact assessment for MedScreen's Model C, appointment predictor. Process starts with scoping: what system does, who it affects, and what decisions it informs. Model C predicts no-show probability for individual patients. Admin team uses predictions to decide who gets reminder calls. Patients with high no-show scores get multiple calls; patients with low scores get none.

Next, stakeholder analysis. Who is affected? Patients getting or not getting reminders. Doctors whose schedules change. Admin staff making calls. Hospital's bottom line. Also — often missed part — patients systematically over-targeted or under-served by model predictions.

Then, harm analysis. What can go wrong? Same zip code problem appears: model may learn patients from certain neighborhoods miss more appointments, and those neighborhoods correlate with race. Reminder system becomes proxy for racial profiling. Harms stack up: over-contacted patients feel watched, under-contacted patients miss appointments and lose access to care, hospital faces legal liability.

Then, mitigation. Remove zip code feature? Helps, but model may rebuild same signal from correlated features. Deeper fix: audit predictions disaggregated by demographic group and ensure no-show rates get predicted with comparable accuracy across groups. Add fairness constraint to training objective. Monitor demographic distribution of reminder calls in production.

Finally, documentation and review. Write it down. Schedule review in six months. When model gets retrained, run assessment again. This separates genuine governance from performative governance.

When Things Go Wrong: AI Incident Response

Career lesson I wish came earlier: your AI system will fail. Not might. Will. Question is not whether incident happens. Question is whether plan exists when incident lands.

OECD launched AI Incidents Monitor (AIM) in 2023, tracking AI harms across world. Data is rough. Lawyer used ChatGPT for legal research and filed court documents citing cases that did not exist — model hallucinated entire judicial proceedings, fake case numbers, fabricated quotations. Deepfake celebrity appeared in real estate ads without consent. AI-generated content got used to manipulate elections in multiple countries.

Documented incidents grew 56% from 2023 to 2024. Breakdown: hallucination and factual errors (38%), bias and discrimination (24%), privacy violations (18%), harmful content generation (14%), transparency failures (6%). Not theoretical risks. Normal Tuesday afternoon in production AI.

AI incident response plan borrows from cybersecurity incident response, but adapts for AI-specific failures. Core phases:

Detection. How do you know something broke? Automated monitoring catches some failures — sudden accuracy drop, spike in user complaints, content filter firing more than usual. But many AI failures are subtle. Model does not crash; model slowly grows more biased, hallucinates a bit more often, or shifts recommendations in ways taking weeks to notice. MedScreen needs automated alerts and regular manual review.

Triage. How bad? Model A misidentifying tumor type is not same as Model B giving snarky response. Severity classification determines response speed and escalation path. Patient-safety incident on Model A triggers immediate pause and investigation. Bad chatbot response on Model B may get logged and queued for weekly review.

Containment. Stop bleeding. For model producing harmful outputs, this may mean routing traffic to fallback system, adding human-in-the-loop requirement, or taking model offline completely. Key point: plan these options before incident. Do not design rollback procedure mid-crisis.

Root cause analysis. Why did this happen? Data quality issue? Concept drift? Gap in training distribution? Adversarial attack? Root cause decides whether you patch problem or need deeper fix.

Communication. Who needs to know? Under EU AI Act, providers of GPAI models with systemic risk must report incidents to EU AI Office. Even without legal duty, communication plan still needed: internal stakeholders, affected users, maybe regulators and public. Companies handling AI incidents well stay transparent about what happened and what they are doing. Companies hiding failures end up in worse position when truth comes out.

AI Ethics Boards: The Uncomfortable Track Record

Many large tech companies built AI ethics boards — advisory bodies meant to provide oversight and support responsible development. Track record: mixed at best.

Google's Advanced Technology External Advisory Council (ATEAC) lasted one week. Launched in 2019 with fanfare. Dissolved almost immediately after backlash over membership and petition signed by thousands of Google employees. Board included member whose views on immigration and trans rights made many employees feel council could not represent affected communities in meaningful way.

Not even Google's biggest ethics-governance failure. In 2020 and 2021, Google fired two co-leads of internal Ethical AI team — Timnit Gebru and Margaret Mitchell — after they published research highlighting risks in large language models. Message to broader AI community was clear: internal ethics work stays tolerated until business interests get challenged.

Microsoft's AI, Ethics, and Effects in Engineering and Research (AETHER) Committee avoided dramatic headlines, but quieter criticism remains. External observers question whether committee has enough authority to influence product decisions, especially with Microsoft's aggressive AI product push and multi-billion-dollar partnership with OpenAI.

Pattern repeats across industry. Ethics boards usually have advisory authority — can recommend, cannot veto. They report to senior leadership, so same executives whose products get reviewed can overrule them. Proceedings rarely go public, making external accountability impossible. Members often get selected by company, creating built-in conflict of interest.

Point is not that ethics boards are useless. Point is that boards without structural power are insufficient. Ethics board that CEO can overrule is PR tool, not governance mechanism. Organizations doing this well embed ethics review inside engineering process — review gates models must pass before deployment, with power to block launches — instead of separate advisory body management can consult or ignore.

Building inspection analogy returns here. City does not use "advisory building safety council" recommending whether building passes inspection. City uses inspectors with legal authority to condemn unsafe structures. Building goes up or does not. Meaningful AI ethics oversight needs that level of structural power.

The Open vs. Closed Model Debate

Should most powerful AI models be open-sourced? Hardest fights in AI governance live here. No clean answer.

Case for open models is strong. Transparency enables independent safety research — cannot audit black box. Open models democratize access, stopping AI power from concentrating in handful of companies. Collective scrutiny finds bugs and biases internal teams miss. Meta's Llama models, Mistral releases, and broader open-source ecosystem drove enormous innovation.

Case against is strong too. Once model weights are released, they cannot be unreleased. Model capable of generating convincing disinformation, helping cyberattacks, or giving detailed instructions for dangerous activities lands in everybody's hands, including people wanting harm. Licenses can restrict misuse, but licenses are only as strong as enforcement, and global enforcement of open-source licenses is effectively impossible.

I changed position on this more than once. Best view so far: openness should be tiered by capability. For models below certain capability threshold, openness benefits clearly outweigh risks. Research community thrives on open models. But for frontier models — most capable systems, ones that may show genuinely dangerous emergent capabilities — some access control makes sense.

Industry seems to be moving toward this view. Several proposals describe "structured access" — not fully open, not fully closed, but tiered. Researchers get access through vetted channels. Companies get API access with usage monitoring. Full model weights for strongest systems stay restricted to organizations that can demonstrate safety infrastructure. Compromise model. Like most compromises, nobody loves it fully.

Meta keeps releasing stronger models openly. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google keep theirs closed, with varying API access. EU AI Act applies obligations whether model is open or closed — if model has systemic risk, provider still has obligations. US export control framework, interestingly, exempts open-weight models from AI model weight controls. Whether exemption survives as models get stronger is unclear.

Compute Governance: Regulating the Hardware

Important insight: you can regulate AI by regulating hardware running it. Training frontier model requires thousands of most advanced GPUs running for months. Hardware comes from small set of companies — mainly NVIDIA, AMD, and to some extent Intel. Fabrication comes from small set of foundries — mainly TSMC in Taiwan. Supply chains are concentrated too. Concentration creates natural governance chokepoint.

In January 2025, US Bureau of Industry and Security released "Framework for Artificial Intelligence Diffusion" — export control regime for advanced AI chips and, for first time, AI model weights. Rules work like this: exports of advanced computing chips are controlled globally, with licenses generally required for sales to any country except 18 designated "AI Authorization Countries" (close US allies). Model weights trained using controlled chips above threshold of 1026 operations are also controlled. Open-weight models are currently exempt.

Geopolitical implications are enormous. US is using dominance in semiconductor design as lever for AI governance, specifically targeting China's ability to develop frontier AI systems. China responded by accelerating domestic chip development, though still years behind on most advanced process nodes.

Compute governance is powerful because compute is physical, quantifiable, and concentrated. Hard to hide data center consuming 100 megawatts of power. Chip shipments can be tracked. Thresholds can be set: models trained with more than X FLOPs trigger extra requirements. EU AI Act uses this approach with 1025 FLOP threshold for systemic risk classification. US export controls use similar logic.

Limitation is obvious: thresholds are arbitrary and age badly. Today's frontier model becomes next year's mid-tier model. Compute threshold catching GPT-4 today may be reachable by well-funded startups in three years. Governance frameworks built on compute thresholds need regular recalibration. And when algorithmic efficiency improves — more capability from less compute — whole idea of using compute as proxy for capability comes under pressure.

Long-term end state for compute governance is still murky. Short-term picture is clear: governments found semiconductor supply chain, found real leverage over AI development, and now use it.

International Coordination: Herding Cats at Global Scale

AI ignores borders. Model trained in US on data scraped from global internet, deployed by API to customers in 40 countries, and running on servers in Ireland creates governance duties under multiple overlapping jurisdictions. That is coordination problem keeping policymakers awake.

Several international initiatives try to address this. OECD AI Principles, adopted in 2019 and updated since, provide shared vocabulary — five principles covering inclusive growth, human-centered values, transparency, robustness, and accountability. Forty-six countries endorsed them. Principles are aspirational, not enforceable, but still give foundation for national regulations.

G7 Hiroshima AI Process (2023) produced voluntary guiding principles and code of conduct for advanced AI systems. AI Safety Summits — first at Bletchley Park in 2023, then Seoul and Paris — brought together governments, companies, and researchers. Bletchley Declaration, signed by 28 countries including US and China, acknowledged AI's potential for "serious, even catastrophic, harm" and called for international cooperation on safety.

Honest assessment: international AI governance coordination moves slowly compared with AI development speed. OECD principles are useful. G7 commitments are voluntary. Summit declarations are non-binding. Meanwhile regulatory divergence across regions keeps growing. EU is prescriptive. US is deregulatory. China is state-controlled. UK is laissez-faire. These systems are not converging.

Most optimistic scenario looks like aviation safety. International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) sets standards most countries adopt, creating reasonably consistent global framework while still allowing national variation. OECD could possibly play that role for AI. But aviation safety took decades to reach current coordination level, and airplanes move much slower than AI capabilities.

For MedScreen, practical implication is clear: if you operate across borders, comply with strictest applicable regulation. Right now that is EU AI Act, and Brussels Effect means likely for some time. Build to that standard. Then you stay compliant almost everywhere.
