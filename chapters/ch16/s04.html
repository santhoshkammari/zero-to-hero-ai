Self-Supervised and Contrastive Learning

Chapter 16, Section 4
TL;DR

Every major foundation model — BERT, GPT, CLIP, DINOv2 — learns its representations without human labels. Self-supervised learning manufactures supervision from the data itself: mask a word and predict it, augment an image two ways and pull them together. Contrastive methods (SimCLR, MoCo) use positive/negative pairs; non-contrastive methods (BYOL, DINO) skip the negatives entirely. Understanding this family of techniques is understanding how modern AI gets built.

The Core Idea: Supervision from Structure

Labeling data is the bottleneck of real-world ML. A million ImageNet labels took years. Medical imaging labels need a specialist at hundreds of dollars per hour. Meanwhile, the internet is drowning in unlabeled data.

Self-supervised learning (SSL) sidesteps the bottleneck entirely. The trick: design a task where the data provides its own labels. Hide part of the input, ask the model to reconstruct it. The model learns rich representations as a side effect of solving the puzzle — and those representations transfer to tasks you actually care about.

Pretext Task vs. Downstream Task

A pretext task is the self-supervised training objective (predict rotations, fill in masked tokens). A downstream task is what you actually care about (classification, detection). You train on the pretext task, throw away its prediction head, and fine-tune the learned backbone on the downstream task. The pretext task is the excuse — the representation is the prize.

You've already seen the two most important pretext tasks in other chapters, even if they weren't called that:

  • Masked Language Modeling (BERT) — mask 15% of tokens, predict the missing words. Forces the model to learn grammar, semantics, and world knowledge.
  • Next Token Prediction (GPT) — predict the next word given all previous words. Same idea, autoregressive flavor. This is how every GPT model is pretrained.

In vision, the equivalent ideas are Masked Image Modeling (MAE: mask 75% of image patches, reconstruct the pixels) and various contrastive objectives that we'll spend most of this section on.

Contrastive Learning: Pull Together, Push Apart

Contrastive learning is the idea that killed hand-designed pretext tasks. Instead of asking the model to predict rotations or solve jigsaw puzzles, you give it a simpler objective: make similar things close in embedding space, and dissimilar things far apart.

Positive and Negative Pairs

Take an image. Apply two random augmentations (crop, flip, color jitter, blur) to produce two "views." These are a positive pair — same content, different appearance. Every other image in the batch is a negative. Train the model to distinguish its positive from the negatives.

That's it. No hand-designed task. No labels. Just "these two things are the same, everything else is different."

The InfoNCE Loss

The standard loss function for contrastive learning. Given a query q, its positive match k⁺, and N-1 negatives, it's essentially a softmax classification: "which of these N candidates is the real match?"

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def info_nce_loss(query, positive, negatives, temperature=0.07):
    """
    query:     (B, D) — anchor embeddings
    positive:  (B, D) — matching embeddings
    negatives: (B, N, D) — non-matching embeddings
    """
    pos_sim = (query * positive).sum(dim=-1, keepdim=True)        # (B, 1)
    neg_sim = torch.bmm(negatives, query.unsqueeze(-1)).squeeze(-1) # (B, N)
    logits = torch.cat([pos_sim, neg_sim], dim=-1) / temperature
    labels = torch.zeros(query.size(0), dtype=torch.long, device=query.device)
    return F.cross_entropy(logits, labels)

The temperature parameter controls how picky the model is. Low temperature (0.05–0.1) forces sharp distinctions between positives and hard negatives. High temperature softens everything. Most methods use 0.05–0.2. Get this wrong and training either collapses or stalls — it's one of those hyperparameters worth sweeping early.

Representation Collapse

The easiest way to get zero loss? Map every input to the same vector. Positive pairs are identical — loss is zero. This is representation collapse, and it's the central failure mode of SSL. Contrastive methods prevent it with negatives (mapping everything to one point makes negative similarities maximal, which the loss penalizes). Methods without negatives need other tricks — we'll get to those. Always monitor the standard deviation of your embeddings. If it trends toward zero, you've collapsed.

SimCLR: The Clean Baseline

SimCLR (Google Brain, 2020) stripped contrastive learning to the essentials and became the reference everyone benchmarks against. The recipe is dead simple:

  1. Take a batch of N images, augment each twice → 2N views
  2. Encode all views through a shared backbone (ResNet-50)
  3. Project through a small MLP head ("projection head")
  4. Apply InfoNCE: each image's two views are positive, the other 2(N-1) views are negatives

What SimCLR Taught Us

Augmentation is the supervision signal. SimCLR's ablation study is the most cited finding in SSL. Random resized crop + color jitter is the minimum viable pair. Without color jitter, the model shortcuts by matching color histograms. Without cropping, it matches global layout. You need both to force the model toward semantic understanding.

The projection head is thrown away. This is counterintuitive. SimCLR adds a 2-layer MLP on top of the encoder and applies the loss to its output. But after training, you discard the MLP and use the encoder's output for downstream tasks. Why? The projection head absorbs augmentation-specific shortcuts, keeping the encoder representation more general.

Bigger batches = more negatives = better representations. SimCLR used batches of 4096–8192 on 32–128 TPUs. This is its Achilles' heel.

import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
import torchvision.transforms as T

class SimCLR(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, backbone, proj_dim=128):
        super().__init__()
        self.backbone = backbone
        feat_dim = backbone.fc.in_features
        backbone.fc = nn.Identity()
        self.projector = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(feat_dim, 2048), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(2048, proj_dim),
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        h = self.backbone(x)
        return F.normalize(self.projector(h), dim=-1)

# The augmentation stack that launched a thousand papers
simclr_augment = T.Compose([
    T.RandomResizedCrop(224, scale=(0.2, 1.0)),
    T.RandomHorizontalFlip(),
    T.RandomApply([T.ColorJitter(0.4, 0.4, 0.4, 0.1)], p=0.8),
    T.RandomGrayscale(p=0.2),
    T.GaussianBlur(kernel_size=23, sigma=(0.1, 2.0)),
    T.ToTensor(),
    T.Normalize([0.485, 0.456, 0.406], [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]),
])

MoCo: Decoupling Negatives from Batch Size

MoCo (Kaiming He, Facebook AI) solved SimCLR's batch size problem with two ideas:

  1. A queue of negatives. Instead of using only the current batch, maintain a FIFO queue of 65,536 past embeddings. Now you have massive negative diversity even with batch size 256.
  2. A momentum encoder. The queue contains embeddings from different training steps. If the encoder changes rapidly, old keys become inconsistent. Solution: use a slowly-updated copy of the encoder (exponential moving average, EMA) to produce queue entries.
# MoCo's key insight: momentum update
@torch.no_grad()
def momentum_update(encoder_q, encoder_k, m=0.999):
    """Key encoder = slow-moving average of query encoder."""
    for q, k in zip(encoder_q.parameters(), encoder_k.parameters()):
        k.data = m * k.data + (1 - m) * q.data

MoCo v2 adopted SimCLR's augmentation pipeline and MLP head for a free +5% accuracy bump. MoCo v3 adapted the framework to Vision Transformers. The momentum encoder trick survived every version — it consistently stabilizes training.

BYOL and DINO: No Negatives Needed

In 2020, DeepMind's BYOL (Bootstrap Your Own Latent) matched contrastive methods without any negative pairs. This was genuinely surprising — without negatives to push things apart, why doesn't everything collapse to a single point?

BYOL's Architecture

Two networks: an online network (encoder + projector + predictor MLP) and a target network (encoder + projector only, updated via EMA). The online network predicts the target's representation of a different augmentation of the same image.

The asymmetry is the key to preventing collapse:

  • The predictor exists only in the online branch
  • The target network has stop_gradient — no gradients flow back through it
  • The target's weights are a slow EMA of the online weights

This asymmetry means the model can't take the trivial shortcut of mapping everything to zero. The predictor must do something non-trivial to match the slowly-changing target. BatchNorm in the projector also helps — it implicitly creates a weak repulsive signal by sharing statistics across the batch.

DINO: Self-Distillation with Vision Transformers

DINO (Meta, 2021) applied a similar student-teacher framework to Vision Transformers, with a striking emergent property: the ViT's self-attention maps automatically discovered object boundaries. No segmentation labels, no object detection supervision — the model just learned to attend to semantically meaningful regions (dog heads, bird wings, car outlines) purely from self-supervised training.

DINOv2 scaled this to 142M curated images with ViT-giant. The result is the closest thing to a universal visual feature extractor we have today — it rivals or beats CLIP features on dense tasks (segmentation, depth estimation) without any fine-tuning. If you need general-purpose vision features in 2024+, DINOv2 is probably your first stop.

Connection to Foundation Model Pretraining

Here's why this section matters beyond academic interest: self-supervised pretraining is how every foundation model is built.

  • BERT = masked language modeling (predict missing tokens)
  • GPT = next token prediction (autoregressive self-supervision)
  • CLIP = contrastive learning across image-text pairs
  • DINOv2 = self-distillation on images
  • MAE = masked image modeling

The pattern is always the same: train on a massive unlabeled (or weakly-labeled) corpus with a self-supervised objective, then either fine-tune on your task or use the features directly. The pretext task varies by modality, but the recipe is universal.

Text vs. Vision: Why the Approaches Differ

Masked modeling dominates NLP because language is discrete — predicting exact token IDs gives a clean loss signal. In vision, pixels are continuous and low-level, so predicting exact pixel values teaches the model about textures more than semantics. That's why contrastive/distillation objectives (SimCLR, DINO) historically worked better for vision, and why MAE had to mask a huge 75% of patches to force semantic learning. The convergence is happening though — both modalities are moving toward "predict abstract representations" (I-JEPA, data2vec).

When to Use Self-Supervised Pretraining in Practice

Not every project needs SSL. Here's the honest decision framework:

Use SSL pretraining when:

  • You have lots of unlabeled domain data but few labels (medical imaging, satellite, industrial inspection)
  • You're building a feature backbone that will serve multiple downstream tasks
  • Off-the-shelf ImageNet features don't transfer well to your domain
  • You want to leverage DINOv2 or CLIP as a frozen feature extractor — this is just using someone else's SSL

Skip SSL and just fine-tune a supervised backbone when:

  • You have 10K+ labeled examples and your domain is close to ImageNet/web data
  • Time-to-ship matters more than squeezing the last 2% accuracy
  • Your task is simple enough that a pretrained ResNet or ViT handles it fine
The Realistic Shortcut

Most practitioners never train SimCLR or BYOL from scratch. Instead, they grab DINOv2 or CLIP features off the shelf, freeze the backbone, and train a lightweight head on their labels. That's still self-supervised learning — just someone else's. Don't reinvent the pretraining wheel unless your domain is genuinely unusual (pathology slides, radar imagery, molecular graphs).

The Modern Landscape: Where Things Are Heading

The field has largely settled on a few winners:

  • DINOv2 is the default visual backbone for anyone who needs general-purpose features
  • MAE-style pretraining remains strong for ViT training from scratch on custom domains
  • Joint embedding prediction (I-JEPA) is the next frontier — predicting in representation space instead of pixel space, no augmentations needed
  • Multimodal SSL (CLIP, SigLIP) bridges vision and language in a shared space

The bigger picture: we've moved from "self-supervised learning as a research topic" to "self-supervised pretraining as an infrastructure primitive." It's not a technique you choose — it's a technique your foundation model already used before you ever touched it.
