Self-Supervised and Contrastive Learning

Chapter 16: Representation Learning & Self-Supervision Section 4
TL;DR

Labeling data is expensive, slow, and doesn't scale. Self-supervised learning sidesteps this by creating free supervision signals from the data itself — predicting rotations, filling in masked patches, or pulling similar examples together in embedding space. Contrastive methods like SimCLR and MoCo learn representations by attracting positive pairs and repelling negatives. BYOL proved you don't even need negatives. Modern descendants — DINO, Barlow Twins, VICReg, I-JEPA — now rival or beat supervised pretraining on downstream tasks, and they're the backbone of foundation models everywhere.

Why Self-Supervised Learning?

Here's the uncomfortable truth about deep learning in production: the model isn't the bottleneck — the labels are. Training a ResNet-50 takes a few GPU-hours. Labeling a million images takes months of human effort and costs real money. Medical imaging is worse — you need a radiologist at $300/hour to draw bounding boxes around tumors. And that labeled dataset? It captures one narrow slice of the visual world. Your model becomes brittle the moment it encounters a distribution it wasn't labeled for.

Meanwhile, the internet is drowning in unlabeled data. Billions of images, petabytes of text, endless hours of video — all sitting there with no annotations. Self-supervised learning (SSL) is the family of techniques that extracts useful representations from this unlabeled ocean. The core trick: invent a task where the labels come free from the data's own structure. Predict the missing word. Guess the rotation. Pull two views of the same image together. The model learns rich, transferable features without a human ever clicking an annotation tool.

This isn't a niche research direction. BERT is self-supervised. GPT is self-supervised. The vision transformers powering image search at Google and Meta were pretrained with self-supervised objectives. If supervised learning is "learning with a teacher," self-supervised learning is "learning by doing puzzles." And it turns out the puzzle-solvers build better internal representations than the students who were spoon-fed answers.

Pretext Tasks: Making Labels from Thin Air

A pretext task is a made-up objective designed so that solving it forces the model to understand something deep about the data. You don't actually care about the task's output. You care about the features the model builds along the way. Think of it as training wheels — the task is the excuse, the representation is the prize.

Rotation Prediction

One of the earliest and most elegant pretext tasks. Take an image, rotate it by 0°, 90°, 180°, or 270°, and ask the model to predict which rotation was applied. It's a 4-class classification problem with free labels — you know the rotation because you applied it.

Why does this work? To tell whether a dog is upside-down, the model needs to understand what a dog is, where its legs go, how gravity affects natural scenes. It's forced to learn semantic features — object shapes, scene layout, orientation cues — even though you never told it "this is a dog."

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torchvision.transforms.functional as TF

class RotationPredictor(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, backbone):
        super().__init__()
        self.backbone = backbone  # e.g., ResNet without final FC
        self.classifier = nn.Linear(2048, 4)  # 4 rotation classes

    def forward(self, x):
        features = self.backbone(x)
        return self.classifier(features)

def create_rotation_batch(images):
    """Create (rotated_image, rotation_label) pairs for free."""
    rotated, labels = [], []
    for img in images:
        for k in range(4):
            angle = k * 90
            rotated.append(TF.rotate(img, angle))
            labels.append(k)
    return torch.stack(rotated), torch.tensor(labels)

Jigsaw Puzzles

Slice an image into a 3×3 grid of patches, shuffle them, and ask the model to predict the original arrangement. With 9 patches, there are 9! = 362,880 possible permutations — too many for a softmax. So in practice, you pick a subset of ~100 carefully chosen permutations and turn it into a 100-class classification problem. To solve this, the model must understand spatial relationships between object parts — it learns that eyes go above mouths, wheels go below car bodies, sky goes above ground.

Colorization

Feed the model a grayscale image and ask it to predict the colors. This is a per-pixel regression (or classification over quantized color bins). The model has to learn that grass is green, sky is blue, and skin tones cluster in a specific hue range. It builds object-level and material-level features because color depends heavily on what the object is.

Masked Image Modeling (MAE, BEiT)

This is the vision equivalent of fill-in-the-blank. Take an image, divide it into patches (just like a Vision Transformer does), randomly mask out 75% of the patches, and train the model to reconstruct the missing pixels. Masked Autoencoders (MAE) from Meta showed this works spectacularly well — you can mask an absurd amount of the image and still reconstruct it, which forces the model to learn holistic scene understanding rather than local texture copying.

BEiT takes a different approach: instead of predicting raw pixels, it predicts discrete visual tokens from a learned codebook (similar to predicting word IDs in NLP). This avoids the blurriness problem of pixel-level regression and gives sharper supervision signals.

Masked Language Modeling

BERT's famous pretext task. Take a sentence, randomly mask 15% of the tokens with a special [MASK] token, and predict what words belong in the blanks. "The cat sat on the [MASK]" → "mat." To fill in the blank, the model must understand grammar, semantics, world knowledge, and context. Masked Language Modeling (MLM) is why BERT representations transfer so well — the task demands deep language understanding.

Next Sentence Prediction

BERT's other pretext task: given two sentences, predict whether sentence B actually follows sentence A in the original document, or whether it's a random sentence. This teaches the model about discourse coherence and inter-sentence relationships. Later work (RoBERTa) found that this task doesn't help much and dropped it — MLM carries most of the weight. But the idea of predicting relationships between data chunks lives on in other forms.

Pretext Tasks vs. Downstream Tasks

The pretext task is the training objective (rotation, masking, colorization). The downstream task is what you actually care about (classification, detection, segmentation). The whole point is that features learned on the pretext task transfer to the downstream task — even though they were trained on completely different objectives. After pretraining, you throw away the pretext head and fine-tune on your real task.

Contrastive Learning Foundations

Pretext tasks are clever, but they have a ceiling. Predicting rotations teaches something about objects, but it doesn't teach everything. In 2018-2020, the field converged on a more powerful paradigm: contrastive learning. The idea is deceptively clean — learn representations by pulling similar things together and pushing dissimilar things apart in embedding space.

Positive and Negative Pairs

The basic unit of contrastive learning is a pair of examples. A positive pair consists of two views that should have similar representations — typically two different augmentations of the same image. A negative pair consists of two views that should be dissimilar — typically augmentations from different images. The training objective says: make positive pairs close in embedding space, make negative pairs far apart.

Where do pairs come from? In vision, the standard recipe is: take an image, apply two random augmentations (crop, flip, color jitter, blur) to create two "views," and treat those as a positive pair. Every other image in the batch provides negatives. In NLP, positive pairs might be different dropout masks on the same sentence (SimCSE) or a sentence and its paraphrase.

InfoNCE Loss

The workhorse loss function of contrastive learning is InfoNCE (Noise Contrastive Estimation with an information-theoretic flavor). Given a query embedding q, one positive key k⁺, and N-1 negative keys k⁻, the loss is:

# InfoNCE Loss — the foundation of contrastive learning
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def info_nce_loss(query, positive_key, negative_keys, temperature=0.07):
    """
    query:         (batch_size, embed_dim) — anchor embeddings
    positive_key:  (batch_size, embed_dim) — matching embeddings
    negative_keys: (batch_size, num_negatives, embed_dim) — non-matching
    temperature:   scalar controlling sharpness of distribution
    """
    # Positive similarity: dot product between query and its match
    pos_sim = torch.sum(query * positive_key, dim=-1, keepdim=True)  # (B, 1)

    # Negative similarities: dot product between query and all negatives
    neg_sim = torch.bmm(
        negative_keys, query.unsqueeze(-1)
    ).squeeze(-1)  # (B, num_negatives)

    # Concatenate: positive logit first, then negatives
    logits = torch.cat([pos_sim, neg_sim], dim=-1) / temperature  # (B, 1+N)

    # Target: the positive is always at index 0
    labels = torch.zeros(query.size(0), dtype=torch.long, device=query.device)

    return F.cross_entropy(logits, labels)

It looks like a classification problem — "which of these N candidates is the real match?" The loss is minimized when the positive pair's similarity is much higher than all negative pairs. This is equivalent to maximizing a lower bound on the mutual information between the two views.

Temperature Parameter

That temperature scalar in the denominator is more important than it looks. It controls how "peaky" the softmax distribution is. Low temperature (0.05-0.1) makes the model very confident — it must sharply distinguish the positive from negatives. High temperature (1.0+) makes the distribution soft and forgiving. In practice, temperature is a critical hyperparameter. Too low and the model focuses only on the hardest negatives, leading to unstable training. Too high and the signal is too weak to learn anything. Most methods land between 0.05 and 0.2.

Data Augmentation Strategies

In contrastive learning, augmentation isn't a regularization trick — it's the entire supervision signal. The quality of your learned representations depends directly on what augmentations you choose. If your augmentations are too weak (e.g., only horizontal flip), the model can "cheat" by memorizing low-level statistics without learning semantic features. If they're too aggressive (e.g., extreme cropping that removes the object), the model gets confused because positive pairs genuinely look different.

The standard augmentation stack that has proven most effective: random resized crop (most important — forces the model to recognize objects at different scales and positions), horizontal flip, color jitter (brightness, contrast, saturation, hue), random grayscale conversion, and Gaussian blur. SimCLR ablations showed that cropping + color jitter provides most of the benefit.

Hard Negative Mining

Not all negatives are created equal. If your query is a golden retriever and your negatives are cars, airplanes, and buildings — that's too easy. The model separates them by texture alone. Hard negatives are examples that are similar to the query but semantically different — a Labrador, a fox, a stuffed animal. These force the model to learn fine-grained distinctions. Some methods mine hard negatives explicitly; others (MoCo) maintain large negative pools where hard negatives appear naturally.

Representation Collapse Prevention

Here's the lurking failure mode. The easiest way to make all positive pairs identical? Map everything to the same point. If every image embeds to the same vector, every positive pair has perfect similarity. The loss is zero — and the representation is useless. This is called representation collapse, and preventing it is the central design challenge of self-supervised learning.

Contrastive methods prevent collapse with negative pairs — if you map everything to the same point, the negative similarities also become maximal, which the loss penalizes. But methods without negatives (BYOL, Barlow Twins) need other tricks: stop-gradients, predictor networks, redundancy reduction, or variance/covariance regularization.

Collapse is Silent

Representation collapse doesn't announce itself with NaN losses or exploding gradients. The training loss can look perfectly healthy while the model outputs constant vectors. Always monitor the standard deviation of your embeddings across a batch. If it drops toward zero, your model has collapsed. Check the rank of the embedding matrix too — if many singular values are near zero, you've lost dimensions to collapse.

SimCLR: The Clean Baseline

SimCLR (Simple Framework for Contrastive Learning of Visual Representations) from Google Brain stripped contrastive learning to its bare essentials and showed that with the right recipe, you don't need complicated architectures. Published in 2020, it became the reference implementation that everyone benchmarks against.

The Augmentation Pipeline

SimCLR's key finding: the composition of augmentations matters enormously. They tested every combination and found that random crop + color jitter is the minimum viable augmentation pair. Without color jitter, the model shortcuts by matching color histograms. Without cropping, it shortcuts by matching global layout. Together, they force the model to learn actual object semantics.

The Projection Head

Here's a counterintuitive result. SimCLR adds a small MLP (2-3 layers) called the projection head on top of the encoder. The contrastive loss is applied to the projection head's output, but after training, you throw the projection head away and use the encoder's output for downstream tasks. Why? The projection head absorbs information that's useful for solving the contrastive task (augmentation-specific details) but harmful for transfer. The encoder representation, free from this burden, is more general.

Large Batch Training and NT-Xent Loss

SimCLR uses a variant of InfoNCE called NT-Xent (Normalized Temperature-scaled Cross Entropy). For a batch of N images, you create 2N augmented views. Each image's two views form a positive pair, and the remaining 2(N-1) views are negatives. Bigger batches = more negatives = better representations. SimCLR used batches of 4096-8192, requiring 32-128 TPUs. This was its main practical limitation.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
import torchvision.transforms as T

class SimCLR(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, backbone, proj_dim=128, hidden_dim=2048):
        super().__init__()
        self.backbone = backbone
        feat_dim = backbone.fc.in_features
        backbone.fc = nn.Identity()  # Remove classification head

        # Projection head: 2-layer MLP
        self.projector = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(feat_dim, hidden_dim),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, proj_dim),
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        features = self.backbone(x)           # Encoder representation
        projections = self.projector(features) # For contrastive loss
        return F.normalize(projections, dim=-1)

def nt_xent_loss(z, temperature=0.5):
    """
    NT-Xent loss for SimCLR.
    z: (2N, proj_dim) — concatenated projections from both views.
    First N are view_1, last N are view_2.
    Positive pairs: (i, i+N) for i in [0, N).
    """
    N = z.size(0) // 2
    sim_matrix = torch.mm(z, z.t()) / temperature  # (2N, 2N)

    # Mask out self-similarity (diagonal)
    mask = torch.eye(2 * N, device=z.device).bool()
    sim_matrix.masked_fill_(mask, -1e9)

    # For each anchor i, positive is at i+N (or i-N)
    # Labels: view_1[i]'s positive is view_2[i] at index i+N
    labels = torch.cat([
        torch.arange(N, 2 * N),  # view_1 -> view_2
        torch.arange(0, N),       # view_2 -> view_1
    ]).to(z.device)

    return F.cross_entropy(sim_matrix, labels)

SimCLR's Batch Size Problem

SimCLR's performance degrades significantly with small batches because fewer negatives means weaker learning signals. At batch size 256, the top-1 accuracy on ImageNet drops by ~5% compared to batch size 8192. If you don't have access to many GPUs, look at MoCo (decouples negatives from batch size) or BYOL (no negatives needed at all). Distributed training with synchronized batch norm helps, but the hardware cost remains SimCLR's Achilles' heel.

MoCo: Momentum Contrast

MoCo, from Kaiming He at Facebook AI, solved SimCLR's batch size problem with two elegant ideas: a momentum encoder and a queue of negatives. Instead of relying on the current batch for negatives, MoCo maintains a dictionary (implemented as a queue) of past encoded representations. This decouples the number of negatives from the batch size — you can have 65,536 negatives with a batch size of 256.

The Momentum Encoder

MoCo uses two encoders: a query encoder (updated by gradient descent as usual) and a key encoder (updated via exponential moving average of the query encoder's weights). Why not just use the same encoder? Because the keys in the queue were encoded at different training steps. If the encoder changes rapidly between steps, old keys and new keys live in inconsistent representation spaces. The momentum update keeps the key encoder changing slowly, ensuring consistency across the queue.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
from copy import deepcopy

class MoCo(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, backbone, proj_dim=128, queue_size=65536,
                 momentum=0.999, temperature=0.07):
        super().__init__()
        self.queue_size = queue_size
        self.momentum = momentum
        self.temperature = temperature

        # Query encoder (trained with gradients)
        self.encoder_q = backbone
        feat_dim = backbone.fc.in_features
        self.encoder_q.fc = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(feat_dim, feat_dim), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(feat_dim, proj_dim),
        )

        # Key encoder (momentum-updated, NO gradients)
        self.encoder_k = deepcopy(self.encoder_q)
        for param in self.encoder_k.parameters():
            param.requires_grad = False

        # Queue: stores past key representations
        self.register_buffer(
            "queue", F.normalize(torch.randn(proj_dim, queue_size), dim=0)
        )
        self.register_buffer("queue_ptr", torch.zeros(1, dtype=torch.long))

    @torch.no_grad()
    def _momentum_update(self):
        """Slowly update key encoder toward query encoder."""
        for q_param, k_param in zip(
            self.encoder_q.parameters(), self.encoder_k.parameters()
        ):
            k_param.data = (
                self.momentum * k_param.data
                + (1.0 - self.momentum) * q_param.data
            )

    @torch.no_grad()
    def _enqueue_dequeue(self, keys):
        """Replace oldest entries in the queue with new keys."""
        batch_size = keys.size(0)
        ptr = int(self.queue_ptr)
        self.queue[:, ptr:ptr + batch_size] = keys.T
        self.queue_ptr[0] = (ptr + batch_size) % self.queue_size

    def forward(self, x_q, x_k):
        # Query representations (gradient flows here)
        q = F.normalize(self.encoder_q(x_q), dim=-1)  # (B, proj_dim)

        # Key representations (no gradient)
        with torch.no_grad():
            self._momentum_update()
            k = F.normalize(self.encoder_k(x_k), dim=-1)  # (B, proj_dim)

        # Positive logits: (B, 1)
        l_pos = torch.einsum("bd,bd->b", q, k).unsqueeze(-1)

        # Negative logits: query vs. queue entries, (B, queue_size)
        l_neg = torch.einsum("bd,dk->bk", q, self.queue.clone().detach())

        # InfoNCE
        logits = torch.cat([l_pos, l_neg], dim=-1) / self.temperature
        labels = torch.zeros(q.size(0), dtype=torch.long, device=q.device)
        loss = F.cross_entropy(logits, labels)

        # Update queue with current batch keys
        self._enqueue_dequeue(k)
        return loss

The Queue: Dictionary as a Data Structure

The queue is first-in-first-out. Each training step, you push the current batch's key embeddings and pop the oldest ones. This means the queue always contains the most recent 65,536 key representations. Because the momentum encoder changes slowly, these keys are approximately consistent — they all come from roughly the same encoder state. The queue gives you a massive and diverse set of negatives at minimal memory cost (you only store the small projection vectors, not the full images).

MoCo v2 and v3 Improvements

MoCo v2 borrowed SimCLR's augmentation pipeline and projection head MLP, boosting accuracy by ~5% with zero architectural changes. MoCo v3 adapted the framework for Vision Transformers, replacing the queue with a large batch approach (since ViTs are harder to train with queues due to instability) and added a prediction head similar to BYOL. The momentum encoder trick survived every version because it consistently improves training stability.

BYOL: No Negatives? No Problem.

In mid-2020, DeepMind dropped a paper that confused everyone. BYOL (Bootstrap Your Own Latent) achieved state-of-the-art self-supervised performance without negative pairs. Wait — if there's nothing pushing dissimilar examples apart, why doesn't everything collapse to a constant vector? This question kept researchers up at night.

Online and Target Networks

BYOL uses two networks. The online network has an encoder, a projector, and crucially, a predictor — an extra small MLP. The target network has the same encoder and projector but no predictor, and its weights are an exponential moving average of the online network (sound familiar? It's MoCo's momentum encoder idea, repurposed). The online network tries to predict the target network's representation of a different augmentation of the same image.

Why It Doesn't Collapse

The asymmetry is the key. The predictor only exists in the online branch. The target network's weights are frozen (updated only via EMA). The stop_gradient on the target branch means gradients never flow back through it. This asymmetry breaks the symmetry that causes collapse — the model can't take the shortcut of mapping everything to zero because the predictor would need to do something non-trivial to match the (slowly changing) target. Later analysis showed that BYOL implicitly performs a form of spectral normalization on the representations.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
from copy import deepcopy

class BYOL(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, backbone, proj_dim=256, pred_dim=4096,
                 hidden_dim=4096, ema_decay=0.996):
        super().__init__()
        self.ema_decay = ema_decay
        feat_dim = backbone.fc.in_features
        backbone.fc = nn.Identity()

        # Online network: encoder + projector + predictor
        self.online_encoder = backbone
        self.online_projector = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(feat_dim, hidden_dim),
            nn.BatchNorm1d(hidden_dim), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, proj_dim),
        )
        self.predictor = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(proj_dim, pred_dim),
            nn.BatchNorm1d(pred_dim), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(pred_dim, proj_dim),
        )

        # Target network: encoder + projector (NO predictor, NO gradients)
        self.target_encoder = deepcopy(self.online_encoder)
        self.target_projector = deepcopy(self.online_projector)
        for p in list(self.target_encoder.parameters()) + \
                 list(self.target_projector.parameters()):
            p.requires_grad = False

    @torch.no_grad()
    def update_target(self):
        """EMA update: target slowly follows online network."""
        for online_p, target_p in zip(
            list(self.online_encoder.parameters()) +
            list(self.online_projector.parameters()),
            list(self.target_encoder.parameters()) +
            list(self.target_projector.parameters()),
        ):
            target_p.data = (
                self.ema_decay * target_p.data
                + (1.0 - self.ema_decay) * online_p.data
            )

    def forward(self, view_1, view_2):
        # Online branch: encode, project, predict
        online_z1 = self.online_projector(self.online_encoder(view_1))
        online_z2 = self.online_projector(self.online_encoder(view_2))
        pred_1 = self.predictor(online_z1)
        pred_2 = self.predictor(online_z2)

        # Target branch: encode and project (stop gradient)
        with torch.no_grad():
            target_z1 = self.target_projector(
                self.target_encoder(view_1)
            ).detach()
            target_z2 = self.target_projector(
                self.target_encoder(view_2)
            ).detach()

        # Symmetric loss: predict each view from the other
        loss = (
            cosine_loss(pred_1, target_z2)
            + cosine_loss(pred_2, target_z1)
        )
        return loss.mean()

def cosine_loss(pred, target):
    """Negative cosine similarity — minimize to align vectors."""
    pred = F.normalize(pred, dim=-1)
    target = F.normalize(target, dim=-1)
    return 2.0 - 2.0 * (pred * target).sum(dim=-1)

The Role of Batch Normalization

An under-appreciated detail: BYOL's projector uses batch normalization. Early analyses suggested that BatchNorm implicitly creates a weak form of negative signal by sharing statistics across the batch — if one image tries to collapse, the batch statistics push it away from the mean. Later work (BYOL without BatchNorm) showed it can work without it if you're careful, but BatchNorm makes collapse much harder. This is a good reminder that seemingly mundane architectural choices carry outsized importance in self-supervised learning.

The EMA Decay Schedule

BYOL starts with an EMA decay of 0.996 and gradually increases it to 1.0 during training using a cosine schedule. Early on, the target network updates more aggressively to track meaningful changes in the online network. Late in training, the target barely moves, providing a stable prediction target. This schedule is critical — a fixed decay of 0.999 throughout training gives noticeably worse results.

The Modern Landscape

SimCLR, MoCo, and BYOL established the foundations. Since then, the field has exploded with methods that push different design axes: no negatives, no asymmetry, clustering, redundancy reduction, and joint embedding prediction. Here's where things stand.

DINO and DINOv2

DINO (Self-Distillation with No Labels) from Meta is self-supervised knowledge distillation applied to Vision Transformers. A student network learns to match the output distribution of a teacher network (EMA-updated, like BYOL's target). The stunning result: DINO's attention maps automatically discover object boundaries without any segmentation supervision. The ViT's self-attention heads learn to attend to semantically meaningful regions — the head of a dog, the wings of a bird — purely from self-supervised training on ImageNet.

DINOv2 scaled this up massively. Trained on 142 million curated images with a ViT-giant backbone, DINOv2 produces features that rival or beat supervised and CLIP features on dense prediction tasks (segmentation, depth estimation) without any fine-tuning. It's the closest thing to a universal visual feature extractor we have today.

SwAV: Clustering-Based Contrastive Learning

SwAV (Swapping Assignments between Views) sidesteps pairwise comparisons entirely. Instead of pulling positive pairs together, it clusters representations online and requires that both views of an image get assigned to the same cluster. One view's cluster assignment becomes the prediction target for the other view. This uses the Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm for balanced clustering and avoids the batch size sensitivity of SimCLR. SwAV works with smaller batches and supports multi-crop augmentation — using a mix of large (224×224) and small (96×96) crops, which gives richer multi-scale supervision at minimal compute cost.

Barlow Twins

Barlow Twins takes an entirely different angle inspired by neuroscience. Forget positive/negative pairs. Instead, compute the cross-correlation matrix between the embeddings of two views across a batch. The loss pushes this matrix toward the identity matrix. Diagonal elements should be 1 (corresponding features of the two views should correlate), and off-diagonal elements should be 0 (different features should be independent). This redundancy reduction approach prevents collapse by ensuring each feature dimension carries unique information. The loss is elegant:

def barlow_twins_loss(z1, z2, lambda_coeff=0.005):
    """
    z1, z2: (batch_size, proj_dim) — normalized embeddings from two views
    """
    batch_size = z1.size(0)

    # Normalize each feature across the batch (zero mean, unit variance)
    z1_norm = (z1 - z1.mean(dim=0)) / (z1.std(dim=0) + 1e-5)
    z2_norm = (z2 - z2.mean(dim=0)) / (z2.std(dim=0) + 1e-5)

    # Cross-correlation matrix: (proj_dim, proj_dim)
    cross_corr = (z1_norm.T @ z2_norm) / batch_size

    # Loss: diagonal should be 1, off-diagonal should be 0
    on_diag = (torch.diagonal(cross_corr) - 1).pow(2).sum()
    off_diag = cross_corr.clone()
    off_diag.fill_diagonal_(0)
    off_diag = off_diag.pow(2).sum()

    return on_diag + lambda_coeff * off_diag

The beauty of Barlow Twins: no asymmetry needed, no momentum encoder, no stop-gradient, no predictor. Two identical branches, a simple loss, and it works. The lambda coefficient down-weights the off-diagonal term because there are many more off-diagonal elements than diagonal ones.

VICReg

VICReg (Variance-Invariance-Covariance Regularization) decomposes the self-supervised objective into three explicit terms. The invariance term pulls positive pairs together (mean squared error between embeddings). The variance term prevents collapse by ensuring each embedding dimension maintains a minimum standard deviation across the batch. The covariance term decorrelates dimensions, similar to Barlow Twins' off-diagonal penalty. By separating these three forces, VICReg makes it transparent what each part of the loss does and is easier to tune. It's particularly robust to hyperparameter choices.

I-JEPA: Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture

I-JEPA from Yann LeCun's group at Meta represents the newest paradigm. Instead of pulling views of the same image together in embedding space (which requires carefully designed augmentations), I-JEPA predicts the representation of a target block from the representations of context blocks. Think of it as masked image modeling, but in representation space instead of pixel space. The model never reconstructs pixels — it predicts abstract features, which forces it to learn higher-level semantics rather than texture details.

I-JEPA uses no handcrafted augmentations at all (no color jitter, no random flip). The "views" are just different spatial regions of the same image. This makes it more principled than augmentation-dependent methods because it doesn't bake in assumptions about what invariances matter. Early results show I-JEPA learns features that transfer better to tasks where augmentation invariances are actually harmful (like counting objects or predicting spatial relationships).

Choosing a Method in Practice

If you have many GPUs and large batches: SimCLR or DINO. If you have limited compute: MoCo or SwAV (multi-crop). If you want the simplest implementation: Barlow Twins or VICReg. If you want the best off-the-shelf features without training: grab DINOv2 pretrained weights. If you're working in a domain where standard augmentations don't apply (medical imaging, satellite imagery): look at I-JEPA or MAE, which rely less on augmentation engineering.

Putting It All Together

Self-supervised learning went from a curiosity ("huh, predicting rotations helps a little") to the dominant paradigm of modern AI in about five years. The progression tells a clean story. First, hand-designed pretext tasks proved the concept but hit a ceiling. Then, contrastive methods (SimCLR, MoCo) systematized the approach with clear loss functions and scalable architectures. BYOL showed that negatives aren't necessary, opening the door to simpler methods. Barlow Twins and VICReg made the collapse-prevention mechanism explicit. DINO brought self-supervision to Vision Transformers with remarkable emergent properties. And I-JEPA is pointing toward the next generation — learning by predicting in abstract space rather than pixel space.

The representations that come out of these methods aren't tricks. They capture genuine semantic structure — similar objects cluster together, relationships between concepts are preserved, fine-grained distinctions emerge naturally. When you fine-tune a self-supervised backbone on a downstream task with even a handful of labels, it often outperforms a supervised model that was trained on the full labeled dataset. That's the real victory: not that we can avoid labels entirely, but that self-supervised pretraining makes every label count for more.

If you take away one thing from this section, let it be this: the labeling bottleneck is solved, not by making labeling cheaper, but by making labels optional for the hardest part of the problem — learning what matters in the data.
