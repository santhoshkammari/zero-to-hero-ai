Ignored privacy side of machine learning too long. Differential privacy looked like Greek soup, so I kept saying "later." Meanwhile, I shipped models on user data with vague "we anonymized it" docs. Then colleague showed paper where researchers reconstructed patients' faces from medical imaging gradients. Actual faces. From gradients. That broke me. This section is dive.

Privacy-preserving machine learning uses math and systems tricks to train and deploy models without exposing individual data points. Field starts with Cynthia Dwork's differential privacy work in 2006 and Google's federated learning in 2016, then speeds up after GDPR (2018) and CCPA (2020) add legal force. It sits between cryptography, statistics, and systems engineering.

Why "Anonymized" Doesn't Mean Private

Start with story. Three hospitals — City General, Valley Medical, Riverside Clinic — each hold thousands of patient records. They want joint disease prediction model. Obvious plan: pool data, strip names and social security numbers, train on combined dataset.

Netflix did this in 2006. It released 10 million movie ratings for recommendation contest, stripped usernames, assumed enough. Narayanan and Shmatikov cross-referenced "anonymous" ratings with public IMDb reviews and re-identified users within weeks. Trick was low-tech: six or eight overlapping ratings on similar dates could fingerprint person. Suddenly user #482,931 had name and private viewing history.

Same problem for hospitals. Even without names, rare mix of diagnosis, age, and zip code can identify person. A 34-year-old in zip code 02139 with rare condition may be only match in whole dataset. "Anonymized" data is often polite fiction. Fix is not better anonymization. Fix is systems with mathematical protection for each record, no matter what attacker already knows.

That is path. First understand threat.

Privacy Attacks: What an Adversary Actually Does

First time I heard "membership inference attack," I tagged it academic fluff. Then one worked on my model. That lands hard.

A membership inference attack asks direct question: "Was this specific data point in training set?" Attacker sends sample to model and watches response. More confidence — lower loss, sharper distribution — means sample was probably in training set. Models memorize some training data. Seen and unseen points get different behavior. Attack uses that gap.

Hospital example: disease prediction model ships as API. Attacker has Bob's medical record and wants to know if Bob was patient at one of three hospitals. Attacker sends Bob's record to model and measures confidence. If confidence is suspiciously high versus random inputs, Bob's data was probably in training set. Now attacker knows Bob got treatment at one of those hospitals. Sensitive fact leaks.

Original 2017 attack from Shokri et al. trained "shadow models" that mimic target model behavior. Build several models on controlled data, watch member vs non-member behavior, then train classifier to separate patterns. Recent work shows attacks still work with little information — sometimes only top-1 prediction, not full probability distribution.

Model inversion attacks go further. Question changes from "was this person in dataset?" to "what did this person's data look like?" Attacker uses model outputs to work backward toward inputs. Fredrikson et al. showed this in 2015 by reconstructing recognizable face images from facial recognition model given only model API and person's name. Faces were not perfect. Close enough to be creepy.

Then training data extraction from large language models. Carlini et al. showed in 2021 that GPT-2 could sometimes regurgitate memorized training data verbatim when prompted with right seed text — email addresses, phone numbers, snippets of copyrighted books. Model absorbed rare unique strings during training, then spit them back out when nudged right. Not theory. Demonstrated on production-scale models.

These three attacks — membership inference, model inversion, data extraction — define threat landscape. Stripping names stops none of them. Need stronger defense.

What "Private" Means Mathematically

Core question: how to define privacy in measurable, enforceable way? "Anonymization" and "de-identification" are not enough.

Answer came from Cynthia Dwork in 2006: differential privacy. Idea is simple. Take two versions of same hospital dataset: one with Bob's record, one without. Mechanism — algorithm, query, model — is differentially private if output looks almost same either way. Adversary staring at output cannot tell, with meaningful confidence, whether Bob participated.

Tiny example. City General has three patients, and we publish average age. Ages are 30, 50, 70. True average is 50. Remove 70-year-old because they requested deletion. New average becomes 40. Adversary who knows other two ages sees change from 50 to 40 and immediately infers removed patient was 70. With three patients, each person has huge influence.

Differential privacy formalizes this leak. Math looks scary first. Meaning is intuitive.

P[M(D) ∈ S] ≤ eε · P[M(D′) ∈ S] + δ

D and D′ are datasets that differ by one person's record. M is mechanism — computation we run. S is any set of possible outputs. Whole definition. Now unpack two knobs.

Epsilon (ε) controls how much output can change when one person's data is added or removed. Use soundproof-wall picture. When ε = 0, wall is infinitely thick — output is pure random noise, useless but perfectly private. When ε = ∞, no wall exists — raw data leaks through. Real deployments sit between. Apple uses ε between 2 and 8 for emoji frequency stats. 2020 US Census used ε ≈ 19.6, which many statisticians called too thin.

Delta (δ) is probability that guarantee fails catastrophically. Safety valve for rare event where mechanism leaks everything. Want it negligible — usually much smaller than 1/n, where n is dataset size. Common choice is δ = 1/n². For 10,000 patients, that is δ = 10-8.

Back to tiny example. With three patients and true average 50, any change in average says a lot. With 10,000 patients, adding or removing one person barely moves average. Differential privacy works better on large datasets because each person's influence is already diluted. Noise only has to cover leftover influence.

Keep soundproof-wall analogy. ε is wall thickness. Each query or training epoch drills another small hole. Question stays same: how many holes before wall stops helping?

Adding Noise the Right Way

Definition alone is not enough. To make computation differentially private, add carefully calibrated noise. Too little noise leaks privacy. Too much noise kills utility. Calibration depends on sensitivity — how much one person's data can change output.

Back to hospital average age, now with 10,000 patients. If ages range from 0 to 120, average-query sensitivity is (120 - 0) / 10,000 = 0.012. That is biggest shift one record can cause. On dataset of 3 patients, sensitivity is (120 - 0) / 3 = 40. Smaller sensitivity means less noise needed.

Laplace mechanism is simplest. Take true answer and add Laplace noise with scale sensitivity/ε. It gives "pure" (ε, 0)-differential privacy — δ is exactly zero, so no catastrophic-failure probability. For 10,000 patients and ε = 1, noise scale is 0.012. Published average stays within fraction of year of truth. Small cost for mathematical guarantee.

Gaussian mechanism adds normal noise instead. It gives slightly weaker (ε, δ)-DP, so tiny δ failure probability exists. People still use it because composition is much tighter across many queries. If you run hundreds of computations on same data — exactly what model training does — Gaussian mechanism usually needs less total noise.

import numpy as np

def laplace_mechanism(true_answer, sensitivity, epsilon):
    scale = sensitivity / epsilon
    return true_answer + np.random.laplace(0, scale)

def gaussian_mechanism(true_answer, sensitivity, epsilon, delta):
    sigma = sensitivity * np.sqrt(2 * np.log(1.25 / delta)) / epsilon
    return true_answer + np.random.normal(0, sigma)

# Our hospital example: average age of 10,000 patients
true_avg_age = 47.3
sensitivity = 120.0 / 10000  # max age range / number of patients

# With epsilon = 1.0, the noise is tiny relative to the answer
dp_avg_laplace = laplace_mechanism(true_avg_age, sensitivity, epsilon=1.0)
dp_avg_gaussian = gaussian_mechanism(true_avg_age, sensitivity, epsilon=1.0, delta=1e-8)

print(f"True average age:    {true_avg_age:.1f}")
print(f"Laplace DP average:  {dp_avg_laplace:.1f}")
print(f"Gaussian DP average: {dp_avg_gaussian:.1f}")
# Both will be very close to 47.3 — the large dataset makes this easy

Noise scale tells story. For 10,000-patient hospital at ε = 1, Laplace scale is 0.012 years — about 4 days. Published average age misses by only few days. Nobody notices. On 3-patient dataset, scale jumps to 40 years. Noise destroys answer. This is core tension of differential privacy: excellent at scale, brutal on small datasets.

Privacy Leaks with Every Question You Ask

Now uncomfortable part. Every query to differentially private dataset spends privacy budget. One question at ε = 1 spends 1 unit. Ask a hundred questions, guarantee weakens. Soundproof wall gets another hole each time.

Simplest accounting is basic composition: k queries at ε each cost kε total. Hundred queries at ε = 1 give ε = 100 total — terrible privacy. Wall mostly gone.

Advanced composition is tighter. It says k queries at ε each cost roughly ε√(2k · ln(1/δ)) total. For 100 queries, that is about ε√(200 · 18) ≈ 60ε instead of 100ε. Better, but still expensive.

Tightest practical tracking uses Rényi Differential Privacy and moments accountant, introduced by Abadi et al. in 2016. Instead of one privacy-loss number, moments accountant tracks full distribution of privacy loss across training steps. That makes DP training practical. Without it, even few dozen epochs would blow budget. Opacus uses this internally, which is why it can train for hundreds of epochs and still report meaningful (ε, δ) at end.

My intuition here is still rough. High-level idea: worst-case losses usually do not stack in same way at every step, and random noise partly cancels over time. I trust result more than I love proof.

Training a Model Privately: DP-SGD

Now payoff. We can privatize one query. Neural network training uses thousands of gradient updates from training data. Need whole process private.

Answer is DP-SGD (Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent), from Abadi et al. in same 2016 paper. It changes standard SGD in two essential ways.

First, per-example gradient clipping. Standard SGD computes gradient for each batch example, averages them, updates model. DP-SGD clips each individual gradient to maximum norm C before averaging. If one patient's record produces norm 50 and threshold is C = 1, scale that gradient down to norm 1. This bounds sensitivity — no single example can move update by more than C.

Second, Gaussian noise addition. After clipping and averaging gradients, add Gaussian noise calibrated to clipping threshold. Noise scale is σ = noise_multiplier × C / batch_size. That makes each gradient update differentially private.

Walk one step. Batch has 64 patient records. We compute 64 individual gradients. Patient #17 has rare condition and huge gradient (norm = 12.5). Clip it to C = 1.0, so divide by 12.5. Average all 64 clipped gradients, then add Gaussian noise with scale σ × 1.0 / 64. Model updates. No single record dominates, and noise makes step look statistically similar whether patient #17 was in batch or not.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

def dp_sgd_step(model, loss_fn, batch_x, batch_y,
                max_grad_norm=1.0, noise_multiplier=1.1, lr=0.01):
    per_example_grads = []

    for x_i, y_i in zip(batch_x, batch_y):
        model.zero_grad()
        loss = loss_fn(model(x_i.unsqueeze(0)), y_i.unsqueeze(0))
        loss.backward()
        grads = [p.grad.clone() for p in model.parameters()]
        per_example_grads.append(grads)

    # Clip: bound each example's influence
    clipped_grads = []
    for grads in per_example_grads:
        total_norm = torch.sqrt(sum(g.norm()**2 for g in grads))
        clip_factor = min(1.0, max_grad_norm / (total_norm + 1e-8))
        clipped_grads.append([g * clip_factor for g in grads])

    # Noise: make the update indistinguishable
    batch_size = len(batch_x)
    noise_scale = noise_multiplier * max_grad_norm / batch_size

    with torch.no_grad():
        for i, param in enumerate(model.parameters()):
            avg_grad = torch.stack([g[i] for g in clipped_grads]).mean(dim=0)
            noise = torch.normal(0, noise_scale, size=avg_grad.shape)
            param -= lr * (avg_grad + noise)

Clipping does sensitivity bounding. Noise does indistinguishability. Together they make each update (ε, δ)-differentially private. Moments accountant tracks total cost across all steps.

Cost is real. DP-SGD usually drops accuracy by 2 to 10 percentage points versus standard training. On CIFAR-10, strong DP models at ε = 8 reach about 96% accuracy versus 99% without DP. On large datasets like ImageNet, gap shrinks because noise averages over more examples. On small medical datasets — like our three hospitals may have — gap can be devastating. I have seen DP-SGD turn model from clinically useful into guessing. Benchmark your own task before locking privacy budget.

For production, use Opacus (PyTorch) or TF Privacy (TensorFlow). They handle per-example gradients with hooks, apply clipping and noise automatically, and track privacy budget with moments accountant so you can report exact (ε, δ) after training.

What if the Data Can't Leave?

Back to hospitals. City General has 5,000 cardiology records. Valley Medical has 3,000. Riverside Clinic has 2,000. Regulations say patient data cannot leave hospital network. Centralized DP-SGD is off table because no central dataset exists.

Key idea behind federated learning: send model to data, not data to model. Main algorithm is FedAvg (Federated Averaging).

Central server holds global model — maybe random neural network. Server sends copy to each hospital. Each hospital trains on local data for few epochs, producing slightly different model. Hospitals send updated models back, not raw data. Server averages updates, weighted by amount of data each hospital used. Averaged model becomes new global model. Cycle repeats.

One round looks like this. Server sends version v0 to all three hospitals. City General trains on 5,000 records for 5 epochs and returns v1_city. Valley Medical returns v1_valley. Riverside returns v1_riverside. Server computes weighted average: (5000 × v1_city + 3000 × v1_valley + 2000 × v1_riverside) / 10000. That becomes v1_global. Send back out. Repeat 50 or 100 rounds.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
from copy import deepcopy

class FederatedServer:
    def __init__(self, model):
        self.global_model = model

    def aggregate(self, client_states, client_sizes):
        total = sum(client_sizes)
        new_state = {}
        for key in self.global_model.state_dict():
            new_state[key] = sum(
                client_states[i][key] * (client_sizes[i] / total)
                for i in range(len(client_states))
            )
        self.global_model.load_state_dict(new_state)

def local_train(model_state, model_fn, data, labels, epochs=5, lr=0.01):
    model = model_fn()
    model.load_state_dict(model_state)
    optimizer = torch.optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=lr)
    loss_fn = nn.CrossEntropyLoss()
    for _ in range(epochs):
        optimizer.zero_grad()
        loss = loss_fn(model(data), labels)
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()
    return deepcopy(model.state_dict())

# One round: server distributes, hospitals train, server aggregates
# No patient record ever leaves its hospital

No patient record crosses hospital network boundary. City General's patients never learn Valley Medical exists, and vice versa. Only shared artifact is model parameters — weights, not raw records.

Now soundproof-wall problem returns. Hospitals have very different patient populations. City General is dense urban area — more hypertension, diabetes, gunshot wounds. Riverside Clinic is rural — more agricultural injuries, fewer specialists. Data across hospitals is non-IID (not independently and identically distributed), but FedAvg assumes data is roughly uniform across clients. When heterogeneity is high, FedAvg can diverge: local models pull in different directions, and average lands in bad middle ground.

FedProx is standard fix. It adds penalty term to local training: μ/2 · ||w - wglobal||². Think elastic band from each hospital model to global model. Local model can drift to fit local data, but band pulls back before it wanders too far. Setting μ between 0.01 and 1.0 works in many cases.

Communication cost is fundamental too. Every round, each hospital uploads and downloads full model. For 100 million parameters at 32-bit precision, that is 400 MB per round per hospital. Over 100 rounds, that is 40 GB transferred per hospital. Gradient compression (send top-k gradients), quantization (drop from 32-bit to 8-bit), and fewer but longer local rounds can help, but each choice interacts with non-IID instability and needs tuning.

Federated Learning Is Not Automatically Private

Need one correction. I said hospitals send "model parameters, not individual data points." True. Still not enough. Model parameters can leak individual data.

In 2019, Zhu et al. showed gradient inversion attack that reconstructed individual training images from one gradient update with startling fidelity. Attacker starts with random image, computes gradient from fake image, compares it to real gradient hospital shared, then adjusts fake image until gradients match. When match happens, fake image looks a lot like true training image. Actual faces. From gradients.

So "data stays on device" does not mean "data is private." Soundproof wall has crack. Gradient updates carry information about individual records.

Secure aggregation patches part of problem. Central server sees only sum of all hospital updates, never one hospital's update alone. Technique uses cryptographic masking: City General adds random mask +r₁, Valley Medical adds -r₁. When server sums updates, masks cancel and true aggregate remains. Server never sees either individual update. Google deployed this for GBoard in 2017.

Secure aggregation alone still fails to give full privacy. If adversary controls server and two of three hospitals, it can subtract known updates and infer third hospital's update. Real guarantees need all three layers: federated learning for local data, secure aggregation for hidden individual updates, differential privacy noise on each update for statistical invisibility. Belt, suspenders, safety pins.

Computing on Data You Can't See

Secure aggregation is special case of broader idea: let multiple parties compute shared result without any party seeing others' inputs. That is secure multi-party computation (SMPC).

Core trick is secret sharing. Suppose City General wants to contribute patient count without revealing exact number. Split 5000 into random shares like 1847, -923, and 4076, which sum to 5000. Send one share to each hospital. No single hospital can recover original count. Combine shares at end, and computation is still correct.

Addition on secret shares is free — each party adds shares locally, and totals still line up. Multiplication is hard — it needs communication round between parties, which creates overhead. For aggregation and linear regression, SMPC is practical. For billion-parameter neural network training, communication blows up because every multiplication in forward and backward pass needs coordination.

Then dream scenario: homomorphic encryption (HE). HE lets you compute directly on encrypted data. Hospital encrypts patient records, sends ciphertext to cloud server, server runs model on ciphertext, returns encrypted result, hospital decrypts. Server never sees raw data.

First time I read about fully homomorphic encryption, I did not buy it. Computing on encrypted data sounds like magic. Reality is slower: fully homomorphic encryption is 1,000 to 1,000,000 times slower than plaintext computation. Neural network inference that takes 10 milliseconds in plaintext may take 10 seconds to 3 hours under FHE. Libraries like Microsoft SEAL and Zama's Concrete-ML have made strong progress, especially for simple models — logistic regression on encrypted medical records is practical now. Large transformer on encrypted data? Not yet.

Practical stack today looks like this: use federated learning + DP for model training. Use SMPC for secure aggregation and other small computations with 2 to 10 parties. Use HE for inference on sensitive data when latency is acceptable. Each tool blocks different threats, and production systems often layer them.

The Law Catches Up: GDPR, CCPA, and Machine Unlearning

So far, problem was technical: how to protect privacy mathematically. There is also legal problem, and it changes system design.

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation), effective in EU in 2018, sets rules that hit ML engineering directly. Data minimization: collect only features you need — 200-column profile table needs justification for every column. Purpose limitation: data collected for disease prediction cannot become insurance scoring without new consent. Right to explanation (Article 22): automated decisions with major effects need meaningful explanation, which ties to interpretability.

Hardest requirement is Article 17: right to erasure. Patient at City General can demand deletion. Deleting database row is easy. Hard part is model trained on that data. Model weights were shaped by patient's record. In real sense, model remembers patient. Question: retrain from scratch or not?

For three-hospital consortium, retraining federated model from scratch every time patient uses erasure rights could cost tens of thousands of dollars in compute and take days. That is machine unlearning problem. Probably hardest open problem in privacy engineering.

CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act) creates similar pressure in US. It is less specific than GDPR about what "deletion" means for models, but direction is clear: regulators are moving toward requirement that trained models can remove individuals too.

Smartest engineering workaround is SISA training (Sharded, Isolated, Sliced, and Aggregated). Instead of one big model on all data, split data into, say, 10 shards and train separate sub-model on each shard. Prediction comes from ensemble of all 10 sub-models. When patient requests deletion, retrain only shard that held that data — one-tenth cost of full retrain.

class SISATrainer:
    def __init__(self, model_fn, num_shards=10):
        self.model_fn = model_fn
        self.num_shards = num_shards
        self.shard_models = [None] * num_shards
        self.record_to_shard = {}

    def assign_record(self, record_id, shard_idx):
        self.record_to_shard[record_id] = shard_idx

    def train_shard(self, shard_idx, data, labels, epochs=10, lr=0.01):
        model = self.model_fn()
        optimizer = torch.optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=lr)
        loss_fn = nn.CrossEntropyLoss()
        for _ in range(epochs):
            optimizer.zero_grad()
            loss = loss_fn(model(data), labels)
            loss.backward()
            optimizer.step()
        self.shard_models[shard_idx] = model

    def predict(self, x):
        logits = []
        for m in self.shard_models:
            m.eval()
            with torch.no_grad():
                logits.append(m(x))
        return torch.stack(logits).mean(dim=0)

    def unlearn(self, record_id, remaining_data, remaining_labels):
        shard_idx = self.record_to_shard.pop(record_id)
        # Retrain only the affected shard — 1/10th the compute
        self.train_shard(shard_idx, remaining_data, remaining_labels)

Exact unlearning means retrained model matches one that never saw deleted record. SISA gives this. Approximate unlearning uses gradient-based tricks to push model away from deleted point's influence — faster, harder to verify. Regulators still have not settled required standard. Legal and technical meaning of "forgotten" is still being negotiated.

Synthetic Data: Fake Records, Real Problems

Popular idea: stop protecting real data, generate fake data with same statistics instead. Train generative model — GAN, VAE, diffusion model — on real hospital records, then publish synthetic dataset. Synthetic records do not map to real patients, so re-identification sounds impossible.

In theory, free lunch. In practice, discounted lunch with hidden fees.

Without differential privacy during training, generative model can still memorize individual records. Membership inference attack on GAN can reveal whether specific patient's data trained it. Synthetic records look different, but model still carries information. DP-GAN — GAN trained with DP-SGD — gives formal guarantees, but quality drops hard. Downstream accuracy can fall 5 to 20 percentage points at ε between 2 and 5.

Synthetic data is genuinely great for one job: development and testing. Engineers can build and debug pipelines on synthetic records without touching real patient data. For regulatory compliance or publishable analysis, you still need differential privacy guarantees on generative model or formal privacy analysis showing synthetic data does not leak. Treat synthetic data as development convenience, not privacy guarantee.

The Fundamental Tradeoff

Whole section comes back to one tension: privacy and utility pull opposite ways. Every tool here — DP noise, gradient clipping, federated training, encryption overhead — trades some performance or efficiency for privacy protection.

Three hospitals face this directly. With no privacy protections, they could pool records and train best model. With DP-SGD at ε = 1, model might lose 8 accuracy points — enough to cross line from clinically useful to useless. With federated learning, non-IID data adds convergence pain on top of DP noise. With homomorphic encryption, inference takes minutes instead of milliseconds.

No magic setting gives perfect privacy and perfect accuracy. But frontier can move. Larger datasets help because noise averages over more examples. Better accounting — Rényi DP and moments accountant — squeezes more utility from same budget. Pre-training on public data, then fine-tuning with DP on private data, is emerging strong pattern: model learns general features publicly, then uses small DP-protected step on sensitive data.

Honest answer for choosing ε: depends on system, people at risk, and cost of failure. Medical diagnosis model that could leak HIV status needs very different ε than emoji frequency counter. No universal right answer. Anyone selling one is selling something else too.
