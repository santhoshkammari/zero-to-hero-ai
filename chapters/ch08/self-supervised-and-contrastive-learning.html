Self-Supervised and Contrastive Learning

Foundation models like BERT, GPT, CLIP, DINOv2 use self-supervised learning for pretraining. Mechanism learns strong representations from huge unlabeled data. Understand SSL to understand modern AI.

Self-supervised learning (SSL) lets model learn representations without human labels. Data gives supervision: hide part of input, ask model to reconstruct it. Solving puzzle builds useful internal representations. Idea is old. Explosion came from 2018 to 2023. Every major foundation model today uses this pretraining.

The Labeling Bottleneck and Why the World Has More Photos Than Patience

Building wildlife classifier for trail cameras. National park has 200 cameras, each snapping photo every 30 seconds. After one month, dataset holds 50,000 images. Deer, foxes, owls, raccoons, occasional hiker, lots of empty forest.

Now label photos. Volunteer sits one Saturday, tags 47 images, gives up. Forty-seven out of 50,000. Only 0.094% of data. Train supervised classifier on 47 labels, result lands between useless and creative fiction.

This is labeling bottleneck. Not specific to trail cameras. ImageNet's million labels took years of human effort. Medical imaging labels need specialists costing hundreds of dollars per hour. Satellite imagery datasets need domain experts who can distinguish crop types from orbit. Same pattern: world drowns in unlabeled data, starves for labels.

Self-supervised learning exists for one question: what if we learn something useful from all 50,000 photos with zero labels? What if data itself teaches model what "deer" and "fox" look like, even when nobody writes words down?

Question has answer. Several answers. Start with surprisingly low-tech idea.

Pretext Tasks: Teaching by Hiding

Core insight of self-supervised learning: create training signal from data itself by damaging input, then ask model to repair it. Repair task is means to end. Goal is not good repair. Goal is useful internal representations that capture data structure.

Technical term for manufactured task: pretext task. Task you care about later — classifying animals, detecting objects, whatever — is downstream task. Train on pretext task, discard prediction head, keep learned backbone for downstream task. Pretext task trains. Representation is prize.

Like training detective. Practice puzzles matter because they build pattern recognition, attention to detail, and spatial reasoning. Skills transfer to real cases.

Now three pretext tasks from 2016–2018 that pioneered idea in computer vision. Trail camera photos stay running example.

Rotation prediction

Take trail camera photo — fox in clearing. Rotate it by 0°, 90°, 180°, or 270°. Show rotated image, ask which rotation got applied. Four-way classification. Label is free because you applied rotation.

Why work? Predicting 90° means model must know what "upright" means for foxes, trees, and ground. Model must know foxes stand on legs, legs point down, sky sits up. Task forces sense of object structure and spatial arrangement.

Rotation prediction came from Gidaris, Singh, and Komodakis in 2018. Crude idea. Strong results. Model trained on rotations learned features that transferred well to object classification — better than most hand-designed features from era.

Jigsaw puzzles

Cut fox photo into 3×3 grid of patches. Shuffle nine patches. Ask model to predict original arrangement — or at least which predefined permutation got used.

Task forces model to learn spatial relationships between image parts. Where head goes relative to body? What does "next to tree trunk" look like? Model can't solve jigsaw without understanding spatial relations. Noroozi and Favaro introduced this in 2016.

Colorization

Convert fox photo to grayscale. Ask model to predict original colors. Foxes are reddish-orange. Forests are green. Model must learn "this shape is fox" and "this texture is foliage" to assign right colors. Zhang, Isola, and Efros explored colorization as pretext task in 2016.

First read about jigsaw puzzles as training method, thought gimmick. Intuition underneath is solid: task that requires understanding image content — instead of exploiting low-level pixel statistics — produces representations with semantic knowledge.

Still, all three approaches share frustrating limitation.

They are brittle. Rotation prediction fails when images lack natural "up" direction (satellite imagery, microscopy). Jigsaw puzzles depend on grid size and permutation set. Colorization assumes color carries semantic information, which is not always true. Every pretext task bakes in hand-designed inductive bias. Designing good ones became almost as much work as hand-designing features.

Researchers spent 2016–2019 inventing smarter pretext tasks. Then contrastive learning arrived and made most obsolete almost overnight.

From Puzzles to Pull-and-Push: Contrastive Learning

Idea that changed everything: stop designing specific puzzle. Give model general objective instead: learn whether two things are same or different.

Take fox photo. Apply two random transformations — different crop, horizontal flip, brightness change, blur. Now two versions look different but show same scene. These views form positive pair.

Every other image in training batch — deer photo, owl photo, empty forest — is negative.

Objective: make model produce similar representations for two fox views, dissimilar representations for fox and everything else. Pull positives together in embedding space. Push negatives apart.

That is contrastive learning. No jigsaw grids, no rotation angles, no colorization. Supervision comes fully from augmentation pipeline: these two came from same photo, those came from different photos. Augmentation strategy is pretext task. It defines invariances (crop position, color shifts) and distinctions (different animals, different scenes).

Profound shift. Hand-designed pretext tasks tell model what kind of understanding to build. Contrastive learning tells model what to ignore — augmentation details — and lets rest emerge. Detective analogy changes too: show photo pairs and say these two come from same crime, those two from different crimes. Learn difference.

The InfoNCE Loss: Picking the Suspect from a Lineup

Need loss function for pull positives together, push negatives apart. Standard choice is InfoNCE (Noise-Contrastive Estimation with information-theoretic flavor), introduced by van den Oord et al. in 2018.

Intuition: police lineup. One suspect is positive match. N-1 innocent people are negatives. Witness — model — must pick suspect. InfoNCE turns this into softmax classification: out of N candidates, which one is true match?

Walk through tiny example. Batch has three trail camera photos: fox, deer, owl. Augment each twice, get six views total. Focus on fox. Two augmented views — fox₁ and fox₂ — form positive pair. Four other views (deer₁, deer₂, owl₁, owl₂) are negatives.

Model encodes all six views into vectors. Compute cosine similarity between fox₁ and every other view. Result is set of similarity scores:

fox₁ · fox₂ = 0.85 (positive — same fox)
fox₁ · deer₁ = 0.21 (negative)
fox₁ · deer₂ = 0.18 (negative)
fox₁ · owl₁ = 0.12 (negative)
fox₁ · owl₂ = 0.15 (negative)

InfoNCE says: run scores through softmax. Loss is negative log probability assigned to positive pair. If model gives fox₂ high probability relative to negatives, loss is low. If model cannot separate fox from deer, loss is high.

One critical ingredient: temperature parameter τ scales all similarity scores before softmax. Divide by small τ (say 0.07), distribution sharpens — model must be very precise about positive. Divide by larger τ (say 0.5), distribution softens — model gets more credit for near miss.

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def info_nce_loss(query, positive, negatives, temperature=0.07):
    # query:    (B, D) — the anchor embedding (e.g., fox view 1)
    # positive: (B, D) — the matching embedding (e.g., fox view 2)
    # negatives:(B, N, D) — embeddings from other images

    # Similarity between anchor and its positive match
    pos_sim = (query * positive).sum(dim=-1, keepdim=True)

    # Similarity between anchor and each negative
    neg_sim = torch.bmm(negatives, query.unsqueeze(-1)).squeeze(-1)

    # Combine: positive is always index 0, then all negatives
    logits = torch.cat([pos_sim, neg_sim], dim=-1) / temperature

    # Target: index 0 is the correct answer (the positive)
    labels = torch.zeros(query.size(0), dtype=torch.long,
                         device=query.device)
    return F.cross_entropy(logits, labels)

Loss above computes dot product between anchor and positive — one number — then between anchor and each negative — N numbers. It stacks them into one vector, divides by temperature, applies cross-entropy where correct answer is always index 0: positive pair. Whole InfoNCE computation.

Temperature parameter trips many people. Low temperature is not automatically better. Too low, model chases trivially hard negatives instead of useful structure. Too high, model never learns fine distinctions. Most methods land between 0.05 and 0.2. Sweep it early in project. This knob can move accuracy by several points.

Lineup analogy extends naturally. Low temperature is witness picking from near-identical twins. High temperature is witness picking fox from lamp, car, houseplant. Need sweet spot: hard enough to force learning, not so hard that model memorizes irrelevant details.

The specter of collapse

Before specific methods, face main failure mode of self-supervised learning: representation collapse.

Problem: easiest way to make every positive pair perfectly similar is mapping every input to same point. Every image gives exact same vector. Positive pairs become identical. Loss is zero. Model solves task by learning nothing.

Like detective deciding every crime scene photo looks same. Never misidentify match. Still completely useless.

Contrastive methods prevent collapse with negatives. Map everything to one point, similarity between anchor and every negative also becomes maximal, and loss explodes. Negatives act like repulsive force that keeps embedding space from collapsing.

Methods without negatives — and several exist — need other tricks. Asymmetric architectures, stop-gradient operations, exponential moving averages. Understanding collapse prevention is often same as understanding method. We return to this for each approach.

Practical diagnostic: monitor standard deviation of embedding dimensions during training. If it trends toward zero, representations are collapsing. Catch early.

SimCLR: The Clean Baseline

SimCLR, published by Google Brain in 2020, stripped contrastive learning to essentials and became reference baseline. Full name — "A Simple Framework for Contrastive Learning of Visual Representations" — fits. This is simplest version that works well.

Trace it with trail camera photos. Batch has 256 images: foxes, deer, owls, empty frames. Each image gets augmented twice, producing 512 views. Every image and second augmentation form positive pair. Everything else in batch is negative. For one fox photo, that means one positive and 510 negatives.

Each view passes through shared encoder — ResNet-50 in original paper — to produce feature vector. Then through small 2-layer MLP called projection head, which maps features to 128-dimensional space. InfoNCE runs in that 128-dimensional space.

After training, throw projection head away. Downstream tasks use encoder output, not projector output. Reason: projection head absorbs augmentation-specific shortcuts. It learns features for contrastive task itself — like was this cropped from left? — rather than is this fox? Encoder behind projector learns cleaner, more transferable representations.

import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
import torchvision.transforms as T

class SimCLR(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, backbone, proj_dim=128):
        super().__init__()
        self.backbone = backbone
        feat_dim = backbone.fc.in_features
        backbone.fc = nn.Identity()   # strip the classification head
        self.projector = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(feat_dim, 2048),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(2048, proj_dim),
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        h = self.backbone(x)          # encoder features
        z = self.projector(h)          # projected for contrastive loss
        return F.normalize(z, dim=-1)  # unit-norm for cosine similarity

Model takes image, runs backbone to get features h, then projects through two-layer MLP to get z. Normalize call at end puts all vectors on unit sphere, so dot products become cosine similarities. After training, keep backbone, discard projector.

The augmentation stack that launched a thousand papers

SimCLR's most cited finding is not architecture or loss. It is augmentations. Authors ran exhaustive ablation study over crop, flip, color jitter, grayscale, and blur. Conclusion: random resized crop + color jitter is minimum viable augmentation pair.

Why these two? Without color jitter, model takes shortcut and matches images by color histogram. Two crops from same photo usually share color distribution, so model learns similar colors = same image, not semantics. Without random cropping, model matches global layout — dark blob top-left — without learning objects.

Need both to block shortcuts and force genuine content understanding. This result reshaped later SSL papers. Augmentation stack became first-class design choice, not afterthought.

simclr_augment = T.Compose([
    T.RandomResizedCrop(224, scale=(0.2, 1.0)),
    T.RandomHorizontalFlip(),
    T.RandomApply([T.ColorJitter(0.4, 0.4, 0.4, 0.1)], p=0.8),
    T.RandomGrayscale(p=0.2),
    T.GaussianBlur(kernel_size=23, sigma=(0.1, 2.0)),
    T.ToTensor(),
    T.Normalize([0.485, 0.456, 0.406], [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]),
])

Each augmented view passes through this pipeline: random crop covering 20% to 100% of image, coin-flip horizontal mirror, 80% chance of color distortion, 20% chance of full grayscale, and Gaussian blur. Normalize at end standardizes pixel values to range pretrained backbone expects.

SimCLR has one major limitation, practical not conceptual. More negatives per batch give better representations. Original paper used batch sizes of 4,096 to 8,192 across 32 to 128 TPU cores. For researcher with single GPU, that recipe is fantasy.

That limitation motivated next major development.

MoCo: Decoupling Negatives from Batch Size

MoCo — Momentum Contrast — came from Kaiming He at Facebook AI in 2020. It solved SimCLR's batch-size problem with two elegant ideas.

First idea: queue of negatives. Instead of using current batch as negative source, MoCo keeps FIFO (first-in, first-out) queue of 65,536 past embeddings. Each training step, current batch embeddings get enqueued and oldest embeddings get dequeued. Result: massive negative diversity even with batch size 256. In trail camera terms, each fox photo gets compared not only with 255 other photos in current batch, but with rolling memory of last 65,536 embeddings.

Second idea fixes problem created by first. Queue holds embeddings produced at different training steps. If encoder weights change fast, old queue embeddings go stale — they came from older model version. Comparing fresh query embeddings against stale key embeddings is like comparing today's crime scene photos against witness sketches drawn by different artist last month.

MoCo's fix is momentum encoder. Instead of one encoder, use two: query encoder updated by normal gradient descent, and key encoder updated as slow-moving average of query encoder. Key encoder produces embeddings stored in queue.

import torch

@torch.no_grad()
def momentum_update(encoder_q, encoder_k, m=0.999):
    """Key encoder = slow exponential moving average of query encoder."""
    for q_param, k_param in zip(encoder_q.parameters(),
                                 encoder_k.parameters()):
        k_param.data = m * k_param.data + (1 - m) * q_param.data

Update rule is one line: each key-encoder parameter becomes 99.9% old value plus 0.1% current query-encoder value. Key encoder changes very slowly — slowly enough that embeddings from 100 steps ago stay reasonably consistent with current embeddings. Queue stays coherent.

Momentum coefficient m=0.999 works well across settings. Values too low (0.9) make key encoder change too fast, hurting queue consistency. Values too high (0.9999) make it change too slowly, so it fails to track improving query encoder.

MoCo v2 borrowed SimCLR's augmentation pipeline and MLP projection head for free accuracy boost. MoCo v3 adapted framework to Vision Transformers. Momentum encoder survived every version because it stabilizes training. Lesson: core engineering challenge in contrastive learning was not loss function or architecture, but logistics of managing negative examples.

BYOL: Dropping the Negatives Entirely

In 2020, DeepMind published BYOL — Bootstrap Your Own Latent — and result was confusing. It matched SimCLR and MoCo without any negative pairs. Positives only. No repulsive force.

Immediate reaction: this should not work. Without negatives, nothing seems to stop model from mapping everything to one point. Whole reason for negatives is collapse prevention. Remove them. How still get useful representations?

Answer is architectural asymmetry. Internalizing why takes time.

BYOL has two networks. Online network contains encoder, projector MLP, and predictor MLP. Target network has same encoder and projector, but no predictor. Target weights update as exponential moving average of online network — MoCo momentum trick reused.

Training loop: take trail camera fox photo. Augment it two ways. Pass one augmentation through online network (encoder → projector → predictor) to get prediction. Pass other augmentation through target network (encoder → projector, no predictor) to get target. Train online network so prediction matches target. Crucial detail: gradients flow only through online network. Target network has stop_gradient — no backprop updates, only slow EMA.

This asymmetry prevents collapse. Think apprenticeship. Online network tries to predict what target network will say. Target changes slowly. If online network uses lazy strategy and predicts same constant every time, target still produces different representations for different images because it started with useful randomness and changes slowly. Loss stays high. Low loss requires learning input structure.

BatchNorm in projector adds another layer of collapse resistance. Batch normalization computes statistics across whole batch, so examples share information implicitly. If representations all try to converge to one point, BatchNorm re-centers and re-scales them, breaking collapse. Subtle effect. Researchers showed removing BatchNorm from BYOL causes collapse.

Favorite thing about BYOL: beyond high-level story above, nobody is fully certain why it works so well. Several theoretical analyses exist — one says implicit contrastive learning, another links it to spectral methods — but none fully explain observed stability. Practice ran ahead of theory. Theory still catching up.

DINO and DINOv2: Self-Distillation and Emergent Attention

DINO (Meta, 2021) applied similar student-teacher framework to Vision Transformers, with one game-changing addition: centering. Instead of BatchNorm for collapse prevention, DINO keeps running mean of teacher outputs and subtracts it. This centers teacher distribution, stopping any one dimension from dominating and pulling everything toward one point.

Student network trains to match teacher's softmax output — hence self-distillation, because teacher is slowly evolving version of student and student distills knowledge from itself. DINO uses different augmentation strengths for student and teacher: teacher sees large global crops covering 50%+ of image, student sees smaller local crops covering as little as 5%. This forces student to predict global structure from local patches.

But DINO's most striking result was not accuracy. It was what Vision Transformer learned to see. When researchers visualized last-layer self-attention maps, model had discovered object boundaries. No segmentation labels. No object-detection supervision. DINO's ViT attended to semantically meaningful regions — outline of dog, wings of bird, contour of car. Pure self-supervised training on unlabeled images.

First saw those attention maps, did not believe them. Too clean. Too purposeful for model never told what object means. But replications are extensive. Effect is real. Something about self-distillation, Vision Transformers, and multi-crop augmentation makes semantic structure emerge.

DINOv2 (Meta, 2023) scaled this approach to 142 million curated images with ViT-giant backbone. Result is closest thing we have to universal visual feature extractor. DINOv2 features rival or beat CLIP on dense prediction tasks — segmentation, depth estimation, surface normal prediction — without task-specific fine-tuning. Freeze DINOv2 backbone, attach lightweight head, get strong results across wide range of vision tasks. For general-purpose computer vision in 2024 and beyond, DINOv2 is often first stop.

Masked Image Modeling: MAE and the Vision Equivalent of BERT

Contrastive and self-distillation methods are not only path. Another SSL line uses completely different principle: mask part of input and reconstruct it.

If you know BERT, you know idea in text: mask 15% of tokens in sentence, train model to predict missing words. Model cannot memorize answers because masks change each time, so it must learn grammar, semantics, and world knowledge to fill blanks.

MAE — Masked Autoencoders — applies this to images. Published by Kaiming He in 2021, MAE works like this: divide image into non-overlapping patches (typically 16×16 pixels for ViT). Randomly mask 75%. Feed only visible 25% into Vision Transformer encoder. Then use lightweight decoder to reconstruct missing patch pixels.

75% masking ratio sounds extreme. It is. Much higher than BERT's 15% token masking. Reason: images are far more redundant than text. Mask one word in sentence, recovery often needs whole sentence understanding. Mask one small image patch, recovery often comes from neighboring patches via texture interpolation — no semantic understanding needed. Need to mask a lot before task forces model to understand scene instead of filling textures.

Why 75% instead of 70% or 80%? Empirical ablation in MAE paper shows clear peak near 75%, with accuracy dropping on both sides. Too low (around 40%), task is too easy — model fills textures without learning semantics. Too high (around 90%), too little context remains for meaningful predictions. Transition is surprisingly sharp.

MAE has practical advantage over contrastive methods: no need for large batches or momentum encoders. Training signal comes from pixel reconstruction, not pair comparison. Simpler training. More stability. Easier scaling to custom domains with limited batch size.

Cost: pixel-level reconstruction teaches more about textures and low-level statistics than high-level semantics. Contrastive methods like SimCLR and DINO usually produce features more immediately useful for classification. MAE features often shine after fine-tuning, when model can adapt texture-heavy representations to downstream task.

Newer approach, BEiT (BERT Pre-Training of Image Transformers), addresses this by first tokenizing image patches into discrete vocabulary with pretrained tokenizer, then predicting token IDs of masked patches instead of raw pixels. This pushes reconstruction target toward more abstract semantic representations — closer to BERT with discrete words.

Why Text and Vision Learned Differently

Pattern worth noticing. In NLP, masked prediction (BERT) and next-token prediction (GPT) dominated early. In vision, contrastive methods (SimCLR, MoCo, DINO) led for years before masked modeling (MAE) became competitive. Why?

Language is discrete. Words are tokens from finite vocabulary. When BERT predicts masked token, target is specific word ID — clean, unambiguous signal. Loss is cross-entropy over vocabulary. Works beautifully.

Images are continuous. Pixels are floating-point numbers. When MAE predicts masked patch, target is grid of RGB values. Raw-pixel loss treats slightly wrong shade of green same as completely wrong object. Pixel-level reconstruction rewards texture matching more than semantic understanding. That gave contrastive methods, which operate on learned representations instead of raw pixels, early edge in vision.

Convergence is happening now. Methods like I-JEPA (Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture, Yann LeCun's group at Meta, 2023) predict in representation space, not pixel space. Instead of reconstructing pixels of masked patches, I-JEPA predicts encoded representation of masked region. This combines simplicity of masking with semantic richness of learned embeddings. Field is moving this way. Method works across text and vision without strong augmentation pipelines contrastive methods need.

CLIP: Contrastive Learning Across Modalities

Everything so far stayed within one modality — image to image, or text to text. CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining, OpenAI, 2021) extends contrastive learning across modalities. Result is arguably most versatile model from SSL era.

Setup: two encoders. Image encoder (ViT or ResNet) and text encoder (Transformer). Given batch of N image-text pairs — say trail camera image plus caption "photo of fox in forest" — CLIP computes cosine similarity between every image and every text in batch. Result is N×N similarity matrix. Diagonal matches should be high. Off-diagonal mismatches should be low.

Loss is InfoNCE applied symmetrically: for each image, classify matching text out of N candidates. For each text, classify matching image. Average both.

What makes CLIP remarkable is downstream capability. Because images and text share one embedding space, you can do zero-shot classification: encode candidate class names as text ("a photo of a fox," "a photo of a deer," "a photo of an owl"), encode test image, pick class whose text embedding is closest. No fine-tuning. No task-specific training. Trail camera project could classify animals from English descriptions — science fiction not long ago.

CLIP trained on 400 million image-text pairs scraped from internet. Scale of data — not clever architecture — gives CLIP generalization power. Contrastive objective is same InfoNCE used in SimCLR. Magic is pairing: natural language supervision is richer training signal than hand-designed pretext tasks.

Evaluation Protocols: How We Know It Worked

Self-supervised learning produces backbone — feature extractor. How measure whether features are good? Two standard evaluation protocols. Distinction matters.

Linear probing

Freeze entire pretrained backbone. Attach single linear layer on top. Train only that layer on labeled downstream dataset (like ImageNet classification). Report accuracy.

This is strict test. It measures how linearly separable learned features are. If linear classifier can distinguish cats from dogs using frozen backbone features, those features already contain meaningful semantics. Backbone did hard work during self-supervised pretraining. Linear layer does minimal extra computation.

Back to trail camera example: freeze SimCLR backbone trained on 50,000 unlabeled photos, add one linear layer, train it on 47 labeled examples. If linear probing gives 78% accuracy, that means self-supervised features expose species information even to trivial classifier.

Fine-tuning

Initialize backbone with pretrained weights, then unfreeze everything. Train whole model end-to-end on labeled downstream dataset. Report accuracy.

This is practical test. It measures how good self-supervised backbone is as an initialization — starting point for supervised training. Fine-tuning almost always beats linear probing because backbone can adapt to specific downstream task. Gap between linear-probing and fine-tuning accuracy shows how much task-specific adaptation features need.

Most SSL papers report both. Linear probing is apples-to-apples comparison of feature quality. Fine-tuning is practical number: what you get in practice. For real projects, fine-tuning result matters most. For comparing SSL methods, linear probing is cleaner signal.

When SSL Beats Supervised Pretraining

Not every project needs self-supervised learning. Honest assessment: when it helps, when it does not, based on consistent empirical literature.

SSL pretraining wins when domain shift exists between available labeled data and target domain. ImageNet features work well when images look like internet photos. They are mediocre for pathology slides, X-rays, satellite imagery, underwater footage, or night infrared trail-camera photos. If your domain looks different from web images used for supervised pretraining, SSL on your own unlabeled domain data gives better features.

SSL also wins when labels are scarce. With 50 labels and 50,000 unlabeled images, supervised model trains on 50 examples. SSL backbone trains on all 50,000, then fine-tunes on 50 labels. SSL backbone has seen far more of data distribution. Empirically, advantage is strongest below about 1,000 labels and mostly disappears above 10,000.

SSL also wins when you need one backbone for many tasks. Trail camera project might need species classification, behavior detection, population counting, and anomaly detection. Training separate supervised model for each is wasteful. Self-supervised backbone trained once on unlabeled data transfers to all four tasks. This is foundation-model philosophy in distilled form.

Counterpoint: if you have 10,000+ labeled examples and domain is close to ImageNet, supervised pretrained backbone (like torchvision ResNet or pretrained ViT) fine-tuned on your labels will often match or beat SSL in practice, with less engineering effort. Supervised pretrained models have decades of optimization behind them. They are well understood, well documented, and fast to train. Do not use SSL for novelty. Use it when data situation demands it.

Shortcut most practitioners take: they do not train SimCLR or BYOL themselves. They grab DINOv2 or CLIP features off shelf, freeze backbone, train lightweight head on labels. Still self-supervised learning — just someone else's. Do not reinvent pretraining wheel unless domain is truly unusual.
