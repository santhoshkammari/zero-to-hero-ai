TL;DR

Weight averaging smooths noisy training, often giving better models. Knowledge distillation shrinks giants into pocket-size students. Curriculum learning and progressive resizing start with easy examples first. Gradient checkpointing and torch.compile train bigger models faster. Exotic optimizers like SAM and LAMB matter when competing or scaling to thousands of GPUs. Experiment tracking (W&B) saves runs from day one.

Weight Averaging & EMA

SGD and Adam training is noisy by design. Each mini-batch nudges weights in slightly different direction. Final weights are where optimizer stopped, not necessarily best point visited. Weight averaging fixes this. Average many snapshots instead of trusting last one.

Stochastic Weight Averaging (SWA)

Idea is simple. Train model normally for most epochs. In final 20–25%, save weights at end of each epoch. When training ends, average saved checkpoints into one weight set. Deploy averaged model.

Why it works: averaged weights land in flatter regions of loss landscape. Flat minimum means small weight perturbations do not spike loss. Sharp minima can look great on training set, then fail under distribution shift. Averaging nearby points spreads solution across wider basin. Wider basins usually generalize better. Izmailov et al. (2018) showed consistent 1–2% accuracy gains for free.

PyTorch has built-in support. Wrap model with torch.optim.swa_utils.AveragedModel, use SWALR during averaging phase, call update_bn at end to recalculate batch norm stats for averaged weights. Easy to forget. Skip it, accuracy drops because running stats match original weights, not averaged ones. When you'll need it: squeezing last bit of performance before deployment, especially in vision tasks. Cheapest upgrade for trained model.

Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

EMA solves same problem differently. Instead of averaging checkpoints at end, keep shadow copy of model weights through training, updating every step: EMA = α × EMA + (1 − α) × current_weights, where α is usually 0.999 or 0.9999. Shadow weights are smoothed training trajectory. Train with real weights. Evaluate and deploy with shadow weights.

Think running diary, not final exam. Real weights study: jump around, correct mistakes, overcorrect sometimes. EMA weights record overall trend, filtering day-to-day noise. This is Polyak averaging. Known since 1992 to improve convergence.

EMA is everywhere in modern deep learning. Diffusion models use it (original DDPM paper applies EMA by default). GANs use it (EMA weights help stabilize training). Self-supervised methods like BYOL use momentum encoder, which is basically EMA of main encoder. Training generative model? You are probably using EMA, even if you do not notice.

Difference from SWA matters. EMA is online: updated every step, old weights decay exponentially. SWA is offline: average last N checkpoints uniformly. EMA adapts more to recent training. SWA treats all saved checkpoints equally. In practice, EMA runs during training; SWA gets added at end. When you'll need it: production models, generative models, or anytime you want more stability without changing training loop.

Training Strategies

Knowledge Distillation

Have 10-billion-parameter model, highly accurate and impossible to deploy on phone. Knowledge distillation transfers what model knows into student a hundred times smaller.

Key insight: model's soft probability outputs contain more information than hard labels. When teacher sees cat image and outputs cat=0.7, tiger=0.2, lynx=0.1, secondary probabilities matter. They encode relationships between classes that hard label "cat" ignores. Hinton et al. (2015) called this "dark knowledge". Information hiding in wrong answers.

To amplify dark knowledge, use temperature scaling. Divide logits by temperature T before softmax: softmax(zi / T). At T=1, probabilities are normal. At T=3 or T=5, distribution softens: dominant class drops, minor classes rise, inter-class relationships get louder. Teacher and student use same temperature. Student learns teacher's softened outputs.

Student loss is usually weighted mix: α × KL-divergence between student and teacher soft outputs, plus (1 − α) × standard cross-entropy with hard labels. First term teaches student how teacher thinks. Second term keeps training grounded. This is how Apple deploys models on-device, how DistilBERT kept 97% of BERT's performance with 40% fewer parameters, and how smaller LLMs increasingly learn from much larger ones. When you'll need it: smaller, faster model that keeps larger-model quality—edge deployment, mobile, latency-critical serving.

Curriculum Learning

Bengio et al. (2009) formalized intuitive idea. Humans learn better with easy material first, hard material later. Neural networks may do same.

Rank training examples by difficulty. Feed easy examples early, hard examples later. Model builds foundation before edge cases. On noisy datasets, this helps more because easy examples are often clean ones. Model gets stable signal first, then sees noisy, ambiguous samples that would confuse untrained model.

Hard part: defining "difficulty." Reason curriculum learning is not universal. You need scoring function. No one-size-fits-all answer. Common choices: loss value itself (low-loss examples on pretrained model are "easy"), model confidence, or hand-made heuristics (shorter sentences easier, lower-resolution images easier). Self-paced learning automates this by letting model decide what is easy during training. Gaining traction in 2024 for LLMs and multi-modal tasks.

In practice, curriculum learning helps on messy real-world datasets and sequence tasks. On clean benchmarks like CIFAR-10, effect is small. Struggling with convergence on noisy data? Worth trying. Data already clean and balanced? Spend time elsewhere. When you'll need it: noisy or imbalanced datasets, sequence modeling, or tasks where "easy" has clear meaning.

Progressive Resizing

This is curriculum learning for image resolution. Start training on small images—64×64 or 128×128. After model learns coarse features (shapes, colors, basic patterns), raise resolution to 224×224 or higher for final phase.

Economics are strong. 64×64 image has 16× fewer pixels than 256×256 image, so forward pass is much cheaper. Same VRAM fits larger batches, which stabilizes gradients. Early epochs run fast, so iteration is fast. Model learns global structure first—the outline of dog, general color palette—then refines details like fur texture or whiskers.

Jeremy Howard and fast.ai popularized this technique. Kaggle image winners use it often. It also regularizes: small images cannot overfit high-frequency details that are not present. When you'll need it: image classification and segmentation tasks, especially when VRAM is limited or you want faster iteration during early experiments.

Memory & Compute Tricks

Gradient Checkpointing

Memory problem in deep training: forward pass stores activations from every layer for backward pass. 100-layer ResNet stores 100 activation sets. Transformer with 96 layers stores 96 attention maps and 96 intermediate-state sets. VRAM disappears fast.

Gradient checkpointing (activation checkpointing) makes deliberate trade. Discard most activations during forward pass, recompute them during backward pass. Keep activations only at selected "checkpoint" layers. When backprop reaches gap, rerun forward pass from nearest checkpoint to regenerate needed values.

Math is elegant. If model has L layers and you place checkpoints every √L layers, memory drops from O(L) to O(√L). Big reduction. Cost: one additional forward pass through each segment. In practice, roughly 60% memory savings for about 20–30% more training time. This is why you can fine-tune 7B-parameter LLM on single consumer GPU. Without it, cluster needed. PyTorch: torch.utils.checkpoint. Hugging Face Transformers: set gradient_checkpointing=True in training config. When you'll need it: any time model barely fits in VRAM, which is often with modern architectures.

torch.compile (PyTorch 2.0+)

For years, PyTorch's eager execution—run each operation immediately, one at a time—was best debugging feature and biggest performance weakness. Every operation launches separate GPU kernel, returns to Python, launches next kernel. Overhead adds up.

torch.compile changes this. One line—model = torch.compile(model)—and TorchDynamo intercepts Python bytecode, extracts tensor operations into computation graph, and hands graph to TorchInductor for optimization. Inductor fuses adjacent operations into single GPU kernels, optimizes memory layouts, and can generate custom Triton kernels on fly.

Typical speedup: 1.5×–3× with no model-code changes. Catch: first forward pass is slow because graph compilation can take 30 seconds to several minutes on large models. Later passes are fast. As of 2024, it is stable for standard architectures—CNNs, transformers, most attention variants—but can fail on dynamic control flow, custom CUDA extensions, or unusual tensor operations. Error messages are improving. Try it early on new projects. When you'll need it: any PyTorch training or inference workload where speed matters.

Exotic Optimizers

Lookahead Optimizer

Lookahead (Zhang et al., 2019) wraps base optimizer—usually Adam or SGD—and adds second set of "slow weights." Inner optimizer updates "fast weights" normally for k steps (usually 5–10). Then Lookahead moves slow weights toward fast weights: slow = slow + α × (fast − slow), then resets fast weights to match. It periodically checks whether optimization is still heading somewhere useful.

This reduces variance in optimization trajectory and can stabilize training when Adam alone oscillates. Overhead is negligible. Catch: gains are inconsistent. Sometimes it helps, sometimes not, hard to predict. It never became common, but idea is elegant. When you'll need it: unstable training after you already tuned learning rate and weight decay. Low-risk experiment.

LAMB & LARS

When batch size reaches 32K, 64K, or more—the scale from hundreds of GPUs—standard optimizers break down. Different layers have very different gradient magnitudes. Learning rate that fits first convolutional layer can explode final classifier head, or reverse.

LARS (Layer-wise Adaptive Rate Scaling, You et al. 2017) solves this by computing per-layer learning rate from ratio of layer weight norm to gradient norm. LAMB (Layer-wise Adaptive Moments for Batch training, You et al. 2020) extends idea to Adam-style optimizers. LARS trained ResNet-50 on ImageNet in under 30 minutes. LAMB trained BERT in 76 minutes. These engineering wins matter mostly at scales most people never touch, but they explain impossibly fast training times in papers. When you'll need it: distributed training across 64+ GPUs with very large batch sizes. Otherwise, Adam with good schedule is enough.

Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM)

SAM (Foret et al., 2021) attacks generalization differently. Instead of asking "find weights where loss is low," it asks "find weights where loss is low and neighborhood is also low." It explicitly seeks flat regions of loss landscape with perturbation step: compute gradient, step in direction of steepest ascent to find worst-case neighbor, then compute gradient there and update in direction of steepest descent.

Two forward-backward passes per step. Main cost of SAM. Generalization gains are consistent and real (0.5–1.5% on standard benchmarks), so Kaggle uses it heavily when accuracy matters more than training cost. For production models with hundreds of hyperparameter runs, 2× cost hurts badly. Variants like ASAM and LookSAM reduce overhead, but core trade-off stays. When you'll need it: competitions, final production training where every fraction of a percent matters, or research focused on generalization.

Large-Scale Training Frameworks

DeepSpeed

Microsoft's DeepSpeed exists because 175-billion-parameter model needs roughly 700 GB of fp32 memory for parameters alone, and ~2.8 TB with Adam optimizer states. No single GPU has that memory. DeepSpeed's ZeRO (Zero Redundancy Optimizer) solves this by sharding. Split memory footprint across many GPUs.

ZeRO has three stages, each more aggressive. Stage 1 shards optimizer states: Adam momenta, variance terms, other state 2–3× model size. Each GPU keeps only its slice. Stage 2 adds gradient sharding. Stage 3 also shards model parameters. At Stage 3, no GPU holds full model. Parameters are gathered just in time for computation, used, then released. This is how 100B+ parameter models train on "commodity" hardware—still 8–64 expensive GPUs, not thousands.

Trade-off is communication. Higher stages mean more data movement between GPUs. Stage 1 adds little communication overhead. Stage 3 needs significant bandwidth. PyTorch also has FSDP (Fully Sharded Data Parallel), native alternative similar to ZeRO Stage 3. When you'll need it: models that do not fit on single GPU even with mixed precision and gradient checkpointing.

Megatron-LM

NVIDIA's Megatron-LM tackles complementary problem. DeepSpeed focuses on memory efficiency. Megatron focuses on compute efficiency across many GPUs. It provides three parallelism types: tensor parallelism (split individual matrix multiplications across GPUs), pipeline parallelism (split model into sequential stages across GPUs), and sequence parallelism (split sequence dimension in attention computations).

In practice, large runs often combine both. Megatron-LM handles parallelism strategy. DeepSpeed handles memory optimization. If paper says "we trained a 530B parameter model," both names are probably in methods section. When you'll need it: training at 10B+ parameter scale with multi-node GPU clusters. For smaller work, PyTorch's DDP or FSDP is enough.

Experiment Tracking

Weights & Biases (W&B)

Every ML engineer reaches same point: 47 training runs, slightly different hyperparameters, no idea which one produced promising loss curve three days ago. That is moment experiment tracking becomes necessary.

W&B is de facto standard. Two lines—wandb.init(project="my-project") and wandb.log({"loss": loss, "lr": lr})—and you get real-time loss curves, system GPU/CPU monitoring, hyperparameter logging, and artifact versioning. Dashboard lets you overlay runs, compare hyperparameters against metrics, and run automated sweeps. Free for personal use. Ubiquitous in research papers.

Alternatives exist. MLflow is fully open-source and gives complete data sovereignty, so many enterprises use it for production model governance because data stays on your infrastructure. TensorBoard is lightweight and fine for quick local experiments. Neptune and Comet sit between those options. Common company pattern: W&B for R&D speed, MLflow for production registry and compliance. When you'll need it: first serious training run. Start logging early. Saves time recreating forgotten experiments.

Interview Corner

Senior interviewers probe whether you used these techniques or only memorized definitions. Internalize few points:

EMA and SWA are not same. EMA updates every step with exponential decay, so recent weights matter more. SWA averages last N checkpoints uniformly, so all saved checkpoints have equal weight. Mix them up, signal shallow understanding.

Knowledge distillation's temperature parameter is key. At T=1, standard softmax. Higher T softens distribution and amplifies "dark knowledge"—secondary class probabilities encoding inter-class similarities. If asked "why not train student directly on same data?" answer: soft targets from teacher carry more information than hard labels.

Gradient checkpointing reduces memory to O(√L), not O(1). It does not eliminate activation storage. It trades full O(L) storage for O(√L) by recomputing segments. If asked about cost, say roughly one extra forward pass through each segment, not double total compute.

If DeepSpeed ZeRO comes up, know stages. Stage 1 shards optimizer states. Stage 2 adds gradient sharding. Stage 3 shards everything, including parameters. Also know PyTorch's FSDP is native equivalent of ZeRO Stage 3. Comparison comes up often.
