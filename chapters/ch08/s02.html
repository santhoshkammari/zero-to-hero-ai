Learning Rate Schedules

Chapter 8: Training Deep Networks Section 2 of 16 ~18 min read
TL;DR

The learning rate is the single most important hyperparameter in deep learning. Too high and your model diverges. Too low and training takes forever (or gets stuck). Learning rate schedules change the learning rate during training — typically starting high for fast progress and decaying to a small value for fine convergence. Warmup prevents instability at the start. Cosine annealing and One-Cycle are the most popular modern schedules. The best schedule depends on your optimizer, batch size, and how much patience you have.

Why the Learning Rate Needs a Schedule

Think of the learning rate as the size of each step your optimizer takes downhill. At the beginning of training, you're far from any minimum — big steps are fine because any direction that reduces the loss is progress. But as you approach a minimum, big steps cause you to overshoot. You leap over the valley floor, bounce off the other side, leap back. You oscillate without ever settling.

A learning rate schedule solves this by reducing the step size over time. Large steps at the start for fast exploration, small steps at the end for precise convergence. The question isn't whether to use a schedule — it's which one.

Step Decay

Step decay is the simplest approach: multiply the learning rate by a constant factor at fixed epochs. Train at lr=0.1 for the first 30 epochs, drop to 0.01 for the next 30, then 0.001 for the final 30. The drops are called milestones.

import torch.optim as optim
from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import StepLR, MultiStepLR

optimizer = optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.1, momentum=0.9)

# Drop LR by 10x every 30 epochs
scheduler = StepLR(optimizer, step_size=30, gamma=0.1)

# Or drop at specific milestones
scheduler = MultiStepLR(optimizer, milestones=[30, 60, 80], gamma=0.1)

for epoch in range(90):
    train_one_epoch(model, optimizer, train_loader)
    scheduler.step()  # must call after each epoch
    print(f"Epoch {epoch}, LR: {scheduler.get_last_lr()[0]:.6f}")

Step decay was the standard for years — ResNet's original paper used it. The downside is that you need to choose the milestones by hand, and the abrupt drops can temporarily destabilize training. Despite its simplicity, step decay is hard to beat on some vision benchmarks where the milestones have been tuned by years of community experimentation.

Exponential Decay

Exponential decay multiplies the learning rate by a constant factor every epoch, not just at milestones. The formula is lr = lr₀ × γ^epoch. This gives a smooth, continuous decrease.

from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import ExponentialLR

scheduler = ExponentialLR(optimizer, gamma=0.95)  # 5% decay per epoch

The advantage is smoothness — no sudden jolts. The disadvantage is that the decay is relentless. If γ=0.95 and you train for 100 epochs, your final LR is 0.1 × 0.95¹⁰⁰ ≈ 0.00059. That might be too small. The decay rate γ needs careful tuning relative to total training length, which makes exponential decay less "set it and forget it" than other schedules.

Cosine Annealing

Cosine annealing decreases the learning rate following a half-cosine curve from the initial value down to near zero. It starts with a gentle decline, accelerates through the middle, and tapers off at the end. The shape is governed by:

lr = lr_min + 0.5 × (lr_max - lr_min) × (1 + cos(π × t / T))

where t is the current step and T is the total number of steps.

from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import CosineAnnealingLR

optimizer = optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.1, momentum=0.9)
scheduler = CosineAnnealingLR(optimizer, T_max=200, eta_min=1e-6)

for epoch in range(200):
    train_one_epoch(model, optimizer, train_loader)
    scheduler.step()

Cosine annealing has become the default schedule for many vision tasks. It requires only one hyperparameter beyond the initial LR: the total training length T_max. No milestones to tune. The smooth curve avoids the instability of step decay's abrupt drops. Research has shown that the gentle tapering at the end allows the optimizer to settle into flatter, better-generalizing minima.

Cosine Annealing with Warm Restarts

Cosine annealing with warm restarts (SGDR) resets the learning rate back to its initial value periodically. Each restart begins a new cosine decay cycle, potentially a longer one. The intuition: restarting kicks the optimizer out of whatever minimum it has settled into, giving it a chance to explore a different (possibly better) region of the landscape.

from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts

# First cycle: 10 epochs, then restart and double the cycle length
scheduler = CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts(optimizer, T_0=10, T_mult=2)
# Cycles: epochs 0-9 (10 epochs), 10-29 (20 epochs), 30-69 (40 epochs), ...

Warm restarts work well when training for many epochs. The increasing cycle length means later restarts are less aggressive — the optimizer spends more time refining each successive minimum. This has been particularly effective in combination with snapshot ensembling, where you save a checkpoint at the end of each cycle and average their predictions.

Warmup

At the very start of training, the model's weights are random. Gradients are large and noisy. If you immediately use a high learning rate, the updates can be so big that the model jumps to a terrible region of the loss landscape and never recovers. Warmup prevents this by starting with a very small learning rate and gradually increasing it to the target value over the first few epochs or steps.

Linear Warmup

Linear warmup increases the learning rate linearly from zero (or near-zero) to the target over a fixed number of steps. If your target LR is 1e-3 and you warm up over 1000 steps, step 500 uses lr=5e-4.

from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import LinearLR, SequentialLR

optimizer = optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3)

# Phase 1: Linear warmup from 0.01× to 1× over 5 epochs
warmup = LinearLR(optimizer, start_factor=0.01, end_factor=1.0, total_iters=5)

# Phase 2: Cosine decay for remaining epochs
cosine = CosineAnnealingLR(optimizer, T_max=95)

# Combine them sequentially
scheduler = SequentialLR(optimizer, schedulers=[warmup, cosine], milestones=[5])

for epoch in range(100):
    train_one_epoch(model, optimizer, train_loader)
    scheduler.step()

Warmup is almost universally used when training transformers. The original "Attention Is All You Need" paper used warmup for the first 4000 steps. The reason is that the attention mechanism is particularly sensitive to large weight updates early in training — the softmax operation can saturate, causing gradients to vanish or explode. Warmup gives the model time to develop reasonable attention patterns before the learning rate ramps up.

Gradual Warmup

Gradual warmup is the same concept with a non-linear ramp. Some implementations use square root or exponential curves instead of a straight line. The difference is usually small. What matters is that you don't hit the model with a full learning rate from step one.

Cyclical Learning Rates

Leslie Smith (2017) proposed a radical idea: instead of monotonically decreasing the learning rate, oscillate it between a lower bound and an upper bound. This is the cyclical learning rate (CLR) policy.

Triangular Policy

The simplest CLR policy linearly increases the LR from base_lr to max_lr, then linearly decreases it back, forming a triangle wave. Each cycle takes a fixed number of steps.

from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import CyclicLR

optimizer = optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.001)

scheduler = CyclicLR(
    optimizer,
    base_lr=1e-4,
    max_lr=1e-2,
    step_size_up=2000,    # steps to ramp up
    mode='triangular'
)

# Call scheduler.step() after each BATCH, not each epoch
for epoch in range(50):
    for batch in train_loader:
        train_one_step(model, optimizer, batch)
        scheduler.step()

The intuition: high learning rates help escape local minima and saddle points. Low learning rates allow fine convergence. By cycling between the two, you get both benefits. The key hyperparameters are the bounds (base_lr and max_lr), and Smith proposed a simple method to find them — the learning rate range test.

One-Cycle Policy

The One-Cycle policy (also from Leslie Smith) is a specific training recipe: use a single cycle that ramps the LR up from a low value to a high value over the first ~30% of training, then anneals it back down for the remaining ~70%, finishing with a very low LR phase. Simultaneously, momentum is cycled inversely — high momentum when LR is low, low momentum when LR is high.

from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import OneCycleLR

optimizer = optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.01, momentum=0.9)

scheduler = OneCycleLR(
    optimizer,
    max_lr=0.1,
    epochs=100,
    steps_per_epoch=len(train_loader),
    pct_start=0.3,          # 30% warmup
    anneal_strategy='cos',   # cosine annealing in decay phase
    div_factor=25,           # initial_lr = max_lr / 25
    final_div_factor=1e4     # final_lr = initial_lr / 10000
)

for epoch in range(100):
    for batch in train_loader:
        train_one_step(model, optimizer, batch)
        scheduler.step()   # per-batch stepping

One-Cycle often achieves the same final accuracy in far fewer epochs — a phenomenon Smith called super-convergence. Instead of training a ResNet for 200 epochs, you might reach the same accuracy in 50–70 epochs. The high LR phase in the middle acts as a regularizer (similar to the noise in small-batch SGD) and helps the optimizer explore broadly before settling.

ReduceLROnPlateau

All the schedules above are time-based — they change the LR according to a predetermined calendar. ReduceLROnPlateau is reactive: it monitors a metric (typically validation loss) and reduces the LR only when the metric stops improving.

from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import ReduceLROnPlateau

optimizer = optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3)

scheduler = ReduceLROnPlateau(
    optimizer,
    mode='min',        # reduce when metric stops decreasing
    factor=0.5,        # multiply LR by 0.5
    patience=5,        # wait 5 epochs of no improvement
    min_lr=1e-6,       # don't go below this
    verbose=True       # print when LR changes
)

for epoch in range(200):
    train_one_epoch(model, optimizer, train_loader)
    val_loss = evaluate(model, val_loader)
    scheduler.step(val_loss)  # pass the monitored metric

This is appealing because it's adaptive — it decays the LR exactly when needed, not on a fixed calendar. The tradeoff: you give up reproducibility and predictability. The schedule depends on the training dynamics, which depend on the random seed, data order, and other factors. ReduceLROnPlateau works well for training runs where you don't know in advance how many epochs you need. It's less popular in settings where you want a fixed compute budget.

Linear Scaling Rule

When you increase the batch size, you see more examples per update, so each gradient is a better estimate. The linear scaling rule says: if you multiply the batch size by k, multiply the learning rate by k too. The reasoning is that larger batches reduce gradient noise, so you can afford bigger steps.

For example, if you're using batch size 256 with lr=0.1, and you scale to batch size 1024 (4×), set lr=0.4. This rule works well up to a point — roughly batch sizes of 8K for standard architectures. Beyond that, training becomes unstable and you need more sophisticated techniques (LARS, LAMB, longer warmup).

The linear scaling rule always requires warmup. When you jump straight into a large learning rate with a large batch, the model hasn't developed useful gradient signals yet, and the large update magnitudes cause divergence. The warmup phase gives the model time to learn basic features before the aggressive LR kicks in.

Learning Rate Finder

How do you choose the initial learning rate? Leslie Smith's learning rate range test is the standard technique. Train the model for one epoch while exponentially increasing the LR from a very small value (e.g., 1e-7) to a very large value (e.g., 10). Plot loss vs. learning rate on a log scale.

The plot will show the loss decreasing as LR increases (the model is learning), hitting a minimum, then shooting upward (the model is diverging). Pick the LR where the loss is still declining steeply — typically 1–10× below the minimum loss point. That's your max_lr for One-Cycle, or your initial LR for other schedules.

# Conceptual LR range test (use torch-lr-finder package in practice)
lrs, losses = [], []
lr = 1e-7
optimizer = optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=lr, momentum=0.9)

for batch in train_loader:
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss = criterion(model(batch[0]), batch[1])
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()

    lrs.append(lr)
    losses.append(loss.item())

    lr *= 1.05  # increase by 5% each step
    for pg in optimizer.param_groups:
        pg['lr'] = lr

    if lr > 10:
        break

# Plot lrs vs losses on log scale, pick LR at steepest descent
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
plt.plot(lrs, losses)
plt.xscale('log')
plt.xlabel('Learning Rate')
plt.ylabel('Loss')
plt.title('LR Range Test')
plt.show()

Schedule Diagnostics

How do you know if your schedule is wrong? Here are the signs:

  • Training loss oscillates wildly: LR is too high. Reduce the max LR or add warmup.
  • Training loss decreases slowly and plateaus early: LR is too low. Increase the initial LR or shorten the warmup.
  • Validation loss spikes after a LR drop (step decay): The drop is too aggressive. Use a smaller gamma or switch to cosine annealing.
  • Loss drops sharply after every LR reduction: You were using too high a LR for too long. Consider decaying earlier or using a smoother schedule.
  • Training and validation loss diverge: Not necessarily a schedule problem — but lowering the LR (or adding decay) can act as regularization.

A good practice is to log the learning rate alongside your loss curves. Many issues that look like architecture or data problems are actually schedule problems. When in doubt, plot the LR curve next to the loss curve and look for correlations.

The learning rate schedule is not a set-and-forget detail. It's a core part of your training recipe that interacts with your optimizer, batch size, model architecture, and regularization. Treat it with the same care you give to your model design.
