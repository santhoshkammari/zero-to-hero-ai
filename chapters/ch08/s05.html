Training Debugging & Stability

Chapter 8: Training Deep Networks Section 5 of 8 ~20 min read
TL;DR

Your loss hit NaN at 3 AM and you don't know why. This section is the survival guide. The loss landscape is non-convex, full of saddle points, and actively hostile to naïve optimization. When training goes wrong—and it will—you need a systematic debugging methodology, not random hyperparameter flailing. Overfit one batch first. Check your data pipeline. Inspect gradients. Scale up gradually. Most "model bugs" are actually data bugs. Most "convergence failures" are actually learning rate problems. This section gives you the checklist.

The Loss Landscape

Before we debug training failures, we need a mental model of what the optimizer is actually navigating. The loss function defines a surface over the space of all possible parameter values. For a model with n parameters, that surface lives in n+1 dimensions. A ResNet-50 has ~25 million parameters. You're searching for a good point on a 25-million-dimensional surface. Good luck visualizing that.

What we do know about this surface:

  • It's non-convex. There are many local minima, not just one global minimum. Classical optimization theory—which assumes convexity—doesn't apply directly.
  • Saddle points dominate. In high dimensions, most critical points (where the gradient is zero) are saddle points, not local minima. The probability that all eigenvalues of the Hessian are positive (a true local minimum) becomes vanishingly small as dimensionality increases. This is actually good news: the optimizer isn't getting "stuck" in bad local minima as much as we feared. It's getting stuck at saddle points, and momentum-based optimizers can escape those.
  • Sharp minima ≠ good minima. A sharp minimum is one surrounded by regions of high loss—the loss function curves steeply upward in every direction. A flat minimum sits in a broad basin. Models that converge to sharp minima tend to generalize poorly because even small perturbations to the weights (which happen naturally when you encounter new data) push you to a high-loss region. Flat minima are robust to perturbation and generalize better.
💡 Key Insight

Batch size affects where you land. Large batch sizes produce more accurate gradient estimates with less noise. This sounds good, but the noise from small batches actually helps—it acts like a regularizer that bounces the optimizer out of sharp minima into flatter ones. Large batch training tends to converge to sharper minima and generalize worse, unless you compensate with learning rate warmup and scaling. This is why "just use a bigger batch" isn't free.

Learning rate warmup also connects to this picture. If you start with a high learning rate on random weights, you can catapult into a bad region of the loss landscape early on—a sharp local minimum that's hard to escape. Warmup starts with a small learning rate, lets the optimizer find the general neighborhood of a good basin, and then increases the rate to explore efficiently within that basin.

Loss Not Decreasing? The Debugging Checklist

You've written your training loop, hit run, and... the loss isn't going down. Or it went down for 10 steps and stopped. Or it's oscillating wildly. Here's the systematic approach. Do these in order—each step assumes the previous ones passed.

Step 1: Overfit a Single Batch

This is the single most important debugging technique in deep learning. Take one batch of data—maybe 8 or 16 samples—and train your model on just that batch for hundreds of iterations. The goal: drive the training loss to essentially zero (or whatever the minimum possible loss is for your task).

If your model can't memorize a handful of examples, something is fundamentally broken. You don't have a hyperparameter problem. You have a bug.

# The single-batch overfit test
model.train()
batch = next(iter(train_loader))  # grab one batch
inputs, targets = batch

for step in range(1000):
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    outputs = model(inputs)
    loss = criterion(outputs, targets)
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()

    if step % 100 == 0:
        print(f"Step {step}: loss = {loss.item():.6f}")

# Expected: loss should drop to near 0
# If it doesn't → bug in model, loss function, or data

Common reasons this test fails:

  • Wrong loss function. Using nn.CrossEntropyLoss with one-hot targets instead of class indices (or vice versa).
  • Data-label mismatch. Your inputs and targets aren't aligned—the loader is shuffling them independently.
  • Model output shape mismatch. The model outputs [batch, 10] but you have 100 classes.
  • Frozen parameters. You accidentally called .detach() or set requires_grad=False on something that needs gradients.
  • Learning rate is absurdly small. Like 1e-10. The loss moves, just too slowly for you to notice in 1000 steps.
🔥 Hard-Won Lesson

If you take away one thing from this entire section, let it be this: always overfit one batch first. Every experienced ML engineer does this. It takes 30 seconds and catches 80% of training bugs. Skip it at your own peril.

Step 2: Check Your Data Pipeline

If the overfit test passes, your model and loss function are probably correct. Now make sure the data pipeline isn't the problem. You'd be amazed how many "model" bugs are actually data bugs.

  • Visualize your actual model inputs. Not the raw data—the tensor that goes into the model after all preprocessing. Plot images. Print text tokens. Verify that what the model sees makes sense.
  • Verify labels. Print a few (input, label) pairs. Are labels correct? Are they in the right format (class indices vs. one-hot)?
  • Check normalization. Are pixel values in [0, 1] or [0, 255]? Did you apply the same normalization as the pretrained model expects? A model trained on ImageNet-normalized inputs will produce garbage if you feed it raw [0, 255] pixels.
  • Check for train/validation leakage. Is the same sample appearing in both splits? Are you applying data augmentation to the validation set? (You shouldn't be.)
  • Check data types. Feeding int tensors where float is expected silently does integer arithmetic and kills your gradients.
# Visualize what the model actually sees
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

batch = next(iter(train_loader))
images, labels = batch

for i in range(4):
    img = images[i].permute(1, 2, 0).numpy()  # CHW → HWC
    # Undo normalization if applied
    img = img * std + mean
    img = img.clip(0, 1)
    plt.subplot(1, 4, i + 1)
    plt.imshow(img)
    plt.title(f"Label: {labels[i].item()}")
    plt.axis("off")
plt.show()

Step 3: Check the Learning Rate

The learning rate is the single most impactful hyperparameter in all of deep learning. Symptoms of getting it wrong:

  • Too high: Loss oscillates wildly, spikes, or goes to NaN within a few steps.
  • Too low: Loss barely moves. You think training is "slow" but actually the learning rate is so small that updates are negligible.
  • Just right: Loss decreases steadily with minor fluctuations.

The learning rate finder (Smith, 2017) is the best tool for finding the right ballpark. Start with an absurdly small LR, increase it exponentially over one epoch, and plot loss vs. LR. The optimal LR is typically one order of magnitude below where the loss starts to explode.

# Simple LR finder
import copy

model_copy = copy.deepcopy(model)
optimizer = torch.optim.SGD(model_copy.parameters(), lr=1e-7)

lrs, losses = [], []
lr = 1e-7
lr_mult = (1.0 / 1e-7) ** (1 / len(train_loader))  # sweep to LR=1

for batch in train_loader:
    optimizer.param_groups[0]["lr"] = lr
    inputs, targets = batch

    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss = criterion(model_copy(inputs), targets)
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()

    lrs.append(lr)
    losses.append(loss.item())
    lr *= lr_mult

    if loss.item() > 4 * losses[0]:  # stop when loss explodes
        break

plt.plot(lrs, losses)
plt.xscale("log")
plt.xlabel("Learning Rate")
plt.ylabel("Loss")
plt.title("LR Finder — pick LR ~10x below the minimum")
plt.show()

Step 4: Check Gradients

Gradients are the engine of training. If they're broken, nothing else matters.

  • NaN gradients: Something produced inf or NaN in the forward pass (division by zero, log(0), overflow) and it propagated backward. Use torch.autograd.detect_anomaly() to find exactly where.
  • Zero gradients everywhere: The computation graph is disconnected. Check for .detach() calls, in-place operations that break autograd, or parameters that aren't part of any computation.
  • Exploding gradients: Gradient norms in the thousands or millions. Clip them with clip_grad_norm_.
  • Vanishing gradients: Gradient norms near zero in early layers. Consider skip connections, better initialization, or replacing sigmoid/tanh with ReLU variants.
# Check gradient health after a backward pass
import math

loss.backward()

for name, param in model.named_parameters():
    if param.grad is not None:
        grad_norm = param.grad.norm().item()
        if math.isnan(grad_norm):
            print(f"  🔴 NaN gradient: {name}")
        elif grad_norm == 0:
            print(f"  🟡 Zero gradient: {name}")
        elif grad_norm > 100:
            print(f"  🟠 Large gradient: {name} = {grad_norm:.2f}")
        else:
            print(f"  🟢 OK: {name} = {grad_norm:.4f}")
    else:
        print(f"  ⚪ No gradient: {name} (not in computation graph!)")
# Enable anomaly detection to find the source of NaN gradients
with torch.autograd.detect_anomaly():
    output = model(inputs)
    loss = criterion(output, targets)
    loss.backward()  # raises error with traceback pointing to the offending op
⚠️ Warning

detect_anomaly() is extremely slow—it checks every single operation. Use it only for debugging, never in production training. Once you find the problem, turn it off.

Step 5: Check the Architecture

Sometimes the model itself has a structural bug that doesn't cause an error but prevents learning:

  • Wrong output size. The final layer has 10 units but you have 100 classes. The loss might still compute (with broadcasting weirdness) but the model can't represent the correct answer.
  • Double softmax. You applied nn.Softmax as the last layer and used nn.CrossEntropyLoss, which already applies LogSoftmax internally. The result: softmax of softmax, which squashes gradients to near-zero and makes training incredibly slow.
  • Missing activation. A stack of linear layers without nonlinearities is just one big linear layer. The model has the representational power of logistic regression regardless of depth.
  • Wrong dimension in pooling/reshaping. You flattened along the wrong axis, mixing batch elements together. The model sees scrambled data and learns nothing useful.
🔥 Hard-Won Lesson

The double-softmax trap is incredibly common among beginners. PyTorch's nn.CrossEntropyLoss expects raw logits, not probabilities. If your model ends with nn.Softmax(dim=1) and you use CrossEntropyLoss, training will appear to work but converge extremely slowly to a mediocre solution. Remove the softmax—pass raw logits to the loss.

Step 6: Scale Up Gradually

Once the single-batch overfit works, don't jump straight to the full dataset. Scale incrementally:

  1. 1 batch → Does the model memorize it? (Tests model + loss + optimizer)
  2. Small subset (100–1000 samples) → Does training loss decrease? Does overfitting occur? (Tests learning dynamics)
  3. Full dataset, few epochs → Does loss converge? Is the learning rate schedule appropriate? (Tests data pipeline at scale)
  4. Full run → Final training with all bells and whistles.

Find where things break. If 1 batch works but 100 samples doesn't, you might have data variety issues or a learning rate that only works for memorization. If a small subset works but full data doesn't, you might have corrupted data, memory issues, or need a different learning rate schedule.

Training Instabilities and Fixes

Even after passing all the sanity checks, training can still go sideways mid-run. Here are the common failure modes and what to do about each one.

Loss Goes to NaN

The dreaded NaN. Training was going fine, then suddenly the loss becomes NaN and never recovers. Causes and fixes:

  • Exploding gradients. A single bad batch produces enormous gradients that push weights to inf, which then propagates as NaN. Fix: gradient clipping. torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0).
  • Learning rate too high. The optimizer overshoots so dramatically that weights become infinite. Fix: lower the learning rate. Use a warmup schedule.
  • Numerical instability. Operations like log(0), sqrt(0), x / 0, or exp(large_number) produce inf or NaN. Fix: add epsilon guards. Use log(x + 1e-8) instead of log(x). Use torch.clamp(x, min=1e-8) before division.
  • Mixed precision gone wrong. float16 has a much smaller dynamic range than float32. Large values overflow to inf. Fix: use a proper GradScaler and keep the loss computation in float32.
# Defensive training loop with NaN detection
for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for batch in train_loader:
        optimizer.zero_grad()
        outputs = model(batch["input"])
        loss = criterion(outputs, batch["target"])

        if torch.isnan(loss):
            print(f"NaN loss detected! Skipping batch.")
            optimizer.zero_grad()  # clear any garbage gradients
            continue

        loss.backward()
        # Clip gradients before optimizer step
        grad_norm = torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(
            model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0
        )
        if torch.isnan(grad_norm):
            print(f"NaN gradients! Skipping step.")
            optimizer.zero_grad()
            continue

        optimizer.step()

Loss Plateaus

The loss decreases initially and then flatlines. It's not diverging—it just stops improving. Possible causes:

  • Learning rate too low. The optimizer is taking steps so tiny it can't make meaningful progress. Fix: increase the learning rate, or use a scheduler with warmup-then-decay.
  • Stuck at a saddle point. The gradient is near-zero but you're not at a minimum. Fix: use an optimizer with momentum (Adam, SGD + momentum). Momentum carries you through saddle points. You can also try briefly increasing the learning rate to "kick" the optimizer out.
  • Model capacity bottleneck. The model is too small to represent the patterns in the data. Fix: increase model size. But verify this is the issue first—if the model can't overfit even a small dataset, capacity isn't the problem.
  • Bad feature scale. If some input features are orders of magnitude larger than others, the loss landscape becomes elongated and the optimizer zig-zags inefficiently. Fix: normalize your inputs.

Validation Loss Diverges from Training Loss

Training loss keeps going down. Validation loss goes down initially, then starts going up. This is textbook overfitting—the model is memorizing training data instead of learning generalizable patterns.

  • Add regularization: dropout, weight decay, data augmentation.
  • Get more data. More training examples is the most reliable fix for overfitting.
  • Use early stopping. Monitor validation loss and stop training when it hasn't improved for k consecutive epochs (patience).
  • Reduce model capacity. Fewer layers, fewer parameters. A smaller model that fits the data well generalizes better than a massive model that memorizes it.
# Simple early stopping
best_val_loss = float("inf")
patience = 5
patience_counter = 0

for epoch in range(max_epochs):
    train_loss = train_one_epoch(model, train_loader, optimizer, criterion)
    val_loss = evaluate(model, val_loader, criterion)

    if val_loss < best_val_loss:
        best_val_loss = val_loss
        patience_counter = 0
        torch.save(model.state_dict(), "best_model.pt")  # save best
    else:
        patience_counter += 1
        if patience_counter >= patience:
            print(f"Early stopping at epoch {epoch}.")
            print(f"Best val loss: {best_val_loss:.4f}")
            break

Training Loss Spikes

Loss is decreasing smoothly and then suddenly jumps up dramatically for a few steps before (hopefully) recovering. Causes:

  • Bad data batch. A batch contains corrupted data, mislabeled examples, or extreme outliers. Fix: log which batch caused the spike. Inspect that data. Clean your dataset.
  • Learning rate too high for the current phase. Works fine early in training when gradients are large, but as the model approaches convergence, the same learning rate causes overshooting. Fix: use a decaying learning rate schedule.
  • Gradient accumulation bug. If you forgot to call optimizer.zero_grad(), gradients accumulate across steps and the effective update becomes enormous. Fix: verify your zero_grad placement, especially in custom training loops.

Monitoring Training

If you're only logging "epoch loss" as a single number, you're flying blind. Here's what to track—and why.

What to Log

MetricGranularityWhy It Matters
Training lossEvery batchPer-epoch averages hide spikes. A batch-level plot shows oscillation, spikes, and whether the optimizer is making steady progress or flailing.
Validation lossEvery epoch (or every N steps)The ground truth for generalization. When it diverges from training loss, you're overfitting.
Learning rateEvery stepVerify the LR follows the schedule you think it does. Off-by-one errors in scheduler step counts are extremely common.
Gradient normsEvery stepThe earliest warning sign for exploding or vanishing gradients. If norms grow exponentially, NaN is coming.
Parameter normsEvery epochIf weight magnitudes keep growing, the model is becoming unstable. Weight decay should keep this in check.
GPU memoryPeriodicallyCatch memory leaks. Growing memory often means you're accidentally storing tensors in the computation graph.
 
# Comprehensive training step with monitoring
import torch

def train_step(model, batch, optimizer, criterion, step, writer=None):
    model.train()
    inputs, targets = batch
    optimizer.zero_grad()

    outputs = model(inputs)
    loss = criterion(outputs, targets)
    loss.backward()

    # Log gradient norms before clipping
    total_norm = 0.0
    for p in model.parameters():
        if p.grad is not None:
            total_norm += p.grad.data.norm(2).item() ** 2
    total_norm = total_norm ** 0.5

    torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)
    optimizer.step()

    # Log everything
    if writer:  # TensorBoard SummaryWriter
        writer.add_scalar("train/loss", loss.item(), step)
        writer.add_scalar("train/grad_norm", total_norm, step)
        writer.add_scalar("train/lr",
                          optimizer.param_groups[0]["lr"], step)

        # Log parameter norms every 100 steps
        if step % 100 == 0:
            for name, param in model.named_parameters():
                writer.add_scalar(
                    f"params/{name}_norm",
                    param.norm().item(), step
                )

    return loss.item()
🔥 Hard-Won Lesson

The memory leak trap: losses.append(loss) stores the entire computation graph in your list because loss is a tensor with a grad_fn. Your GPU memory grows every step until it OOMs. Always use losses.append(loss.item()) to store just the scalar value. This one mistake has killed more late-night training runs than any other.

Pre-Training Sanity Checks

Run through this list before every serious training run. It takes five minutes and saves five days.

  • ✅ Model can overfit 1 batch. If it can't, don't even think about the full dataset.
  • ✅ Data loading is correct. Visualize at least 5 samples with their labels. Check after all preprocessing.
  • ✅ Initial loss matches expected value. For classification with C classes and random predictions, the expected cross-entropy loss is -log(1/C) = log(C). For 10 classes, that's ~2.30. For 1000 classes, ~6.91. If your initial loss is far from this, something is wrong with your loss function or output layer.
  • ✅ Gradients flow to all parameters. After one backward pass, check that every parameter that should have a gradient actually has one (non-None, non-zero).
  • ✅ Correct dtype. Model and data should be the same dtype. If using mixed precision, verify your autocast context and GradScaler are set up correctly.
  • ✅ Reproducibility seed is set. Set torch.manual_seed(), np.random.seed(), random.seed(), and torch.cuda.manual_seed_all(). Also set torch.backends.cudnn.deterministic = True if you need exact reproducibility (at the cost of speed).
  • ✅ Model is in train mode. Forgetting model.train() means dropout and batchnorm behave as if you're doing inference. Training will "work" but produce worse results, and the bug is almost invisible.
# Quick sanity check script
import torch
import math

def sanity_check(model, train_loader, criterion, optimizer, num_classes):
    # 1. Check initial loss
    model.eval()
    batch = next(iter(train_loader))
    inputs, targets = batch
    with torch.no_grad():
        outputs = model(inputs)
        initial_loss = criterion(outputs, targets).item()
    expected_loss = math.log(num_classes)
    print(f"Initial loss: {initial_loss:.3f} "
          f"(expected ~{expected_loss:.3f})")
    if abs(initial_loss - expected_loss) > 1.0:
        print("  ⚠️  Far from expected — check output layer!")

    # 2. Check gradients flow
    model.train()
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    outputs = model(inputs)
    loss = criterion(outputs, targets)
    loss.backward()

    no_grad_params = []
    for name, param in model.named_parameters():
        if param.requires_grad:
            if param.grad is None or param.grad.abs().sum() == 0:
                no_grad_params.append(name)
    if no_grad_params:
        print(f"  🔴 No gradient: {no_grad_params}")
    else:
        print("  🟢 Gradients flow to all parameters")

    # 3. Overfit one batch
    model.train()
    for step in range(200):
        optimizer.zero_grad()
        outputs = model(inputs)
        loss = criterion(outputs, targets)
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()

    print(f"After 200 steps on 1 batch: loss = {loss.item():.6f}")
    if loss.item() < 0.01:
        print("  🟢 Overfit test passed")
    else:
        print("  🔴 Cannot overfit — fundamental bug likely")

Debugging and Monitoring Tools

You don't have to build all of this from scratch. These tools exist and are battle-tested.

Weights & Biases (W&B)

The most popular experiment tracking tool. Logs metrics, hyperparameters, system stats, and model artifacts. Produces beautiful dashboards with two lines of integration:

import wandb
wandb.init(project="my-project",
           config={"lr": 1e-3, "batch_size": 32})

# In your training loop:
wandb.log({
    "loss": loss.item(),
    "grad_norm": total_norm,
    "lr": current_lr,
})

TensorBoard

Built into PyTorch via torch.utils.tensorboard. Free, local, no account needed. Slightly more verbose than W&B but completely private—your data never leaves your machine.

from torch.utils.tensorboard import SummaryWriter

writer = SummaryWriter("runs/experiment_1")
writer.add_scalar("loss/train", loss.item(), global_step)
writer.add_histogram("layer1/weights",
                     model.layer1.weight, global_step)

torchinfo

The successor to torchsummary. Shows model architecture, parameter counts, and output shapes at each layer. Catches shape mismatches before training even starts.

from torchinfo import summary

# batch=1, channels=3, H=W=224
summary(model, input_size=(1, 3, 224, 224))
# Shows: layer names, output shapes, param counts, total memory

torch.autograd.detect_anomaly()

Already covered above. Wraps forward and backward passes to check every operation for NaN/inf. Slow but invaluable when you have mystery NaN gradients. The traceback tells you exactly which operation produced the bad value.

Gradient Flow Visualization

Plot gradient norms layer-by-layer to see where gradients vanish or explode:

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def plot_grad_flow(named_parameters):
    """Plot gradient norms per layer. Call after loss.backward()."""
    ave_grads, max_grads, layers = [], [], []
    for name, param in named_parameters:
        if param.requires_grad and param.grad is not None:
            layers.append(name)
            ave_grads.append(param.grad.abs().mean().item())
            max_grads.append(param.grad.abs().max().item())

    plt.bar(range(len(max_grads)), max_grads,
            alpha=0.3, color="c", label="max")
    plt.bar(range(len(ave_grads)), ave_grads,
            alpha=0.3, color="b", label="mean")
    plt.xticks(range(len(layers)), layers,
               rotation=90, fontsize=7)
    plt.xlabel("Layers")
    plt.ylabel("Gradient magnitude")
    plt.title("Gradient Flow")
    plt.legend()
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

  • ☑ Apply the single-batch overfit test as the first step in debugging any training failure
  • ☑ Systematically diagnose why loss isn't decreasing using the 6-step checklist
  • ☑ Identify and fix common instabilities: NaN loss, loss plateaus, overfitting, and training spikes
  • ☑ Set up comprehensive training monitoring (loss, gradient norms, LR, parameter norms)
