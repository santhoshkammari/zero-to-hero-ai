Optimization: Algorithms & Schedules

Chapter 8: Training Deep Networks Section 1 of 8 ~30 min read
TL;DR

Training a neural network = finding good weights by minimizing a loss function. SGD is the baseline; momentum fixes its oscillation problem. Adam adapts per-parameter learning rates using gradient statistics — it's the reason your model converges without careful LR tuning. AdamW decouples weight decay from the adaptive update and is what you should actually use. For learning rate schedules: cosine annealing with linear warmup is the modern default. The optimizer picks the direction; the schedule controls how far you step. Start with AdamW + cosine + warmup — it works for 90% of tasks.

The Optimization Landscape

The loss function of a deep network is a surface in extremely high-dimensional space — millions of dimensions for a typical model. This surface is non-convex: it has saddle points, plateaus, sharp ravines, and many local minima. You're not looking for the global minimum (good luck finding it with 100M parameters). You're looking for a good enough minimum that generalizes to unseen data.

One insight that matters in practice: flat minima generalize better than sharp ones. A flat minimum means small perturbations to the weights don't change the loss much — which is exactly what happens when you evaluate on different data. Sharp minima are brittle. Much of the optimizer and schedule design we'll discuss is implicitly about steering the model toward these flat regions.

That's enough geometry. The point is: optimizer choice matters because different optimizers navigate this landscape differently. Some barrel through saddle points. Some adapt their step size per parameter. Some are better at finding flat minima. Let's see how.

SGD: The Baseline

Stochastic Gradient Descent is the simplest optimizer. Compute the gradient of the loss with respect to each parameter on a mini-batch, then update:

# Vanilla SGD — conceptually
theta = theta - lr * gradient

That's it. It works, but it has problems:

  • Same learning rate for all parameters. A gradient for a rarely-updated embedding and a gradient for a heavily-used dense layer get the same step size. That's wasteful.
  • Oscillation in ravines. If the loss surface is steep in one direction and shallow in another (common), SGD bounces back and forth across the steep direction while making slow progress along the shallow one.
  • Noisy. Mini-batch gradients are noisy estimates of the true gradient. Vanilla SGD amplifies that noise.

SGD with Momentum

Momentum adds a "velocity" term that accumulates past gradients. Think of a ball rolling downhill — it builds up speed in consistent directions and dampens oscillations in noisy directions.

# SGD with momentum
v = momentum * v + gradient        # accumulate velocity
theta = theta - lr * v             # update with velocity

Typical momentum value: 0.9. This means 90% of the previous velocity carries over. The effect is dramatic: oscillations across ravines get damped, and movement along consistent gradient directions gets amplified. SGD+momentum is still the workhorse for training vision models (ResNets, etc.) when paired with a good LR schedule.

Nesterov Accelerated Gradient (NAG)

Nesterov's trick: before computing the gradient, look ahead to where momentum would take you, then compute the gradient at that lookahead position. This gives the optimizer a "corrective" character — it can slow down before overshooting.

# Nesterov momentum (conceptual)
lookahead = theta + momentum * v
gradient = compute_gradient(lookahead)
v = momentum * v + gradient
theta = theta - lr * v

In practice, Nesterov gives a small but consistent improvement over standard momentum. PyTorch makes it a one-flag change:

optimizer = torch.optim.SGD(
    model.parameters(), lr=0.1, momentum=0.9, nesterov=True
)
When to reach for SGD+momentum: Computer vision tasks (image classification, object detection) where you're willing to tune the learning rate schedule. SGD+momentum often finds flatter minima than Adam, leading to better generalization — but it requires more LR tuning effort.

Adam: Why It Works

Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) is probably the most-used optimizer in deep learning. Understanding why it works is more important than memorizing the update equations.

The key insight: Adam maintains per-parameter adaptive learning rates. It does this by tracking two quantities for each parameter:

  • First moment (m) — exponential moving average of the gradients. This is like momentum: it tells you the direction the gradient has been pointing.
  • Second moment (v) — exponential moving average of the squared gradients. This tells you the magnitude of recent gradients for each parameter.

The update divides by √v, which means:

  • Parameters that get frequent large gradients → large v → smaller effective learning rate. The optimizer takes cautious steps for these well-constrained parameters.
  • Parameters that get rare or small gradients → small v → larger effective learning rate. The optimizer takes bolder steps for under-trained parameters.

This is why Adam converges faster than SGD on most tasks without careful LR tuning. It's essentially doing automatic per-parameter LR adaptation.

# Adam update (simplified, for one parameter)
m = beta1 * m + (1 - beta1) * gradient        # first moment
v = beta2 * v + (1 - beta2) * gradient ** 2   # second moment

# Bias correction (explained below)
m_hat = m / (1 - beta1 ** t)
v_hat = v / (1 - beta2 ** t)

theta = theta - lr * m_hat / (sqrt(v_hat) + epsilon)

Bias Correction

Both m and v are initialized to zero. In the first few steps, they're biased toward zero because they haven't accumulated enough gradient history. Without correction, the effective learning rate in early training would be way too small.

The bias correction (m / (1 - β₁ᵗ)) compensates for this. At t=1 with β₁=0.9, the correction multiplies m by 10×. By t=20, the correction is negligible. It's a startup fix — important but not deep.

Default Hyperparameters

The original paper's defaults are still what everyone uses:

  • β₁ = 0.9 — momentum decay for the first moment
  • β₂ = 0.999 — decay for the second moment (slower, so the magnitude estimate is more stable)
  • ε = 1e-8 — prevents division by zero
  • lr = 0.001 — starting point, almost always needs a schedule on top
# Adam in PyTorch — the defaults are sensible
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3)
Common mistake: Using Adam's default LR of 1e-3 for fine-tuning pretrained models. Pretrained weights are already in a good region — a LR of 1e-3 will blast them out of it. Use 1e-5 to 5e-5 for fine-tuning.

AdamW: The One You Should Actually Use

AdamW is not just "Adam with weight decay." It fixes a subtle but important bug in how the original Adam handles regularization.

The Problem with Adam + L2 Regularization

In the original Adam paper, weight decay was implemented as L2 regularization — adding λ * θ² to the loss. This means the weight decay gradient (2λθ) flows through the adaptive learning rate mechanism. The result: large weights that get large gradients also get their decay scaled down by the adaptive term. The regularization is fighting itself.

AdamW: Decoupled Weight Decay

AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) fixes this by applying weight decay directly to the weights, outside the adaptive gradient step:

# Adam with L2 (WRONG way)
gradient = gradient + weight_decay * theta     # decay inside gradient
m = beta1 * m + (1 - beta1) * gradient
v = beta2 * v + (1 - beta2) * gradient ** 2
theta = theta - lr * m_hat / (sqrt(v_hat) + eps)

# AdamW (RIGHT way)
m = beta1 * m + (1 - beta1) * gradient        # no decay in gradient
v = beta2 * v + (1 - beta2) * gradient ** 2
theta = theta - lr * m_hat / (sqrt(v_hat) + eps)
theta = theta - lr * weight_decay * theta      # decay applied separately

The difference seems tiny, but it matters. AdamW produces better generalization because the regularization actually does what you think it does — it pushes all weights toward zero uniformly, regardless of their gradient history.

# AdamW in PyTorch — this is what you should default to
optimizer = torch.optim.AdamW(
    model.parameters(),
    lr=1e-3,
    weight_decay=0.01    # typical range: 0.01 - 0.1
)
Rule of thumb: If someone says "Adam" in 2024, they almost certainly mean AdamW. It's the default optimizer for transformers, LLMs, and most modern architectures. Use torch.optim.AdamW, not torch.optim.Adam.

Optimizer Comparison

Optimizer When to Use Convergence Memory Tuning Difficulty
SGD+Momentum Vision tasks, when you can tune the LR schedule Slower initially, but often better final accuracy Low (1 buffer per param) High — needs LR schedule
Adam Quick prototyping, GANs Fast convergence High (2 buffers per param) Low — works out of box
AdamW Transformers, LLMs, general default Fast convergence + better generalization High (2 buffers per param) Low-Medium
RMSprop RNNs (historical), reinforcement learning Moderate Medium (1 buffer per param) Medium
Adagrad Sparse features (NLP embeddings, recommender systems) Good early, decays too aggressively Medium Low

RMSprop is essentially "Adam without the first moment" — it adapts per-parameter LRs using only the second moment. It was the go-to before Adam existed and still shows up in RL codebases. Adagrad accumulates squared gradients without decay, which means the learning rate monotonically decreases. Great for sparse data, terrible for dense networks where it effectively stops learning after many steps.

Learning Rate Schedules

The optimizer gives you the direction to update. The learning rate controls how far you step. Getting this wrong is one of the most common training failures.

  • LR too high → loss diverges or oscillates wildly. The model overshoots good minima.
  • LR too low → training is painfully slow, and the model gets stuck in the first mediocre minimum it finds.
  • Fixed LR → a compromise that's rarely optimal. You want large steps early (explore the landscape) and small steps later (settle into a minimum).

The answer is almost always: start moderate, warm up, then decay. The schedule automates what you'd do manually if you were watching the loss curve.

Warmup

In the first few steps of training, everything is noisy. The weights are randomly initialized, gradients are unreliable, and adaptive optimizer statistics (like Adam's moment estimates) haven't converged yet. Taking large steps based on these garbage gradients is a recipe for early instability.

Linear warmup solves this: start with a very small LR and linearly increase it to the target LR over a set number of steps.

# Linear warmup (manual implementation)
warmup_steps = 1000
target_lr = 1e-3

for step in range(total_steps):
    if step < warmup_steps:
        lr = target_lr * (step / warmup_steps)
    else:
        lr = decay_schedule(step)  # cosine, step decay, etc.

    for param_group in optimizer.param_groups:
        param_group['lr'] = lr

Typical warmup length: 5-10% of total training steps. For a 100-epoch run on ImageNet, that's ~5 epochs. For fine-tuning a transformer, maybe 500-2000 steps. Warmup is especially important for:

  • Large learning rates
  • Large batch sizes (gradients are more accurate but the effective LR is larger)
  • Transformer models (attention layers are particularly sensitive to early instability)

Cosine Annealing

Cosine annealing is the default modern LR schedule. It smoothly decays the learning rate following a cosine curve from the initial LR down to a minimum (often 0 or near 0).

# Cosine annealing in PyTorch
scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.CosineAnnealingLR(
    optimizer,
    T_max=total_epochs,   # one full cosine cycle
    eta_min=1e-6          # minimum LR
)

# Training loop
for epoch in range(total_epochs):
    train_one_epoch(model, optimizer)
    scheduler.step()

Why cosine works so well: the gradual, smooth decay lets the model spend more time at moderate learning rates (where it's still exploring and making meaningful updates) and slowly settles into a minimum at the end. The tail of the cosine curve is very gentle — the model has many steps to fine-tune into a flat region of the loss landscape.

Compare this to abrupt step decay: the model is cruising at one LR, then suddenly gets a smaller one. The transition can be wasteful — you might spend epochs at a LR that's too high, then jump to one that's too low.

Cosine with Warm Restarts

A popular variant is CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts — the LR decays to zero, then snaps back to the initial LR and decays again. Each restart lets the model escape local minima. The idea is that periodic "shocks" of high LR help exploration.

# Cosine with warm restarts
scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts(
    optimizer,
    T_0=10,       # steps in the first restart cycle
    T_mult=2      # each subsequent cycle is 2x longer
)

Step Decay

The simplest schedule: reduce the LR by a fixed factor every N epochs.

# Step decay: reduce LR by 10x every 30 epochs
scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.StepLR(
    optimizer, step_size=30, gamma=0.1
)

# Multi-step: reduce at specific epochs
scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.MultiStepLR(
    optimizer, milestones=[60, 120, 160], gamma=0.2
)

Step decay was the standard for training ResNets: train at LR=0.1, drop to 0.01 at epoch 60, drop to 0.001 at epoch 120. It's less smooth than cosine but easier to reason about — you know exactly when the LR changes. Still a perfectly valid choice for well-understood tasks.

One-Cycle Policy

Leslie Smith's one-cycle policy is a more aggressive schedule: warm up the LR to a high peak, hold it there, then anneal it down to near zero. The insight: a higher learning rate in the middle of training acts as regularization — it prevents the model from settling into sharp minima too early.

# One-cycle policy in PyTorch
scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.OneCycleLR(
    optimizer,
    max_lr=0.01,
    total_steps=total_steps,
    pct_start=0.3,         # 30% of training is warmup
    anneal_strategy='cos'  # cosine annealing for decay
)

# IMPORTANT: step per batch, not per epoch
for batch in train_loader:
    loss = train_step(model, batch, optimizer)
    scheduler.step()

The one-cycle policy often reaches the same final accuracy in fewer epochs than a traditional schedule. It's particularly effective with SGD+momentum. The high LR phase in the middle is what makes it work — it's essentially exploring more of the loss landscape before committing.

Gotcha: OneCycleLR steps per batch, not per epoch. If you step per epoch, the schedule will be stretched out over your entire training run and the peak LR will come at the wrong time. This is a common bug.

Schedule Comparison

Schedule Best For Complexity Notes
Cosine Annealing General default, transformers, LLMs Low Smooth decay, pairs well with warmup
Step Decay Well-understood tasks (ResNets, benchmarks) Low Requires knowing good milestone epochs
One-Cycle Fast training, SGD+momentum Medium Fewer epochs to converge; step per batch
Linear Decay Fine-tuning pretrained models Low Simple and effective for short training runs
Cosine + Warm Restarts Long training, escaping local minima Medium Periodic LR resets for exploration

LR Finder

Before committing to a learning rate, you can empirically find a good one. The LR finder (Leslie Smith, 2017) is simple:

  1. Start with a tiny LR (e.g., 1e-7).
  2. Train for one epoch, exponentially increasing the LR each batch.
  3. Record the loss at each step.
  4. Plot loss vs. LR.
  5. Pick the LR where the loss is decreasing fastest (steepest downward slope) — NOT the minimum.
import math

def lr_finder(model, train_loader, optimizer, criterion,
              lr_start=1e-7, lr_end=10, num_steps=100):
    """Sweep LR from lr_start to lr_end, record loss at each step."""
    lrs, losses = [], []
    lr_mult = (lr_end / lr_start) ** (1 / num_steps)
    lr = lr_start

    model.train()
    data_iter = iter(train_loader)

    for step in range(num_steps):
        try:
            batch = next(data_iter)
        except StopIteration:
            data_iter = iter(train_loader)
            batch = next(data_iter)

        x, y = batch
        for pg in optimizer.param_groups:
            pg['lr'] = lr

        optimizer.zero_grad()
        loss = criterion(model(x), y)
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()

        lrs.append(lr)
        losses.append(loss.item())
        lr *= lr_mult

        # Stop early if loss explodes
        if step > 0 and loss.item() > 4 * min(losses):
            break

    return lrs, losses

# Usage
# lrs, losses = lr_finder(model, train_loader, optimizer, criterion)
# plt.plot(lrs, losses); plt.xscale('log'); plt.show()
# Pick LR where the curve is steepest (e.g., 1e-3 to 3e-3)
Why not pick the minimum? The minimum loss on the LR sweep usually corresponds to a LR that's too high for stable long-term training. The point of steepest descent is where learning is most efficient — the model is making rapid progress without instability. That's your sweet spot.

The Practical Defaults

If you're starting a new project and don't know what to pick, use this:

import torch
from torch.optim import AdamW
from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import CosineAnnealingLR

# The 2024 default that works for 90% of tasks
model = YourModel()
optimizer = AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3, weight_decay=0.01)
scheduler = CosineAnnealingLR(optimizer, T_max=num_epochs, eta_min=1e-6)

# With warmup (for transformers and large models)
warmup_steps = int(0.05 * total_steps)  # 5% warmup

for step in range(total_steps):
    # Linear warmup
    if step < warmup_steps:
        lr_scale = step / warmup_steps
        for pg in optimizer.param_groups:
            pg['lr'] = 1e-3 * lr_scale

    loss = train_step(model, batch, optimizer)

    # Only step scheduler after warmup
    if step >= warmup_steps:
        scheduler.step()

For specific domains:

  • Computer vision (training from scratch): SGD + momentum (0.9) + cosine annealing, LR=0.1
  • Computer vision (fine-tuning): AdamW, LR=1e-4 to 5e-5, linear decay
  • NLP / Transformers: AdamW, LR=1e-4 to 5e-4, cosine + linear warmup
  • LLM pre-training: AdamW, LR=3e-4, cosine decay, 2000-step warmup
  • Quick prototyping: Adam, LR=1e-3, no schedule. Just get something running.
Production gotcha: When resuming training from a checkpoint, you must also load the optimizer and scheduler state. If you only load model weights, the optimizer's moment estimates reset to zero, and the scheduler restarts from epoch 0. This silently degrades training. Always save and restore: optimizer.state_dict() and scheduler.state_dict().
