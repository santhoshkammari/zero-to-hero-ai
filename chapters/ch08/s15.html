Advanced Training Techniques

Chapter 8: Training Deep Networks Section 15 of 16 6 subtopics
TL;DR

Once you've mastered standard training, these advanced techniques push accuracy further or reduce the data and compute you need. Knowledge distillation compresses a large model's knowledge into a smaller one. Curriculum learning feeds easy examples first, then hard ones — mimicking how humans learn. Progressive resizing trains on small images first for speed, then scales up for accuracy. Multi-task learning shares representations across related tasks. Auxiliary losses inject extra supervision signals to help intermediate layers learn. Self-training uses a model's own confident predictions as labels for unlabeled data. Each technique has clear tradeoffs — this section tells you when each one is worth the added complexity.

Knowledge Distillation

The Core Idea

You've trained a huge model — maybe an ensemble of five transformers — and it's extremely accurate. But it's too slow and expensive for production. You need a small model that runs fast. The naive approach is to train the small model from scratch on the same data. But there's a better way: let the large model teach the small one.

Knowledge distillation trains a small "student" model to mimic the outputs of a large "teacher" model. Instead of training the student on hard labels (0 or 1), you train it on the teacher's soft probability distributions. The insight, from Hinton et al. (2015), is that these soft probabilities contain far more information than hard labels. When a teacher outputs [0.7, 0.2, 0.1] for classes [cat, dog, fox], the student learns not just "this is a cat" but also "cats look somewhat like dogs and slightly like foxes." This inter-class similarity information, encoded in the soft targets, helps the student generalize better than it could from hard labels alone.

The Distillation Loss

The student is trained with a weighted combination of two losses:

Hard loss: Standard cross-entropy between the student's predictions and the true labels. This keeps the student grounded in reality.

Soft loss: KL divergence between the student's softened predictions and the teacher's softened predictions. Both outputs are divided by a temperature T before applying softmax. Higher temperature produces softer distributions — spreading the probability mass more evenly — which reveals more of the teacher's learned relationships between classes.

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def distillation_loss(student_logits, teacher_logits, labels,
                      temperature=4.0, alpha=0.7):
    """
    Combined distillation + hard label loss.
    
    alpha: weight for soft loss (1-alpha for hard loss)
    temperature: softens probability distributions
    """
    # Soft targets from teacher (detach — don't backprop into teacher)
    soft_targets = F.softmax(teacher_logits.detach() / temperature, dim=-1)
    soft_student = F.log_softmax(student_logits / temperature, dim=-1)
    
    # KL divergence, scaled by T² (Hinton et al. recommend this)
    soft_loss = F.kl_div(soft_student, soft_targets, reduction='batchmean')
    soft_loss = soft_loss * (temperature ** 2)
    
    # Standard cross-entropy with true labels
    hard_loss = F.cross_entropy(student_logits, labels)
    
    return alpha * soft_loss + (1 - alpha) * hard_loss

# Training loop
teacher.eval()  # Teacher is frozen
student.train()

for inputs, labels in train_loader:
    inputs, labels = inputs.to(device), labels.to(device)
    
    with torch.no_grad():
        teacher_logits = teacher(inputs)
    
    student_logits = student(inputs)
    loss = distillation_loss(student_logits, teacher_logits, labels)
    
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()

Temperature and Alpha: The Key Knobs

The temperature T controls how much information flows from teacher to student. At T=1 (no scaling), the teacher's output might be [0.99, 0.005, 0.005] — almost a hard label, and the student learns little beyond "this is class 0." At T=4, the same logits become [0.58, 0.21, 0.21] — now the student sees that classes 1 and 2 are equally unlikely, which is useful information.

Typical temperatures range from 2 to 20. Higher temperatures work better when classes have meaningful similarity structure. The weight alpha (typically 0.5–0.9) controls the balance between learning from the teacher vs. learning from true labels. When the teacher is very accurate, higher alpha works well. When the teacher makes mistakes, lower alpha lets the true labels correct for teacher errors.

When Distillation Works (and When It Doesn't)

Distillation works best when the teacher is significantly larger and more accurate than the student. The bigger the gap, the more "knowledge" the teacher has that the student can absorb. In practice, a distilled student typically closes 50–80% of the accuracy gap between a from-scratch student and the teacher.

Distillation works poorly when the student is too small to represent what the teacher knows. You can't distill GPT-4's knowledge into a 2-layer MLP — the student simply doesn't have the capacity. Distillation also struggles with tasks where the teacher's soft outputs don't carry much information — binary classification with highly separated classes, for instance.

Curriculum Learning

Easy Examples First

When you learn a new programming language, you start with "Hello World," not a distributed systems project. Curriculum learning applies this same principle to model training: present easy examples first, then gradually introduce harder ones.

The original paper by Bengio et al. (2009) showed that training on a curriculum — sorted from easy to hard — often converges faster and to a better final solution than training on randomly shuffled data. The intuition is that easy examples establish a good initial representation, and this representation provides a better foundation for learning from hard examples later.

Defining "Easy" and "Hard"

The tricky part is defining difficulty. Several approaches work in practice:

Loss-based. Run the model on the full dataset, compute the loss for each sample, and sort by loss. Low-loss examples are "easy." Re-sort periodically as the model improves — what's hard at epoch 1 might be easy by epoch 10.

Confidence-based. Similar to loss-based, but use the model's prediction confidence. Examples where the model assigns high probability to the correct class are easy.

Data characteristics. For images: smaller objects, less occlusion, and cleaner backgrounds are easier. For text: shorter sentences with common words are easier. For classification: examples far from the decision boundary are easier.

# Simple curriculum learning based on loss
import numpy as np

def curriculum_training(model, dataset, optimizer, criterion,
                        num_epochs=100, initial_fraction=0.3):
    """Train with linear curriculum: start easy, add harder examples."""
    
    # Score difficulty using a pre-trained or randomly init model
    model.eval()
    difficulties = []
    with torch.no_grad():
        for i, (x, y) in enumerate(dataset):
            x, y = x.unsqueeze(0).to(device), torch.tensor([y]).to(device)
            loss = criterion(model(x), y)
            difficulties.append((i, loss.item()))
    
    # Sort by difficulty (ascending = easy first)
    difficulties.sort(key=lambda x: x[1])
    sorted_indices = [idx for idx, _ in difficulties]
    
    model.train()
    for epoch in range(num_epochs):
        # Linear schedule: start with 30%, reach 100% at 60% of training
        progress = min(epoch / (0.6 * num_epochs), 1.0)
        fraction = initial_fraction + (1.0 - initial_fraction) * progress
        n_samples = int(fraction * len(sorted_indices))
        
        # Use subset of data sorted by difficulty
        epoch_indices = sorted_indices[:n_samples]
        np.random.shuffle(epoch_indices)  # Shuffle within selected set
        
        subset = torch.utils.data.Subset(dataset, epoch_indices)
        loader = torch.utils.data.DataLoader(subset, batch_size=32, shuffle=True)
        
        for inputs, targets in loader:
            inputs, targets = inputs.to(device), targets.to(device)
            optimizer.zero_grad()
            loss = criterion(model(inputs), targets)
            loss.backward()
            optimizer.step()
        
        if epoch % 10 == 0:
            print(f"Epoch {epoch}: using {n_samples}/{len(dataset)} samples "
                  f"({fraction:.0%})")

Anti-Curriculum and Self-Paced Learning

Interestingly, the opposite sometimes works too. Anti-curriculum learning — training on hard examples first — can help when your model needs to focus on the long tail of difficult cases (common in medical imaging or rare event detection).

Self-paced learning lets the model itself decide what's easy. At each epoch, the model selects examples it can currently learn from (moderate loss — not too easy, not too hard). This avoids the need for an external difficulty scorer and adapts naturally as the model improves.

Progressive Resizing

Progressive resizing is a simple but powerful trick popularized by fast.ai for image training. You start training on small images (e.g., 64×64), then increase the resolution in stages (128×128, then 224×224, then 384×384).

Why this works: small images train much faster. A 64×64 image has 16x fewer pixels than 256×256, so the forward and backward passes are dramatically cheaper, and you can fit larger batch sizes in GPU memory. The model learns coarse features (edges, textures, basic shapes) quickly on small images. When you then increase the resolution, the model already has good feature extractors and only needs to refine them for finer details.

# Progressive resizing for image classification
import torchvision.transforms as T

stages = [
    {"size": 64,  "epochs": 10, "lr": 1e-3, "batch_size": 256},
    {"size": 128, "epochs": 10, "lr": 5e-4, "batch_size": 128},
    {"size": 224, "epochs": 15, "lr": 2e-4, "batch_size": 64},
    {"size": 384, "epochs": 10, "lr": 5e-5, "batch_size": 32},
]

for stage in stages:
    sz = stage["size"]
    print(f"\n--- Stage: {sz}x{sz} for {stage['epochs']} epochs ---")
    
    transform_train = T.Compose([
        T.RandomResizedCrop(sz),
        T.RandomHorizontalFlip(),
        T.ToTensor(),
        T.Normalize(mean=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406],
                     std=[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]),
    ])
    
    # Recreate data loader with new transform and batch size
    train_dataset.transform = transform_train
    train_loader = torch.utils.data.DataLoader(
        train_dataset, batch_size=stage["batch_size"],
        shuffle=True, num_workers=4, pin_memory=True
    )
    
    # Adjust learning rate
    for pg in optimizer.param_groups:
        pg['lr'] = stage["lr"]
    
    # Train for this stage
    for epoch in range(stage["epochs"]):
        train_one_epoch(model, train_loader, optimizer, criterion)

The total wall-clock time is often 2–3x less than training at the final resolution from the start, with comparable or even better final accuracy. The early small-image stages act as a form of regularization and pre-training, making the model more robust.

One caveat: when changing resolution, batch normalization statistics shift. Some practitioners freeze batch norm layers briefly after a resolution change, or use a short warmup period with a lower learning rate. The transition doesn't need to be complicated — a few epochs at each stage is usually enough.

Multi-Task Learning

Shared Representations

Multi-task learning (MTL) trains a single model on multiple related tasks simultaneously. The key bet: related tasks share underlying structure, and learning them together forces the model to build more general, more robust representations than learning any single task alone.

For example, a model that predicts both the bounding box of a car and its make/model simultaneously will learn richer car representations than a model trained on either task alone. The bounding box task forces the model to learn spatial features; the classification task forces it to learn fine-grained appearance features. Together, they push the shared backbone to be good at both.

Hard vs. Soft Parameter Sharing

In hard parameter sharing, all tasks share the same backbone network and have separate task-specific heads. This is the most common approach — it's simple, reduces overfitting (the shared layers get gradients from multiple tasks), and uses less memory than separate models.

In soft parameter sharing, each task has its own model, but the models are regularized to stay similar. You add a penalty term that discourages the weights from diverging too much. This is more flexible — tasks can learn different representations when needed — but uses more memory and is harder to tune.

import torch.nn as nn

class MultiTaskModel(nn.Module):
    """Hard parameter sharing: shared backbone + task-specific heads."""
    
    def __init__(self, backbone, task_configs):
        super().__init__()
        self.backbone = backbone  # e.g., ResNet without final FC
        self.heads = nn.ModuleDict()
        
        for name, config in task_configs.items():
            self.heads[name] = nn.Sequential(
                nn.Linear(config['input_dim'], config['hidden_dim']),
                nn.ReLU(),
                nn.Linear(config['hidden_dim'], config['output_dim']),
            )
    
    def forward(self, x):
        features = self.backbone(x)  # Shared representation
        return {name: head(features) for name, head in self.heads.items()}

# Example: object detection + classification
task_configs = {
    'bbox': {'input_dim': 2048, 'hidden_dim': 256, 'output_dim': 4},
    'class': {'input_dim': 2048, 'hidden_dim': 256, 'output_dim': 10},
}
model = MultiTaskModel(resnet_backbone, task_configs)

Loss Balancing: The Hard Part

The biggest practical challenge in MTL is balancing the losses from different tasks. If one task has a loss 100x larger than another, it dominates the gradients and the other task barely learns.

Fixed weighting multiplies each task's loss by a manually chosen weight. Simple but brittle — the right weights depend on the data and change during training.

Uncertainty weighting (Kendall et al., 2018) learns the weights automatically by modeling each task's homoscedastic uncertainty. Each task gets a learnable noise parameter σ. Tasks with higher uncertainty (harder to predict) get down-weighted, preventing them from dominating training. In practice, this works well and requires minimal tuning.

class UncertaintyWeightedLoss(nn.Module):
    """Automatically balance multi-task losses using learned uncertainty."""
    
    def __init__(self, num_tasks):
        super().__init__()
        # log(sigma^2) for numerical stability
        self.log_vars = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(num_tasks))
    
    def forward(self, losses):
        """losses: list of scalar task losses."""
        total = 0
        for i, loss in enumerate(losses):
            precision = torch.exp(-self.log_vars[i])  # 1/sigma^2
            total += precision * loss + self.log_vars[i]
        return total

# Usage
mtl_loss = UncertaintyWeightedLoss(num_tasks=2)
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(
    list(model.parameters()) + list(mtl_loss.parameters()), lr=1e-3
)

outputs = model(inputs)
bbox_loss = F.mse_loss(outputs['bbox'], bbox_targets)
class_loss = F.cross_entropy(outputs['class'], class_targets)
loss = mtl_loss([bbox_loss, class_loss])

GradNorm (Chen et al., 2018) takes a different approach: it normalizes the gradient magnitudes from each task to be roughly equal. If one task's gradients are 10x larger than another's, GradNorm scales them down. This prevents any single task from dominating the shared backbone updates.

Auxiliary Losses

Auxiliary losses add extra supervision signals at intermediate layers of the network, not just the final output. The idea is to give deeper layers direct gradient signal, which helps them learn useful representations faster and more reliably.

The most famous example is GoogLeNet (Inception v1), which added classifier heads at intermediate layers during training. These auxiliary classifiers received the same labels as the main output and contributed a weighted loss (typically 0.3× the main loss). At inference time, the auxiliary heads are removed — they're purely a training aid.

Why do auxiliary losses help? In very deep networks, gradients from the final loss can become very small by the time they reach early layers — the vanishing gradient problem. Auxiliary losses inject fresh gradients directly into intermediate layers, ensuring they learn meaningful representations even if the main loss signal is weak at that depth.

class ModelWithAuxLoss(nn.Module):
    """Model with auxiliary classifier at an intermediate layer."""
    
    def __init__(self, backbone, aux_layer_dim, num_classes):
        super().__init__()
        self.backbone_early = backbone[:len(backbone)//2]
        self.backbone_late = backbone[len(backbone)//2:]
        
        # Auxiliary head at the midpoint
        self.aux_head = nn.Sequential(
            nn.AdaptiveAvgPool2d(1),
            nn.Flatten(),
            nn.Linear(aux_layer_dim, num_classes),
        )
        
        # Main head at the end
        self.main_head = nn.Sequential(
            nn.AdaptiveAvgPool2d(1),
            nn.Flatten(),
            nn.Linear(aux_layer_dim * 2, num_classes),
        )
    
    def forward(self, x):
        mid_features = self.backbone_early(x)
        final_features = self.backbone_late(mid_features)
        
        main_out = self.main_head(final_features)
        aux_out = self.aux_head(mid_features) if self.training else None
        
        return main_out, aux_out

# Training with auxiliary loss
main_out, aux_out = model(inputs)
main_loss = F.cross_entropy(main_out, targets)
aux_loss = F.cross_entropy(aux_out, targets) if aux_out is not None else 0
loss = main_loss + 0.3 * aux_loss

Modern architectures like ResNets largely solved the vanishing gradient problem with skip connections, making auxiliary losses less critical. But they remain useful in multi-task setups, very deep networks without residual connections, and situations where you want to regularize intermediate representations.

Self-Training and Pseudo-Labels

Unlabeled Data Is Everywhere

In most real-world scenarios, you have a small labeled dataset and a massive unlabeled dataset. Labeling data is expensive — it requires human experts, time, and quality control. But unlabeled data is practically free. Self-training is a way to exploit that unlabeled data without any additional human annotation.

How Self-Training Works

The process is straightforward. First, train a teacher model on your labeled data. Then, use the teacher to predict labels for the unlabeled data. These predictions are called pseudo-labels. Finally, train a student model on the combination of real labels and pseudo-labels.

The critical decision is which pseudo-labels to trust. If you use all predictions, including the teacher's mistakes, you'll introduce noise that degrades the student. The standard approach is confidence thresholding — only keep predictions where the teacher is very confident (e.g., maximum softmax probability above 0.95).

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def generate_pseudo_labels(teacher, unlabeled_loader, device,
                           confidence_threshold=0.95):
    """Generate pseudo-labels from unlabeled data."""
    teacher.eval()
    pseudo_data = []
    
    with torch.no_grad():
        for inputs in unlabeled_loader:
            inputs = inputs.to(device)
            logits = teacher(inputs)
            probs = F.softmax(logits, dim=-1)
            
            max_probs, predictions = probs.max(dim=-1)
            
            # Only keep confident predictions
            mask = max_probs >= confidence_threshold
            if mask.any():
                pseudo_data.append({
                    'inputs': inputs[mask].cpu(),
                    'labels': predictions[mask].cpu(),
                    'confidence': max_probs[mask].cpu(),
                })
    
    if pseudo_data:
        all_inputs = torch.cat([d['inputs'] for d in pseudo_data])
        all_labels = torch.cat([d['labels'] for d in pseudo_data])
        print(f"Generated {len(all_labels)} pseudo-labels "
              f"from {sum(len(b) for b in unlabeled_loader)} unlabeled samples")
        return torch.utils.data.TensorDataset(all_inputs, all_labels)
    return None

# Iterative self-training
model = create_model()
labeled_dataset = load_labeled_data()
unlabeled_loader = load_unlabeled_data()

for iteration in range(3):  # Typically 2-4 rounds
    print(f"\n--- Self-training round {iteration + 1} ---")
    
    # Train on current labeled + pseudo-labeled data
    train(model, labeled_dataset, num_epochs=20)
    
    # Generate pseudo-labels
    pseudo_dataset = generate_pseudo_labels(
        model, unlabeled_loader, device,
        confidence_threshold=0.95 - iteration * 0.05  # Relax over rounds
    )
    
    if pseudo_dataset:
        # Combine real and pseudo labels
        labeled_dataset = torch.utils.data.ConcatDataset(
            [labeled_dataset, pseudo_dataset]
        )

Noisy Student Training

Google's Noisy Student (Xie et al., 2020) is a refined version that achieved state-of-the-art on ImageNet. The key differences: the student is equal-sized or larger than the teacher (unlike distillation, where the student is smaller), and the student is trained with aggressive data augmentation and dropout while the teacher generates clean pseudo-labels. The noise injected into the student forces it to learn more robust representations than the teacher.

This creates an interesting dynamic: the student can actually surpass the teacher. When you then use the student as a new teacher, the next generation's student improves further. Multiple rounds of this process yield steady accuracy improvements — limited only by the amount of unlabeled data available.

Honest Tradeoffs

Self-training has real limitations. If the teacher is wrong in systematic ways (e.g., consistently confusing two similar classes), pseudo-labels amplify those errors. The confidence threshold helps, but it also biases the pseudo-label distribution toward "easy" examples that the teacher handles well, ignoring the hard cases where additional data would help most.

Domain shift is another risk. If your unlabeled data comes from a slightly different distribution than your labeled data, the teacher's confidence scores may not be well-calibrated. The teacher might be confidently wrong on out-of-distribution examples. Temperature scaling or other calibration methods can mitigate this.

Despite these caveats, self-training is one of the most practically useful techniques for teams with limited labeled data. It's simple to implement, works across tasks and domains, and consistently improves accuracy by 1–5% when abundant unlabeled data is available.
