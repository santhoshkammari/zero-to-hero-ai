Mixed Precision Training

Chapter 8: Training Deep Networks 6 subtopics
TL;DR

Training in full 32-bit precision wastes memory and compute on bits your model doesn't need. Mixed precision training keeps a master copy of weights in FP32 but runs the forward and backward passes in 16-bit (FP16 or BF16), cutting memory roughly in half and unlocking Tensor Core hardware for 2–8× speedups. The catch: small gradients vanish in FP16's limited range, so you scale the loss up before backprop and scale gradients back down before the optimizer step. PyTorch's AMP makes this nearly automatic — three lines of code for a training loop that's faster, leaner, and converges just as well.

FP16 vs FP32

Every number your GPU touches during training lives in a floating-point format. The default is FP32 — 32 bits per number, with 1 sign bit, 8 exponent bits, and 23 mantissa (fraction) bits. That gives you roughly 7 decimal digits of precision and a dynamic range from about 1.2 × 10⁻³⁸ to 3.4 × 10³⁸. It's more than enough for almost every operation in a neural network. The problem is that every one of those 32 bits costs memory bandwidth and compute cycles, and modern models have billions of parameters.

FP16 — half precision — uses 16 bits: 1 sign bit, 5 exponent bits, and 10 mantissa bits. You get about 3.3 decimal digits of precision and a dynamic range from roughly 6.1 × 10⁻⁵ to 65504. That's a much tighter window. But for the vast majority of neural network operations — matrix multiplications, convolutions, element-wise activations — it's plenty. The weights, activations, and most gradients in a well-behaved network sit comfortably within FP16's range.

Memory Savings

The arithmetic is straightforward. Each FP32 parameter occupies 4 bytes. Each FP16 parameter occupies 2 bytes. Consider a model with 100 million parameters. In FP32, the model weights alone consume 100M × 4 bytes = 400 MB. In FP16, those same weights take 200 MB. But it gets better: the activations stored for the backward pass are also halved, and activations often dominate GPU memory. A ResNet-50 training at batch size 32 might use 4 GB of activation memory in FP32. In FP16, that drops to around 2 GB. For large language models with billions of parameters, the savings mean the difference between fitting on one GPU and needing four.

The full picture includes optimizer states too. Adam stores two extra FP32 buffers (momentum and variance) per parameter. In a pure FP32 setup with Adam, a 100M-parameter model needs: 400 MB (weights) + 400 MB (gradients) + 400 MB (momentum) + 400 MB (variance) = 1.6 GB for parameters alone. With mixed precision, the forward/backward pass uses FP16 weights and gradients (200 MB + 200 MB), while the optimizer keeps FP32 master weights and states (400 MB + 400 MB + 400 MB). Total: 1.6 GB. You don't save on optimizer states, but you massively save on activation memory, which is usually the bottleneck.

Compute Speedup

Modern NVIDIA GPUs (Volta, Turing, Ampere, Hopper) contain specialized hardware called Tensor Cores. These are matrix-multiply-accumulate units that operate on small tiles of FP16 data and can accumulate results in FP32. A single Tensor Core can perform a 4×4 matrix multiply-accumulate in one clock cycle. When your matrix dimensions are multiples of 8 (for FP16) or multiples of 16 (for INT8), the GPU can route your operations through Tensor Cores instead of the regular CUDA cores.

The speedup is dramatic. On an A100 GPU, FP32 throughput is 19.5 TFLOPS. FP16 with Tensor Cores hits 156 TFLOPS — an 8× improvement. Even accounting for overhead from casting and memory transfers, real-world training speedups of 1.5× to 3× are typical for convolutional and transformer models. The key requirement is that your tensor dimensions need to be friendly numbers. That's why you'll see people pad their vocabulary sizes to multiples of 8 or 64 — it's not superstition, it's Tensor Core alignment.

💡 Key Insight

Tensor Cores only activate when matrix dimensions are multiples of 8 for FP16 and multiples of 16 for TF32/BF16. If your batch size, hidden dimension, or sequence length doesn't align, the GPU falls back to slower CUDA cores. Always check your dimensions. Padding a vocabulary from 30522 to 30528 costs almost nothing but can unlock significant speedups.

Loss Scaling

Here's the catch with FP16. The smallest positive number it can represent is about 6 × 10⁻⁵. That sounds small, but gradients in deep networks routinely fall below this threshold — especially in the early layers, where the chain rule multiplies many small numbers together. When a gradient falls below FP16's minimum representable value, it becomes zero. This is called underflow, and it silently kills your training. The model appears to train but stops learning in certain layers because their gradient updates are literally zero.

The solution is loss scaling. The idea is beautifully simple: before the backward pass, multiply the loss by a large constant (say, 1024). By the chain rule, every gradient in the network also gets multiplied by 1024, pushing small gradients back into FP16's representable range. After the backward pass but before the optimizer step, divide the gradients by the same constant to restore their true values. The math is exact — you get the same gradients you would have gotten without scaling, except now they survived the FP16 bottleneck.

Static Loss Scaling

Static loss scaling uses a fixed multiplier throughout training. You pick a number — commonly somewhere between 128 and 32768 — and stick with it. It's simple to implement and adds zero overhead. The downside is that no single scale factor is perfect for all phases of training. Too small, and you still get underflow. Too large, and gradients overflow (exceed 65504 in FP16), producing inf or NaN values that corrupt your training. In practice, static scaling works fine for well-understood architectures where someone has already tuned the factor, but it's fragile for new models or experimental setups.

# Static loss scaling — manual approach
scale_factor = 1024.0

for inputs, targets in dataloader:
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    outputs = model(inputs)
    loss = criterion(outputs, targets)

    # Scale the loss before backward
    scaled_loss = loss * scale_factor
    scaled_loss.backward()

    # Unscale gradients before optimizer step
    for param in model.parameters():
        if param.grad is not None:
            param.grad.data /= scale_factor

    optimizer.step()

Dynamic Loss Scaling

Dynamic loss scaling adapts the scale factor on the fly. The algorithm is: start with a large scale factor (e.g., 2¹⁵ = 32768). After each backward pass, check whether any gradients are inf or NaN. If they are, the scale was too large — halve it and skip that optimizer step (the gradients are garbage anyway). If you go a certain number of consecutive iterations without overflow (typically 2000), double the scale factor. This ratchets up to the largest safe value automatically.

Dynamic scaling is strictly better than static scaling for almost every use case. It reacts to the actual gradient distribution in your model, which changes over the course of training. Early in training, gradients tend to be larger and a smaller scale factor is sufficient. As training progresses and gradients shrink, the algorithm increases the scale to keep capturing them. The only cost is occasional skipped steps when overflow is detected, but these are rare — typically less than 0.1% of steps.

⚠️ Warning

When dynamic loss scaling skips a step due to overflow, the learning rate scheduler still advances unless you explicitly handle it. This means your effective learning rate schedule is slightly off. For short training runs this is negligible, but for precise reproducibility, check whether your scheduler increments on "attempted steps" or "successful steps."

BFloat16

Google designed BFloat16 (BF16) to solve FP16's dynamic range problem. BF16 uses the same 16 bits as FP16 but allocates them differently: 1 sign bit, 8 exponent bits, and 7 mantissa bits. Those 8 exponent bits give BF16 the exact same dynamic range as FP32 — from about 1.2 × 10⁻³⁸ to 3.4 × 10³⁸. The trade-off is precision: 7 mantissa bits give you only about 2.4 decimal digits of precision, compared to FP16's 3.3 digits.

Why does this matter? With FP32's dynamic range, gradient underflow essentially vanishes. You don't need loss scaling at all with BF16. Gradients that would underflow in FP16 are perfectly representable in BF16. This simplifies your training code and eliminates an entire class of numerical bugs. No more debugging mysterious NaN losses that turn out to be overflow from an aggressive scale factor.

So when does BF16 beat FP16, and when does FP16 win? BF16 is better for training because gradient underflow is the dominant numerical issue, and BF16 eliminates it entirely. FP16 is better for inference where dynamic range matters less (activations in a trained model tend to be well-scaled) but precision matters more — FP16's extra mantissa bits produce slightly more accurate predictions. For training large language models, BF16 has become the default at companies like Google, Meta, and OpenAI. The convenience of not needing loss scaling is worth the small precision trade-off.

Hardware support is the deciding factor. BF16 Tensor Cores are available on NVIDIA Ampere (A100) and later, plus Google TPUs (which is why Google invented the format). If you're on a V100 or older GPU, you're stuck with FP16. On an A100 or H100, BF16 and FP16 deliver the same TFLOPS, so there's no compute penalty — use BF16 and skip the loss scaling headache.

💡 Key Insight

BF16 is a drop-in replacement for FP16 in almost every training scenario. If your hardware supports it, prefer BF16 — you get the same memory savings and compute speedup but without loss scaling. The only time to prefer FP16 is when you specifically need higher precision (e.g., certain scientific computing workloads) or when your hardware doesn't support BF16.

FP8 Training

The next frontier is FP8 — 8-bit floating point. NVIDIA's Hopper architecture (H100) introduced hardware FP8 support with two variants: E4M3 (4 exponent bits, 3 mantissa bits) and E5M2 (5 exponent bits, 2 mantissa bits). E4M3 has better precision and is used for the forward pass. E5M2 has better dynamic range and is used for gradients in the backward pass. Together, they halve memory and double throughput compared to FP16.

FP8 training is trickier than FP16 or BF16 because 8 bits give you almost no headroom. The representable range for E4M3 is roughly ±448, and for E5M2 it's roughly ±57344. That's absurdly tight. To make it work, each tensor needs its own per-tensor scaling factor that maps its values into FP8's representable range. This is more aggressive than loss scaling — it's calibration for every single tensor, updated every iteration based on the actual distribution of values.

In practice, FP8 training requires careful integration with the framework. NVIDIA's Transformer Engine library handles the per-tensor scaling automatically for transformer architectures. The results are impressive: on H100 GPUs, FP8 training delivers nearly 2× speedup over BF16 with minimal accuracy loss for large language models. But it's still early days. Not all operations support FP8, not all models tolerate the precision loss, and the tooling is less mature. If you're training a standard transformer at scale on H100s, FP8 is worth trying. For everything else, BF16 remains the safer choice.

Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP)

Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) is PyTorch's built-in system for mixed precision training. It handles the hard parts for you: deciding which operations run in FP16/BF16 and which stay in FP32, managing the casting between formats, and performing dynamic loss scaling. You get most of the speedup with minimal code changes.

AMP has two core components. The first is torch.autocast, a context manager that automatically selects the precision for each operation. Matrix multiplications and convolutions run in FP16 (or BF16) because they're safe and benefit most from Tensor Cores. Operations like softmax, loss functions, and layer normalization stay in FP32 because they're numerically sensitive. The second component is torch.GradScaler, which handles dynamic loss scaling — it scales the loss, checks for overflow, and adjusts the scale factor automatically.

Here's a minimal training loop with AMP. Compare it to a standard loop — the changes are minor but the impact is significant:

import torch
from torch.amp import autocast, GradScaler

model = MyModel().cuda()
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=3e-4)
scaler = GradScaler("cuda")

for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for inputs, targets in dataloader:
        inputs, targets = inputs.cuda(), targets.cuda()
        optimizer.zero_grad()

        # autocast handles FP16 casting for eligible ops
        with autocast("cuda", dtype=torch.float16):
            outputs = model(inputs)
            loss = loss_fn(outputs, targets)

        # GradScaler scales loss, calls backward, unscales grads
        scaler.scale(loss).backward()
        scaler.step(optimizer)
        scaler.update()

Let's walk through what happens on each step. The autocast context manager intercepts every operation inside its block and decides the precision. When your model performs torch.matmul or a nn.Linear forward pass, autocast casts the inputs to FP16 and runs the operation in FP16. When it encounters a softmax or a cross-entropy loss, it keeps everything in FP32. You don't specify this — AMP has an internal allowlist and blocklist of operations.

After the forward pass, scaler.scale(loss) multiplies the loss by the current scale factor (default starting value: 2¹⁶ = 65536). Then .backward() computes gradients — all scaled up. scaler.step(optimizer) does two things: first, it unscales the gradients (divides by the scale factor). Then it checks for inf or NaN gradients. If found, it skips the optimizer step. If not, it calls optimizer.step() normally. Finally, scaler.update() adjusts the scale factor — decreasing it if overflow occurred, increasing it if enough clean steps have passed.

For BF16 training, you don't need the GradScaler at all:

# BF16 training — no GradScaler needed
model = MyModel().cuda()
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=3e-4)

for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for inputs, targets in dataloader:
        inputs, targets = inputs.cuda(), targets.cuda()
        optimizer.zero_grad()

        with autocast("cuda", dtype=torch.bfloat16):
            outputs = model(inputs)
            loss = loss_fn(outputs, targets)

        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()

This is cleaner and one of the main reasons BF16 has become the preferred format for large-scale training. No scaler, no overflow checks, no skipped steps. Just cast and go.

⚠️ Warning

Never call scaler.scale(loss).backward() inside the autocast context. The backward pass should run outside autocast — PyTorch handles backward precision automatically based on the forward pass. Nesting backward inside autocast can cause subtle precision issues that are hard to debug.

FP32 Master Weights & Safe Operations

Mixed precision doesn't mean everything runs in low precision. The strategy is deliberate: use low precision where it's safe and fast, keep full precision where it matters for correctness. Understanding which operations need FP32 is the difference between a training run that converges properly and one that mysteriously diverges at step 50,000.

Operations Requiring FP32

Three categories of operations must stay in FP32. First, reductions over large dimensions. Softmax computes exp(x) / sum(exp(x)) over potentially thousands of logits. In FP16, the exponentials overflow easily (e⁹ ≈ 8103 is already close to FP16's max of 65504), and the sum accumulates rounding errors across thousands of terms. Cross-entropy loss has the same issue since it includes a log-softmax. These operations must run in FP32.

Second, normalization layers. Layer normalization and batch normalization compute means and variances over activations, then normalize by dividing by the standard deviation. The mean computation accumulates many small values — classic territory for FP16 rounding errors. Worse, the variance computation involves squaring the deviations, which can overflow. The normalization division can amplify any errors. Keep the mean/variance computation and the normalization step in FP32.

Third, small addition operations near zero. The epsilon term in Adam's update rule (θ = θ - lr * m / (√v + ε)) is typically 1e-8. FP16 can't even represent 1e-8 — it rounds to zero, and you get division by zero. Similarly, numerical stability constants added inside log() calls vanish in FP16. Any operation where the correctness depends on very small numbers needs FP32.

# These run in FP16 — safe and fast on Tensor Cores
# nn.Linear (the Q, K, V projections)
# torch.matmul (attention scores)
# nn.Conv2d

# These stay in FP32 — numerically sensitive
# F.softmax (attention weights)
# nn.LayerNorm (mean/variance computation)
# F.cross_entropy (log-softmax + nll_loss)
# torch.norm (accumulates many squared values)

Master Weight Copy

The master weight copy is the keystone of mixed precision training. Here's the problem: if you store your weights in FP16 and update them with small gradient steps, the update can vanish. Imagine a weight of 10000.0 in FP16. The smallest change FP16 can represent at that magnitude is about 8.0 (FP16 has only 10 mantissa bits). If your gradient update is 0.5, the addition 10000.0 + 0.5 = 10000.0 in FP16 — the update is simply lost. This is called the swamping problem, and it happens whenever the weight magnitude is much larger than the update magnitude.

The solution is to keep a master copy of all weights in FP32. The training loop looks like this: (1) cast the FP32 master weights to FP16 for the forward pass, (2) run the forward and backward passes in FP16, (3) unscale the FP16 gradients, (4) cast the gradients to FP32, (5) apply the optimizer update to the FP32 master weights. Step 5 is where it matters — the update 10000.0 + 0.5 = 10000.5 in FP32 is exact. Then the updated FP32 weights get cast back to FP16 for the next forward pass.

This means you store two copies of the weights: FP16 for compute and FP32 for the optimizer. That's 6 bytes per parameter instead of 4 (FP32 only). But you save far more on activation memory. PyTorch's AMP does this transparently — when you pass your FP32 model into an autocast block, it creates temporary FP16 copies for the forward pass while the original FP32 parameters remain untouched for the optimizer.

#
# 1. model.parameters() are FP32 (master weights)
# 2. Inside autocast, Linear layers cast weights to FP16:
#      fp16_weight = param.half()    # temporary copy
#      output = input @ fp16_weight  # fast Tensor Core matmul
# 3. Gradients are computed in FP16
# 4. scaler.step(optimizer):
#      - Unscale FP16 gradients (divide by scale factor)
#      - Check for inf/NaN
#      - optimizer.step() updates FP32 master weights
#        using the unscaled gradients (cast to FP32 internally)
# 5. Next iteration: new FP16 copies from updated FP32 masters

Convergence Impact Analysis

The natural question is: does mixed precision actually converge to the same solution as FP32? The empirical answer, across hundreds of studies and production systems, is yes — with proper loss scaling and FP32 master weights. The theoretical intuition is that stochastic gradient descent is inherently noisy. The noise from mini-batch sampling dwarfs the noise from FP16 rounding. You're adding a tiny rounding error to an already noisy gradient estimate, and the optimizer's momentum smooths it out.

That said, there are edge cases. Very deep networks (100+ layers) with residual connections can accumulate FP16 rounding errors through the residual stream. Models with very uneven gradient distributions — where some layers have gradients in the thousands and others have gradients below 1e-4 — may need careful per-layer scaling (though dynamic loss scaling usually handles this). Reinforcement learning workloads, which have notoriously high gradient variance already, sometimes show instability with mixed precision.

The practical recipe is simple. Start with BF16 on supported hardware. If you must use FP16, use dynamic loss scaling. Monitor for NaN or inf values in your loss — if they appear, it's almost always a scaling issue. Compare a short training run (a few thousand steps) in mixed precision against FP32 to verify the loss curves track each other. Once validated, run the full training in mixed precision and pocket the 1.5–3× speedup.

💡 Key Insight

Mixed precision is not an approximation trick — it's an engineering optimization. With FP32 master weights and proper loss scaling, the mathematical updates are identical to full FP32 training. The only differences are: (1) activations in the forward pass have slightly less precision, and (2) gradients have slightly less precision. Both of these are completely dominated by the inherent noise of stochastic gradient descent. If your mixed precision run diverges but your FP32 run doesn't, the bug is in your scaling setup, not in the concept.

Here's a complete training loop that puts all the pieces together — AMP, gradient clipping, and proper validation handling:

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
from torch.amp import autocast, GradScaler

def train_one_epoch(model, dataloader, optimizer, scaler, device):
    model.train()
    total_loss = 0.0

    for inputs, targets in dataloader:
        inputs = inputs.to(device)
        targets = targets.to(device)
        optimizer.zero_grad(set_to_none=True)  # slightly faster

        with autocast("cuda", dtype=torch.float16):
            outputs = model(inputs)
            loss = nn.functional.cross_entropy(outputs, targets)

        scaler.scale(loss).backward()

        # Unscale before clipping so clip threshold is in true scale
        scaler.unscale_(optimizer)
        torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)

        scaler.step(optimizer)
        scaler.update()
        total_loss += loss.item()

    return total_loss / len(dataloader)

@torch.no_grad()
def validate(model, dataloader, device):
    model.eval()
    total_loss = 0.0

    for inputs, targets in dataloader:
        inputs = inputs.to(device)
        targets = targets.to(device)

        # autocast during eval too — faster inference, same precision
        with autocast("cuda", dtype=torch.float16):
            outputs = model(inputs)
            loss = nn.functional.cross_entropy(outputs, targets)

        total_loss += loss.item()

    return total_loss / len(dataloader)

Notice the subtle detail: scaler.unscale_(optimizer) is called explicitly before gradient clipping. This is necessary because the gradients are still scaled at that point — clipping scaled gradients would use the wrong threshold. After unscaling, the gradients are in their true scale, and the clip norm of 1.0 means what you expect it to mean. This is a common mistake that leads to either overly aggressive clipping (if the scale factor is large, the scaled gradients look huge) or no clipping at all (if you clip after scaler.step, which already unscales internally). Get the order right: scale → backward → unscale → clip → step → update.
