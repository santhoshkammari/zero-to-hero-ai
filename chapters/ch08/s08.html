Chapter 8: Training Deep Networks 12 topics
TL;DR

Techniques and tools you'll encounter in papers, production codebases, and Kaggle winning solutions. You don't need any of these on day one — but knowing they exist means you won't be lost when someone mentions them.

The previous sections cover 95% of what you need to train deep networks well. Everything below is the other 5% — niche optimizers, memory tricks, scaling frameworks, and tooling. Skim now, bookmark for later.

Weight Averaging & EMA

Stochastic Weight Averaging (SWA) — instead of using the final model weights, average the weights from the last several epochs of training. This tends to land in flatter minima that generalize better — typically a free 1–2% accuracy boost. PyTorch has it built in: torch.optim.swa_utils.AveragedModel. Worth trying whenever you're squeezing out the last bit of performance.

Exponential Moving Average (EMA) — maintain a shadow copy of your model weights that's an exponentially decaying average of the training weights. Use the shadow weights at inference time. Standard practice in diffusion models and many production setups. Smooths out the noise of individual gradient updates without changing training dynamics.

Training Strategies

Knowledge Distillation — train a small "student" model to mimic the soft probability outputs of a large "teacher" model. The teacher's soft predictions carry richer information than hard labels — a cat image scored 0.7 cat / 0.2 tiger / 0.1 lynx teaches more than just "cat." This is how you compress a 10B-parameter model into something that runs on a phone. Hinton's "dark knowledge" paper started the whole field.

Curriculum Learning — feed the model easy examples first, then gradually introduce harder ones. The intuition is that humans learn this way — algebra before calculus. In practice, it helps sometimes (noisy datasets, sequence tasks) and does nothing other times. Try it if convergence is slow, but don't expect miracles.

Progressive Resizing — start training on small images (e.g., 64×64), then increase resolution (128, 224, etc.) as training progresses. Early epochs are fast because small images are cheap. Later epochs fine-tune at full resolution. Popular in fast.ai workflows and competitive vision training. Essentially curriculum learning applied to input resolution.

Memory & Compute Tricks

Gradient Checkpointing — normally, the forward pass stores all intermediate activations for the backward pass. Gradient checkpointing throws most of them away and recomputes them during backprop. You save roughly 60% of GPU memory at about a 20% speed cost. Essential when you're trying to train a model that barely fits in VRAM — and the reason why you can fine-tune 7B-parameter LLMs on a single GPU.

torch.compile (PyTorch 2.0+) — one line of code: model = torch.compile(model). The JIT compiler fuses operations, eliminates Python overhead, and can give you a ~2x training speedup with zero changes to your model code. Still maturing (some models don't compile cleanly), but increasingly the default for any serious PyTorch training. Try it first — fall back only if it breaks.

Exotic Optimizers

Lookahead Optimizer — wraps any base optimizer (usually Adam). It keeps a set of "slow weights" that periodically sync with the "fast weights" the inner optimizer is updating. Think of it as the optimizer occasionally stepping back to check if the fast exploration is heading somewhere useful. Can improve training stability, though it's not widely adopted. Interesting idea, worth a paper read.

LAMB / LARS — layer-wise adaptive learning rate optimizers designed for very large batch training. When you're training with batch sizes of 32K or more, standard Adam struggles — the learning rate that works for one layer can be wildly wrong for another. LARS (for vision) and LAMB (for language) scale per-layer. Used to train BERT and ResNet at massive batch sizes. You'll only need these if you're doing serious distributed training.

Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) — instead of just minimizing the loss, SAM simultaneously minimizes how "sharp" the loss landscape is around the current point. Flat minima generalize better. The catch: it requires two forward-backward passes per step, doubling compute. Consistently improves generalization in benchmarks but the 2x training cost is hard to justify outside competitions and final production models.

Large-Scale Training Frameworks

DeepSpeed — Microsoft's library for training massive models. Its ZeRO optimizer stages shard model states (parameters, gradients, optimizer states) across GPUs so you can train models that wouldn't fit on any single device. ZeRO Stage 3 can train 100B+ parameter models on commodity hardware. If you ever see a paper saying they trained a huge model on "only" 8 GPUs, DeepSpeed is probably involved.

Megatron-LM — NVIDIA's framework for training large Transformers with tensor parallelism, pipeline parallelism, and sequence parallelism. While DeepSpeed focuses on memory efficiency, Megatron-LM focuses on compute efficiency across many GPUs. Many large-scale training setups combine both. You won't need either unless you're training 10B+ parameter models, but knowing the names helps you read the papers.

Experiment Tracking

Weights & Biases (W&B) — the experiment tracking tool you'll wish you'd set up from your very first training run. Log metrics, hyperparameters, system stats, and model artifacts with a few lines of code. Compare runs visually, run hyperparameter sweeps, share results with your team. Free for personal use. Alternatives exist (MLflow, Neptune, TensorBoard), but W&B has become the de facto standard in research and industry. Two lines to integrate: wandb.init() and wandb.log().

When You'll Need These

These are tools you'll reach for when the basics aren't enough. The main sections of this chapter cover 95% of what you need — come back here when you hit the edge cases. A model that won't fit in memory? Gradient checkpointing. Squeezing the last percent? SWA or SAM. Deploying a huge model to a phone? Knowledge distillation. You'll know when you need them.
