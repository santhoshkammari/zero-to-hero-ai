Training Stability & Loss Landscape

Chapter 8: Training Deep Networks 4 subtopics
TL;DR

The loss landscape — the surface defined by your loss function over the space of all possible parameters — determines whether training succeeds or fails. Flat minima generalize better than sharp ones; understanding this geometry explains why SGD with noise outperforms exact gradient descent. Training instabilities (NaN losses, loss spikes, divergence) have specific root causes and specific fixes. Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) explicitly seeks flat minima by optimizing for worst-case loss in a parameter neighborhood, producing models that reliably generalize better. Monitoring loss curves, gradient norms, and the Hessian spectrum gives you diagnostic power to fix training before it fails catastrophically.

Why Loss Landscapes Matter

Here's a question that sounds philosophical but has deeply practical consequences: when your model converges to a solution with training loss 0.01, is that solution any good? Two different parameter configurations can achieve the same training loss but generalize completely differently on unseen data. The geometry of the region around the solution — the loss landscape — determines which outcome you get.

Think of the loss function as a mountain range in extremely high-dimensional space. Your parameters are a single point in that space, and training is a journey across this terrain, seeking valleys (low-loss regions). Some valleys are narrow and steep-sided — a tiny change in parameters rockets the loss upward. Others are broad and flat — parameters can vary substantially without affecting the loss much. The shape of the valley you land in matters more than how deep it is.

Loss Landscape Visualization

You can't visualize a surface in a million-dimensional space, so researchers project it down to 2D. The landmark technique is from Li et al. (2018), who introduced filter-wise normalization. The idea: pick two random directions in parameter space, but normalize each filter (or weight matrix) in those directions to have the same norm as the corresponding filter in the trained model. Without this normalization, the visualization is dominated by scale differences between layers and tells you nothing about the actual geometry.

# Conceptual: loss landscape visualization (Li et al. 2018)
# 1. Start from trained parameters θ*
# 2. Pick two random direction vectors δ₁ and δ₂
# 3. Normalize each filter in δ to match filter norms in θ*
# 4. Evaluate loss at θ* + α·δ₁ + β·δ₂ for a grid of (α, β)

import numpy as np

def filter_normalize(direction, reference):
    """Normalize each layer's direction to match reference filter norms."""
    normalized = []
    for d, r in zip(direction, reference):
        d_normalized = d * (np.linalg.norm(r) / (np.linalg.norm(d) + 1e-10))
        normalized.append(d_normalized)
    return normalized

These visualizations reveal dramatic differences. Wide ResNets show broad, smooth basins. Thin, deep networks without skip connections show chaotic, jagged landscapes with many sharp local minima. This visually confirms why some architectures are easier to train than others — and why skip connections (which smooth the loss landscape) were such a breakthrough.

Mode Connectivity

Mode connectivity is a surprising discovery: independently trained networks (from different random initializations) often lie in the same broad basin, connected by paths of low loss. You can find a curve in parameter space connecting two solutions where the loss stays low along the entire curve. Linear mode connectivity is a stronger property: the straight line between two solutions has low loss everywhere along it. Models trained from the same initialization but with different data ordering tend to be linearly mode-connected, while models from different initializations require curved paths.

This has practical implications. If two models are linearly mode-connected, you can average their weights and get a good model (this is the basis of model soups and weight averaging). If they're not, averaging produces garbage — the midpoint sits on a high ridge between two separate valleys.

Sharp vs Flat Minima

The central claim, supported by extensive empirical evidence: flat minima generalize better than sharp minima. A flat minimum is one where the loss changes slowly as you perturb the parameters. A sharp minimum is one where a small perturbation causes a large loss increase.

The intuition: a sharp minimum is a narrow spike in the loss landscape. The training data happened to create this spike, but the test data (which is slightly different) doesn't. Move parameters slightly — as you implicitly do by evaluating on new data drawn from the same distribution — and the loss jumps. A flat minimum, by contrast, is robust to small perturbations. The model's predictions don't change much when parameters shift slightly, which means they don't change much when the data shifts slightly. Flat = robust = generalizes.

Formally, the "sharpness" at a minimum can be measured by the eigenvalues of the Hessian matrix — the matrix of second derivatives of the loss with respect to parameters. Large eigenvalues mean high curvature (sharp). Small eigenvalues mean low curvature (flat). The largest eigenvalue of the Hessian, often called the spectral norm, characterizes the sharpest direction.

Why SGD Finds Flat Minima

Stochastic gradient descent, by using mini-batches instead of the full dataset, introduces noise into the gradient estimate. This noise acts as implicit regularization — it makes it hard for the optimizer to settle into a sharp minimum because the noisy gradients keep bouncing it out. The optimizer naturally gravitates toward flat minima that are wide enough to tolerate the noise. Larger batch sizes produce less noise, which is one reason why extremely large-batch training can hurt generalization — the noise that helps you escape sharp minima goes away.

This connects to the batch size effect: small batches ↔ more noise ↔ flatter minima ↔ better generalization (up to a point). Large batches ↔ less noise ↔ sharper minima ↔ worse generalization (unless you use other techniques to compensate). The learning rate also plays a role: the ratio of learning rate to batch size (often called the "noise scale") determines how much the optimizer explores. Keeping this ratio constant when scaling batch size is one version of the linear scaling rule.

Edge of Stability

The edge of stability phenomenon, identified by Cohen et al. (2021), reveals surprising training dynamics. For a fixed learning rate η, the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian initially grows during training until it reaches approximately 2/η. Then it hovers at this value — right at the edge where gradient descent would become unstable. The loss continues to decrease, but the sharpness stays pinned at 2/η.

This is counterintuitive. Classical optimization theory says gradient descent with step size η diverges when the curvature exceeds 2/η. But modern neural networks don't diverge — they self-stabilize by implicitly reducing sharpness whenever it gets too high. The training dynamics push the network toward regions where the curvature is exactly at the stability boundary. This self-regulation is one reason why neural networks can train successfully with learning rates that classical theory says should fail.

Training Instabilities

Loss Curve Analysis

Before diving into instabilities, know what healthy training looks like. A healthy training curve shows loss decreasing rapidly at first, then gradually leveling off. The validation loss follows a similar trajectory, slightly above the training loss. The gap between them stays roughly constant or grows slowly.

Overfitting appears as the training loss continuing to decrease while the validation loss starts increasing. The model is memorizing training data rather than learning generalizable patterns. Fixes: more data, stronger regularization (dropout, weight decay), early stopping, or a smaller model.

Underfitting appears as both training and validation loss remaining high and failing to decrease significantly. The model lacks capacity to learn the pattern. Fixes: a larger model, a higher learning rate (you might be stuck in a flat initial region), longer training, or a better architecture.

NaN and Inf Loss

The most dramatic failure: loss becomes NaN (Not a Number) or Inf (infinity). Common root causes:

Exploding gradients: a gradient becomes astronomically large, the parameter update overshoots, the next forward pass produces extreme values, and subsequent operations (like exp or log) overflow to Inf, which propagates to NaN. Fix: gradient clipping (torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_), lower learning rate, or better initialization.

Numerical instability in softmax or log: computing log(softmax(x)) when softmax produces values extremely close to zero. The log of zero is -Inf. Fix: use log_softmax (which computes both operations in a numerically stable way) instead of log(softmax(x)).

Division by zero: normalizing by a standard deviation that's zero, or dividing by a sum that's zero. Fix: add epsilon (1e-8) to denominators. Check your batch normalization when batch size is 1 (variance is zero).

FP16 overflow: in mixed-precision training, FP16's max value is ~65,504. Large activations or gradients exceed this and become Inf. Fix: use BF16 instead (which has the same range as FP32), or ensure loss scaling is working correctly.

# Debugging NaN loss — systematic approach
def train_step_debug(model, batch, optimizer):
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    output = model(batch)

    # Check for NaN in forward pass output
    if torch.isnan(output).any():
        print("NaN in model output!")
        for name, param in model.named_parameters():
            if torch.isnan(param).any():
                print(f"  NaN in parameter: {name}")
        return None

    loss = loss_fn(output, batch.target)
    if torch.isnan(loss):
        print(f"NaN loss! Output range: [{output.min():.4f}, {output.max():.4f}]")
        return None

    loss.backward()

    # Check gradient norms before clipping
    total_norm = torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)
    if torch.isnan(total_norm):
        print("NaN in gradients!")
        return None
    print(f"Gradient norm: {total_norm:.4f}")

    optimizer.step()
    return loss.item()

Loss Spikes

Loss spikes — sudden, temporary jumps in loss that recover after a few steps — are common in large-scale training. They're often caused by: bad data batches (corrupted samples, extremely unusual examples), sudden shifts in data distribution (when using curriculum learning or data mixing), or numerical edge cases triggered by specific input patterns.

For large language model training, loss spikes are somewhat expected and usually not catastrophic if they recover quickly. Teams often log the specific batches that caused spikes for investigation. If a spike doesn't recover within 50–100 steps, it may indicate a deeper problem — consider rolling back to a recent checkpoint.

Gradient Norm Monitoring

Tracking the gradient norm over training is one of the most informative diagnostics. A healthy training run shows a gradient norm that starts moderate, may increase during a warm-up phase, and then gradually decreases as the model converges. Sudden spikes in gradient norm often precede loss spikes by a few steps. A steadily increasing gradient norm is a warning sign of impending explosion. A gradient norm that drops to near-zero indicates vanishing gradients — the model has stopped learning effectively.

# Log gradient norms per layer for diagnostics
def log_gradient_norms(model, step):
    norms = {}
    for name, param in model.named_parameters():
        if param.grad is not None:
            norms[name] = param.grad.norm().item()

    # Total norm
    total = sum(n**2 for n in norms.values()) ** 0.5

    # Log to your tracking system (wandb, tensorboard, etc.)
    wandb.log({
        "grad_norm/total": total,
        **{f"grad_norm/{k}": v for k, v in norms.items()}
    }, step=step)

    return total

Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM)

If flat minima generalize better, why not directly optimize for flatness? That's exactly what Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) does.

The idea: instead of minimizing the loss at the current parameters, minimize the worst-case loss in a neighborhood around the current parameters. Formally, SAM solves:

# SAM objective:
# min_θ  max_{||ε|| ≤ ρ}  L(θ + ε)
#
# Instead of: "find parameters where the loss is low"
# SAM asks: "find parameters where the loss is low EVEN IF
#            parameters are perturbed by up to ρ"

If the loss is low at θ but a tiny perturbation ε sends it skyrocketing, SAM penalizes that solution. It naturally favors flat regions where the loss is uniformly low across a neighborhood.

How SAM Works in Practice

Computing the exact worst-case perturbation is intractable. SAM approximates it with a single gradient ascent step: the worst perturbation direction is approximately the gradient direction (the direction of steepest loss increase), scaled to norm ρ.

import torch

class SAM(torch.optim.Optimizer):
    """Simplified SAM wrapper around a base optimizer."""

    def __init__(self, params, base_optimizer, rho=0.05):
        defaults = dict(rho=rho)
        super().__init__(params, defaults)
        self.base_optimizer = base_optimizer

    @torch.no_grad()
    def first_step(self):
        """Compute ε = ρ * grad / ||grad|| and perturb parameters."""
        grad_norm = self._grad_norm()
        for group in self.param_groups:
            scale = group['rho'] / (grad_norm + 1e-12)
            for p in group['params']:
                if p.grad is None:
                    continue
                # Save original param and compute perturbation
                self.state[p]['old_p'] = p.data.clone()
                epsilon = p.grad * scale
                p.add_(epsilon)  # θ → θ + ε (ascent step)

    @torch.no_grad()
    def second_step(self):
        """Restore parameters and apply base optimizer update."""
        for group in self.param_groups:
            for p in group['params']:
                if p.grad is None:
                    continue
                p.data = self.state[p]['old_p']  # θ + ε → θ
        self.base_optimizer.step()  # standard update with SAM gradient

    def _grad_norm(self):
        norm = torch.norm(torch.stack([
            p.grad.norm() for group in self.param_groups
            for p in group['params'] if p.grad is not None
        ]))
        return norm

# Training loop with SAM
optimizer = SAM(model.parameters(),
                torch.optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.1, momentum=0.9),
                rho=0.05)

for batch in dataloader:
    # First forward-backward: compute gradient at θ
    loss = criterion(model(batch.x), batch.y)
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.first_step()     # perturb θ → θ + ε
    optimizer.zero_grad()

    # Second forward-backward: compute gradient at θ + ε
    loss_sam = criterion(model(batch.x), batch.y)
    loss_sam.backward()
    optimizer.second_step()    # restore θ, update with SAM gradient
    optimizer.zero_grad()

The two-step process means SAM requires roughly twice the computation per step (two forward-backward passes). In practice, the improved generalization often outweighs this cost — SAM consistently improves test accuracy by 0.5–2% across vision and language tasks, which is substantial in competitive settings.

ASAM and Adaptive Variants

Adaptive SAM (ASAM) addresses a limitation of vanilla SAM: the perturbation radius ρ is the same for all parameters, regardless of their scale. ASAM normalizes the perturbation by each parameter's magnitude, so that large parameters get proportionally larger perturbations. This makes SAM less sensitive to the choice of ρ and generally improves results.

Other variants include LookSAM (which amortizes the cost by computing the SAM perturbation every k steps instead of every step) and GSAM (which adds a term encouraging the gradient of the sharpness to align with the gradient of the loss, promoting convergence toward flatter regions more efficiently).

The practical takeaway: monitoring the loss landscape isn't just academic curiosity — it's a diagnostic superpower. When training goes wrong, the first thing to check is the gradient norm trajectory. If you want reliably better generalization, SAM is one of the simplest techniques to add — it's architecture-agnostic, optimizer-agnostic, and empirically effective across domains. The 2× compute cost is the only real downside, and for final training runs where quality matters, it's often worth it.
