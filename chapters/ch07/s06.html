Gradient Problems

Chapter 7: Deep Learning Foundations Section 6 of 8 2 subtopics
TL;DR

Backpropagation multiplies gradients across every layer. In deep networks, those multiplied values either shrink to zero (vanishing gradients) or blow up to infinity (exploding gradients). Both problems make training impossible — early layers either stop learning or produce NaN. The fixes are surprisingly simple: use activations that don't squash gradients (ReLU), clip gradients that get too large, initialize weights carefully, and add skip connections so gradients have a shortcut through the network.

Why Can't We Just Stack 50 Layers?

Here's a question that confused researchers for decades. A network with 5 hidden layers can learn more complex patterns than one with 2 layers. Surely a network with 50 layers would be even better? In theory, yes. In practice, for most of deep learning's early history, adding more layers made networks worse. Not slightly worse — catastrophically worse. The network would sit there, weights barely changing, loss refusing to budge. Or the loss would suddenly explode to NaN on step 37.

The culprit is backpropagation itself — specifically, how the chain rule interacts with deep networks. Understanding this failure mode is essential before you can understand why modern architectures like ResNets actually work.

Vanishing Gradients

The Chain Rule Multiplies — Over and Over

Recall how backpropagation works. To compute how the loss changes with respect to a weight in layer 1, you apply the chain rule through every layer between the output and that weight. For a 50-layer network, the gradient for the first layer looks roughly like this:

∂Loss/∂W₁ = ∂Loss/∂a₅₀ · ∂a₅₀/∂a₄₉ · ∂a₄₉/∂a₄₈ · ... · ∂a₂/∂a₁ · ∂a₁/∂W₁

That's a product of ~50 terms. Each term is the local gradient at one layer — the derivative of that layer's activation function times the weights. If each of those local gradients is even slightly less than 1, the product shrinks exponentially. That's the vanishing gradient problem.

Cause: Saturating Activations

The sigmoid function was the default activation for years. Its output is a nice smooth curve between 0 and 1. But look at its derivative: σ'(x) = σ(x) · (1 − σ(x)). The maximum value of this derivative is 0.25, and it occurs only when x = 0 (where σ(x) = 0.5). Everywhere else, the derivative is smaller than 0.25.

Now imagine backpropagating through 50 layers, all using sigmoid activations. In the best possible case — every single neuron sitting right at its sweet spot — the gradient gets multiplied by 0.25 at each layer:

# Best-case gradient magnitude after 50 sigmoid layers
gradient_50_layers = 0.25 ** 50

print(f"After 50 layers: {0.25 ** 50:.2e}")
# After 50 layers: 7.89e-31

print(f"After 20 layers: {0.25 ** 20:.2e}")
# After 20 layers: 9.09e-13

print(f"After 10 layers: {0.25 ** 10:.2e}")
# After 10 layers: 9.54e-07

Read that again. After just 10 layers, the gradient reaching layer 1 is less than one millionth of the gradient at the output. After 50 layers, it's 10⁻³¹ — a number so small that 32-bit floating point rounds it to exactly zero. The first layer receives literally no learning signal. Its weights might as well be frozen.

Sigmoid/Tanh Gradient Shrinkage

Tanh is slightly better than sigmoid — its derivative peaks at 1.0 when x = 0. But in practice, neurons don't sit perfectly at x = 0. Once the input drifts even a little, tanh'(x) drops below 1. For typical inputs, you're looking at derivatives around 0.2–0.8. Multiply 50 values of 0.5 together and you get 2⁻⁵⁰ ≈ 8.9 × 10⁻¹⁶. Still vanishingly small.

Both sigmoid and tanh are saturating activations — when inputs become very positive or very negative, the output flatlines and the gradient approaches zero. A neuron in this state is "saturated." It has effectively stopped learning. In deep networks, saturation in even a few layers is enough to kill gradient flow to the earlier layers entirely.

⚠️ The Real Killer

The 0.25 calculation is the best case for sigmoid. In practice, many neurons saturate (inputs far from zero), making their local gradient nearly zero. The actual gradient reaching early layers is often far smaller than 0.25⁵⁰.

Effect on Deep Networks

Vanishing gradients don't just slow training — they create an asymmetry. The last few layers, closest to the loss, receive healthy gradients and learn quickly. The first few layers, farthest from the loss, receive near-zero gradients and barely change. The network ends up with well-trained final layers sitting on top of essentially random early layers. It's like building a penthouse on a foundation of sand.

This is why, for most of the 1990s and 2000s, "deep" meant 3–5 layers. Anything deeper simply wouldn't train. The field was stuck — not because the architectures were wrong, but because the math of gradient flow broke down at depth.

Solutions: ReLU and Skip Connections

The first breakthrough was embarrassingly simple. ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) computes f(x) = max(0, x). Its derivative is either 0 (for negative inputs) or 1 (for positive inputs). There's no squashing. When a ReLU neuron is active, it passes the gradient through unchanged — a multiplier of exactly 1. Chain 50 layers of active ReLUs and the gradient doesn't shrink at all: 1⁵⁰ = 1.

# Gradient flow comparison
import torch

# Sigmoid: gradient shrinks exponentially
x = torch.zeros(1, requires_grad=True)
y = x
for _ in range(50):
    y = torch.sigmoid(y)
y.backward()
print(f"Sigmoid after 50 layers: {x.grad.item():.2e}")
# ~7.89e-31 (or underflows to 0.0)

# ReLU: gradient passes through when active
x = torch.ones(1, requires_grad=True)  # positive, so ReLU is active
y = x
for _ in range(50):
    y = torch.relu(y)
y.backward()
print(f"ReLU after 50 layers:    {x.grad.item():.2e}")
# 1.00e+00 — gradient preserved perfectly

ReLU has a tradeoff: the "dead neuron" problem. If a neuron's input is always negative, its gradient is always zero and it never updates. Variants like Leaky ReLU (small positive slope for negative inputs) address this, but vanilla ReLU remains the workhorse of deep learning precisely because it doesn't squash gradients.

The second breakthrough was the skip connection (also called a residual connection), introduced in ResNets. Instead of computing y = f(x), you compute y = f(x) + x. That "+ x" is the skip connection — it adds the layer's input directly to its output. Why does this help? During backpropagation, the gradient through a skip connection is simply 1 (the derivative of x with respect to x). Even if f(x) has vanishing gradients, the gradient still flows through the skip path undiminished:

# Without skip connection
∂y/∂x = ∂f(x)/∂x                    # can vanish

# With skip connection: y = f(x) + x
∂y/∂x = ∂f(x)/∂x + 1                # always at least 1

This is why ResNets can train networks with 152 layers — even 1,000+ layers. The skip connections create a "gradient highway" that lets the learning signal reach early layers regardless of what the intermediate layers are doing. The network can always fall back to the identity function (f(x) = 0, so y = x) at any layer, making it impossible for added depth to hurt performance.

Exploding Gradients

Cause: Large Weight Magnitudes

Vanishing gradients happen when local gradients are less than 1. The opposite problem — exploding gradients — happens when they're greater than 1. If each layer amplifies the gradient by even a factor of 1.5, watch what happens across 50 layers:

print(f"1.5^50 = {1.5 ** 50:.2e}")
# 1.5^50 = 6.38e+08

print(f"2.0^50 = {2.0 ** 50:.2e}")
# 2.0^50 = 1.13e+15

print(f"3.0^50 = {3.0 ** 50:.2e}")
# 3.0^50 = 7.18e+23

With a gradient multiplier of just 1.5 per layer, the gradient reaching the first layer is 638 million times larger than the gradient at the output. With a multiplier of 2, it's a quadrillion. The weight update becomes enormous, the weights jump to absurd values, and on the next forward pass the numbers overflow to infinity or NaN. Training collapses instantly.

This typically happens when weight matrices have large eigenvalues. If you initialize weights too large, or if the network's architecture encourages weight growth, gradients will multiply faster than they should. In recurrent neural networks (RNNs), exploding gradients are especially brutal because the same weight matrix gets multiplied by itself at every time step — it's literally matrix exponentiation.

Numerical Overflow

Modern hardware uses 32-bit floating point by default. The largest representable value in float32 is about 3.4 × 10³⁸. The smallest positive value is about 1.2 × 10⁻³⁸. When exploding gradients push values beyond these limits, you get inf or NaN — and once a single NaN enters the computation, it poisons everything it touches. Every subsequent operation produces NaN. The loss displays as nan, and training is over.

🔥 Spotting the Problem

If your loss suddenly jumps to nan or inf after training normally for a while, exploding gradients are the most likely cause. If your loss plateaus and early-layer weights barely change, vanishing gradients are the culprit.

Solutions: Gradient Clipping

Gradient clipping is the simplest fix for exploding gradients: if the gradient norm exceeds a threshold, scale it down. You're putting a ceiling on how large gradients can be. The most common variant clips the global norm of all gradients:

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

model = nn.Sequential(
    nn.Linear(256, 256),
    nn.ReLU(),
    nn.Linear(256, 256),
    nn.ReLU(),
    nn.Linear(256, 10),
)

optimizer = torch.optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.01)

# Training loop with gradient clipping
for inputs, targets in dataloader:
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss = criterion(model(inputs), targets)
    loss.backward()

    # Clip gradients: if total norm > 1.0, scale all gradients down
    torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)

    optimizer.step()

The clip_grad_norm_ call computes the total L2 norm across all parameter gradients. If that norm exceeds max_norm, every gradient is scaled by max_norm / total_norm. The gradient direction is preserved — only the magnitude is reduced. Typical max_norm values range from 0.5 to 5.0, with 1.0 being a common starting point.

Proper Initialization

Prevention is better than cure. Proper weight initialization sets the initial weights so that signals neither amplify nor shrink as they pass through layers. The two most common schemes are:

Xavier/Glorot initialization — designed for sigmoid and tanh activations. Draws weights from a distribution with variance 2 / (fan_in + fan_out), where fan_in is the number of input units and fan_out is the number of output units. This keeps the variance of activations roughly constant across layers.

He/Kaiming initialization — designed for ReLU activations. Uses variance 2 / fan_in. The factor of 2 compensates for the fact that ReLU zeroes out half the inputs (negative values), which would otherwise halve the variance at each layer.

import torch.nn as nn

# Xavier initialization (for sigmoid/tanh)
layer = nn.Linear(256, 256)
nn.init.xavier_uniform_(layer.weight)

# He/Kaiming initialization (for ReLU)
layer = nn.Linear(256, 256)
nn.init.kaiming_normal_(layer.weight, nonlinearity='relu')

# PyTorch default for nn.Linear is Kaiming uniform
# Usually you only need to change this for custom architectures

Monitoring Gradients in Practice

You shouldn't guess whether gradients are vanishing or exploding. Measure them. Here's a practical PyTorch utility that hooks into your model and tracks gradient statistics during training:

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

def monitor_gradients(model):
    """Register hooks that print gradient stats per layer after backward()."""
    gradient_stats = {}

    def hook_fn(name):
        def hook(grad):
            gradient_stats[name] = {
                'mean': grad.abs().mean().item(),
                'max':  grad.abs().max().item(),
                'norm': grad.norm().item(),
            }
        return hook

    # Attach hooks to all weight parameters
    handles = []
    for name, param in model.named_parameters():
        if param.requires_grad:
            handle = param.register_hook(hook_fn(name))
            handles.append(handle)

    return gradient_stats, handles


# Example: build a 20-layer network and inspect gradient flow
layers = []
for _ in range(20):
    layers.extend([nn.Linear(64, 64), nn.Sigmoid()])
layers.append(nn.Linear(64, 1))
deep_model = nn.Sequential(*layers)

grad_stats, handles = monitor_gradients(deep_model)

# Run one forward + backward pass
x = torch.randn(32, 64)
target = torch.randn(32, 1)
loss = nn.MSELoss()(deep_model(x), target)
loss.backward()

# Print gradient stats per layer (first and last 3)
print("Layer Name                       | Mean Grad  | Max Grad   | Norm")
print("-" * 72)
for name, stats in list(grad_stats.items())[:3]:
    print(f"{name:35s}| {stats['mean']:.2e} | {stats['max']:.2e} | {stats['norm']:.2e}")
print("...")
for name, stats in list(grad_stats.items())[-3:]:
    print(f"{name:35s}| {stats['mean']:.2e} | {stats['max']:.2e} | {stats['norm']:.2e}")

# Clean up hooks
for h in handles:
    h.remove()

When you run this with sigmoid activations and 20 layers, you'll see the early-layer gradient means hovering around 10⁻¹⁰ or smaller, while the final layers show values around 10⁻² to 10⁻¹. The ratio tells you exactly how badly gradients are vanishing. Switch the activation to ReLU and the gap shrinks dramatically.

💡 Practical Rule of Thumb

If gradient norms differ by more than ~100× between the first and last layers, you likely have a gradient flow problem. Healthy networks keep gradient magnitudes within 1–2 orders of magnitude across all layers.

Putting It All Together

The gradient problem is really two sides of one coin: the chain rule multiplies local gradients, and multiplication amplifies small deviations exponentially. Anything less than 1 vanishes. Anything greater than 1 explodes. Modern deep learning works because we've learned to keep those multipliers close to 1: ReLU gives exactly 1 for active neurons, skip connections add a guaranteed 1 to the gradient path, proper initialization keeps weight magnitudes in a stable range, and gradient clipping handles the occasional spike.

These aren't just implementation details. They're the reason the "deep" in deep learning became possible. Without them, we'd still be stuck at 5 layers.
