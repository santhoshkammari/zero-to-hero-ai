Activation Functions

Chapter 7: Deep Learning Foundations 10 subtopics
TL;DR

Activation functions are the nonlinear gatekeepers in a neural network — without them, stacking layers is just multiplying matrices together, which collapses into a single linear transformation. The field evolved from sigmoid (biologically inspired but slow to train) to tanh (zero-centered but still saturating) to ReLU (fast and sparse but sometimes dead) to modern smooth activations like GELU and Swish that power today's transformers. Each was invented to fix a specific training problem. This section tells that story, covers softmax for classification outputs, explains how to choose an activation, and dives into Gumbel-Softmax for the tricky case of discrete variables.

Why Activation Functions Exist

Start with the simplest possible neural network: one layer that computes y = Wx + b. That's a linear function. Stack two of them: y = W₂(W₁x + b₁) + b₂. Expand it, and you get y = W₂W₁x + W₂b₁ + b₂. That's still a linear function — just with different constants. You could replace those two layers with a single matrix multiply and get the same result. Ten layers, a hundred layers — doesn't matter. Without nonlinearity, depth buys you nothing.

An activation function is the nonlinear function you apply after each layer's linear transformation. It's what allows a neural network to approximate any continuous function (the universal approximation theorem), to model curves instead of flat planes, to separate data that isn't linearly separable. The choice of activation function affects training speed, gradient flow, and what kinds of functions the network can represent efficiently.

Let's trace the evolution. Each activation function was invented because the previous one had a concrete, measurable problem.

Sigmoid

Formula & Intuition

The sigmoid function (also called the logistic function) squashes any real number into the range (0, 1):

σ(x) = 1 / (1 + e⁻ˣ)

Think of it as a soft switch. Feed in a very negative number, you get something close to 0. Feed in a very positive number, you get something close to 1. Around zero, the transition is smooth and S-shaped. Biologically, it was inspired by the firing rate of neurons — they're either mostly off or mostly on, with a smooth transition in between.

The Problems

Saturation and vanishing gradients. Look at the curve. When x is very positive or very negative, the function is nearly flat. The derivative is close to zero. During backpropagation, gradients get multiplied through layers — if each layer multiplies by a number near zero, the gradient shrinks exponentially. By the time it reaches the early layers, it's effectively zero. The network can't learn. This is the vanishing gradient problem, and it's the single biggest reason sigmoid fell out of favor for hidden layers.

Non-zero centered outputs. Sigmoid outputs are always positive (between 0 and 1). This means the gradients on the weights of the next layer are either all positive or all negative (depending on the upstream gradient's sign), forcing updates to zig-zag instead of heading straight toward the optimum. It's not catastrophic, but it slows convergence.

Expensive exponent. Computing e⁻ˣ is slower than a simple comparison. For a single neuron this is negligible, but in a network with millions of neurons, it adds up.

Where Sigmoid Still Belongs

Sigmoid is the right choice for the output layer of a binary classifier. When you need a probability — "what's the chance this email is spam?" — you want a number between 0 and 1. That's exactly what sigmoid gives you. Paired with binary cross-entropy loss, it's mathematically clean. Just don't use it in hidden layers.

Tanh

Formula & Intuition

The tanh (hyperbolic tangent) function is sigmoid's zero-centered cousin:

tanh(x) = (eˣ - e⁻ˣ) / (eˣ + e⁻ˣ)

It maps inputs to the range (-1, 1). In fact, tanh is just a rescaled sigmoid: tanh(x) = 2σ(2x) - 1. The shape is the same S-curve, just centered at zero and stretching from -1 to 1.

Why Tanh Was an Improvement

Zero-centered outputs. Because tanh outputs range from -1 to 1, the outputs are centered around zero. This fixes the zig-zag gradient problem that sigmoid has. Gradients can flow in both directions naturally, making optimization smoother.

Stronger gradients near zero. The maximum derivative of tanh is 1 (at x=0), while sigmoid's maximum derivative is only 0.25. So tanh propagates gradients more effectively through layers — at least near the origin.

The Remaining Problem

Tanh still saturates. For large positive or negative inputs, the derivative drops to zero. The vanishing gradient problem is reduced compared to sigmoid, but not eliminated. Deep networks (20+ layers) still struggle to train with tanh. We needed something fundamentally different.

ReLU

Formula & Intuition

The Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) is brutally simple:

ReLU(x) = max(0, x)

If the input is positive, pass it through unchanged. If it's negative, output zero. That's it. No exponents, no divisions. Just a comparison and a multiplication.

Why ReLU Changed Everything

ReLU was first proposed in 2000 but gained fame in 2012 with AlexNet. Three properties made it revolutionary:

No saturation (for positive inputs). The derivative of ReLU is 1 for all positive inputs. Gradients don't shrink as they flow backward through layers. A 50-layer network can train where sigmoid-based networks could not. This was the key insight that enabled truly deep networks.

Sparsity. Any neuron with a negative input outputs exactly zero. In practice, around 50% of neurons in a ReLU network are inactive for any given input. This makes the representation sparse, which has both computational and representational benefits — sparse representations tend to be more interpretable and more efficient to process.

Computational efficiency. A comparison (is x > 0?) is vastly cheaper than computing an exponential. Training a ReLU network is roughly 6x faster than a sigmoid network, according to Krizhevsky et al.'s original AlexNet paper.

The Dying ReLU Problem

Here's the tradeoff. If a neuron's input is always negative (due to a bad weight initialization or a large learning rate pushing weights in the wrong direction), its output is always zero. Its gradient is always zero. It can never recover. It's dead.

In practice, with careful initialization (like He initialization) and reasonable learning rates, dying ReLU affects a small percentage of neurons and doesn't kill performance. But it motivated a family of fixes.

ReLU Variants

Each variant fixes the dying ReLU problem by giving negative inputs a small, nonzero output. They differ in exactly how.

Leaky ReLU

LeakyReLU(x) = x       if x > 0
               αx      if x ≤ 0     (α = 0.01 typically)

Instead of zeroing out negative inputs, multiply them by a small constant (usually 0.01). Now the gradient is never zero — dead neurons can recover. The downside: the slope α is a hyperparameter you choose by hand.

Parametric ReLU (PReLU)

PReLU(x) = x       if x > 0
           αx      if x ≤ 0     (α is learned)

Same as Leaky ReLU, but α is a learnable parameter. The network decides the best negative slope during training. Introduced by He et al. in 2015, PReLU helped win the ImageNet competition that year. The cost: one extra parameter per channel — negligible in practice.

ELU (Exponential Linear Unit)

ELU(x) = x                 if x > 0
         α(eˣ - 1)         if x ≤ 0     (α = 1.0 typically)

For negative inputs, ELU curves smoothly toward -α instead of using a straight line. This makes it smoother at x = 0 (unlike ReLU's sharp kink) and pushes mean activations closer to zero, which acts like a built-in batch normalization. The tradeoff: you're computing exponentials again for negative inputs.

SELU (Scaled Exponential Linear Unit)

SELU(x) = λ · x                 if x > 0
          λ · α(eˣ - 1)         if x ≤ 0

where λ ≈ 1.0507, α ≈ 1.6733

SELU is ELU with very specific, mathematically derived scale constants. The remarkable property: in a network using only fully connected layers with SELU activations and a specific weight initialization (LeCun normal), the activations self-normalize — they automatically maintain zero mean and unit variance across layers, without needing batch normalization. It was proven mathematically by Klambauer et al. in 2017. The catch: the self-normalizing property is fragile. It requires fully connected architectures and breaks with skip connections or convolutions.

GELU

The Idea

The Gaussian Error Linear Unit (GELU) takes a probabilistic approach. Instead of using a hard threshold (ReLU) or a simple slope (Leaky ReLU), GELU asks: "what's the probability that this input is greater than other inputs from a standard normal distribution?" and uses that probability to scale the input.

GELU(x) = x · Φ(x)

where Φ(x) is the CDF of the standard normal distribution

In practice, this is approximated as:

GELU(x) ≈ 0.5x(1 + tanh(√(2/π)(x + 0.044715x³)))

Intuitively: large positive inputs pass through almost unchanged (Φ(x) ≈ 1). Large negative inputs get zeroed out (Φ(x) ≈ 0). But unlike ReLU, the transition is smooth and probabilistic. Inputs near zero are partially scaled, not abruptly killed.

Why Transformers Love GELU

GELU is the default activation in BERT, GPT, and most modern transformer architectures. Why? Transformers operate on continuous representations of language where small differences matter. GELU's smooth, non-monotonic shape (it slightly dips below zero near x ≈ -0.17) gives the network more expressive power than ReLU for these fine-grained patterns. Empirically, GELU consistently beats ReLU in NLP benchmarks — the differences are small (0.1-0.5%) but consistent across tasks.

Swish

The Self-Gated Activation

Swish was discovered by Google Brain through automated search over activation function spaces in 2017:

Swish(x) = x · σ(βx)

where σ is the sigmoid function and β is a learnable parameter (often fixed at β = 1)

When β = 1, Swish(x) = x · sigmoid(x). The input gates itself through its own sigmoid. For large positive x, sigmoid(x) ≈ 1, so Swish(x) ≈ x (like ReLU). For large negative x, sigmoid(x) ≈ 0, so Swish(x) ≈ 0 (like ReLU). But near zero, Swish is smooth and slightly non-monotonic — it dips below zero before coming back up.

Swish and GELU are remarkably similar in shape. The practical differences are minimal, and both outperform ReLU in deep networks. Swish tends to appear more in vision architectures (EfficientNet), while GELU dominates in transformers — more convention than fundamental difference.

Softmax

From Scores to Probabilities

Softmax is different from the activations above. It's not applied element-wise to a single neuron. It operates on a vector of values (called logits) and converts them into a probability distribution:

Softmax(zᵢ) = eᶻⁱ / Σⱼ eᶻʲ

Each output is between 0 and 1, and all outputs sum to 1. Feed in raw scores like [2.0, 1.0, 0.1], get out probabilities like [0.659, 0.242, 0.099]. The highest score gets the highest probability, but all classes get some probability. This is exactly what you need for multi-class classification — "this image is 65.9% cat, 24.2% dog, 9.9% bird."

Temperature Scaling

You can control how "sharp" or "soft" the distribution is by dividing logits by a temperature parameter T before applying softmax:

Softmax(zᵢ / T)

When T → 0 (cold), the distribution becomes a hard one-hot vector — all probability mass on the highest logit. When T → ∞ (hot), the distribution becomes uniform — every class gets equal probability. T = 1 is the default. Temperature scaling is critical in knowledge distillation (training a small model to mimic a large one) and in controlling the "creativity" of language model outputs.

Temperature Scaling Effect (logits = [2.0, 1.0, 0.1]):

T = 0.5 (sharp):  [0.844, 0.114, 0.042]  ← very confident
T = 1.0 (normal): [0.659, 0.242, 0.099]  ← balanced
T = 2.0 (soft):   [0.510, 0.307, 0.183]  ← less confident
T = 5.0 (flat):   [0.382, 0.330, 0.288]  ← nearly uniform

Putting It All Together: PyTorch Code

Here's every activation we've discussed, implemented in PyTorch. Run this to see them side by side.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

x = torch.linspace(-5, 5, 100)

# --- Classic activations ---
sigmoid_out   = torch.sigmoid(x)
tanh_out      = torch.tanh(x)
relu_out      = F.relu(x)

# --- ReLU variants ---
leaky_out     = F.leaky_relu(x, negative_slope=0.01)
elu_out       = F.elu(x, alpha=1.0)
selu_out      = F.selu(x)

# --- Modern activations ---
gelu_out      = F.gelu(x)
swish_out     = x * torch.sigmoid(x)   # SiLU in PyTorch

# --- PReLU (learnable slope) ---
prelu          = nn.PReLU(num_parameters=1)
prelu_out      = prelu(x)

# --- Softmax with temperature ---
logits = torch.tensor([2.0, 1.0, 0.1])
for T in [0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0]:
    probs = F.softmax(logits / T, dim=0)
    print(f"T={T:.1f}: {probs.numpy().round(3)}")

# --- Quick comparison ---

# Using activations in a real network
class FlexibleMLP(nn.Module):
    """MLP where you can swap activation functions easily."""

    def __init__(self, input_dim, hidden_dim, output_dim,
                 activation='relu'):
        super().__init__()
        self.fc1 = nn.Linear(input_dim, hidden_dim)
        self.fc2 = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim)
        self.fc3 = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, output_dim)

        # Pick your activation
        activations = {
            'relu':      nn.ReLU(),
            'leaky':     nn.LeakyReLU(0.01),
            'prelu':     nn.PReLU(),
            'elu':       nn.ELU(),
            'selu':      nn.SELU(),
            'gelu':      nn.GELU(),
            'swish':     nn.SiLU(),       # SiLU = Swish with β=1
            'tanh':      nn.Tanh(),
            'sigmoid':   nn.Sigmoid(),
        }
        self.act = activations[activation]

    def forward(self, x):
        x = self.act(self.fc1(x))
        x = self.act(self.fc2(x))
        return self.fc3(x)    # No activation on output layer

# Example: compare training loss with different activations
model_relu = FlexibleMLP(784, 256, 10, activation='relu')
model_gelu = FlexibleMLP(784, 256, 10, activation='gelu')
model_selu = FlexibleMLP(784, 256, 10, activation='selu')

Activation Function Selection Criteria

Computational Cost

In terms of raw speed, ReLU is the cheapest — one comparison, one multiply. Leaky ReLU and PReLU are nearly identical in cost. Sigmoid, tanh, GELU, and Swish require exponentials or error functions, making them 2-5x slower per evaluation. For most architectures, this doesn't dominate total training time (matrix multiplications are the bottleneck), but on edge devices or in real-time inference, it matters.

Activation    | FLOPs per element | Relative cost
──────────────┼───────────────────┼──────────────
ReLU          | 1 comparison      | 1x (baseline)
Leaky ReLU    | 1 cmp + 1 mul     | ~1x
PReLU         | 1 cmp + 1 mul     | ~1x
ELU           | 1 cmp + 1 exp     | ~3x
Sigmoid       | 1 exp + 1 div     | ~3x
Tanh          | 2 exp + 1 div     | ~4x
GELU (approx) | 1 tanh + muls     | ~4x
Swish         | 1 sigmoid + 1 mul | ~4x

Architecture-Specific Choices

CNNs: ReLU remains the default. It's fast, works well, and decades of architectures are tuned for it. Use Leaky ReLU or ELU if you observe dying neurons. Swish was used in EfficientNet and showed small but consistent gains.

Transformers: GELU is the standard. GPT, BERT, ViT, and most modern LLMs use it. The smoothness matters more than the computational cost, since attention mechanisms are the real bottleneck.

Recurrent networks (LSTMs, GRUs): These use sigmoid and tanh internally by design — sigmoid for gates (values between 0 and 1 control information flow) and tanh for candidate values. You don't change these.

Fully connected networks (MLPs): If you don't need batch normalization and want a self-normalizing architecture, SELU with LeCun initialization is elegant. Otherwise, ReLU or GELU.

Smoothness & Differentiability

ReLU has a kink at x = 0 — technically not differentiable there (in practice, we just define the derivative as 0 or 1 at that point). For methods that require smooth second derivatives (some optimization techniques, neural ODEs), use GELU, Swish, or ELU. Smoothness also helps in architectures where small input changes should produce small output changes (like attention layers).

Custom Activation Functions

Design Principles

You can define your own activation function. PyTorch makes this straightforward:

class CustomActivation(nn.Module):
    """Example: a shifted softplus with learnable sharpness."""

    def __init__(self, beta_init=1.0):
        super().__init__()
        self.beta = nn.Parameter(torch.tensor(beta_init))

    def forward(self, x):
        # softplus(x) = log(1 + exp(βx)) / β
        # Smooth approximation of ReLU
        return F.softplus(x * self.beta) / self.beta

# Or simply use a lambda for non-learnable activations
mish = lambda x: x * torch.tanh(F.softplus(x))

When designing a custom activation, consider these properties:

Nonlinearity — if it's linear, it's useless (the whole point is to add nonlinearity). Differentiability — gradients need to flow through it, so it must be differentiable almost everywhere. Non-saturation — at least for positive inputs, the derivative should not go to zero. Monotonicity — traditional activations are monotonic (if input goes up, output goes up), but recent work shows that non-monotonic activations like GELU and Swish can outperform monotonic ones, especially in deep networks.

The honest truth: custom activations rarely beat GELU or Swish on standard benchmarks. The cases where they help are domain-specific — audio processing, physics-informed neural networks, or architectures with unusual constraints. If you're not sure, start with GELU for transformers and ReLU for everything else.

Discrete Relaxation Techniques

The Problem: Backprop Can't Handle Discrete Choices

Sometimes your network needs to make a discrete decision. "Pick one of these 10 categories." "Choose which expert to route this token to." "Sample a one-hot vector from a categorical distribution." The problem: discrete operations have no gradient. You can't differentiate through argmax. Backpropagation dies.

This is a fundamental tension. You want discrete outputs (for interpretability, for structure, for efficiency), but you need continuous gradients (for training).

Gumbel-Softmax (The Concrete Distribution)

The Gumbel-Softmax trick (also called the Concrete distribution, published independently by Jang et al. and Maddison et al. in 2017) solves this with a clever relaxation. The idea has three steps:

Step 1: Add Gumbel noise. To sample from a categorical distribution with log-probabilities (logits) πᵢ, you add noise drawn from the Gumbel distribution: gᵢ = -log(-log(uᵢ)) where uᵢ ~ Uniform(0,1). Then the argmax of (πᵢ + gᵢ) gives you an exact sample from the categorical distribution. This is the Gumbel-Max trick — it converts categorical sampling into an argmax operation.

Step 2: Replace argmax with softmax. Argmax isn't differentiable. But softmax is. So replace argmax with a temperature-controlled softmax:

yᵢ = exp((πᵢ + gᵢ) / τ) / Σⱼ exp((πⱼ + gⱼ) / τ)

where τ is the temperature. When τ → 0, this approaches a one-hot vector (hard discrete choice). When τ is large, it approaches a uniform distribution (soft, continuous).

Step 3: Anneal the temperature. Start training with a high temperature (τ = 1.0 or higher) so gradients flow freely. Gradually decrease τ toward 0 during training so the outputs become increasingly discrete. By the end, you get nearly-discrete decisions with gradients that were well-behaved throughout training.

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def gumbel_softmax(logits, tau=1.0, hard=False):
    """Sample from categorical distribution with gradients.

    Args:
        logits: unnormalized log probabilities, shape (batch, num_classes)
        tau: temperature (lower = more discrete)
        hard: if True, returns one-hot but with straight-through gradients
    """
    # Step 1: Sample Gumbel noise
    u = torch.rand_like(logits).clamp(1e-8, 1.0)
    gumbels = -torch.log(-torch.log(u))

    # Step 2: Soft argmax
    y_soft = F.softmax((logits + gumbels) / tau, dim=-1)

    if hard:
        # Straight-through: forward pass is one-hot, backward uses soft
        index = y_soft.argmax(dim=-1, keepdim=True)
        y_hard = torch.zeros_like(logits).scatter_(-1, index, 1.0)
        return y_hard - y_soft.detach() + y_soft   # gradient trick
    return y_soft

# Example: discrete latent variable
logits = torch.randn(4, 10)  # batch of 4, 10 categories

# High temperature: soft, continuous
soft = gumbel_softmax(logits, tau=1.0)
print(f"Soft (τ=1.0): {soft[0].round(decimals=2)}")

# Low temperature: nearly one-hot
sharp = gumbel_softmax(logits, tau=0.1)
print(f"Sharp (τ=0.1): {sharp[0].round(decimals=2)}")

# Hard: true one-hot with straight-through gradient
hard = gumbel_softmax(logits, tau=0.5, hard=True)
print(f"Hard: {hard[0]}")  # exactly one-hot

Temperature Annealing Schedule

The annealing schedule matters. Too fast and the model gets stuck in bad discrete configurations before it explores enough. Too slow and training takes forever. A common approach:

# Exponential annealing: τ starts high, decays each epoch
tau = max(tau_min, tau_init * exp(-anneal_rate * step))

# Concrete example:
tau_init = 1.0
tau_min  = 0.1
anneal_rate = 3e-5
# After 10k steps: τ ≈ 0.74
# After 50k steps: τ ≈ 0.22
# After 100k steps: τ ≈ 0.10 (clipped at tau_min)

Applications

Variational Autoencoders with discrete latents. Standard VAEs use continuous Gaussian latent variables. But many real-world structures are discrete — categories, clusters, parse trees. Gumbel-Softmax lets you have discrete latent variables in a VAE while still training end-to-end with the reparameterization trick. The ELBO (evidence lower bound) is computed normally; you just use Gumbel-Softmax samples instead of Gaussian samples.

Neural architecture search. "Should this layer use a 3×3 conv or a 5×5 conv?" That's a discrete choice. DARTS (Differentiable Architecture Search) relaxes it with a softmax-weighted mixture during search, then discretizes at the end. Gumbel-Softmax provides a more principled way to handle this relaxation.

Hard attention and routing. Mixture-of-experts models (like those in GPT-4) route tokens to specific expert subnetworks. The routing decision is inherently discrete ("send this token to expert 3"), but you need gradients for training. Gumbel-Softmax or similar relaxations make this possible.

Output layers:
 ├── Binary classification  → Sigmoid
 ├── Multi-class           → Softmax
 └── Regression            → None (linear)

Discrete outputs:
 └── Gumbel-Softmax        → differentiable discrete sampling

When someone asks you "which activation function should I use?" — the answer in 2024 is almost always: GELU for transformers, ReLU for CNNs, and don't overthink it. The choice of activation function matters far less than your data quality, model architecture, and training hyperparameters. But understanding why each activation exists — what problem it solves and what tradeoff it makes — gives you the intuition to debug training problems and make informed decisions when the defaults don't work.
