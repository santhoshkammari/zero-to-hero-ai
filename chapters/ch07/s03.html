Loss Functions

Chapter 7: Deep Learning Foundations Section 3 of 7

What a Loss Function Actually Is

A loss function measures how wrong your model is on a single prediction. The optimizer's job is to make this number smaller. That's the entire training loop.

But here's the thing people gloss over: the loss function defines what "wrong" means. Two models with identical architectures, identical data, identical optimizers — train one with squared error and the other with absolute error and you get different models. Not because of randomness. Because they disagree on what counts as a bad prediction. MSE thinks an error of 10 is a hundred times worse than an error of 1. MAE thinks it's ten times worse. Those are fundamentally different value systems, and the model faithfully optimizes whichever one you hand it.

Every loss function encodes a statistical assumption about your data, whether you realize it or not. Pick the right one and your model converges fast to something useful. Pick the wrong one and you're optimizing for the wrong objective — your model will dutifully get better at something you don't actually care about. This section is about building the intuition to make that choice deliberately.

Mean Squared Error (MSE)

The regression default. You already know the formula:

MSE = (1/n) × Σ(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)²

Why squaring? It's not arbitrary. Squaring does three things simultaneously. First, it makes all errors positive — an overshoot of 5 and an undershoot of 5 both contribute 25. Second, and more importantly, it creates a disproportionate penalty for large errors. An error of 10 costs 100. An error of 1 costs 1. That's not 10× worse — it's 100× worse. The model is screaming at itself to fix the big mistakes first, even at the cost of making small mistakes slightly worse.

Third — and this is the part most people miss — minimizing MSE is equivalent to maximizing the likelihood of the data under a Gaussian noise assumption. If you assume your targets are generated as y = f(x) + ε where ε ~ N(0, σ²), then the log-likelihood of observing your data given model predictions is:

log P(data | model) = -n/(2σ²) × Σ(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)² + constant

Maximizing this is the same as minimizing Σ(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)². MSE is maximum likelihood estimation under Gaussian noise. That's not a coincidence — it's the derivation. When someone says "MSE assumes Gaussian errors," this is what they mean. If your errors actually are roughly Gaussian (symmetric, concentrated near zero, light tails), MSE is the statistically optimal choice.

When it hurts: outliers. One sample with an error of 100 contributes 10,000 to the loss. A thousand samples each off by 1 contribute a total of 1,000. That single outlier dominates the entire gradient signal. The model warps itself to accommodate that one point, degrading performance on everything else. If your data has heavy tails — fat-tailed distributions, corrupted labels, measurement artifacts — MSE will betray you.

The gradient tells the same story: ∂MSE/∂ŷ = 2(ŷ - y). Linear in the error. Big errors → big gradients → big parameter updates. That's exactly what makes MSE fast on clean data and dangerous on dirty data.

Mean Absolute Error (MAE)

MAE = (1/n) × Σ|yᵢ - ŷᵢ|

Linear penalty. An error of 10 is exactly 10× worse than an error of 1. No quadratic amplification. This makes MAE fundamentally more robust to outliers — that error-of-100 sample contributes 100, not 10,000. It can't hijack the gradient.

The statistical connection: minimizing MAE is equivalent to maximum likelihood under Laplacian noise — a distribution with heavier tails than the Gaussian. If ε ~ Laplace(0, b), the log-likelihood leads directly to the L1 penalty. So MAE isn't just "MSE but simpler." It's the optimal estimator when your errors follow a Laplace distribution. In practice, this means MAE fits the median of the conditional distribution y|x, while MSE fits the mean. For symmetric distributions, those are the same. For skewed or heavy-tailed distributions, the median is more robust — and MAE gives it to you automatically.

The problem: the gradient of |x| is a step function — it's +1 for positive errors, -1 for negative errors, and undefined at exactly zero. That discontinuity at zero means the gradient doesn't shrink as you approach the optimum. The model takes equally aggressive steps whether it's off by 10.0 or 0.001, which causes oscillation around the minimum and makes precise convergence difficult. In practice, optimizers like Adam smooth this out somewhat, but MSE still converges more gracefully near the solution.

Huber Loss

Best of both worlds. Quadratic when the error is small (like MSE — smooth gradients, precise convergence), linear when the error is large (like MAE — robust to outliers). The δ parameter controls the transition:

Huber(y, ŷ) =
  0.5 × (y - ŷ)²             if |y - ŷ| ≤ δ
  δ × (|y - ŷ| - 0.5 × δ)    if |y - ŷ| > δ

For errors smaller than δ, you're in MSE territory — the gradient scales with the error magnitude, convergence is smooth. For errors larger than δ, you're in MAE territory — the gradient is constant (magnitude δ), so outliers can't dominate. The function is continuous and differentiable everywhere, including at the transition points. That's by design — Huber specifically constructed it to be the smoothest possible interpolation between L2 and L1.

The catch is choosing δ. It encodes your definition of "what counts as an outlier." Too small and everything is an outlier (you're using MAE). Too large and nothing is (you're using MSE). A good starting point: set δ to the median absolute deviation of your residuals, then tune from there. In PyTorch, nn.HuberLoss(delta=1.0) — that default of 1.0 is reasonable when your targets are standardized.

Binary Cross-Entropy

This is where loss functions stop being about distances and start being about probabilities. Pay attention — the reasoning here carries through to everything in classification.

Setup: your model outputs a probability p for class 1. The true label is y ∈ {0, 1}. How do we measure how good that probability is?

Think about it from a gambling perspective. Before seeing the answer, the model places a bet: "I think there's a p chance this is class 1." Then the answer is revealed. If y = 1, the model had assigned probability p to the correct outcome. If y = 0, the model had assigned probability (1 - p) to the correct outcome. Either way, we can define pt — the probability the model assigned to whatever actually happened:

pₜ = p    if y = 1
pₜ = 1-p  if y = 0

A good model assigns high probability to things that actually happen. So we want to maximize pt. Taking the negative log gives us a loss function that's small when pt is large and large when pt is small:

Loss = -log(pₜ) = -[y·log(p) + (1-y)·log(1-p)]

That's binary cross-entropy. One line. But the why behind the log is everything.

Why Logarithm?

Because we're measuring information. The log converts multiplicative probability into additive "surprise." If you predicted p = 0.99 and the answer was 1, you were barely surprised: -log(0.99) ≈ 0.01. If you predicted p = 0.5, you were moderately surprised: -log(0.5) ≈ 0.69. If you predicted p = 0.01 and the answer was 1, you were catastrophically wrong: -log(0.01) ≈ 4.6.

Look at the asymmetry. Going from 0.99 to 0.999 barely changes the loss (0.01 → 0.001). But going from 0.01 to 0.001 explodes the loss (4.6 → 6.9). Confident wrong predictions get destroyed. The log creates a penalty structure where the model is punished exponentially more for putting high confidence on the wrong answer. This is exactly the behavior you want — a classifier that says "95% class A" when the answer is class B should be punished far more than one that says "55% class A."

The Probability Connection

Here's the deep result: minimizing cross-entropy is exactly equivalent to maximizing the likelihood of the data under the model's predicted probability distribution.

If the model predicts P(y=1|x) = p for each sample, the likelihood of the observed labels is:

L = Π pᵢ^yᵢ × (1-pᵢ)^(1-yᵢ)

Taking the negative log:

-log L = -Σ [yᵢ·log(pᵢ) + (1-yᵢ)·log(1-pᵢ)]

That's cross-entropy. Cross-entropy IS negative log-likelihood. When you minimize BCE, you're doing maximum likelihood estimation. This isn't a metaphor — it's an identity. Every time you train a classifier with cross-entropy, you're implicitly fitting a Bernoulli distribution to each data point and maximizing the probability of the observed labels under that model.

Numerical Stability

There's a practical landmine: log(0) = -∞. If your model ever predicts exactly p = 0 or p = 1 (which sigmoid can approach but not reach — but floating-point arithmetic can), the loss becomes infinite and training explodes.

The naive fix is clamping:

eps = 1e-7
pred = torch.clamp(pred, eps, 1 - eps)
loss = -(y * torch.log(pred) + (1 - y) * torch.log(1 - pred))

The proper fix is to never compute sigmoid and log separately. F.binary_cross_entropy_with_logits takes raw logits (before sigmoid) and fuses the sigmoid + log into a single numerically stable operation using the identity:

-log(sigmoid(z)) = log(1 + e⁻ᶻ) = softplus(-z)

This never computes sigmoid explicitly, so it never produces the 0.0 or 1.0 that would blow up the log. Always use the _with_logits variant. If you find yourself writing torch.sigmoid followed by BCELoss, refactor immediately.

Categorical Cross-Entropy

Same idea, K classes instead of 2. The true label is a one-hot vector (only one class is 1, the rest are 0). The model outputs a probability distribution over K classes via softmax. The loss is:

CE = -Σₖ yₖ · log(pₖ)

Since y is one-hot, this collapses to just -log(p_correct) — the negative log of whatever probability the model assigned to the true class. Predicted 0.9 on the correct class? Loss is 0.105. Predicted 0.01? Loss is 4.605. The model is hammered for assigning low probability to the truth.

Connection to KL Divergence

Cross-entropy between distributions P (true) and Q (predicted) decomposes as:

H(P, Q) = H(P) + D_KL(P ‖ Q)

where H(P) is the entropy of the true distribution and DKL(P ‖ Q) is the KL divergence from Q to P. Since the true distribution (one-hot labels) has fixed entropy (zero, for hard labels), minimizing cross-entropy is the same as minimizing KL divergence. You're making your model's distribution as close as possible to the true distribution, measured by KL divergence — the natural information-theoretic distance between probability distributions.

Same numerical stability story as BCE: never apply softmax and then log separately. PyTorch's nn.CrossEntropyLoss takes raw logits and computes softmax + log internally via the log-sum-exp trick. It's not optional — it's how you avoid NaN.

Sparse vs Dense Cross-Entropy

Same math, different input format. Dense cross-entropy expects one-hot encoded labels: [0, 0, 1, 0, 0]. Sparse cross-entropy expects integer class indices: 2. The computation is identical — both just look up -log(p_correct).

Use sparse when you have many classes. ImageNet has 1,000 classes. One-hot encoding every label means storing 999 zeros per sample for no reason. Sparse format stores a single integer. In PyTorch, nn.CrossEntropyLoss already takes integer labels (sparse format) — it's the default, not the exception. TensorFlow/Keras makes the distinction explicit with CategoricalCrossentropy vs SparseCategoricalCrossentropy.

Focal Loss

Standard cross-entropy treats every sample equally. When 99.9% of your samples are easy negatives (background in object detection, majority class in imbalanced datasets), the easy examples dominate the total loss. The model spends its gradient budget polishing already-correct predictions instead of learning the hard cases.

Focal loss fixes this by downweighting easy examples:

FL(pₜ) = -αₜ × (1 - pₜ)ᵞ × log(pₜ)

The modulating factor (1 - pt)γ does the work. When pt → 1 (model is confident and correct), the factor → 0 and this sample contributes almost nothing. When pt → 0 (model is wrong), the factor → 1 and the sample contributes fully. The γ parameter (typically 2) controls the focus strength. At γ = 0 it's standard cross-entropy. At γ = 5 only the hardest examples matter.

Introduced in the RetinaNet paper (Lin et al., 2017), focal loss was the key insight that let a simple single-stage detector beat all existing two-stage architectures. The bottleneck wasn't the model — it was the loss function drowning signal in noise. Beyond detection, focal loss helps anywhere class imbalance is extreme: fraud detection, rare disease diagnosis, anomaly detection.

The Decision Rule

For 95% of cases, this is the entire decision tree:

Regression                → MSE (or Huber if outliers exist)
Binary classification     → BCE with logits
Multi-class classification → Cross-entropy with logits (includes softmax)
Imbalanced classification → Focal loss
Ranking / embeddings      → Triplet or contrastive loss (different chapter)

That's it. If you're reaching for something exotic, make sure you've exhausted the basics first. The loss function is rarely the bottleneck in a pipeline — data quality, model capacity, and regularization matter more. But when the loss is wrong, nothing else can compensate.

PyTorch: Proper Usage Patterns

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

# ── Regression: MSE, MAE, Huber ──────────────────────────
y_true = torch.tensor([3.0, 5.0, 2.5, 8.0])
y_pred = torch.tensor([2.8, 5.5, 2.0, 15.0])  # last value is an outlier

mse  = F.mse_loss(y_pred, y_true)        # 12.565 — outlier dominates
mae  = F.l1_loss(y_pred, y_true)          #  1.925 — outlier tamed
huber = F.huber_loss(y_pred, y_true, delta=1.0)  # 3.083 — compromise

# ── Binary classification: ALWAYS use logits variant ─────
labels = torch.tensor([1.0, 0.0, 1.0, 0.0])
logits = torch.tensor([2.5, -1.8, 0.3, -3.2])  # raw scores, BEFORE sigmoid

# CORRECT: numerically stable — sigmoid + log fused internally
loss_bce = F.binary_cross_entropy_with_logits(logits, labels)

# WRONG: sigmoid then BCE separately → risk of log(0) = NaN
# probs = torch.sigmoid(logits)
# loss_bce = F.binary_cross_entropy(probs, labels)  # don't do this

# ── Multi-class: CrossEntropyLoss takes RAW LOGITS + integer labels ──
logits_mc = torch.randn(8, 10)          # batch of 8, 10 classes
targets_mc = torch.tensor([3, 7, 0, 2, 9, 1, 5, 4])  # integer labels

# CORRECT: softmax + log fused internally, integer (sparse) labels
loss_ce = F.cross_entropy(logits_mc, targets_mc)

# WRONG: manual softmax then log then nll_loss — numerically fragile
# probs = F.softmax(logits_mc, dim=1)
# loss_ce = F.nll_loss(torch.log(probs), targets_mc)  # don't do this

# ── Why logits versions exist: the numerical stability argument ──
z = torch.tensor([100.0])  # extreme logit
# sigmoid(100) ≈ 1.0 in float32 → log(1 - 1.0) = log(0) = -inf → NaN
# But F.binary_cross_entropy_with_logits handles this cleanly:
safe_loss = F.binary_cross_entropy_with_logits(z, torch.tensor([0.0]))
print(f"Loss on extreme logit: {safe_loss.item():.4f}")  # 100.0, not NaN
The Logits Trick — Why It Matters

The _with_logits variants exist for one reason: numerical stability. Computing sigmoid(z) then log(sigmoid(z)) as separate operations loses precision at extremes. For large positive z, sigmoid(z) → 1.0 in float32, so log(1 - sigmoid(z)) = log(0) = -∞. The fused version uses the identity log(sigmoid(z)) = z - softplus(z), which never computes sigmoid explicitly. Same for softmax + log in CrossEntropyLoss — it uses log-sum-exp internally. If you're ever computing activation then loss as two steps, you're probably doing it wrong.
