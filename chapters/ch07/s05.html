Chapter 7: Deep Learning Foundations Gradient Problems · Weight Initialization
Section Overview

This section combines Gradient Problems and Weight Initialization into one comprehensive reference.

Gradient Problems

The chain rule multiplies gradients across layers. If those per-layer gradients are consistently less than 1, they vanish. If consistently greater than 1, they explode. Either way, deep networks can't train. This isn't an edge case — it's the default behavior unless you actively prevent it with the right activation functions, weight initialization, and architectural choices.

Vanishing Gradients

Backpropagation computes the gradient for a weight in layer 1 by multiplying local gradients from every layer between the output and that weight. For a 50-layer network, the gradient for the first layer is a product of ~50 terms. Each term includes the derivative of that layer's activation function. If those derivatives are consistently less than 1, the product collapses exponentially toward zero.

Sigmoid's derivative is σ'(x) = σ(x)(1 − σ(x)). The maximum value is 0.25, occurring only at x = 0. Everywhere else it's smaller. Tanh is better — its derivative peaks at 1.0 — but in practice, neurons rarely sit at exactly zero input, so the derivative typically ranges 0.2–0.8.

The arithmetic is damning:

# Best-case gradient magnitude with sigmoid
# Derivative peaks at 0.25 — multiply that across layers
for depth in [10, 20, 50]:
    grad = 0.25 ** depth
    print(f"After {depth:2d} layers: 0.25^{depth} = {grad:.2e}")

# After 10 layers: 0.25^10 = 9.54e-07
# After 20 layers: 0.25^20 = 9.09e-13
# After 50 layers: 0.25^50 = 7.89e-31

After 10 layers, the gradient is one millionth of what it was at the output. After 50, it's 10⁻³¹ — float32 rounds that to exactly zero. And 0.25 is the best case. Once neurons saturate, their local gradient drops toward zero. The real numbers are worse.

What It Looks Like in Practice

The symptom is deceptive. Loss plateaus early. You think the model has converged — but it hasn't learned anything useful. The last few layers near the loss have healthy gradients and train fine. The first few layers receive near-zero gradients and barely move from their initial random values. A penthouse on sand.

⚠️ The Deceptive Plateau

A flat loss curve doesn't always mean convergence. If you're using sigmoid/tanh in a deep network and loss stops improving early, check the gradient norms on your first few layers before concluding the model has learned what it can.

Solutions

ReLU. f(x) = max(0, x). Derivative is 0 for negatives, 1 for positives. No squashing. When a ReLU neuron is active, it passes the gradient through untouched. Chain 50 layers of active ReLUs: 1⁵⁰ = 1. Problem solved — at least for the active neurons. (Dead neurons are the tradeoff — Leaky ReLU addresses this.)

Residual connections. Instead of y = f(x), compute y = f(x) + x. The gradient through the skip path is always 1 — it's just ∂x/∂x. Even if f(x) has vanishing gradients, the learning signal flows through the shortcut undiminished. This is why ResNets can train 152+ layers where plain networks collapse at 20.

# Without skip: gradient can vanish through f
# ∂y/∂x = ∂f(x)/∂x              → can shrink to ~0

# With skip: y = f(x) + x
# ∂y/∂x = ∂f(x)/∂x + 1          → always ≥ 1

Proper initialization. Covered in the next section. The short version: if your initial weights keep the variance of activations stable across layers, the per-layer gradient multiplier stays near 1.

Exploding Gradients

Same chain rule, opposite direction. If local gradients are consistently greater than 1, the product grows exponentially. This happens when weight matrices have large eigenvalues — they amplify the gradient at each layer.

# Gradient amplification across layers
for factor in [1.5, 2.0, 3.0]:
    result = factor ** 50
    print(f"{factor}^50 = {result:.2e}")

# 1.5^50 = 6.38e+08
# 2.0^50 = 1.13e+15
# 3.0^50 = 7.18e+23

A per-layer multiplier of just 1.5 means the first layer's gradient is 638 million times the output gradient. Weight updates become enormous, weights jump to absurd values, and the next forward pass produces inf or NaN. Training is over.

What It Looks Like in Practice

Exploding gradients are louder than vanishing ones. Your loss is training normally, maybe even decreasing — then suddenly spikes to nan or inf. Weight values shoot to astronomical magnitudes. In RNNs it's especially brutal: the same weight matrix multiplied at every time step is literally matrix exponentiation.

🔥 Quick Diagnostic

Loss suddenly jumps to nan? Exploding gradients. Loss plateaus and early-layer weights barely change? Vanishing gradients. Same root cause — the chain rule amplifying deviations from 1 — but they fail in very different ways.

Solutions

Gradient clipping. The simplest and most common fix. Cap the total gradient norm — direction preserved, only magnitude is limited. One line in PyTorch:

import torch.nn as nn

# Inside your training loop, after loss.backward():
torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)
optimizer.step()

Proper initialization. Xavier init for sigmoid/tanh, He/Kaiming init for ReLU — both keep signal variance stable. Next section.

Normalization layers. Batch norm and layer norm re-center and re-scale activations at each layer, keeping magnitudes in a stable range. This indirectly stabilizes gradients too.

The Connection Triangle

Activation functions, weight initialization, and gradient problems are three vertices of a single triangle. They're deeply coupled, and understanding that coupling is what separates people who can debug training from people who just try random things.

Sigmoid + random large init = saturated neurons = vanishing gradients. Guaranteed.
ReLU + He init = stable variance = stable gradients. Reliable.
Tanh + Xavier init = reasonable gradients for moderate depth.
ReLU + random large init = exploding activations = broken training.

Every combination has a predictable outcome. When training fails, check: what activation, what initialization, and what do the gradient norms look like? The answer almost always points directly to the fix.

Monitoring Gradients in Practice

Don't guess. Measure:

def gradient_health_check(model):
    """Print per-layer gradient norms after a backward pass."""
    for name, param in model.named_parameters():
        if param.grad is not None:
            norm = param.grad.norm().item()
            flag = ""
            if norm < 1e-7:
                flag = " ← vanishing!"
            elif norm > 1e3:
                flag = " ← exploding!"
            print(f"{name:<40s} {norm:>12.2e}{flag}")

# Use after loss.backward():
# gradient_health_check(model)
💡 Rule of Thumb

If gradient norms differ by more than ~100× between the first and last layers, you have a gradient flow problem. Healthy networks keep gradient magnitudes within 1–2 orders of magnitude across all layers. Check this before training for 12 hours.

Weight Initialization

The Symmetry Problem

If you initialize all weights to zero, every neuron in a layer computes the same weighted sum, produces the same output, receives the same gradient, and gets the same update. After one step of training, they're still identical. After a million steps, still identical. You've paid for 512 neurons and gotten the computational equivalent of one. This is called the symmetry problem — identical neurons have no mechanism to become different.

Random initialization breaks symmetry. Each neuron starts with slightly different weights, computes a slightly different function, receives a slightly different gradient, and specializes during training. Randomness isn't a compromise — it's a requirement.

Random Isn't Enough — Scale Matters

So you initialize randomly. From what distribution? If you draw weights from N(0, 10), activations explode — by layer 5 you're hitting float overflow. If you draw from N(0, 0.001), activations collapse to zero — by layer 5 every neuron outputs approximately the same value and gradients vanish. The goal is to choose a variance that keeps the variance of activations roughly constant across layers. Not growing, not shrinking — stable.

Xavier / Glorot Initialization (2010)

Xavier Glorot and Yoshua Bengio worked out the math for sigmoid and tanh activations. The key insight: if you want the variance of the output to equal the variance of the input at each layer, you need to account for how many connections feed into the neuron (fan_in) and how many go out (fan_out):

Var(W) = 2 / (fan_in + fan_out)

In practice, sample from N(0, 2/(fan_in + fan_out)) or U(−√(6/(fan_in + fan_out)), +√(6/(fan_in + fan_out))). The derivation assumes linear activations, but works well for tanh and sigmoid because these are approximately linear near zero — which is where properly initialized neurons start.

He / Kaiming Initialization (2015)

ReLU breaks the Xavier derivation. ReLU sets all negative inputs to zero, killing roughly half the activations in each layer. That halving means Xavier's variance is too small by a factor of 2 — activations shrink layer by layer. Kaiming He's fix: double the variance and only use fan_in.

Var(W) = 2 / fan_in

The factor of 2 compensates exactly for ReLU zeroing out the negative half. If you're using ReLU (and you probably are), this is your default.

In PyTorch

PyTorch's nn.Linear defaults to Kaiming uniform (He) initialization. nn.Conv2d does the same. For most ReLU networks, the defaults are already correct. But when you need explicit control:

import torch.nn as nn

# He / Kaiming — use for ReLU networks
nn.init.kaiming_normal_(layer.weight, mode='fan_in', nonlinearity='relu')

# Xavier / Glorot — use for sigmoid/tanh networks
nn.init.xavier_normal_(layer.weight)

# Common pattern: custom init for a whole model
def init_weights(m):
    if isinstance(m, (nn.Linear, nn.Conv2d)):
        nn.init.kaiming_normal_(m.weight, nonlinearity='relu')
        if m.bias is not None:
            nn.init.zeros_(m.bias)

model.apply(init_weights)

Biases are typically initialized to zero — they don't cause the symmetry problem because the weight matrices are already different.

When Initialization Matters Less

Batch normalization re-normalizes activations at every layer, correcting for bad initialization on the fly. Residual connections let gradients bypass problematic layers. With both (the standard recipe for modern CNNs and Transformers), initialization sensitivity drops dramatically.

But "matters less" isn't "doesn't matter." Vanilla MLPs without normalization, GANs (already unstable), and custom architectures — wrong initialization still kills training. Even in BatchNorm-heavy architectures, good initialization means faster convergence. There's no reason to get it wrong.

⚠️ Other Schemes

Orthogonal initialization, LSUV, and Fixup exist for specific edge cases (very deep residual networks without normalization, RNNs). You'll encounter them in papers. For day-to-day work, He and Xavier cover 95% of cases.
