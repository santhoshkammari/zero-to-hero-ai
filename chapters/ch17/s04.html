Causal Inference

Chapter 17: Learning Theory & Advanced ML 8 subtopics
TL;DR

Every ML model you have ever trained is a correlation machine. Correlations are great for prediction — but the moment you want to change something (launch a treatment, redesign a feature, set a policy), you need causation. Causal inference gives you the mathematical machinery to go from "X and Y move together" to "changing X will change Y." The key tools: structural causal models (DAGs), the do-operator, the backdoor criterion, and counterfactual reasoning. This is arguably the most important gap in a typical ML education.

Why Causal Inference Matters

Here is a fact: ice cream sales and drowning deaths are highly correlated. If you trained a model on this data, it would confidently predict that selling more ice cream causes more drownings. Your model's R² might look great. Its predictions on held-out data might be spot on. And yet, banning ice cream would not save a single life.

The lurking variable, of course, is summer heat. Hot weather drives both ice cream consumption and swimming activity (and therefore drowning risk). The correlation between ice cream and drowning is real — it is just not causal.

This matters in practice more than you think. Consider:

  • Medicine: Does this drug reduce mortality, or do healthier patients simply choose it more?
  • Product: Did our new feature increase retention, or did power users adopt it while casual users churned regardless?
  • Policy: Does increasing police presence reduce crime, or do we deploy more police to already-safe neighborhoods?

Standard ML gives you P(Y|X) — the probability of Y given that you observe X. Causal inference gives you P(Y|do(X)) — the probability of Y if you intervene and set X. That tiny word "do" is the entire difference between prediction and action.

The Core Insight

Prediction asks: "Given that we see X, what do we expect for Y?" Causal inference asks: "If we force X to a particular value, what happens to Y?" These are different questions with different answers. Every time you use a model to make a decision rather than a forecast, you are implicitly assuming the causal version — whether you know it or not.

Structural Causal Models

Judea Pearl's framework for causal reasoning rests on three ingredients: a directed acyclic graph (DAG) encoding your assumptions about what causes what, structural equations specifying the functional relationships, and exogenous noise variables capturing everything else.

The DAG

A causal DAG is deceptively simple. Nodes are variables. A directed edge from A → B means "A has a direct causal effect on B." The key rule: no cycles — you cannot cause yourself. Here is our ice cream example:

# Causal DAG as an adjacency list
ice_cream_dag = {
    "Temperature":  ["IceCream", "Swimming"],
    "Swimming":     ["Drowning"],
    "IceCream":     [],   # does NOT cause Drowning
    "Drowning":     []
}

# Temperature → IceCream
# Temperature → Swimming → Drowning
# No edge from IceCream → Drowning!

The DAG encodes your qualitative causal assumptions. It says temperature causes ice cream sales and swimming, and swimming causes drowning. Crucially, there is no arrow from ice cream to drowning. The observed correlation between them arises entirely because they share a common cause.

Three Critical Structures

Every path in a causal DAG is built from three elementary patterns. Understanding these is essential — they determine everything about what you can and cannot estimate from observational data.

StructurePatternBehaviorCondition on Z?
Confounder (fork) X ← Z → Y Z causes both X and Y, creating a spurious correlation between them ✅ Yes — conditioning on Z removes the spurious path
Mediator (chain) X → Z → Y X affects Y through Z. The causal effect flows along this path ⚠️ Careful — conditioning on Z blocks the causal pathway
Collider X → Z ← Y X and Y both cause Z. They are independent until you condition on Z 🚫 No — conditioning on Z creates a spurious association

The collider is the most counterintuitive of these. Here is a classic example: talent and attractiveness are independent in the general population. But if you condition on "is a Hollywood actor" (which requires either talent or attractiveness), suddenly talent and attractiveness appear negatively correlated among actors. Conditioning on the collider opened a path that was previously blocked.

The Collider Trap

Conditioning on a collider (or any descendant of a collider) creates a spurious association between its parents. This is the opposite of what you do with confounders. Many analyses get this wrong — adding "more controls" to a regression can actually introduce bias rather than remove it if one of those controls is a collider.

Structural Equations

The DAG tells you what causes what. Structural equations tell you how. Each variable is a deterministic function of its parents plus independent noise:

import numpy as np

def generate_causal_data(n=5000, seed=42):
    """Generate data from a known structural causal model.

    DAG:  Z → X → Y  (Z is a confounder if Z also → Y)
          Z ------→ Y
    """
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)

    # Structural equations:
    Z = rng.normal(0, 1, n)                      # exogenous
    X = 0.8 * Z + rng.normal(0, 0.5, n)          # X caused by Z
    Y = 0.5 * X + 0.6 * Z + rng.normal(0, 0.3, n)  # Y caused by X and Z

    # True causal effect of X on Y: 0.5
    # Naive regression of Y on X (ignoring Z) will overestimate it
    # because Z confounds the relationship
    return Z, X, Y

Z, X, Y = generate_causal_data()

# Naive estimate (biased — ignores confounder Z)
from numpy.polynomial.polynomial import polyfit
naive_slope = np.polyfit(X, Y, 1)[0]
print(f"Naive estimate of X→Y effect: {naive_slope:.3f}")

# Correct estimate (controls for confounder Z)
from numpy.linalg import lstsq
A = np.column_stack([X, Z, np.ones(len(X))])
coeffs, _, _, _ = lstsq(A, Y, rcond=None)
print(f"Adjusted estimate of X→Y effect: {coeffs[0]:.3f}")
print(f"True causal effect: 0.500")

Run this and you will see the naive estimate is noticeably higher than 0.5 (typically around 0.8), because it absorbs the confounding effect of Z. Once you control for Z, you recover the true causal effect.

The do-Operator

This is the single most important concept in causal inference. Understand this, and everything else falls into place.

P(Y | X = x) — conditional probability — asks: "Among all cases where X happens to equal x, what is the distribution of Y?" This includes cases where X = x because of some confounder Z.

P(Y | do(X = x)) — interventional probability — asks: "If I reach into the system, force X to equal x regardless of anything else, what happens to Y?" This is what happens when you actually flip the switch, administer the drug, or deploy the feature.

Surgery on the Graph

Here is Pearl's brilliant insight: the do-operator corresponds to a specific surgery on the causal DAG. When you do(X = x), you:

  1. Delete all incoming edges to X (because you are overriding whatever normally causes X)
  2. Fix X to the value x
  3. Leave everything else in the graph untouched
# Original DAG:
#   Z → X → Y
#   Z ------→ Y
# (Z confounds X and Y)

# After do(X = x): we "cut" the arrow Z → X
#   Z    X=x → Y
#   Z --------→ Y
# Now X is fixed. Z still affects Y directly, but NOT through X anymore.
# The spurious path Z → X → Y is broken.

# Observational:  P(Y | X=x) mixes the causal effect with confounding
# Interventional: P(Y | do(X=x)) gives the PURE causal effect

# In the "mutilated" graph:
# P(Y | do(X=x)) = Σ_z P(Y | X=x, Z=z) * P(Z=z)
#                 ≠ P(Y | X=x)  in general!

The magic: in the mutilated graph (with incoming edges to X deleted), the confounding path is severed. Z still affects Y directly, but it no longer influences our estimate through X. What remains is the pure causal effect of X on Y.

The do-Operator in One Sentence

P(Y|X) uses the natural distribution of X. P(Y|do(X)) replaces the natural mechanism generating X with your intervention. If there are no confounders, they are equal. If there are confounders, they diverge — and the difference is exactly the bias that naive ML models carry.

The Backdoor Criterion

Okay, so we need P(Y | do(X)). We know conceptually what it means (surgery on the graph). But can we estimate it from observational data — data where nobody did any intervening?

Often, yes. The backdoor criterion tells you which variables to control for. A set of variables Z satisfies the backdoor criterion relative to (X, Y) if:

  1. No variable in Z is a descendant of X
  2. Z blocks every "backdoor path" from X to Y — that is, every path that starts with an arrow into X

If such a Z exists, the backdoor adjustment formula applies:

P(Y | do(X=x)) = Σz P(Y | X=x, Z=z) · P(Z=z)

In words: average the conditional distribution P(Y|X,Z) over the marginal distribution of Z. This is not the same as simply conditioning on Z — the distinction is in using P(Z) rather than P(Z|X).

A Concrete Example

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

def backdoor_adjustment(data, treatment='X', outcome='Y', confounders=['Z']):
    """Estimate causal effect via backdoor adjustment.

    For each stratum of confounders, compute E[Y|X=1,Z=z] - E[Y|X=0,Z=z],
    then weight by P(Z=z).
    """
    df = pd.DataFrame(data)

    # Discretize confounders into bins for stratification
    for c in confounders:
        df[f'{c}_bin'] = pd.qcut(df[c], q=10, labels=False, duplicates='drop')

    bin_cols = [f'{c}_bin' for c in confounders]
    strata = df.groupby(bin_cols)

    causal_effect = 0
    total_weight = 0

    for name, group in strata:
        if len(group) < 5:
            continue
        treated = group[group[treatment] > group[treatment].median()]
        control = group[group[treatment] <= group[treatment].median()]
        if len(treated) == 0 or len(control) == 0:
            continue
        effect = treated[outcome].mean() - control[outcome].mean()
        weight = len(group)
        causal_effect += effect * weight
        total_weight += weight

    return causal_effect / total_weight if total_weight > 0 else None

# Generate confounded data
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
n = 10000
Z = rng.normal(0, 1, n)
X = 0.8 * Z + rng.normal(0, 1, n)
Y = 0.5 * X + 1.2 * Z + rng.normal(0, 0.5, n)  # true effect of X: 0.5

data = {'X': X, 'Y': Y, 'Z': Z}

# Naive correlation (biased)
naive = np.corrcoef(X, Y)[0, 1]
print(f"Naive correlation X-Y: {naive:.3f}")

# Backdoor-adjusted estimate
adjusted = backdoor_adjustment(data)
print(f"Backdoor-adjusted effect: {adjusted:.3f}")
print(f"True causal effect: 0.500")
Don't Just "Control for Everything"

A common reflex in regression is to throw every available variable in as a control. This is dangerous. If you control for a mediator (a variable on the causal path from X to Y), you block part of the causal effect. If you control for a collider, you introduce bias that was not there before. The backdoor criterion tells you exactly which variables to control for — no more, no less. Draw the DAG first.

Practical Tools

You do not have to implement all of this from scratch. Three Python libraries cover most practical causal inference needs:

DoWhy in Action

DoWhy enforces a four-step workflow: Model (specify DAG), Identify (find estimand using causal graph), Estimate (compute the effect), Refute (stress-test). This structure forces you to be explicit about your causal assumptions.

import dowhy
from dowhy import CausalModel
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

# Generate data with known causal structure
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
n = 5000
Z = rng.normal(0, 1, n)
X = 0.6 * Z + rng.normal(0, 1, n)
Y = 0.5 * X + 0.8 * Z + rng.normal(0, 0.5, n)
df = pd.DataFrame({'Z': Z, 'X': X, 'Y': Y})

# Step 1: MODEL — specify the causal DAG
model = CausalModel(
    data=df,
    treatment='X',
    outcome='Y',
    common_causes=['Z'],  # Z is a confounder
    graph="digraph { Z -> X; Z -> Y; X -> Y; }"
)

# Step 2: IDENTIFY — find the causal estimand
identified = model.identify_effect()
print(identified)  # shows backdoor adjustment via Z

# Step 3: ESTIMATE — compute the causal effect
estimate = model.estimate_effect(
    identified,
    method_name="backdoor.linear_regression"
)
print(f"Estimated causal effect: {estimate.value:.3f}")
print(f"True causal effect: 0.500")

# Step 4: REFUTE — stress-test the estimate
refutation = model.refute_estimate(
    identified,
    estimate,
    method_name="random_common_cause"  # add a random confounder
)
print(refutation)  # if estimate changes a lot, your model is fragile

The refutation step is what makes DoWhy special. It automatically runs sensitivity analyses: "What if there is an unobserved confounder? How strong would it need to be to nullify the result?" This is exactly the right question to ask, and most ad-hoc analyses skip it.

Counterfactuals — "What Would Have Happened?"

Pearl describes three levels of causal reasoning, which he calls the Ladder of Causation:

RungLevelQuestionExample
1 Association What if I see? "Customers who buy X also buy Y" (standard ML)
2 Intervention What if I do? "If I recommend X, will the customer buy Y?" (do-operator)
3 Counterfactual What if I had done differently? "The customer bought Y. Would they have bought it had I not recommended X?"

Most ML lives on rung 1. The do-operator takes us to rung 2. Counterfactuals take us to rung 3.

Counterfactuals are about specific individuals, not populations. We observe that Alice took the drug and recovered. The counterfactual question: "Would Alice have recovered had she not taken the drug?" This is fundamentally unobservable — you cannot rewind the universe. But with a structural causal model, you can reason about it:

  1. Abduction: Use Alice's observed data to infer her specific noise terms (what makes Alice, Alice)
  2. Action: Modify the model (remove the drug in the structural equation)
  3. Prediction: Propagate through the modified model with Alice's specific noise terms

This three-step process — abduction, action, prediction — is how structural causal models answer counterfactual questions. It powers applications like explainable AI ("this loan was rejected; what minimum income change would have flipped the decision?") and legal reasoning ("but for the defendant's action, would the harm have occurred?").

Why Counterfactuals Cannot Come From Data Alone

Two different structural causal models can produce identical observational distributions and identical interventional distributions, yet give different counterfactual answers. Counterfactuals require the strongest assumptions — you need the full structural equations, not just the DAG. This is why Pearl insists that "no causes in, no causes out." You must bring causal assumptions to get causal answers.

A/B Testing as Causal Inference

If you have run an A/B test, congratulations — you have already done causal inference. You just did it the expensive way.

A randomized controlled trial (RCT) is the gold standard because randomization physically implements the do-operator. When you randomly assign users to treatment and control groups, you sever every incoming arrow to the treatment variable. No confounder — observed or unobserved — can create a backdoor path, because the treatment was determined by a coin flip that does not care about anything else.

import numpy as np

def run_ab_test(n_per_group=1000, true_effect=0.05, seed=42):
    """Simulate an A/B test — the physical do-operator."""
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)

    # Confounders exist — but randomization makes them irrelevant
    user_engagement = rng.normal(0, 1, 2 * n_per_group)  # confounder
    user_experience = rng.normal(0, 1, 2 * n_per_group)  # confounder

    # RANDOM assignment — this is the key!
    # This is equivalent to do(treatment = 0 or 1)
    treatment = np.array([0]*n_per_group + [1]*n_per_group)
    rng.shuffle(treatment)

    # Outcome depends on confounders AND treatment
    conversion = (
        0.10                            # base rate
        + 0.03 * user_engagement        # confounder effect
        + 0.02 * user_experience        # confounder effect
        + true_effect * treatment       # TRUE causal effect
        + rng.normal(0, 0.01, 2*n_per_group)  # noise
    )

    # Because treatment was randomized, simple difference-in-means
    # gives the CAUSAL effect (confounders cancel out)
    treated = conversion[treatment == 1]
    control = conversion[treatment == 0]

    estimated_effect = treated.mean() - control.mean()
    se = np.sqrt(treated.var()/len(treated) + control.var()/len(control))

    print(f"True effect:      {true_effect:.4f}")
    print(f"Estimated effect: {estimated_effect:.4f}")
    print(f"95% CI:           [{estimated_effect - 1.96*se:.4f}, "
          f"{estimated_effect + 1.96*se:.4f}]")
    return estimated_effect

run_ab_test()

So why bother with all the DAGs and do-operators if we can just run A/B tests?

  • Cost: A/B tests require you to actually intervene. Sometimes this is expensive, slow, or unethical.
  • Retrospective questions: You have historical data. You cannot go back in time to randomize.
  • Speed: You want to answer dozens of causal questions. Running a separate A/B test for each takes months.
  • Spillover effects: One user's treatment affects another user's outcome (network effects). Randomization does not cleanly solve this.

Causal inference from observational data is the attempt to get A/B-test-quality answers without the A/B test. It requires stronger assumptions, but when those assumptions hold, it works.

Common Pitfalls

Collider Bias (Berkson's Paradox)

We mentioned this briefly, but it deserves a worked example because it trips up even experienced researchers.

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

def demonstrate_collider_bias(n=10000, seed=42):
    """Show how conditioning on a collider creates spurious correlation."""
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)

    # True DAG: Talent → Success ← Attractiveness
    # Talent and Attractiveness are INDEPENDENT
    talent = rng.normal(0, 1, n)
    attractiveness = rng.normal(0, 1, n)

    # Success (the collider) depends on both
    success = talent + attractiveness + rng.normal(0, 0.5, n)

    # In the full population: talent and attractiveness are independent
    full_corr = np.corrcoef(talent, attractiveness)[0, 1]
    print(f"Full population correlation: {full_corr:.3f}")  # ≈ 0

    # Among successful people (condition on collider):
    successful = success > np.percentile(success, 75)
    cond_corr = np.corrcoef(talent[successful],
                            attractiveness[successful])[0, 1]
    print(f"Among successful people:     {cond_corr:.3f}")  # negative!

    # Among successful people, talent and attractiveness appear
    # NEGATIVELY correlated — if you're successful and not talented,
    # you must be attractive (and vice versa).

demonstrate_collider_bias()

The lesson: among successful people, talent and attractiveness appear negatively correlated — not because they actually are, but because we selected on their common effect. This happens whenever you condition on a collider, whether through explicit filtering, regression controls, or sample selection.

Simpson's Paradox

A trend that appears in several groups reverses when the groups are combined. This is not a statistical curiosity — it is a causal problem. The correct action (aggregate or stratify?) depends on the causal DAG.

import pandas as pd

# Classic example: a drug appears harmful overall but helpful in each group

data = {
    'Group':    ['Mild']*4 + ['Severe']*4,
    'Treatment':['Drug','Drug','No Drug','No Drug'] * 2,
    'Outcome':  ['Recovered','Died','Recovered','Died'] * 2,
    'Count':    [81, 9,       # Drug + Mild: 90% recovery
                 234, 26,     # No Drug + Mild: 90% recovery
                 192, 48,     # Drug + Severe: 80% recovery
                 55, 45]      # No Drug + Severe: 55% recovery
}

df = pd.DataFrame(data)

# Within each severity group, the drug HELPS
# But overall, drug patients have worse outcomes...
# Because severe patients are MORE LIKELY to get the drug!
# Severity is a confounder → stratified analysis is correct.

print("=== Drug helps within each group ===")
print("Mild:   Drug 90% vs No Drug 90%")
print("Severe: Drug 80% vs No Drug 55%")
print()
print("=== But aggregate looks bad ===")
drug_total = 81 + 192
drug_recovered = 81 + 192
print(f"Drug overall:    {(81+192)/(81+9+192+48)*100:.0f}% recovery")
print(f"No Drug overall: {(234+55)/(234+26+55+45)*100:.0f}% recovery")

The key insight: whether to look at the aggregate or stratified data is not a statistical question — it is a causal question. If severity is a confounder (it causes both treatment assignment and outcome), you should stratify. If it were a mediator, you should not. The DAG tells you which.

Simpson's Paradox Is a Causal Problem

There is no purely statistical rule that resolves Simpson's paradox. "Always stratify" is wrong. "Always aggregate" is wrong. The answer depends on the causal structure. This is why causal inference exists — it provides the framework to answer questions that statistics alone cannot.

Putting It All Together

Here is the causal inference workflow, from question to answer:

  1. State your causal question — What is the effect of X on Y?
  2. Draw the DAG — Encode your domain knowledge about what causes what. Be honest about what you do and do not know.
  3. Check identifiability — Can P(Y|do(X)) be expressed in terms of observational distributions? Use the backdoor criterion (or front-door, or instrumental variables for harder cases).
  4. Estimate — Use the appropriate formula or method (regression adjustment, inverse propensity weighting, doubly robust estimators, matching).
  5. Refute — Sensitivity analysis. How strong would an unobserved confounder need to be to change your conclusion?

  • Articulate the difference between P(Y|X) and P(Y|do(X)) and why it matters for decision-making
