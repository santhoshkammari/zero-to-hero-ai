Causal Inference in ML

TL;DR

Standard ML finds correlations. Causal inference asks why — it lets you reason about interventions ("What happens if we force X to change?") and counterfactuals ("What would have happened?"). Using Structural Causal Models, the do-operator, and tools like DoWhy and EconML, you can move from prediction to understanding actual cause-and-effect relationships in data. This matters whenever you need to make decisions, not forecasts.

Why Correlation Breaks Down

Here's a story every data team has lived through. A retail company notices that ice cream sales are strongly correlated with drowning deaths. The correlation is real — you can plot it, compute the r-value, even build a regression model that predicts drownings from ice cream purchases. Should the company lobby to ban ice cream to save lives?

Of course not. Both variables are driven by a hidden third factor: summer heat. Hot weather makes people buy ice cream and go swimming, which increases drowning risk. The ice cream itself causes nothing. This is the fundamental problem that causal inference solves — it gives you a mathematical framework to distinguish "X and Y move together" from "X actually makes Y happen."

Every ML model you've trained so far is a correlation machine. A neural network that predicts hospital readmission doesn't tell you why patients are readmitted. A recommender system that notices "users who buy diapers also buy beer" doesn't know if one causes the other. The moment you want to intervene — change a treatment plan, redesign a feature, set a price — you need causal reasoning. Predictions tell you what to expect. Causes tell you what to do.

Structural Causal Models (SCM)

A Structural Causal Model is the formal language for encoding cause-and-effect relationships. Think of it as a blueprint for how the world generates data. An SCM has three components: a set of variables, a set of structural equations that describe how each variable is determined by its causes, and a set of noise terms representing everything else the model doesn't capture.

Causal DAGs

The visual face of an SCM is a Causal DAG (Directed Acyclic Graph). Each node is a variable. Each arrow points from cause to effect. "Directed" means arrows have direction. "Acyclic" means you can't follow arrows in a circle back to where you started — causes flow forward in time.

Consider a medical example. A doctor decides whether to prescribe a drug based on how sick the patient is. The drug may help recovery. Severity of illness also directly affects recovery. Draw it: Severity → Drug, Severity → Recovery, Drug → Recovery. Severity is a confounder — it influences both the treatment (drug) and the outcome (recovery). If you ignore it, you'll get a biased estimate of the drug's effect, because sicker patients are more likely to get the drug and less likely to recover, making the drug look harmful.

Structural Equations

Behind the DAG, each node has a structural equation that specifies exactly how it's computed from its parents plus noise. For our medical example: 

# Structural equations (simplified)
# U_s, U_d, U_r are independent noise terms

Severity = U_s
Drug     = f_d(Severity, U_d)    # doctor's decision depends on severity
Recovery = f_r(Drug, Severity, U_r)  # recovery depends on drug AND severity

These aren't statistical regressions — they're claims about the mechanism that generates the data. The equation for Recovery says the drug has a direct causal pathway to recovery, separate from severity's effect. That's a modeling commitment, not something you learn from data alone.

Exogenous and Endogenous Variables

Variables in an SCM come in two flavors. Endogenous variables are the ones your model explains — they appear on the left side of some structural equation. Exogenous variables are the unexplained noise terms (U_s, U_d, U_r) that represent background factors outside the model. They're the "given" randomness of the world. In our medical example, Severity, Drug, and Recovery are endogenous. The U terms are exogenous.

Interventions and the do-Operator

Here is where causal inference diverges from statistics. The do-operator, written do(X = x), represents a physical intervention — you reach into the system and force a variable to a specific value, breaking all arrows that point into it.

Think of it as "surgery on the graph." When you write P(Recovery | do(Drug = 1)), you're asking: "What's the probability of recovery if we force every patient to take the drug?" This is different from P(Recovery | Drug = 1), which asks: "Among patients who happened to take the drug, what fraction recovered?" The second mixes in the confounder — patients who took the drug were sicker. The first removes the confounder by cutting the arrow from Severity to Drug.

ℹ️ Key Insight

The difference between P(Y | X) (conditioning) and P(Y | do(X)) (intervening) is the central distinction in causal inference. Conditioning filters data. Intervening changes the data-generating process itself. Confusing the two is the root cause of most "correlation ≠ causation" mistakes.

Identification of Causal Effects

The big question: can you compute P(Y | do(X)) from observational data alone, without actually running an experiment? This is called identification. Sometimes the answer is yes, sometimes no — it depends entirely on the structure of your causal graph.

Backdoor Criterion and Adjustment Formula

The backdoor criterion is the most common identification strategy. A set of variables Z satisfies the backdoor criterion relative to (X, Y) if: (1) no node in Z is a descendant of X, and (2) Z blocks every path between X and Y that has an arrow into X (these are the "backdoor paths" that create confounding).

In our medical example, Severity satisfies the backdoor criterion for the effect of Drug on Recovery. The adjustment formula then lets you compute the causal effect by summing over the confounder: P(Recovery | do(Drug)) = Σ P(Recovery | Drug, Severity) × P(Severity). You condition on severity within each stratum, then average across severity levels. This is what "controlling for confounders" actually means — it's justified by the backdoor criterion, not by hand-waving.

Frontdoor Criterion

What if you can't measure the confounder? The frontdoor criterion sometimes saves you. If the causal effect of X on Y is fully mediated through a variable M, and there's no unblocked backdoor path from X to M, you can identify the effect through M. Classic example: smoking → tar deposits → cancer, where genetic predisposition confounds smoking and cancer but doesn't directly affect tar. You can identify the effect of smoking on cancer through the tar mediator.

do-Calculus (Three Rules)

Pearl's do-calculus provides three rules for manipulating expressions involving do() operators. Rule 1 lets you add or remove observations. Rule 2 lets you convert interventions into observations (or vice versa) when certain paths are blocked. Rule 3 lets you remove interventions when the variable has no causal effect. Together, these three rules are complete — if a causal effect can be identified from observational data at all, do-calculus can derive the formula. The backdoor and frontdoor criteria are special cases of do-calculus.

Instrumental Variables

An instrumental variable (IV) is a variable that affects the treatment but has no direct effect on the outcome except through the treatment. In economics, distance to a college is used as an instrument for education when studying education's effect on earnings — distance affects whether you attend college but doesn't directly affect your salary (arguably). IVs let you identify causal effects even with unmeasured confounders, though they require strong assumptions.

Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)

Randomized Controlled Trials are the gold standard because randomization physically implements the do-operator. When you randomly assign patients to drug or placebo, you sever the arrow from Severity to Drug — the treatment is no longer caused by patient characteristics. That's why P(Y | X) equals P(Y | do(X)) in an RCT. But RCTs are expensive, slow, and sometimes unethical. That's the entire motivation for the observational methods above.

Treatment Effect Estimation

Once you've identified what to adjust for, you need to estimate the magnitude of the causal effect from finite data. Here's where ML meets causal inference.

Average Treatment Effect (ATE)

The Average Treatment Effect is the expected difference in outcomes between treated and untreated populations: ATE = E[Y(1)] - E[Y(0)], where Y(1) is the outcome if treated and Y(0) if untreated. The fundamental problem of causal inference is that you only ever observe one of these for each individual — you can't simultaneously give and not give someone a drug.

Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE)

The Conditional Average Treatment Effect asks: does the treatment work differently for different people? CATE(x) = E[Y(1) - Y(0) | X = x]. A drug might work well for young patients and poorly for elderly ones. Estimating CATE is called heterogeneous treatment effect estimation, and it's where modern ML methods really shine.

Propensity Score Matching

Propensity score matching is a classic technique. The propensity score is the probability of receiving treatment given observed covariates: e(x) = P(T=1 | X=x). The idea: match each treated individual with an untreated individual who had a similar probability of being treated. If the propensity scores are the same, the two individuals are "comparable" in terms of observed confounders — any difference in outcome is attributable to the treatment.

Double Machine Learning (DML)

Double Machine Learning, developed by Chernozhukov et al., is a modern approach that uses ML models for nuisance parameter estimation while maintaining valid statistical inference for the causal parameter. The key idea: use one ML model to predict the outcome from confounders, another to predict the treatment from confounders, then estimate the causal effect from the residuals. Cross-fitting (like cross-validation) prevents overfitting bias. 

from econml.dml import DML
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingRegressor, GradientBoostingClassifier

# Double ML: use flexible ML models for nuisance estimation
# while getting valid causal effect estimates
dml = DML(
    model_y=GradientBoostingRegressor(),   # predict outcome from confounders
    model_t=GradientBoostingClassifier(),  # predict treatment from confounders
    model_final=GradientBoostingRegressor(),
    discrete_treatment=True
)

# X = confounders, T = treatment (0/1), Y = outcome
dml.fit(Y=outcome, T=treatment, X=confounders, W=confounders)

# Estimate the average treatment effect
ate = dml.ate(X=confounders)
print(f"Estimated ATE: {ate:.4f}")

# Get individual-level treatment effects (CATE)
cate = dml.effect(X=new_patient_features)
print(f"Treatment effect for this patient: {cate[0]:.4f}")

CausalForest for Heterogeneous Effects

CausalForest, from Athey and Imbens, adapts random forests to estimate heterogeneous treatment effects. Instead of splitting to minimize prediction error, causal forests split to maximize the difference in treatment effects across the two child nodes. The result is a model that tells you not only "does the drug work on average?" but "which patients benefit most?" 

from econml.dml import CausalForestDML

# CausalForest: discover who benefits most from treatment
cf = CausalForestDML(
    model_y=GradientBoostingRegressor(),
    model_t=GradientBoostingClassifier(),
    discrete_treatment=True,
    n_estimators=200,
    random_state=42
)

cf.fit(Y=outcome, T=treatment, X=features, W=confounders)

# Heterogeneous effects: how does the treatment effect vary?
effects = cf.effect(X=features)
print(f"Treatment effects range: [{effects.min():.3f}, {effects.max():.3f}]")

# Confidence intervals for individual treatment effects
lb, ub = cf.effect_interval(X=features, alpha=0.05)
print(f"95% CI for first patient: [{lb[0]:.3f}, {ub[0]:.3f}]")
When to Use What

Use propensity score matching when you want simplicity and interpretability with moderate-dimensional confounders. Use Double ML when you have high-dimensional confounders and want valid confidence intervals. Use CausalForest when you care about who benefits from treatment, not only the average effect. All three assume you've measured the right confounders — none can fix unmeasured confounding.

Causal Discovery

Everything so far assumed you know the causal graph. But what if you don't? Causal discovery algorithms attempt to learn the graph structure from data. This is harder than it sounds — multiple graphs can produce the same observed distribution.

PC Algorithm

The PC algorithm (named after its creators Peter Spirtes and Clark Glymour) starts with a fully connected undirected graph and progressively removes edges by testing for conditional independence. If X and Y are independent given some set Z, remove the edge between them. Then it orients edges using v-structures: if X → Z ← Y and there's no edge between X and Y, the arrows into Z must both point inward. The PC algorithm outputs a CPDAG (Completed Partially Directed Acyclic Graph) — a set of graphs that are all consistent with the data.

GES (Greedy Equivalence Search)

GES takes a score-based approach. It starts with an empty graph and greedily adds edges that improve a scoring function (typically BIC), then greedily removes edges that improve it further. GES is consistent — with enough data, it recovers the correct equivalence class. It tends to be more robust than PC in practice because it doesn't rely on a sequence of fragile conditional independence tests.

Granger Causality (and Its Limitations)

Granger causality is popular in time series: X "Granger-causes" Y if past values of X help predict Y beyond what past values of Y alone provide. It's easy to compute (it's a hypothesis test on regression coefficients), but be warned: Granger causality is really about predictive precedence, not true causation. A rooster crowing Granger-causes sunrise because it happens first — but the rooster doesn't make the sun rise. Granger causality can't handle confounders and should be considered a useful screening tool, not a definitive causal claim.

NOTEARS (Continuous Optimization)

NOTEARS (Non-combinatorial Optimization via Trace Exponential and Augmented lagrangian for Structure learning) reframes causal discovery as a continuous optimization problem. Traditional approaches search over discrete graph structures — combinatorially explosive. NOTEARS instead optimizes a weighted adjacency matrix subject to an acyclicity constraint expressed as a smooth equality constraint: tr(e^{W ∘ W}) - d = 0. This converts structure learning into something standard optimizers can handle. It doesn't scale perfectly, but it opened the door to gradient-based causal discovery.

⚠️ Warning

Causal discovery from purely observational data is fundamentally limited. You can only recover the graph up to its Markov equivalence class — multiple graphs that encode the same conditional independencies. To pin down the true graph, you typically need interventional data, domain knowledge, or time-ordering information. Don't trust a causal discovery algorithm's output without serious domain validation.

Counterfactuals

Counterfactuals are the deepest level of causal reasoning. They answer questions like: "This specific patient took the drug and recovered — would they have recovered without the drug?" This is about an individual in a specific situation, not a population average.

Pearl's Three-Step Counterfactual Procedure

Pearl defined a precise three-step procedure for computing counterfactuals from an SCM:

Step 1 — Abduction: Use the observed evidence to infer the values of the exogenous (noise) variables. If you know a patient was severely ill and took the drug and recovered, you can back-compute what the noise terms U_s, U_d, U_r must have been.

Step 2 — Action: Modify the model to reflect the counterfactual intervention. Replace the structural equation for the treatment: set Drug = 0 (the patient didn't take the drug), breaking all arrows into Drug.

Step 3 — Prediction: Use the modified model with the inferred noise values to compute the counterfactual outcome. With the same illness severity (same U_s) and same recovery noise (same U_r), but Drug = 0, what would Recovery be?

The key insight is that the noise terms capture everything specific to this individual. By holding them fixed and changing only the treatment, you isolate the causal effect for this particular person in this particular situation.

Counterfactual Fairness

Counterfactual fairness applies this reasoning to algorithmic fairness. A decision is counterfactually fair if it would have been the same had the individual belonged to a different demographic group, all else being equal. Formally: P(Ŷ_{A=a} | X=x, A=a) = P(Ŷ_{A=a'} | X=x, A=a). Would this loan applicant have been approved if their race were different, but everything causally unrelated to race stayed the same? This is a much stronger fairness criterion than demographic parity or equalized odds because it reasons about individual-level causation.

Counterfactual Explanations

Counterfactual explanations answer: "What's the smallest change to the input that would flip the model's decision?" For a loan denial: "If your income were $5,000 higher, the loan would have been approved." These are popular in explainable AI because they're actionable — they tell people what to change, not how the model works internally. Libraries like DiCE generate diverse counterfactual explanations automatically.

Causal Phenomena and Paradoxes

Simpson's Paradox

Simpson's Paradox is the phenomenon where a trend that appears in several groups of data reverses when the groups are combined. Here's a concrete example. A hospital tests a drug. Among mild cases: 80% recovery with drug, 90% without. Among severe cases: 30% recovery with drug, 40% without. In both groups, the drug looks harmful. But combined: 70% recovery with drug, 60% without — the drug looks helpful! How?

The trick: doctors gave the drug preferentially to severe cases. Severe cases dominate the treatment group, pulling its overall average down. When you combine the groups, you're comparing a mostly-severe treated group with a mostly-mild untreated group. The causal graph tells you the right answer: since severity is a confounder, you should stratify (look at each group separately). The within-group numbers are the correct causal estimate. Simpson's Paradox is not a statistical curiosity — it's a failure to account for confounding structure, and causal reasoning resolves it.

Confounding and Selection Bias

Confounding occurs when a common cause of treatment and outcome creates a spurious association. Selection bias (also called collider bias) is subtler: it occurs when you condition on a common effect of two variables. If talent and luck both cause success, and you study only successful people, you'll find a negative correlation between talent and luck — because within the "successful" group, less of one must be compensated by more of the other. Conditioning on a collider opens a path that was blocked, creating a spurious association. The fix: know your graph, and be careful about what you condition on.

Independent Causal Mechanisms (ICM) Principle

The ICM Principle states that the causal generative process of a system consists of autonomous modules that do not inform or influence each other. Changing the mechanism that generates X should not change the mechanism that generates Y given X. This principle underlies transfer learning and domain adaptation — if the causal mechanism P(Y | X) is stable across domains but P(X) shifts, models that learn the causal direction should generalize better than those that memorize the joint distribution.

Causal Reasoning for OOD Generalization

Standard ML models fail under distribution shift because they exploit spurious correlations. A cow classifier trained on photos where cows are always on grass will fail when it sees a cow on a beach — it learned "grass → cow," not "cow features → cow." Causal reasoning suggests learning invariant predictors: features whose relationship to the label is stable across environments. Methods like IRM (Invariant Risk Minimization) formalize this — they look for representations where the optimal predictor is the same across training environments. The intuition: causal features are invariant because they reflect the true mechanism, while spurious features change across settings.

ℹ️ Key Insight

The causal perspective reframes the entire ML generalization problem. Instead of asking "How do I regularize my model to perform well on test data?" you ask "Which features reflect the true causal mechanism, and which are environmental artifacts?" Models that learn causal features are inherently more robust because causal mechanisms, by the ICM principle, are stable across domains. This is the theoretical foundation for robust ML beyond i.i.d. assumptions.

Putting It Together with DoWhy

Microsoft's DoWhy library implements the full causal inference workflow: model, identify, estimate, and refute. Here's a complete example: 

import dowhy
from dowhy import CausalModel
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

# Simulate observational data with a confounder
np.random.seed(42)
n = 5000
severity = np.random.normal(0, 1, n)                          # confounder
treatment = (0.6 * severity + np.random.normal(0, 1, n) > 0).astype(int)  # influenced by severity
outcome = 0.4 * treatment - 0.8 * severity + np.random.normal(0, 0.5, n)  # true ATE = 0.4

df = pd.DataFrame({"severity": severity, "treatment": treatment, "outcome": outcome})

# Step 1: Model — specify the causal graph
model = CausalModel(
    data=df,
    treatment="treatment",
    outcome="outcome",
    graph="""digraph {
        severity -> treatment;
        severity -> outcome;
        treatment -> outcome;
    }"""
)

# Step 2: Identify — find a valid adjustment strategy
identified = model.identify_effect(proceed_when_unidentifiable=True)
print(f"Estimand type: {identified.estimands['backdoor']}")

# Step 3: Estimate — compute the causal effect
estimate = model.estimate_effect(
    identified,
    method_name="backdoor.propensity_score_matching"
)
print(f"Estimated ATE: {estimate.value:.4f}")  # should be close to 0.4

# Step 4: Refute — stress-test the estimate
refutation = model.refute_estimate(
    identified, estimate,
    method_name="random_common_cause"       # add a random confounder
)
print(f"Refutation result: {refutation.new_effect:.4f}")  # should stay near 0.4
print(f"p-value: {refutation.refutation_result['p_value']:.4f}")

The four-step workflow — model, identify, estimate, refute — is the discipline that separates rigorous causal analysis from ad hoc adjustment. The refutation step is especially important: it checks whether your estimate is sensitive to unobserved confounders, random noise, or violations of your modeling assumptions. A causal estimate that survives refutation is one you can trust more, but never completely — unobserved confounders remain the Achilles' heel of observational causal inference.

The Bottom Line

Causal inference doesn't replace ML — it complements it. Use ML for prediction when all you need is "what will happen?" Use causal inference when you need "what should I do?" or "why did this happen?" The best data science teams combine both: ML models for operational predictions, causal models for strategic decisions. When the stakes are high — medical treatments, policy changes, pricing decisions — you owe it to your users to go beyond correlation and think causally.
