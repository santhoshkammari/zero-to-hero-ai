Sparse Models and Compressed Sensing

Chapter 17: Learning Theory & Advanced ML 4 subtopics
TL;DR

Most real-world signals are sparse — they can be described by far fewer numbers than their raw representation suggests. Sparse models exploit this by hunting for the simplest explanation that fits the data. Compressed sensing goes further: if a signal is sparse, you don't even need to measure it fully — a small set of random measurements is enough to reconstruct it perfectly. This has revolutionized MRI imaging, radar, and genomics. The algorithmic backbone is L1 minimization (LASSO and friends), which turns an impossible combinatorial search into a tractable convex optimization.

Sparse Representations

Here's the setup. You have a signal — maybe an image, an audio clip, or a gene expression profile. It lives in a high-dimensional space. A 1-megapixel image has a million dimensions. But is it truly a million degrees of freedom? Of course not. Most natural images can be described by far fewer numbers if you pick the right basis. A sunset photo might need only a few thousand wavelet coefficients to look nearly perfect. The rest are essentially zero.

A vector is called sparse when most of its entries are zero (or negligibly small). If a vector of length n has only k non-zero entries, we say it is k-sparse. When k << n, you have a massive compression opportunity. The challenge is finding the right representation — the right basis or dictionary — where the signal becomes sparse.

Sparsity in Signal Processing

Signal processing has known about this for decades. The Fourier transform makes sinusoidal signals sparse: a pure tone is a single spike in frequency space. Wavelets make piecewise-smooth signals sparse: edges and textures need only a handful of wavelet coefficients. The JPEG and MP3 codecs exploit this — they transform the signal, keep the large coefficients, and throw away the rest. That's lossy compression, and it works because natural signals are approximately sparse in some transform domain.

But what if, instead of compressing after the fact, we could design our entire measurement process around sparsity? That's the leap compressed sensing makes, and we'll get there. First, let's formalize what "finding the sparsest signal" actually means.

The L0 "Norm" (NP-Hard)

The most direct measure of sparsity is the L0 "norm": count the number of non-zero entries. It's not technically a norm (it violates homogeneity), but everyone calls it that anyway. The sparse recovery problem is: given measurements y = Ax, find the sparsest x — the one with the smallest L0 value.

minimize ‖x‖₀   subject to   y = Ax

This looks clean, but it's computationally brutal. L0 minimization is NP-hard. You'd have to try every possible subset of columns of A, solve a least-squares problem for each, and pick the sparsest solution. For a vector of length 1000, that's 2^1000 subsets. Not happening.

L1 Relaxation (Basis Pursuit)

Here's the beautiful trick. Replace L0 with the L1 norm — the sum of absolute values. This is called Basis Pursuit:

minimize ‖x‖₁   subject to   y = Ax

Why does this work? Think geometrically. The L1 ball (the set of vectors with L1 norm ≤ 1) is a diamond shape — it has sharp corners that sit on the coordinate axes. When you grow an L1 ball until it touches the constraint set {x : Ax = y}, the contact point tends to land on a corner. Corners of the L1 ball are sparse vectors. The L2 ball is a smooth sphere with no corners, so it doesn't favor sparsity at all.

The L1 problem is convex. It can be cast as a linear program and solved in polynomial time. Under certain conditions on the matrix A (more on this shortly), L1 minimization recovers the exact same solution as the NP-hard L0 problem. That's one of the most remarkable results in applied mathematics from the past two decades.

Sparse Coding

Sparse coding learns the dictionary itself from data, rather than using a fixed transform like wavelets. You have a collection of signals, and you want to find a dictionary matrix D and sparse coefficient vectors α such that each signal x ≈ Dα with α as sparse as possible.

minimize  Σᵢ ‖xᵢ - Dαᵢ‖² + λ‖αᵢ‖₁
over D and αᵢ

This is solved by alternating: fix D and solve for all α (a LASSO problem), then fix α and update D (a constrained least-squares problem). Algorithms like K-SVD iterate this until convergence. Sparse coding is widely used in image denoising, texture synthesis, and as a feature extractor — the learned dictionary atoms capture recurring patterns in the data, and the sparse codes tell you which patterns are present in each sample.

Compressed Sensing

Now we get to the headline result. Classical sampling theory (Nyquist-Shannon) says you need at least 2B samples per second to faithfully capture a signal with bandwidth B. But what if the signal is sparse? Do you really need all those measurements?

Sub-Nyquist Sampling

The answer is no, and it's not even close. Compressed sensing (CS) says that if your signal is k-sparse in some basis, you can reconstruct it perfectly from roughly m = O(k log(n/k)) random linear measurements. For a 1-million-dimensional signal that's 1000-sparse, that's maybe 10,000–20,000 measurements instead of a million. A 50–100x reduction.

The measurement process is: y = Φx, where Φ is an m × n measurement matrix with m << n. This is a massively underdetermined system — there are infinitely many solutions. But if you add the constraint that x is sparse, the solution becomes unique (under the right conditions on Φ).

Restricted Isometry Property (RIP)

The key condition is the Restricted Isometry Property. A matrix Φ satisfies RIP of order k with constant δₖ if, for every k-sparse vector x:

(1 - δₖ)‖x‖² ≤ ‖Φx‖² ≤ (1 + δₖ)‖x‖²

In plain English: Φ approximately preserves the length of every sparse vector. It doesn't squish them to zero or blow them up. If Φ preserves lengths, it also preserves differences — meaning two distinct sparse signals can't produce the same measurements. That's what makes recovery possible.

The catch: checking whether a given matrix satisfies RIP is itself NP-hard. But random matrices satisfy RIP with high probability. That's the magic.

Measurement Matrices

Two types of random matrices are commonly used. Random Gaussian matrices have entries drawn i.i.d. from N(0, 1/m). Random Bernoulli matrices have entries that are ±1/√m with equal probability. Both satisfy RIP with high probability when m = O(k log(n/k)). Gaussian matrices have slightly better constants but Bernoulli matrices are cheaper to store and multiply with.

In practice, you often can't use fully random matrices — your measurement hardware imposes structure. Partial Fourier matrices (random subsets of Fourier rows) work for MRI, and random convolutions work for optical systems. The theory has been extended to cover many structured random matrices.

Recovery Guarantees

The Candès-Tao theorem (2005) guarantees: if Φ satisfies RIP with δ₂ₖ < √2 - 1, then solving Basis Pursuit recovers every k-sparse signal exactly from y = Φx. For approximately sparse signals (most entries small but not zero), recovery is approximate with bounded error. For noisy measurements y = Φx + e, you use Basis Pursuit Denoising (BPDN): minimize ‖x‖₁ subject to ‖Φx - y‖₂ ≤ ε. The recovery error is proportional to the noise level and the "tail" of the sparse approximation.

Recovery Algorithms

Basis Pursuit (L1 minimization) is the workhorse. Cast it as a linear program or use second-order cone programming (SOCP). Mathematically clean, but can be slow for very large problems.

Matching Pursuit and Orthogonal Matching Pursuit (OMP) are greedy alternatives. At each iteration, OMP finds the column of Φ most correlated with the current residual, adds it to the support set, and solves a least-squares problem on the selected columns. After k iterations (for a k-sparse signal), you have your answer. OMP is fast — O(kmn) per run — but less robust than L1 methods when conditions are marginal.

import numpy as np

def omp(Phi, y, k):
    """Orthogonal Matching Pursuit for sparse recovery."""
    n = Phi.shape[1]
    residual = y.copy()
    support = []
    
    for _ in range(k):
        # Find column most correlated with residual
        correlations = np.abs(Phi.T @ residual)
        j = np.argmax(correlations)
        support.append(j)
        
        # Solve least squares on selected columns
        Phi_s = Phi[:, support]
        x_s = np.linalg.lstsq(Phi_s, y, rcond=None)[0]
        
        # Update residual
        residual = y - Phi_s @ x_s
    
    # Build full sparse solution
    x_hat = np.zeros(n)
    x_hat[support] = x_s
    return x_hat

# Demo: recover a 10-sparse signal from 50 measurements (n=200)
n, k, m = 200, 10, 50
x_true = np.zeros(n)
x_true[np.random.choice(n, k, replace=False)] = np.random.randn(k)
Phi = np.random.randn(m, n) / np.sqrt(m)
y = Phi @ x_true
x_recovered = omp(Phi, y, k)
print(f"Recovery error: {np.linalg.norm(x_true - x_recovered):.2e}")
# Typically ~1e-13 (machine precision)

Iterative Hard Thresholding (IHT) takes a gradient step and then keeps only the k largest entries. It's extremely simple, memory-efficient, and works well in practice. Each iteration is: x ← Hₖ(x + Φᵀ(y - Φx)), where Hₖ keeps the top-k entries and zeros the rest.

Algorithms for Sparse Recovery

The algorithms above are the classical compressed sensing methods. In machine learning, we encounter sparsity in a slightly different wrapper — as regularization. The algorithms overlap heavily, but the framing shifts from "recover a signal" to "fit a model with few features."

LASSO

The LASSO (Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator) is the statistical cousin of Basis Pursuit:

minimize  ½‖y - Xβ‖² + λ‖β‖₁

It's a regression problem with an L1 penalty. The penalty drives coefficients to exactly zero, performing feature selection automatically. The strength of λ controls how sparse the solution is: large λ means more zeros. LASSO was proposed by Robert Tibshirani in 1996, and it's become one of the most widely used tools in statistics and ML. For high-dimensional problems where p >> n (more features than samples), LASSO is often the default starting point.

ISTA and FISTA

ISTA (Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm) solves LASSO by alternating a gradient step on the smooth part (the squared loss) with a proximal step on the non-smooth part (the L1 penalty). The proximal step is the soft-thresholding operator: shrink each coefficient toward zero by λ, and set it to zero if it's already within λ of zero.

def soft_threshold(x, threshold):
    return np.sign(x) * np.maximum(np.abs(x) - threshold, 0)

def ista(X, y, lam, n_iter=1000, lr=None):
    """Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm for LASSO."""
    n, p = X.shape
    if lr is None:
        lr = 1.0 / np.linalg.norm(X.T @ X, ord=2)  # 1/L
    beta = np.zeros(p)
    
    for _ in range(n_iter):
        grad = X.T @ (X @ beta - y)        # gradient of squared loss
        beta = soft_threshold(beta - lr * grad, lam * lr)
    
    return beta

ISTA converges at rate O(1/t) — each iteration is cheap, but you need many of them. FISTA (Fast ISTA) adds a momentum term inspired by Nesterov's accelerated gradient. It achieves O(1/t²) convergence — the same work per iteration but quadratically faster convergence. The only change is maintaining an auxiliary variable that "overshoots" the current iterate:

def fista(X, y, lam, n_iter=1000, lr=None):
    """Fast ISTA with Nesterov momentum."""
    n, p = X.shape
    if lr is None:
        lr = 1.0 / np.linalg.norm(X.T @ X, ord=2)
    beta = np.zeros(p)
    z = beta.copy()
    t_k = 1.0
    
    for _ in range(n_iter):
        grad = X.T @ (X @ z - y)
        beta_new = soft_threshold(z - lr * grad, lam * lr)
        t_new = (1 + np.sqrt(1 + 4 * t_k**2)) / 2
        z = beta_new + ((t_k - 1) / t_new) * (beta_new - beta)
        beta = beta_new
        t_k = t_new
    
    return beta

ADMM

The Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers splits the problem into simpler sub-problems that are easier to solve individually. For LASSO, you introduce an auxiliary variable z = β and solve:

minimize  ½‖y - Xβ‖² + λ‖z‖₁   subject to  β = z

ADMM alternates between updating β (a ridge regression problem — closed form), updating z (soft-thresholding), and updating a dual variable that enforces the constraint β = z. Each step is cheap, and the method handles constraints and non-smooth penalties gracefully. ADMM is the algorithmic Swiss Army knife for convex optimization — it generalizes to distributed settings, consensus problems, and much more.

Proximal Gradient Methods

Proximal gradient methods are the general framework underlying ISTA, FISTA, and many others. The idea: when your objective is f(x) + g(x) where f is smooth and g is non-smooth (like the L1 norm), you can't just take a gradient of the whole thing. Instead, take a gradient step on f, then apply the proximal operator of g — which finds the nearest point that also minimizes g. For L1, the proximal operator is soft-thresholding. For indicator functions of convex sets, it's projection. The framework is clean, modular, and extensible to many structured sparsity problems (group LASSO, nuclear norm, etc.).

Applications

MRI Reconstruction

This is the application that put compressed sensing on the map. Traditional MRI scans are slow because you need to acquire enough k-space (Fourier) samples to satisfy Nyquist. But MRI images are sparse in the wavelet domain. By acquiring a random subset of k-space lines and solving an L1 recovery problem, you can reconstruct high-quality images from 3–8x fewer measurements. That means shorter scan times — from 45 minutes to 10 minutes for some protocols. Lustig et al. (2007) demonstrated this, and compressed sensing MRI is now FDA-approved and used clinically.

Single-Pixel Camera

Rice University built a camera with literally one pixel. It projects the scene through a sequence of random binary patterns (using a digital micromirror device) and records one intensity measurement per pattern. After enough measurements (~50,000 for a megapixel image, versus a million), compressed sensing recovers the full image. It's impractical for visible-light photography, but at wavelengths where sensor arrays are expensive (infrared, terahertz), a single-pixel camera is a real engineering win.

Genomics

Gene expression data is high-dimensional: thousands of genes, but only a few dozen or hundred samples. Most genes are irrelevant to a given disease. LASSO and its variants identify the small set of genes that actually matter — the sparse set of predictive features. Group LASSO can enforce that entire pathways (groups of related genes) are selected or rejected together, incorporating biological structure into the sparsity pattern.

Network Tomography

You want to know the traffic on every link in a large network, but you can only measure end-to-end path delays. The link delays are the unknowns, the path delays are the measurements, and the routing matrix maps links to paths. Network traffic is sparse — most links are uncongested at any given time. Compressed sensing recovers per-link delays from a small number of path measurements, enabling network operators to diagnose problems without instrumenting every router.

The Sparsity Assumption Is Everywhere

Once you internalize sparsity thinking, you see it everywhere in ML. Dropout in neural networks creates sparse activations. L1 regularization in logistic regression does feature selection. Attention mechanisms produce sparse weight distributions. Mixture-of-experts models activate only a sparse subset of parameters. The core idea — most things are simpler than they look, and exploiting that simplicity leads to better algorithms — is one of the deepest themes in machine learning.
