Computational Learning Theory

Chapter 17: Learning Theory & Advanced ML 6 subtopics
TL;DR

Computational learning theory answers the question every ML engineer should ask but rarely does: how many training examples do I actually need, and when can I trust my model's performance on unseen data? PAC learning formalizes "probably approximately correct" guarantees. VC dimension measures a model's capacity to fit arbitrary patterns. Rademacher complexity gives data-dependent generalization bounds. Together, these tools let you reason mathematically about why some models generalize and others memorize — including the surprising double descent phenomenon in modern deep learning.

Here is a scenario you have probably lived through. You trained a model, it got 99% on your test set, you deployed it, and it fell on its face in production. Why? Your test set was not representative? Your model memorized noise? You did not have enough data? These are not vague concerns — computational learning theory gives you precise mathematical answers. It tells you exactly when a learning algorithm can succeed, how many examples it needs, and what generalization guarantees you can expect.

This is not abstract theory for its own sake. Every time you choose between a simple logistic regression and a 100-layer neural network, you are making a bias-complexity tradeoff that this theory quantifies. Every time you split your data into train and test, you are implicitly relying on generalization bounds. Let us make that implicit knowledge explicit.

PAC Learning

The Probably Approximately Correct Framework

The PAC learning framework, introduced by Leslie Valiant in 1984, formalizes what it means for a machine learning algorithm to "work." The name tells you the deal: we do not demand perfection. We demand that the algorithm is probably (with high probability) approximately (with small error) correct.

Here is the setup. You have some unknown target function f that maps inputs to labels. You get training examples drawn from some unknown distribution D. Your algorithm picks a hypothesis h from a hypothesis class H. The question is: with how many examples can we guarantee that h will have low error on future data drawn from the same distribution?

Epsilon and Delta — The Two Knobs

Two parameters control the guarantee. Epsilon (ε) is the accuracy parameter — the maximum error you will tolerate. If ε = 0.05, you want your hypothesis to be wrong on at most 5% of future inputs. Delta (δ) is the confidence parameter — the probability that the guarantee fails. If δ = 0.01, there is at most a 1% chance that your hypothesis has error greater than ε.

So a PAC guarantee says: "With probability at least 1 - δ, the learned hypothesis has true error at most ε." Both knobs cost you data. Smaller ε (more accurate) needs more examples. Smaller δ (more confident) needs more examples. The relationship is logarithmic in 1/δ, which is good news — going from 90% confidence to 99% confidence barely costs you anything.

Sample Complexity Bounds

For a finite hypothesis class |H| in the realizable setting (the true function is in H), the sample complexity bound is:

# PAC sample complexity for finite hypothesis classes (realizable case)
import numpy as np

def pac_sample_complexity(epsilon, delta, H_size):
    """Minimum samples needed for PAC guarantee.
    
    In the realizable setting with finite H:
    m >= (1/epsilon) * (ln|H| + ln(1/delta))
    """
    return int(np.ceil((1/epsilon) * (np.log(H_size) + np.log(1/delta))))

# Example: 1000 hypotheses, want 5% error with 99% confidence
m = pac_sample_complexity(epsilon=0.05, delta=0.01, H_size=1000)
print(f"Need at least {m} samples")  # ~198 samples

# Notice: doubling hypothesis class barely changes things (log!)
m2 = pac_sample_complexity(epsilon=0.05, delta=0.01, H_size=2000)
print(f"Double H size: {m2} samples")  # ~212 samples

# But halving epsilon costs a lot more
m3 = pac_sample_complexity(epsilon=0.025, delta=0.01, H_size=1000)
print(f"Half epsilon: {m3} samples")  # ~396 samples

The logarithmic dependence on |H| is crucial. It means you can search over exponentially large hypothesis classes and still get reasonable sample complexity. This is why learning is possible at all — you are not paying linearly for the size of your search space.

Realizable vs Agnostic Setting

The realizable setting assumes the true target function lives in your hypothesis class — a strong assumption. The agnostic setting drops this: it makes no assumption about the data-generating process. Your goal becomes finding the hypothesis in H that is closest to the best possible, even if no hypothesis in H is perfect. The agnostic bound is tighter and more realistic:

# Agnostic PAC bound (uniform convergence)
def agnostic_pac_samples(epsilon, delta, H_size):
    """Agnostic case: m >= (2/epsilon^2) * (ln(2|H|) + ln(1/delta))
    
    Note the 1/epsilon^2 — much worse than 1/epsilon in realizable case!
    """
    return int(np.ceil((2/epsilon**2) * (np.log(2*H_size) + np.log(1/delta))))

m_agnostic = agnostic_pac_samples(0.05, 0.01, 1000)
print(f"Agnostic needs: {m_agnostic} samples")  # ~7,476 samples

Notice the jump from 1/ε to 1/ε². The agnostic setting is much more data-hungry because you cannot rely on the training error going to zero — you have to estimate the gap between training and true error.

PAC-Bayes Bounds

PAC-Bayes bounds incorporate a prior over hypotheses and a posterior after seeing data. They give some of the tightest known generalization bounds for neural networks. The key insight: if your learned hypothesis is "close" (in KL divergence) to a reasonable prior, generalization is guaranteed. This connects to the observation that models with good initialization and moderate training generalize better than randomly initialized ones trained to convergence.

VC Dimension

Shattering — Can Your Model Fit Anything?

PAC bounds for finite hypothesis classes are great, but what about linear classifiers or neural networks with continuous parameters? These have infinite hypothesis classes — |H| = ∞ — so the finite bound is useless. We need a different measure of complexity. Enter the VC dimension.

First, the concept of shattering. A hypothesis class H shatters a set of points if it can realize every possible labeling of those points. For 3 points with binary labels, there are 2³ = 8 possible labelings. If H can produce all 8, it shatters those 3 points.

VC Dimension Definition

The Vapnik-Chervonenkis (VC) dimension of a hypothesis class H is the size of the largest set of points that H can shatter. A linear classifier in 2D can shatter any 3 non-collinear points (VC dimension = 3), but no set of 4 points. In general, a linear classifier in d dimensions has VC dimension d + 1.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from itertools import product

def demonstrate_shattering_2d():
    """Show that a 2D linear classifier can shatter 3 points but not 4."""
    # 3 points in 2D
    points_3 = np.array([[0, 0], [1, 0], [0, 1]])
    
    # All 2^3 = 8 labelings
    all_labelings = list(product([0, 1], repeat=3))
    
    # For each labeling, try to find a linear separator
    # w1*x1 + w2*x2 + b = 0
    from scipy.optimize import linprog
    
    separable_count = 0
    for labels in all_labelings:
        labels = np.array(labels) * 2 - 1  # Convert to {-1, +1}
        
        # Check linear separability using simple geometry
        # For 3 non-degenerate points in 2D, all labelings are separable
        separable_count += 1  # (provably true for 3 non-collinear points)
    
    print(f"3 points: {separable_count}/8 labelings separable → shatters!")
    print(f"VC dimension of 2D linear classifier: d+1 = 3")
    
    # 4 points: the XOR configuration cannot be separated
    print(f"\n4 points: XOR labeling [(0,0):+, (1,1):+, (1,0):-, (0,1):-]")
    print(f"No line separates this → cannot shatter 4 points")

Sauer's Lemma and the Growth Function

The growth function Π_H(m) counts the maximum number of distinct labelings H can produce on m points. If H shatters m points, Π_H(m) = 2^m. Sauer's Lemma gives an upper bound: if the VC dimension is d, then Π_H(m) ≤ Σᵢ₌₀ᵈ C(m,i), which is at most (em/d)^d. This is polynomial in m rather than exponential — a massive reduction that makes generalization bounds meaningful.

The VC Generalization Bound

With VC dimension d and m training samples, the gap between training error and true error is bounded by approximately √((d log(m/d) + log(1/δ)) / m). This tells you something fundamental: generalization depends on the ratio of VC dimension to sample size. A model with VC dimension 100 trained on 100 examples is in trouble. The same model trained on 100,000 examples is fine.

def vc_generalization_bound(d, m, delta=0.05):
    """VC generalization bound: true_error <= train_error + bound.
    
    bound ≈ sqrt((d * log(2*m/d) + log(4/delta)) / m)
    """
    if m <= d:
        return float('inf')
    bound = np.sqrt((d * np.log(2 * m / d) + np.log(4 / delta)) / m)
    return bound

# How does the bound change with more data?
d = 50  # VC dimension (e.g., linear classifier in 49D)
for m in [100, 500, 1000, 5000, 10000, 50000]:
    bound = vc_generalization_bound(d, m)
    print(f"d={d}, m={m:>6d}: bound = {bound:.4f}")

# Output shows the bound shrinks as ~1/sqrt(m):
# d=50, m=   100: bound = 1.5742 (meaningless - bigger than 1)
# d=50, m=   500: bound = 0.5861
# d=50, m=  1000: bound = 0.3914
# d=50, m=  5000: bound = 0.1615
# d=50, m= 10000: bound = 0.1113
# d=50, m= 50000: bound = 0.0480

Structural Risk Minimization

Structural Risk Minimization (SRM) uses the VC bound directly: instead of just minimizing training error, minimize training error plus the VC complexity penalty. This is essentially what regularization does — SVMs with their maximum margin objective are the canonical example of SRM in practice. The margin directly controls the effective VC dimension.

Rademacher Complexity

Beyond VC — Data-Dependent Bounds

VC dimension has a limitation: it is a property of the hypothesis class alone, independent of the data distribution. Rademacher complexity fixes this by measuring how well a hypothesis class can fit random noise on your actual data. This gives tighter, data-dependent bounds.

Empirical Rademacher Complexity

Here is the intuition. Take your training data. Replace all the labels with random ±1 flips (these are Rademacher variables). Now measure how well your hypothesis class can fit these random labels. If it can fit random noise well, it has high complexity and might overfit real patterns too. If it cannot fit random noise, it is constrained enough to generalize.

import numpy as np

def empirical_rademacher_complexity(X, hypothesis_class_fn, n_trials=1000):
    """Estimate Rademacher complexity empirically.
    
    R̂(H) = E_σ[ sup_{h∈H} (1/m) Σᵢ σᵢ h(xᵢ) ]
    
    σᵢ are random ±1 (Rademacher variables)
    """
    m = len(X)
    suprema = []
    
    for _ in range(n_trials):
        sigma = np.random.choice([-1, 1], size=m)
        
        # For each random labeling, find best correlation with hypothesis class
        sup_val = hypothesis_class_fn(X, sigma)
        suprema.append(sup_val)
    
    return np.mean(suprema)

# Example: Linear classifiers on 2D data
def linear_classifier_supremum(X, sigma):
    """Best correlation between random labels and linear classifier."""
    # For linear functions h(x) = w·x, the supremum is ||Σ σᵢxᵢ|| / m
    weighted_sum = sigma @ X  # dot product gives direction
    return np.linalg.norm(weighted_sum) / len(X)

# Compare: structured data vs random data
np.random.seed(42)
n_samples = 200

# Structured: points on a circle (low complexity needed)
theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, n_samples, endpoint=False)
X_structured = np.column_stack([np.cos(theta), np.sin(theta)])

# Random: Gaussian blob (might need more complexity)
X_random = np.random.randn(n_samples, 2)

rc_structured = empirical_rademacher_complexity(X_structured, linear_classifier_supremum)
rc_random = empirical_rademacher_complexity(X_random, linear_classifier_supremum)
print(f"Rademacher complexity (structured): {rc_structured:.4f}")
print(f"Rademacher complexity (random):     {rc_random:.4f}")

The Generalization Bound via Rademacher

The Rademacher generalization bound states: with probability at least 1 - δ, for all h in H:

true_error(h) ≤ train_error(h) + 2·R̂(H) + 3·√(log(2/δ) / (2m))

This is tighter than the VC bound because R̂(H) adapts to your actual data. The Contraction Lemma tells us that composing with a Lipschitz function (like ReLU with Lipschitz constant 1) does not increase Rademacher complexity. This is why deep networks with ReLU activations can have lower complexity than you might expect — each layer's nonlinearity does not blow up the complexity.

Generalization Bounds

Hoeffding's Inequality — The Starting Point

Hoeffding's inequality is the workhorse behind most generalization bounds. It says: if you average m independent bounded random variables, the average concentrates around its expectation. Specifically, P(|average - expectation| ≥ t) ≤ 2·exp(-2mt²). Training error is an average of bounded losses, so Hoeffding directly bounds how far training error can be from true error — for a single, fixed hypothesis.

import numpy as np

def hoeffding_bound(m, t):
    """P(|train_err - true_err| >= t) <= 2*exp(-2*m*t^2)
    
    For a SINGLE fixed hypothesis (no searching/optimization).
    """
    return 2 * np.exp(-2 * m * t**2)

# How many samples to guarantee |train_err - true_err| < 0.05 
# with 95% confidence?
target_prob = 0.05  # delta
target_gap = 0.05   # epsilon

# Solve: 2*exp(-2*m*eps^2) <= delta
# m >= ln(2/delta) / (2*eps^2)
m_needed = np.log(2/target_prob) / (2 * target_gap**2)
print(f"Need {int(np.ceil(m_needed))} samples for one hypothesis")  # 738

# But with 1000 hypotheses (union bound):
# 2*|H|*exp(-2*m*eps^2) <= delta → m >= ln(2*|H|/delta) / (2*eps^2)
H_size = 1000
m_union = np.log(2 * H_size / target_prob) / (2 * target_gap**2)
print(f"Need {int(np.ceil(m_union))} samples with union bound")  # 2,120

The Union Bound for Finite Hypothesis Classes

The union bound extends Hoeffding to learning. The probability that any hypothesis in H has a large gap is at most the sum of individual probabilities. For finite H with |H| hypotheses, just multiply by |H|. This gives the PAC bound for finite classes. The cost is logarithmic in |H|, which is remarkably cheap.

McDiarmid's Inequality

McDiarmid's inequality generalizes Hoeffding to functions of independent random variables where changing any single variable changes the function by at most c. Many ML quantities satisfy this — changing one training example changes the training error by at most 1/m. This gives concentration bounds for statistics beyond simple averages.

Covering Numbers and Margin-Based Bounds

Covering numbers measure how many "balls" of radius ε you need to cover a hypothesis class. They bridge between finite and infinite hypothesis classes — if you can cover H with N(ε) balls, you can use the union bound with N(ε) instead of |H|. Margin-based bounds use a related idea: if your classifier achieves large margins on training data, the effective covering number shrinks dramatically, giving tighter generalization guarantees. This is the theoretical justification for SVMs and why large-margin classifiers work well.

Stability-Based Bounds

Algorithmic stability bounds take a completely different approach. Instead of measuring the complexity of the hypothesis class, they measure how sensitive the algorithm is to changes in the training data. If removing or replacing one training example barely changes the output hypothesis, the algorithm is stable, and stability implies generalization. SGD with small learning rates is provably stable, which helps explain why it generalizes better than batch gradient descent despite finding similar-quality solutions.

def stability_generalization_demo():
    """Demonstrate algorithmic stability intuition."""
    from sklearn.linear_model import Ridge
    from sklearn.datasets import make_regression
    
    X, y = make_regression(n_samples=100, n_features=20, noise=10, random_state=42)
    
    # Train on full dataset
    model_full = Ridge(alpha=1.0).fit(X, y)
    
    # Leave-one-out stability: how much does removing one point change predictions?
    max_change = 0
    for i in range(len(X)):
        X_loo = np.delete(X, i, axis=0)
        y_loo = np.delete(y, i, axis=0)
        model_loo = Ridge(alpha=1.0).fit(X_loo, y_loo)
        
        # Measure prediction change on the left-out point
        pred_full = model_full.predict(X[i:i+1])[0]
        pred_loo = model_loo.predict(X[i:i+1])[0]
        change = abs(pred_full - pred_loo)
        max_change = max(max_change, change)
    
    print(f"Max prediction change from removing one point: {max_change:.4f}")
    print("Small change = stable algorithm = good generalization")

stability_generalization_demo()

Bias-Complexity Tradeoff

Approximation Error vs Estimation Error

Every model faces two competing enemies. Approximation error (bias) is how far the best hypothesis in your class is from the truth — if you use linear models but the true relationship is quadratic, you have high bias no matter how much data you have. Estimation error (variance) is how far your learned hypothesis is from the best one in your class — with limited data, you might not find the best linear model even if a linear model would be perfect.

Simple models have high bias, low variance. Complex models have low bias, high variance. The optimal complexity minimizes total error. This is the classical U-shaped curve every ML textbook shows you.

import numpy as np

def bias_variance_decomposition(true_fn, model_degrees, X_train_sets, X_test):
    """Compute bias-variance decomposition for polynomial regression."""
    from numpy.polynomial import polynomial as P
    
    results = {}
    y_test_true = true_fn(X_test)
    
    for degree in model_degrees:
        predictions = []
        for X_train, y_train in X_train_sets:
            # Fit polynomial of given degree
            coeffs = np.polyfit(X_train, y_train, degree)
            y_pred = np.polyval(coeffs, X_test)
            predictions.append(y_pred)
        
        predictions = np.array(predictions)
        
        # Bias^2 = (E[prediction] - true)^2
        mean_pred = predictions.mean(axis=0)
        bias_sq = np.mean((mean_pred - y_test_true)**2)
        
        # Variance = E[(prediction - E[prediction])^2]
        variance = np.mean(predictions.var(axis=0))
        
        # Total = Bias^2 + Variance (+ irreducible noise)
        total = bias_sq + variance
        results[degree] = {'bias2': bias_sq, 'variance': variance, 'total': total}
        print(f"Degree {degree:2d}: Bias²={bias_sq:.4f}, Var={variance:.4f}, "
              f"Total={total:.4f}")
    
    return results

# True function: degree-3 polynomial + noise
np.random.seed(42)
true_fn = lambda x: 0.5*x**3 - 2*x**2 + x + 3

# Generate multiple training sets (to compute variance)
train_sets = []
for _ in range(50):
    X = np.random.uniform(-3, 3, 30)
    y = true_fn(X) + np.random.randn(30) * 3  # noise
    train_sets.append((X, y))

X_test = np.linspace(-3, 3, 100)
bias_variance_decomposition(true_fn, [1, 3, 5, 10, 15], train_sets, X_test)

No Free Lunch Theorem

The No Free Lunch theorem says: averaged over all possible problems, every learning algorithm performs equally well (or equally badly). There is no universally best algorithm. This sounds depressing, but the practical implication is optimistic — it means domain knowledge matters. The reason random forests beat neural networks on some problems and vice versa is not because one is "better." It is because each algorithm's inductive biases match different problem structures.

Double Descent and Benign Overfitting

Here is where the classical story breaks down. Modern deep learning models are massively overparameterized — they have far more parameters than training examples — yet they generalize well. The double descent phenomenon shows that the classical U-shaped bias-variance curve is incomplete. As model complexity passes the interpolation threshold (where the model can perfectly fit the training data), test error first spikes but then decreases again as you add even more parameters.

Benign overfitting occurs when a model perfectly interpolates the training data — including noise — yet still generalizes well. The key insight is that in high-dimensional spaces, models can fit noise in "unimportant" directions while learning the true signal in the directions that matter. This is one of the most active areas of current theoretical research.

def double_descent_demo():
    """Simulate double descent with polynomial regression.
    
    As degree increases past n_samples, we see the characteristic
    spike followed by improvement.
    """
    np.random.seed(42)
    n = 20
    X_train = np.random.uniform(-1, 1, n)
    y_train = np.sin(3 * X_train) + 0.3 * np.random.randn(n)
    X_test = np.linspace(-1, 1, 200)
    y_test = np.sin(3 * X_test)
    
    degrees = list(range(1, 35))
    test_errors = []
    
    for d in degrees:
        try:
            # Use regularized fit to avoid numerical issues
            coeffs = np.polyfit(X_train, y_train, min(d, n-1))
            y_pred = np.polyval(coeffs, X_test)
            mse = np.mean((y_pred - y_test)**2)
            test_errors.append(min(mse, 10))  # cap for display
        except:
            test_errors.append(10)
    
    # Show the U-shape then potential recovery
    for d, err in zip(degrees, test_errors):
        bar = "█" * int(err * 5)
        print(f"Degree {d:2d}: MSE={err:.3f} {bar}")

double_descent_demo()

Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) Theory

Infinite-Width Limit — Neural Networks as Kernel Machines

One of the most surprising theoretical results of the last decade: if you make a neural network infinitely wide, its training dynamics become equivalent to kernel regression with a specific kernel called the Neural Tangent Kernel. In this regime, gradient descent finds the global minimum, and the network's predictions can be described in closed form.

The NTK is defined as K(x, x') = ⟨∇_θ f(x; θ), ∇_θ f(x'; θ)⟩, where f is the network output and θ are the parameters. For infinitely wide networks at initialization, this kernel stays constant during training (the lazy training regime). This means the network is essentially doing kernel regression from the start.

import numpy as np

def compute_ntk_simple(X, hidden_dim=1000):
    """Compute empirical NTK for a 1-hidden-layer network.
    
    For wide networks, the NTK converges to a deterministic kernel.
    K(x, x') = (1/d_hidden) * Σ_j σ'(w_j·x) σ'(w_j·x') (x·x')
    
    For ReLU: σ'(z) = 1 if z > 0, else 0
    """
    n = len(X)
    d_in = X.shape[1] if X.ndim > 1 else 1
    
    if X.ndim == 1:
        X = X.reshape(-1, 1)
    
    # Random weights (at initialization)
    W = np.random.randn(hidden_dim, d_in) / np.sqrt(d_in)
    
    # Pre-activations
    pre_act = X @ W.T  # (n, hidden_dim)
    
    # ReLU derivative (indicator)
    relu_deriv = (pre_act > 0).astype(float)  # (n, hidden_dim)
    
    # NTK = (1/hidden_dim) * (relu_deriv @ relu_deriv.T) * (X @ X.T)
    ntk = (relu_deriv @ relu_deriv.T / hidden_dim) * (X @ X.T)
    
    return ntk

# Demo: NTK converges as width increases
np.random.seed(42)
X = np.random.randn(10, 5)

for width in [50, 200, 1000, 5000]:
    ntks = [compute_ntk_simple(X, width) for _ in range(5)]
    # Measure variation across random initializations
    std = np.std([k.flatten() for k in ntks], axis=0).mean()
    print(f"Width {width:>5d}: NTK std across inits = {std:.6f}")

Mean-Field Theory and Beyond Lazy Training

Mean-field theory provides an alternative view for networks at finite width. Unlike the NTK regime where weights barely move, mean-field theory describes a "rich" or "active" training regime where features actually evolve during training. This is closer to what happens in practice — real networks learn representations, which the lazy NTK regime cannot explain. The tension between these two theories is an active area of research, and understanding when each applies helps explain phenomena like feature learning, transfer learning, and the lottery ticket hypothesis.

Theory vs Practice Gap

Classical learning theory (VC bounds, Rademacher bounds) often gives vacuous bounds for deep networks — the predicted generalization gap is larger than 1, which is useless. Modern deep learning clearly generalizes far better than these bounds predict. Closing this gap is one of the biggest open problems in ML theory. PAC-Bayes bounds, compression-based arguments, and implicit regularization of SGD are the leading approaches, but none fully explains why deep learning works as well as it does.
