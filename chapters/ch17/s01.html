Learning Theory Foundations

Every time you train a model on data and then deploy it on new, unseen data, you are making a bet. You are betting that patterns in your training set generalize — that they reflect something real about the world, not just noise in the sample you happened to collect.

Learning theory is the math behind that bet. It answers questions like:

  • How many training examples do I need before I can trust my model?
  • Why does a more complex model sometimes perform worse on test data?
  • What exactly makes overfitting happen — and what are the precise conditions where it won't?

These are not abstract curiosities. If you have ever argued with a colleague about whether 10K examples is "enough" for a project, or debated whether to use a simpler model, learning theory gives you the vocabulary — and sometimes the actual formulas — to settle those arguments.

PAC Learning: Probably Approximately Correct

The framework that kicked off the entire field of computational learning theory was introduced by Leslie Valiant in 1984. It is called PAC learning, which stands for Probably Approximately Correct. The name is unusually honest about its ambitions — it does not promise perfection. It promises that, with enough data, your learner will probably find a hypothesis that is approximately correct.

Here is the setup. You have:

  • An unknown target concept c (the "truth" you are trying to learn)
  • A hypothesis class H (the set of all functions your learning algorithm can consider)
  • Training examples drawn i.i.d. from some unknown distribution D

PAC learning gives you two knobs to control:

The Two Knobs of PAC Learning

ε (epsilon) — accuracy: How close does your hypothesis need to be to the true concept? An error rate below ε means "approximately correct."

δ (delta) — confidence: How sure do you want to be? A probability of at least 1 − δ means "probably." If δ = 0.05, you are 95% confident.

A concept class is PAC learnable if there exists an algorithm that, for any ε and δ, can find an approximately correct hypothesis with high probability — using a number of samples and running time that is polynomial in 1/ε, 1/δ, and the size of the problem.

The Sample Complexity Formula

For a finite hypothesis class of size |H|, the number of training examples you need is:

m ≥ (1/ε) · ln(|H|/δ)

Read that carefully. The sample complexity grows:

  • Linearly with 1/ε — want twice the accuracy? Need roughly twice the data.
  • Logarithmically with |H| — a hypothesis class that is a million times bigger only needs ~14x more data. This is remarkably forgiving.
  • Logarithmically with 1/δ — going from 90% to 99% confidence barely costs anything.

The logarithmic dependence on |H| is the key insight. It means learning is feasible even with enormous hypothesis classes, as long as they are finite.

import numpy as np

def pac_sample_complexity(epsilon, delta, h_size):
    """Minimum samples needed for PAC learning with a finite hypothesis class.

    Args:
        epsilon: Maximum allowed error rate (accuracy knob)
        delta: Maximum allowed failure probability (confidence knob)
        h_size: Size of the hypothesis class |H|
    Returns:
        Minimum number of training samples needed
    """
    return int(np.ceil((1 / epsilon) * np.log(h_size / delta)))

# Example: Learning boolean conjunctions over 10 features
# H = all conjunctions of up to 10 literals → |H| = 3^10 = 59049
h_size = 3**10
print(f"|H| = {h_size:,}")

# Want error < 5%, confidence 95%
m = pac_sample_complexity(epsilon=0.05, delta=0.05, h_size=h_size)
print(f"Samples needed (ε=0.05, δ=0.05): {m}")

# Want error < 1%, confidence 99%
m_strict = pac_sample_complexity(epsilon=0.01, delta=0.01, h_size=h_size)
print(f"Samples needed (ε=0.01, δ=0.01): {m_strict}")

# Notice: 5x tighter accuracy + 5x tighter confidence → only ~6x more data
print(f"Ratio: {m_strict / m:.1f}x more data for much tighter guarantees")

Realizable vs. Agnostic PAC

The formula above assumes the realizable case: the true concept is actually in your hypothesis class H. Your model family is expressive enough to represent the truth perfectly.

That is a big assumption. In practice, the truth is almost never in your hypothesis class. Linear models cannot capture XOR. Decision trees struggle with smooth boundaries. This is the agnostic setting — your goal shifts from finding the perfect hypothesis to finding the best one available in H, even though none of them are exactly right.

Agnostic PAC learning is harder. The sample complexity bounds are worse (they grow as 1/ε² instead of 1/ε), and the guarantees are relative: you are competing against the best hypothesis in H, not against perfection.

Interview Gotcha: When someone says "this model can learn the concept," ask: realizable or agnostic? Realizable PAC assumes the truth lives in your hypothesis class. Agnostic PAC does not. The distinction changes everything — the sample complexity, the algorithms, and the guarantees.

VC Dimension: Measuring Model Capacity

PAC learning with finite hypothesis classes is clean. But what about infinite hypothesis classes — like linear classifiers, neural networks, or SVMs? The |H| in our formula is infinity. Does that mean we need infinite data?

No. And the reason is one of the most beautiful ideas in learning theory: VC dimension (Vapnik-Chervonenkis dimension).

Shattering: The Core Concept

A hypothesis class H shatters a set of points if it can realize every possible labeling of those points. For m points with binary labels, that is 2m possible labelings.

Think of it this way: if H can shatter m points, then no matter how you label those points (all positives, all negatives, alternating, any combination), there exists some hypothesis in H that classifies them perfectly. H is flexible enough to memorize any pattern on those m points.

The VC dimension of H is the largest number of points that H can shatter. More precisely:

VC(H) = max { m : there exists a set of m points that H shatters }

A Concrete Example: Lines in 2D

Consider linear classifiers in 2D — lines that divide the plane into + and − regions.

Can they shatter 3 points? Yes. Place three points in "general position" (not collinear). For every one of the 2³ = 8 possible labelings, you can draw a line that separates the + points from the − points. Try it on paper — it works for all 8 cases.

Can they shatter 4 points? No. Arrange four points in a square and label opposite corners the same way (an XOR pattern). No line can separate them. You can try every possible arrangement of 4 points — there will always be at least one labeling a line cannot realize.

Therefore: VC dimension of linear classifiers in 2D = 3.

The VC Dimension of Linear Classifiers

For linear classifiers in d dimensions (including the bias term as a threshold), the VC dimension is d + 1. A hyperplane in ℝd can shatter any d+1 points in general position, but no set of d+2 points.

This is one of the few cases where VC dimension has a clean closed-form answer. 
import numpy as np
from itertools import product

def can_linear_classifier_realize(points, labels):
    """Check if a linear classifier (line in 2D) can separate points by labels.
    Uses a simple linear program approach.
    """
    from scipy.optimize import linprog
    n = len(points)
    # We want: y_i * (w · x_i + b) > 0 for all i
    # Reformulate: find w, b such that y_i * (w · x_i + b) >= 1
    d = points.shape[1]
    # Variables: [w1, w2, ..., wd, b]
    c = np.zeros(d + 1)  # No objective, just feasibility
    A_ub = np.zeros((n, d + 1))
    b_ub = -np.ones(n)
    for i in range(n):
        label = 1 if labels[i] else -1
        A_ub[i, :d] = -label * points[i]
        A_ub[i, d] = -label
    result = linprog(c, A_ub=A_ub, b_ub=b_ub, method='highs')
    return result.success

# Three non-collinear points in 2D
points_3 = np.array([[0, 0], [1, 0], [0, 1]], dtype=float)
all_labelings_3 = list(product([False, True], repeat=3))
# Skip all-False and all-True (trivial)
non_trivial = [l for l in all_labelings_3 if any(l) and not all(l)]

shattered_3 = all(
    can_linear_classifier_realize(points_3, np.array(lab))
    for lab in non_trivial
)
print(f"Can shatter 3 points? {shattered_3}")  # True

# Four points in a square
points_4 = np.array([[0, 0], [1, 0], [0, 1], [1, 1]], dtype=float)
all_labelings_4 = list(product([False, True], repeat=4))
non_trivial_4 = [l for l in all_labelings_4 if any(l) and not all(l)]

shattered_4 = all(
    can_linear_classifier_realize(points_4, np.array(lab))
    for lab in non_trivial_4
)
print(f"Can shatter 4 points? {shattered_4}")  # False — XOR kills it

# VC dimension of linear classifiers in d dimensions = d + 1
for d in [2, 5, 10, 100]:
    print(f"  d={d}: VC dim = {d + 1}")

Why VC Dimension Matters for You

The VC dimension replaces |H| in our sample complexity bounds. For an infinite hypothesis class with VC dimension d, you need roughly:

m = O((d/ε²) · ln(d/ε))

This is profound. An infinite hypothesis class can still be learned with a finite number of examples — as long as its VC dimension is finite. The VC dimension captures the effective complexity of a model class, not its raw size.

Hypothesis ClassVC DimensionIntuition
Threshold on ℝ (h(x) = x > θ)1One knob to turn
Intervals on ℝ2Two endpoints
Linear classifiers in ℝdd + 1d weights + bias
Axis-aligned rectangles in ℝd2dTwo boundaries per axis
k-nearest neighbors∞Can memorize anything
Decision stumps2One feature, one threshold
Neural net with W weightsO(W log W)Roughly proportional to parameters

Notice k-NN has infinite VC dimension. Theory says it should always overfit. In practice it works because the data distribution constrains what it actually does — a reminder that VC bounds are worst-case over all distributions.

Generalization Bounds

Now we connect everything. The whole point of learning theory is to answer: how far can your test error be from your training error?

The VC Generalization Bound

For any hypothesis h chosen from a class H with VC dimension d, with probability at least 1 − δ:

Ltrue(h) ≤ Ltrain(h) + √( (d · ln(2m/d) + ln(4/δ)) / m )

where m is the number of training examples. That square root term is the complexity penalty. It tells you the maximum gap between what you see on training data and what you will see in the wild.

Three things to notice:

  • More data (larger m) → smaller penalty → training error is more trustworthy
  • Higher VC dimension (larger d) → bigger penalty → more complex models get a bigger "tax"
  • The bound holds simultaneously for all hypotheses in H — it is a uniform convergence result
VC bounds are almost always too loose in practice. For a neural network with millions of parameters, the VC bound gives astronomically large sample complexity requirements — far more data than any real dataset. Nobody uses VC bounds to compute actual numbers for deep learning. Their value is conceptual: they tell you why generalization happens and what the key variables are (model complexity, sample size, training error).

Rademacher Complexity: A Data-Dependent Alternative

VC dimension is a property of the hypothesis class alone — it does not depend on the actual data. This is both a strength (universal guarantees) and a weakness (loose bounds when the data is well-behaved).

Rademacher complexity fixes this by measuring how well your hypothesis class can fit random noise on your actual data. The idea is clever:

  1. Take your training set
  2. Replace the labels with random ±1 (Rademacher variables)
  3. Measure how well the best hypothesis in H can correlate with this noise
  4. Average over many random labelings

If your hypothesis class can fit random noise well, it has high Rademacher complexity and is prone to overfitting. If it cannot fit noise, it is well-constrained.

import numpy as np

def empirical_rademacher_complexity(X, hypothesis_class_fn, n_trials=1000):
    """Estimate Rademacher complexity of a hypothesis class on data X.

    Args:
        X: Training data, shape (m, d)
        hypothesis_class_fn: Function that, given X and random labels sigma,
                             returns max correlation achievable by H
        n_trials: Number of random label draws
    """
    m = X.shape[0]
    complexities = []
    for _ in range(n_trials):
        sigma = np.random.choice([-1, 1], size=m)  # Rademacher variables
        max_corr = hypothesis_class_fn(X, sigma)
        complexities.append(max_corr)
    return np.mean(complexities)

def linear_classifier_correlation(X, sigma):
    """Max correlation of a linear classifier with random labels."""
    # For linear classifiers, max_w (1/m) * sigma^T X w / ||w||
    # = (1/m) * ||X^T sigma||_2 (by Cauchy-Schwarz)
    m = X.shape[0]
    return np.linalg.norm(X.T @ sigma) / m

# Compare: more data → lower Rademacher complexity
np.random.seed(42)
for m in [50, 200, 1000, 5000]:
    X = np.random.randn(m, 10)
    rc = empirical_rademacher_complexity(X, linear_classifier_correlation)
    print(f"m={m:5d}, d=10: Rademacher complexity ≈ {rc:.4f}")

The generalization bound using Rademacher complexity is: with probability at least 1 − δ,

Ltrue(h) ≤ Ltrain(h) + 2·R̂m(H) + √(ln(1/δ) / (2m))

where R̂m(H) is the empirical Rademacher complexity. These bounds are typically tighter than VC bounds because they incorporate the geometry of the actual data, not just worst-case combinatorial capacity.

The Bias-Complexity Tradeoff

Learning theory formalizes something you already know intuitively: simple models underfit, complex models overfit, and the sweet spot is somewhere in between.

The bias-complexity tradeoff (the theoretical name for bias-variance tradeoff) says that for a given training set size m, your true error decomposes as:

True Error ≤ Approximation Error + Estimation Error

  • Approximation error (bias): The best possible error achievable by any hypothesis in H. A tiny hypothesis class might not contain any hypothesis close to the truth. This is the error from using too simple a model.
  • Estimation error (complexity/variance): The gap between the best hypothesis in H and the one you actually find from finite data. A huge hypothesis class contains great hypotheses, but you might not find them — or you might pick one that fits noise instead.

Increase model complexity: approximation error goes down (more expressive family), estimation error goes up (harder to find the right hypothesis with limited data). Decrease model complexity: the opposite.

Structural Risk Minimization

SRM is the principled answer to the tradeoff. Instead of picking one hypothesis class and hoping for the best, you consider a nested sequence of increasingly complex classes:

H1 ⊂ H2 ⊂ H3 ⊂ ...

For each class, you minimize training error plus a complexity penalty that grows with the VC dimension (or another capacity measure). You then pick the class that minimizes this combined objective.

import numpy as np

def structural_risk_minimization(train_errors, vc_dims, m, delta=0.05):
    """Select the best model complexity via SRM.

    Args:
        train_errors: List of training errors for each model complexity
        vc_dims: List of VC dimensions for each model class
        m: Number of training samples
        delta: Confidence parameter
    Returns:
        Index of the best model complexity
    """
    bounds = []
    for train_err, d in zip(train_errors, vc_dims):
        # VC generalization bound (simplified)
        if d == 0:
            penalty = 0
        else:
            penalty = np.sqrt((d * np.log(2 * m / d) + np.log(4 / delta)) / m)
        bound = train_err + penalty
        bounds.append(bound)
    best_idx = np.argmin(bounds)
    return best_idx, bounds

# Simulated scenario: polynomial regression of degree 1 through 10
# VC dim for degree-k polynomial in 1D = k + 1
np.random.seed(42)
m = 100
degrees = range(1, 11)
vc_dims = [k + 1 for k in degrees]
# Simulate: training error drops, but complex models overfit
train_errors = [0.25, 0.12, 0.06, 0.03, 0.015, 0.008, 0.004, 0.002, 0.001, 0.0005]

best, bounds = structural_risk_minimization(train_errors, vc_dims, m)
print("Degree | Train Err | Penalty   | SRM Bound")
print("-------|-----------|-----------|----------")
for i, (deg, te, b) in enumerate(zip(degrees, train_errors, bounds)):
    marker = " ← best" if i == best else ""
    penalty = b - te
    print(f"  {deg:4d} | {te:9.4f} | {penalty:9.4f} | {b:.4f}{marker}")

This is exactly what regularization does in practice. L2 regularization, early stopping, dropout — they all implement variations of "minimize training error plus a complexity penalty." SRM is the theoretical justification.

SRM in practice: You have been doing SRM every time you tune a regularization hyperparameter via cross-validation. You are searching over a nested sequence of hypothesis classes (one per regularization strength) and picking the one with the best estimated generalization. Cross-validation is the empirical version of what SRM prescribes theoretically.

Double Descent: The Modern Puzzle

Everything above paints a neat picture: increase model complexity, and at some point overfitting kicks in, true error goes up. The classic U-shaped curve. Simple story.

Then deep learning broke it.

The Classical View vs. Reality

In 2019, Belkin et al. documented something practitioners had been noticing for years: if you keep increasing model complexity past the point of perfect training fit (the "interpolation threshold"), test error does not stay high. It comes back down.

The error curve is not U-shaped. It is double-descent shaped:

  1. Under-parameterized regime: Classical U-curve. More parameters help, then hurt.
  2. Interpolation threshold: The model has just enough capacity to perfectly fit the training data. This is the worst point — the model is barely fitting the data, and any noise in the labels gets memorized exactly. Test error peaks here.
  3. Over-parameterized regime: More parameters than needed to fit the data. Among the many hypotheses that perfectly fit training data, the learning algorithm (via its implicit bias) finds one that generalizes well. Test error decreases again.
import numpy as np

def simulate_double_descent():
    """Demonstrate the double descent phenomenon with polynomial regression."""
    np.random.seed(42)
    n_train, n_test = 30, 1000

    # True function + noise
    X_train = np.random.uniform(-1, 1, n_train)
    y_train = np.sin(3 * X_train) + 0.3 * np.random.randn(n_train)
    X_test = np.random.uniform(-1, 1, n_test)
    y_test = np.sin(3 * X_test)

    max_degree = 60
    train_errors, test_errors = [], []

    for d in range(1, max_degree + 1):
        # Polynomial features
        X_poly = np.vander(X_train, d + 1, increasing=True)
        X_test_poly = np.vander(X_test, d + 1, increasing=True)

        # Minimum-norm least squares (pseudoinverse for over-parameterized case)
        w = np.linalg.lstsq(X_poly, y_train, rcond=None)[0]

        train_pred = X_poly @ w
        test_pred = X_test_poly @ w

        train_mse = np.mean((train_pred - y_train) ** 2)
        test_mse = np.mean((test_pred - y_test) ** 2)

        # Clip extreme values for display
        test_mse = min(test_mse, 10.0)
        train_errors.append(train_mse)
        test_errors.append(test_mse)

    # Show the three regimes
    print("Degree | Train MSE | Test MSE  | Regime")
    print("-------|-----------|-----------|-------")
    for d in [2, 5, 10, 15, 25, 29, 30, 35, 45, 55]:
        idx = d - 1
        if d < 25:
            regime = "under-param"
        elif d <= 31:
            regime = "INTERPOLATION THRESHOLD"
        else:
            regime = "over-param"
        print(f"  {d:4d} | {train_errors[idx]:9.4f} | {test_errors[idx]:9.4f} | {regime}")

simulate_double_descent()

Why This Challenges Classical Theory

Classical learning theory — VC bounds, SRM, the whole framework — predicts that the interpolation threshold is the beginning of disaster. Once you can perfectly fit training data, you have "memorized the noise," and things should only get worse with more parameters.

But that is not what happens. The key insight is the implicit bias of the learning algorithm. In the over-parameterized regime, there are many hypotheses that perfectly fit the training data. Gradient descent does not pick an arbitrary one — it picks the one with minimum norm (for linear models) or follows a specific trajectory through parameter space that favors "simpler" solutions.

The implicit regularization of SGD is one of the most important open questions in modern ML theory. Gradient descent, applied to an over-parameterized model, acts as if it were regularized — even though you never added an explicit regularization term. Understanding exactly why this happens and when it breaks down is an active research frontier.

Double descent is not just a curiosity. It has been observed in:

  • Random Fourier features and kernel methods
  • Decision trees and random forests
  • Deep neural networks (with respect to both model size and training epochs)
  • Epoch-wise double descent: even for a fixed model size, test error can exhibit double descent as you train longer

The practical takeaway: do not panic if your model is "too big." In modern deep learning, larger models with proper training often generalize better, not worse. The old advice of "use the smallest model that fits" is incomplete.

PAC-Bayes: The Tightest Bounds We Have

Classical VC theory treats the learning algorithm as a black box — it only cares about the hypothesis class. PAC-Bayes theory opens that box and asks: what distribution over hypotheses does your learning algorithm actually produce?

The Core Idea

Instead of picking a single hypothesis, think of your learning algorithm as outputting a posterior distribution Q over hypotheses (after seeing data), starting from a prior distribution P (before seeing data). The PAC-Bayes bound says:

Ltrue(Q) ≤ Ltrain(Q) + √( (KL(Q || P) + ln(m/δ)) / (2m) )

where KL(Q || P) is the Kullback-Leibler divergence from posterior to prior.

The beauty is in the KL term. If your learned model (posterior) is close to your prior beliefs, the complexity penalty is small. If learning dramatically changed your beliefs, the penalty is large. It is a formal version of Occam's razor: solutions that are "unsurprising" given your prior generalize better.

Why This Matters for Deep Learning

PAC-Bayes bounds are currently the tightest non-vacuous generalization bounds we have for neural networks. While VC bounds give absurdly large numbers for deep nets, PAC-Bayes bounds can sometimes produce meaningful predictions.

The connection to practice is direct:

  • Weight decay (L2 regularization) can be interpreted as setting a Gaussian prior on the weights centered at zero. The KL term penalizes weights that drift far from zero.
  • Pre-trained models fine-tuned on small datasets generalize well because the fine-tuned weights (posterior) stay close to the pre-trained weights (prior). Small KL divergence → tight generalization bound.
  • Flat minima in the loss landscape correspond to posteriors with low KL divergence from broad priors, connecting PAC-Bayes to the loss landscape geometry.
PAC-Bayes explains fine-tuning. When you fine-tune a large pre-trained model on 1,000 examples and it generalizes well, classical theory says this should not work — too many parameters, too little data. PAC-Bayes explains it: the pre-trained weights serve as an informative prior, and fine-tuning only moves a small KL distance away. The "effective" complexity is not the number of parameters — it is how far you moved from your starting point. 
import numpy as np

def pac_bayes_bound(train_error, kl_divergence, m, delta=0.05):
    """Compute the PAC-Bayes generalization bound.

    Args:
        train_error: Average training error of the posterior
        kl_divergence: KL(posterior || prior)
        m: Number of training examples
        delta: Confidence parameter
    Returns:
        Upper bound on true error
    """
    complexity_penalty = np.sqrt(
        (kl_divergence + np.log(m / delta)) / (2 * m)
    )
    return train_error + complexity_penalty

# Scenario: Fine-tuning a pre-trained model
# Prior = pre-trained weights, Posterior = fine-tuned weights

# Case 1: Training from scratch (large KL from random init prior)
bound_scratch = pac_bayes_bound(
    train_error=0.02, kl_divergence=50000, m=1000
)

# Case 2: Fine-tuning (small KL from pre-trained prior)
bound_finetune = pac_bayes_bound(
    train_error=0.03, kl_divergence=50, m=1000
)

print(f"Training from scratch:  bound = {bound_scratch:.4f}")
print(f"Fine-tuning pre-trained: bound = {bound_finetune:.4f}")
print(f"\nFine-tuning gives a {bound_scratch/bound_finetune:.1f}x tighter bound")
print("despite slightly higher training error!")

Putting It All Together

Here is how these ideas connect to form a coherent picture of generalization:

FrameworkMeasures Complexity ViaStrengthWeakness
PAC (finite H) log |H| Simple, clean formulas Only works for finite classes
VC Theory VC dimension Works for infinite classes Bounds too loose for deep nets
Rademacher Data-dependent capacity Tighter, data-aware bounds Harder to compute
PAC-Bayes KL(posterior || prior) Tightest known for neural nets Requires choosing a prior

The unifying theme: generalization depends on the "effective" complexity of what your algorithm actually does, not just what it could do. A neural net with a billion parameters has enormous capacity, but if SGD + weight decay keeps it close to a simple solution, it generalizes. The challenge for theory is measuring that effective complexity.

Classical theory (VC, SRM) tells you the rules for the under-parameterized regime. Modern theory (double descent, implicit bias, PAC-Bayes) is working to explain the over-parameterized regime where deep learning lives. Neither is complete. Both are useful.

What You Should Now Be Able To Do

  • Explain PAC learning to a colleague: what ε and δ mean, and compute sample complexity for a finite hypothesis class
  • Define VC dimension and work through the shattering argument for linear classifiers (VC dim = d+1)
  • Write down the VC generalization bound and explain what each term means — training error, complexity penalty, sample size
  • Explain why VC bounds are too loose for neural networks and why Rademacher/PAC-Bayes are preferred
  • Describe the bias-complexity tradeoff and how SRM formalizes the choice of model complexity
  • Draw the double descent curve and explain why it challenges the classical U-shaped generalization picture
  • Articulate why fine-tuning pre-trained models generalizes well through the PAC-Bayes lens (small KL divergence from an informative prior)
  • In a model selection discussion, use the right vocabulary: "We are in the over-parameterized regime, so adding capacity might actually help generalization"
