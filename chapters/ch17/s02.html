Online Learning & Bandits

Chapter 17: Learning Theory & Advanced ML Section 2 7 major topics
TL;DR

Most ML assumes you collect all the data first, then train. Online learning flips that — you learn one example at a time, updating as you go. Multi-armed bandits take it further: you don't just predict, you act, and your actions affect what data you see next. The core tension is exploration vs. exploitation. Epsilon-greedy is the simplest fix, UCB is the principled fix, and Thompson Sampling is the Bayesian fix that often wins in practice. These aren't academic curiosities — they power A/B testing, ad placement, and recommendation systems at every major tech company.

Online Learning: One Example at a Time

Here's the standard ML story you've been told a hundred times: collect a dataset, split it, train on the training set, evaluate on the test set, deploy. Nice and tidy. But what if your data arrives as a never-ending stream? What if storing everything is impractical? What if the distribution is shifting under your feet?

That's online learning. The protocol is brutally simple:

  1. Receive an example xt
  2. Make a prediction ŷt
  3. Observe the true label yt
  4. Update your model
  5. Repeat forever

No second passes through the data. No reshuffling. You see each example exactly once and you have to deal with it right now. Stock price prediction, spam filtering on incoming email, ad click prediction — these are all naturally online problems.

Regret: The Online Scorecard

In batch learning, you measure accuracy or loss on a held-out test set. In online learning, the question is different: how much worse did I do compared to the best strategy I could have picked in hindsight?

That's regret:

Regret(T) = Σt=1..T loss(ŷt, yt) − minf∈F Σt=1..T loss(f(xt), yt)

Your cumulative loss minus the cumulative loss of the single best fixed predictor from your hypothesis class, computed with full knowledge of all T examples. You're not competing against an oracle that changes strategy each round — just the single best fixed strategy in hindsight.

A good online algorithm achieves sublinear regret: Regret(T) = o(T). That means the average regret per round goes to zero. You're converging to the best fixed strategy even though you never had the data all at once.

Batch vs Online: The Key Shift

Batch learning optimizes a loss function on a fixed dataset. Online learning makes sequential decisions and measures regret against the best fixed strategy in hindsight. The adversary can be anything — there's no i.i.d. assumption required. That's why online learning theory works even when the world is adversarial.

Online Gradient Descent

The workhorse of online learning is Online Gradient Descent (OGD). It's exactly what it sounds like: SGD but for real.

import numpy as np

def online_gradient_descent(stream, lr=0.01, d=10):
    """Online gradient descent for linear regression."""
    w = np.zeros(d)
    cumulative_loss = 0.0

    for t, (x, y) in enumerate(stream, 1):
        # Predict
        y_hat = w @ x

        # Suffer loss
        loss = 0.5 * (y_hat - y) ** 2
        cumulative_loss += loss

        # Update
        gradient = (y_hat - y) * x
        w -= lr * gradient

        if t % 1000 == 0:
            print(f"Step {t}: avg loss = {cumulative_loss / t:.4f}")

    return w

With a learning rate of η = 1/√T, OGD achieves O(√T) regret for convex losses — the best you can do without stronger assumptions. With strongly convex losses you can get O(log T) regret, which is dramatically better. The Perceptron algorithm from the 1950s? It's online gradient descent on the hinge loss. You've been doing online learning longer than you thought.

The Multi-Armed Bandit Problem

Now let's make things more interesting. In online learning, you see the true answer after each prediction. In bandits, you only see the reward for the action you actually took. The road not traveled stays mysterious.

The name comes from a gambler facing a row of slot machines (one-armed bandits). Each machine has a different unknown payout distribution. You have a fixed budget of pulls. Which machines do you play?

Pull the machine that's paid out the most so far? You might miss a better one you haven't tried enough. Try every machine equally? You're wasting pulls on machines you already know are bad. This is the exploration vs. exploitation tradeoff — arguably the most fundamental tension in decision-making under uncertainty.

Formally: you have K arms. At each round t, you choose arm at and receive reward rt drawn from that arm's (unknown) reward distribution. Your goal is to maximize total reward, which is equivalent to minimizing regret against always pulling the best arm:

Regret(T) = T · μ* − Σt=1..T μat

where μ* is the mean reward of the best arm and μat is the mean reward of the arm you chose. Every time you pull a suboptimal arm, you pay a gap of Δa = μ* − μa.

Bandits ≠ Online Learning

In online learning, you observe the loss for all actions (full information). In bandits, you only observe the reward for the action you chose (partial information). This makes bandits fundamentally harder — you can't just compute the gradient. The Lai-Robbins lower bound proves that even the best algorithm must incur Ω(log T) regret, because you have to explore.

Epsilon-Greedy: The "Just Wing It" Strategy

The simplest bandit algorithm. With probability (1 − ε) pick the arm with the highest estimated mean reward. With probability ε pick an arm uniformly at random.

import numpy as np

class EpsilonGreedy:
    def __init__(self, n_arms, epsilon=0.1):
        self.n_arms = n_arms
        self.epsilon = epsilon
        self.counts = np.zeros(n_arms)      # pulls per arm
        self.values = np.zeros(n_arms)      # estimated mean reward

    def select_arm(self):
        if np.random.random() < self.epsilon:
            return np.random.randint(self.n_arms)  # explore
        return np.argmax(self.values)              # exploit

    def update(self, arm, reward):
        self.counts[arm] += 1
        n = self.counts[arm]
        # Incremental mean update
        self.values[arm] += (reward - self.values[arm]) / n

It works. Honestly, it works surprisingly well for how dumb it is. But there's a problem: ε is fixed. After 10,000 rounds, when you're pretty sure arm 3 is the best, you're still exploring 10% of the time. That's wasted reward.

Decaying Epsilon

The fix is obvious: explore less over time. A common schedule is εt = min(1, C/(d² · t)) where C is a constant and d is the number of arms. Or simply εt = 1/t. In practice, people often use εt = ε₀ / (1 + t/τ) where τ controls the decay speed.

Decaying epsilon-greedy can achieve O(log T) regret with the right schedule. The catch: "the right schedule" depends on the reward gaps Δa that you don't know in advance. So you're tuning a hyperparameter in a setting where the whole point was to avoid needing full information. Feels a bit circular, doesn't it?

UCB: Optimism in the Face of Uncertainty

Here's a more principled idea. Instead of exploring randomly, explore strategically. For each arm, maintain both an estimate of its mean reward and a measure of your uncertainty. Then pick the arm with the highest upper confidence bound — the arm that could be the best given your uncertainty.

The UCB1 formula:

at = argmaxa [ μ̂a + √(2 ln t / Na) ]

where μ̂a is the empirical mean reward of arm a, t is the current round, and Na is how many times you've pulled arm a.

The intuition is elegant. The first term is exploitation — pick arms with high estimated reward. The second term is exploration — it's large when Na is small (you haven't tried this arm much) and shrinks as you gather data. Arms you've barely tried get a big confidence bonus. Arms you've pulled a thousand times — you know what they are, no bonus needed.

import numpy as np

class UCB1:
    def __init__(self, n_arms):
        self.n_arms = n_arms
        self.counts = np.zeros(n_arms)
        self.values = np.zeros(n_arms)

    def select_arm(self):
        # Pull each arm once first
        for arm in range(self.n_arms):
            if self.counts[arm] == 0:
                return arm

        total = np.sum(self.counts)
        ucb_values = self.values + np.sqrt(2 * np.log(total) / self.counts)
        return np.argmax(ucb_values)

    def update(self, arm, reward):
        self.counts[arm] += 1
        n = self.counts[arm]
        self.values[arm] += (reward - self.values[arm]) / n

UCB1 achieves O(log T) regret — matching the Lai-Robbins lower bound up to constant factors. No hyperparameters to tune. No random exploration. The algorithm figures out on its own how much to explore and when to stop. That's the beauty of it.

The UCB Principle Beyond Bandits

"Optimism in the face of uncertainty" shows up everywhere in ML. It's the same principle behind MCTS in AlphaGo (UCT = UCB applied to trees), optimistic initialization in RL, and even some active learning strategies. When you don't know something, assume it might be great — then go find out.

Thompson Sampling: Let the Posterior Decide

Thompson Sampling (1933 — yes, it's that old) takes a Bayesian approach. For each arm, maintain a posterior distribution over its reward parameter. At each round, sample from each arm's posterior and pull the arm whose sample is highest.

For Bernoulli rewards (click / no click), this is beautifully simple. Each arm's reward rate follows a Beta distribution:

  1. Start with Beta(1, 1) prior for each arm (uniform — you know nothing)
  2. Sample θ̂a ~ Beta(αa, βa) for each arm
  3. Pull arm a* = argmax θ̂a
  4. Observe reward r ∈ {0, 1}
  5. Update: αa* += r, βa* += (1 − r)

That's it. The posterior naturally balances exploration and exploitation. An arm with few pulls has a wide posterior — its samples will be high sometimes, triggering exploration. An arm with many pulls and low reward has a tight, low posterior — it almost never gets selected. No confidence bounds, no epsilon, no tuning.

import numpy as np

class ThompsonSampling:
    def __init__(self, n_arms):
        self.n_arms = n_arms
        self.alpha = np.ones(n_arms)   # successes + 1
        self.beta = np.ones(n_arms)    # failures + 1

    def select_arm(self):
        # Sample from each arm's posterior
        samples = np.random.beta(self.alpha, self.beta)
        return np.argmax(samples)

    def update(self, arm, reward):
        # Conjugate update for Bernoulli likelihood
        self.alpha[arm] += reward
        self.beta[arm] += 1 - reward

# --- Simulate: 5 arms with different click-through rates ---
np.random.seed(42)
true_rates = [0.02, 0.05, 0.03, 0.08, 0.04]
n_rounds = 10000
ts = ThompsonSampling(n_arms=5)

rewards = []
for t in range(n_rounds):
    arm = ts.select_arm()
    reward = np.random.binomial(1, true_rates[arm])
    ts.update(arm, reward)
    rewards.append(reward)

# After 10k rounds, Thompson mostly exploits the best arm (0.08)
print(f"Estimated rates: {ts.alpha / (ts.alpha + ts.beta)}")
print(f"Pull counts:     {ts.alpha + ts.beta - 2}")
print(f"Average reward:  {np.mean(rewards):.4f}")
# Best possible average: 0.08

Thompson Sampling achieves the Lai-Robbins lower bound asymptotically. In practice, it often outperforms UCB because the posterior does a better job of allocating exploration where it matters. If two arms are close in performance, Thompson samples between them naturally. If one arm is clearly worse, the posterior becomes tight and it stops wasting pulls quickly.

Why Thompson Sampling Wins in Practice

UCB explores all uncertain arms equally. Thompson Sampling concentrates exploration on arms that are likely to be near-optimal. When you have many arms and most are clearly bad, Thompson converges faster. It also handles non-stationary environments more gracefully — just use a sliding-window posterior instead of accumulating all history.

Thompson Sampling for Gaussian Rewards

For continuous rewards (revenue, time-on-page), use a Normal-Inverse-Gamma posterior, or the simpler approximation: track the mean and variance of each arm, then sample from a Normal distribution. With enough data, this works well:

class ThompsonSamplingGaussian:
    def __init__(self, n_arms):
        self.n_arms = n_arms
        self.counts = np.zeros(n_arms)
        self.means = np.zeros(n_arms)
        self.sq_diffs = np.zeros(n_arms)  # for online variance

    def select_arm(self):
        samples = np.zeros(self.n_arms)
        for a in range(self.n_arms):
            if self.counts[a] < 2:
                return a  # not enough data yet
            variance = self.sq_diffs[a] / (self.counts[a] - 1)
            # Sample from posterior: mean ± uncertainty
            samples[a] = np.random.normal(
                self.means[a],
                np.sqrt(variance / self.counts[a])
            )
        return np.argmax(samples)

    def update(self, arm, reward):
        self.counts[arm] += 1
        n = self.counts[arm]
        old_mean = self.means[arm]
        self.means[arm] += (reward - old_mean) / n
        # Welford's online variance
        self.sq_diffs[arm] += (reward - old_mean) * (reward - self.means[arm])

Contextual Bandits: When Context Matters

Plain bandits assume every user, every moment is the same. But a 25-year-old browsing from a phone at midnight has different preferences than a 60-year-old on a desktop at 9 AM. Contextual bandits extend the framework: at each round, you observe a context vector xt (user features, time of day, device) and must choose an action whose expected reward depends on that context.

This is exactly what happens in recommendation systems. The "arms" are items to show (articles, ads, products). The context is user features. The reward is a click, purchase, or engagement signal.

LinUCB

The most influential contextual bandit algorithm is LinUCB (Li et al., 2010, used at Yahoo for news recommendation). It assumes the expected reward of arm a given context x is linear: E[r|x, a] = θaTx.

For each arm, maintain a ridge regression estimate of θa and its confidence set. Then pick the arm with the highest UCB in context:

import numpy as np

class LinUCB:
    def __init__(self, n_arms, d, alpha=1.0):
        self.n_arms = n_arms
        self.alpha = alpha  # exploration parameter
        # Per-arm parameters
        self.A = [np.eye(d) for _ in range(n_arms)]       # d×d
        self.b = [np.zeros(d) for _ in range(n_arms)]     # d×1

    def select_arm(self, context):
        ucb_values = np.zeros(self.n_arms)
        for a in range(self.n_arms):
            A_inv = np.linalg.inv(self.A[a])
            theta_hat = A_inv @ self.b[a]
            # UCB = predicted reward + exploration bonus
            ucb_values[a] = (
                theta_hat @ context
                + self.alpha * np.sqrt(context @ A_inv @ context)
            )
        return np.argmax(ucb_values)

    def update(self, arm, context, reward):
        self.A[arm] += np.outer(context, context)
        self.b[arm] += reward * context

The exploration bonus √(xTA−1x) is large when the context is in a direction the model hasn't seen much data for. It's the multi-dimensional version of the 1/√Na term in UCB1. When contexts are diverse and high-dimensional, LinUCB is significantly better than ignoring context.

From Contextual Bandits to RL

Contextual bandits sit neatly between supervised learning and full reinforcement learning:

SettingFeedbackActions Affect Future?Example
Supervised learningFull labelsNoImage classification
Online learningFull labels, streamingNoSpam filtering
Multi-armed banditsReward for chosen action onlyNoA/B testing
Contextual banditsReward for chosen action + contextNoAd selection

The key jump to RL is that your actions change the state — showing a user a video affects what they want to watch next. In contextual bandits, each round is independent. That simplification is what makes bandits tractable and practical.

Real-World Applications

A/B Testing: Why Bandits Beat Fixed Splits

Traditional A/B testing: split traffic 50/50 between variant A and variant B, wait two weeks, compute a p-value. Done.

The problem? During those two weeks, half your users are seeing the worse variant. If variant B is clearly better after day 3, you're still forcing 50% of traffic to the loser for 11 more days. That's a lot of lost revenue, engagement, or whatever metric you care about.

Bandit-based testing uses Thompson Sampling (or UCB) to gradually shift traffic toward the winning variant while the test is running. Early on, traffic is roughly 50/50. As evidence accumulates, the better variant gets more traffic. By the end, you're sending 90%+ to the winner and still collecting enough data on the loser to confirm your conclusion.

def bandit_ab_test(variants, n_visitors=10000):
    """Bandit-based A/B test with Thompson Sampling."""
    n = len(variants)
    ts = ThompsonSampling(n_arms=n)

    results = {v: {"shown": 0, "converted": 0} for v in variants}
    variant_names = list(variants.keys())

    for _ in range(n_visitors):
        arm = ts.select_arm()
        name = variant_names[arm]
        true_rate = variants[name]

        converted = np.random.binomial(1, true_rate)
        ts.update(arm, converted)

        results[name]["shown"] += 1
        results[name]["converted"] += converted

    for name, data in results.items():
        rate = data["converted"] / max(data["shown"], 1)
        print(f"{name}: {data['shown']:>5} shown, "
              f"{rate:.3f} conv rate (true: {variants[name]:.3f})")

# Variant B is 20% better — bandit allocates traffic accordingly
bandit_ab_test({
    "control":   0.050,
    "variant_b":  0.060,
    "variant_c":  0.045,
})
The Statistical Validity Trap

Bandit algorithms minimize regret, but they do not give you a valid p-value out of the box. Because the sampling is adaptive (you're sending more traffic to the winner), standard frequentist confidence intervals are invalid. If you need statistical guarantees, use "always-valid" confidence sequences or mix bandit allocation with proper sequential testing. Google, Netflix, and Microsoft all use hybrid approaches.

Recommendation Systems

Every time you open Netflix, Spotify, or YouTube, a contextual bandit (or something close to it) is deciding what to show you. The arms are content items, the context is your profile + session features, and the reward is engagement.

Why not just use a supervised model? Because supervised learning requires labeled examples, and the labels are generated by your current recommendation system. If your system never shows jazz to rock fans, you'll never know that some rock fans actually love jazz. This is the feedback loop problem — supervised models can't escape local optima because they only learn from what they already serve. Bandits, by construction, explore.

Ad Placement

Online advertising is the canonical bandit application. Each ad impression is a bandit round: the context is the user + page content, the arms are candidate ads, and the reward is a click (or purchase). The advertiser wants to maximize click-through rate while still discovering which creatives work best for which audiences. Thompson Sampling is the industry standard here — it handles the large action space (thousands of ads) and non-stationarity (ad fatigue, seasonal trends) better than UCB.

Clinical Trials

Perhaps the most impactful application: adaptive clinical trials. Traditional trials assign patients equally to treatment and placebo groups. Bandit-based trials (like the response-adaptive randomization used during COVID vaccine trials) shift more patients toward the treatment that's performing better, reducing the number of patients receiving inferior treatment while still gathering statistically valid evidence.

Practical Tips

Start with Thompson Sampling. Unless you have a specific reason to use something else, Thompson is the default choice in industry. It's simple to implement, naturally handles many arms, and its Bayesian nature makes it easy to incorporate prior knowledge.

Batched updates are fine. In practice, you don't update after every single observation. Updating in batches of 100 or 1,000 barely affects regret and is much more practical for production systems where the reward signal is delayed (did the user buy something three days after clicking?).

Beware non-stationarity. All the regret bounds assume rewards are stationary. In reality, user preferences shift, ad creatives fatigue, and seasonal effects dominate. Use a sliding window or exponential discounting on your reward estimates:

class SlidingWindowThompson:
    def __init__(self, n_arms, window_size=1000):
        self.n_arms = n_arms
        self.window = window_size
        self.history = []  # (arm, reward) pairs

    def select_arm(self):
        recent = self.history[-self.window:]
        alpha = np.ones(self.n_arms)
        beta = np.ones(self.n_arms)
        for arm, reward in recent:
            alpha[arm] += reward
            beta[arm] += 1 - reward
        return np.argmax(np.random.beta(alpha, beta))

    def update(self, arm, reward):
        self.history.append((arm, reward))

Cold-start with priors. If you're launching a new ad variant or product, you don't start from total ignorance. Use historical data from similar items to set informative priors. A new ad by an advertiser whose ads typically get 3% CTR? Start with Beta(3, 97) instead of Beta(1, 1).
