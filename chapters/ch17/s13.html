Optimal Transport in ML

Chapter 17: Learning Theory & Advanced ML 12 subtopics
TL;DR

Optimal transport asks: what is the cheapest way to reshape one distribution into another? The answer — the Wasserstein distance — gives us a metric that works even when two distributions don't overlap, unlike KL divergence. This unlocks better GAN training (WGAN), domain adaptation, NLP similarity (Word Mover's Distance), and surprising connections to attention mechanisms. The Sinkhorn algorithm makes it all fast enough to use as a training loss. If you've ever compared probability distributions and wondered why KL divergence was misbehaving, optimal transport is the fix.

The Dirt-Moving Problem

Imagine two piles of dirt. One is spread across the left side of a field; you need it arranged on the right side in a different shape. You have a shovel and a wheelbarrow. Every scoop costs effort proportional to how far you carry it. What's the cheapest plan to rearrange the dirt?

That's optimal transport. Replace "dirt" with "probability mass" and "field" with "some metric space," and you get one of the most elegant ideas in mathematics — one that turns out to be shockingly useful in machine learning.

Why should you care? Because whenever you train a model, you're implicitly comparing distributions. The generator in a GAN produces a distribution of fake images. Your domain adaptation model maps a source distribution to a target. Your language model produces a distribution over tokens. The question "how different are these two distributions?" is central to everything. And the answer you pick — KL divergence, Jensen-Shannon, or Wasserstein — determines whether your gradients are useful or useless.

Monge and Kantorovich Formulations

The French mathematician Gaspard Monge posed the original problem in 1781: given a source distribution μ and a target distribution ν, find a transport map T that pushes μ onto ν while minimizing the total cost. Think of T as a set of instructions: "take dirt from location x and dump it at location T(x)." Each grain of dirt goes to exactly one destination. The total cost is the integral of the distance each grain travels.

The catch? The Monge formulation requires a one-to-one map. Every source point maps to exactly one target point. No splitting allowed. This is the "one shovel" version of the problem. It's clean, but it's also restrictive. What if you have a single heavy pile that needs to feed two distant locations? Monge says you can't split it. Worse, if the distributions are discrete with different numbers of points, a valid Monge map might not even exist.

Enter Leonid Kantorovich, about 160 years later. Instead of a deterministic map, Kantorovich allows a transport plan — a joint distribution γ over source-target pairs. Each source point can split its mass across multiple destinations. Each destination can receive mass from multiple sources. The only constraints: the marginals of γ must match μ and ν. This is the "splitting piles" version — more flexible, always has a solution, and turns the problem into a linear program.

ℹ️ Key Insight

Monge says "each source goes to one destination." Kantorovich says "each source can spread across many destinations." Kantorovich's formulation is a relaxation of Monge's — it's always solvable and has the same optimal cost when a Monge map exists. Nearly all modern ML uses the Kantorovich version.

In matrix terms: if you have n source points and m target points, the transport plan is an n×m matrix where entry γ_ij says how much mass flows from source i to target j. The row sums must equal the source weights, the column sums must equal the target weights, and you minimize the sum of γ_ij × c_ij where c_ij is the cost of moving mass from i to j.

Wasserstein Distance (Earth Mover's Distance)

Solve the Kantorovich problem and the optimal cost you get is the Wasserstein distance — also called the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD). Formally, the p-Wasserstein distance W_p(μ, ν) is the p-th root of the minimum expected cost when the cost function is distance raised to the p-th power. In practice, you'll almost always see W_1 (costs are just distances) or W_2 (costs are squared distances).

The earth mover metaphor is perfect. W_1(μ, ν) is the minimum total work to move dirt from the shape of μ into the shape of ν, where work equals mass times distance. If two distributions are identical, the cost is zero — no dirt needs to move. If they're far apart, the cost is high. If you shift one distribution slightly, the cost changes smoothly and proportionally. That last property is what makes Wasserstein special.

Wasserstein vs KL Divergence

Here's why this matters. Consider two distributions that don't overlap at all: a Gaussian centered at 0 and another centered at 100. KL divergence between them is infinite. Jensen-Shannon divergence is a constant (log 2). Neither tells you anything useful about the distance between them. But Wasserstein? It's 100. Move the second distribution ten units closer to the first, and Wasserstein drops to 90. You get gradients that point in the right direction.

This is the core problem that motivated WGANs. When a GAN's generator is bad (early in training), the real and fake distributions have near-zero overlap. KL and JS divergence are flat — their gradients vanish. The discriminator wins perfectly, and the generator learns nothing. Wasserstein distance still provides a meaningful gradient, even with non-overlapping supports. It measures geometry, not just overlap.

⚠️ Warning

KL divergence is not a true distance — it's asymmetric (KL(P‖Q) ≠ KL(Q‖P)) and can be infinite. Wasserstein distance is a proper metric: symmetric, satisfies the triangle inequality, and is always finite for distributions with finite moments. This matters for convergence guarantees and training stability.

Let's compute both and see the difference:

import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import wasserstein_distance
from scipy.special import kl_div

# Two non-overlapping 1D distributions
np.random.seed(42)
P = np.random.normal(loc=0, scale=1, size=1000)
Q = np.random.normal(loc=5, scale=1, size=1000)

# Wasserstein-1 distance (always finite, meaningful)
w_dist = wasserstein_distance(P, Q)
print(f"Wasserstein-1 distance: {w_dist:.4f}")   # ~5.0

# For KL divergence, we need density estimates
from scipy.stats import gaussian_kde
x_grid = np.linspace(-5, 10, 500)
p_density = gaussian_kde(P)(x_grid) + 1e-10  # avoid log(0)
q_density = gaussian_kde(Q)(x_grid) + 1e-10

kl = np.sum(p_density * np.log(p_density / q_density)) * (x_grid[1] - x_grid[0])
print(f"KL divergence (approx): {kl:.4f}")        # very large

# Now slide Q closer and watch Wasserstein respond smoothly
for shift in [5, 3, 1, 0.1, 0]:
    Q_shifted = np.random.normal(loc=shift, scale=1, size=1000)
    w = wasserstein_distance(P, Q_shifted)
    print(f"  shift={shift:.1f} -> W1={w:.4f}")

Sinkhorn Algorithm and Entropic Regularization

There's a problem with the Wasserstein distance: computing it exactly is expensive. Solving the linear program for n points costs O(n³ log n). For a batch of 64×64 images (4096 pixels), that's brutal. For modern datasets, it's impossible.

The fix is entropic regularization. Instead of finding the transport plan that minimizes cost alone, you minimize cost minus ε times the entropy of the plan. The entropy term H(γ) = −Σ γ_ij log(γ_ij) acts as a smoothing force — it penalizes transport plans that are too "spiky" and prefers plans that spread mass more evenly. The regularized problem has a beautiful structure: the optimal plan takes the form γ_ij = u_i × K_ij × v_j, where K is the Gibbs kernel K_ij = exp(−c_ij / ε) and u, v are scaling vectors.

Finding u and v is the Sinkhorn algorithm: alternate between normalizing rows and columns of the matrix u ⊙ K ⊙ v until convergence. Each iteration is a matrix-vector multiply — embarrassingly parallel on a GPU. The algorithm converges linearly, and in practice 50–100 iterations suffice.

import torch

def sinkhorn_distance(cost_matrix, a, b, epsilon=0.1, n_iters=100):
    """
    Compute Sinkhorn divergence between distributions a and b.

    Args:
        cost_matrix: (n, m) pairwise cost between points
        a: (n,) source distribution weights (sum to 1)
        b: (m,) target distribution weights (sum to 1)
        epsilon: entropic regularization strength
        n_iters: number of Sinkhorn iterations
    Returns:
        Approximate Wasserstein distance (scalar)
    """
    # Gibbs kernel
    K = torch.exp(-cost_matrix / epsilon)

    # Initialize scaling vectors
    u = torch.ones_like(a)

    for _ in range(n_iters):
        # Row normalization: make row sums match source
        u = a / (K @ (b / (K.T @ u)))

    v = b / (K.T @ u)

    # Transport plan and cost
    transport_plan = u.unsqueeze(1) * K * v.unsqueeze(0)
    distance = (transport_plan * cost_matrix).sum()
    return distance

# Example: compare two 2D point clouds
n, m = 100, 120
X = torch.randn(n, 2)          # source points
Y = torch.randn(m, 2) + 2.0   # target points, shifted

# Pairwise squared Euclidean cost
cost = torch.cdist(X, Y, p=2).pow(2)

# Uniform weights
a = torch.ones(n) / n
b = torch.ones(m) / m

dist = sinkhorn_distance(cost, a, b, epsilon=0.05)
print(f"Sinkhorn distance: {dist.item():.4f}")

Sinkhorn Divergence as Training Loss

There's a subtlety. The Sinkhorn distance with finite ε is biased — it doesn't go to zero when the two distributions are identical (because the entropy term adds a baseline cost). The fix is the Sinkhorn divergence: S_ε(μ, ν) = OT_ε(μ, ν) − ½ OT_ε(μ, μ) − ½ OT_ε(ν, ν). This debiased version is zero when μ = ν, positive otherwise, and differentiable everywhere — perfect as a training loss.

Libraries like geomloss implement this efficiently on GPU with automatic differentiation. You can drop it into a PyTorch training loop as a replacement for KL divergence or MSE when comparing distributions or point clouds.

Choosing ε

The regularization parameter ε controls the bias-variance tradeoff. Large ε gives a smooth, fast-converging but blurry approximation. Small ε gives a sharper approximation closer to the true Wasserstein distance but needs more iterations and can be numerically unstable (the Gibbs kernel entries become near-zero). In practice, start with ε around 0.01–0.1 times the median pairwise cost and tune from there.

Sliced Wasserstein Distance

Even Sinkhorn can be too slow for high-dimensional data. The Sliced Wasserstein Distance (SWD) takes a different approach: project both distributions onto random 1D lines, compute the exact 1D Wasserstein distance on each projection (which is trivially the difference of sorted samples), and average over many random projections.

Why does this work? In one dimension, the Wasserstein distance between two distributions is the L1 distance between their quantile functions — you sort both sets of samples and sum the absolute differences. That's O(n log n) for the sort. Do this for L random projections and you get an O(L × n log n) algorithm that scales to any dimension. The Sliced Wasserstein distance is a proper metric and approximates the true Wasserstein distance well, especially as the number of projections grows.

import torch

def sliced_wasserstein(X, Y, n_projections=200):
    """
    Sliced Wasserstein distance between point clouds X and Y.

    Args:
        X: (n, d) source points
        Y: (m, d) target points (m should equal n for simplicity)
        n_projections: number of random 1D projections
    Returns:
        Approximate Wasserstein-2 distance (scalar)
    """
    d = X.shape[1]

    # Random unit vectors on the d-dimensional sphere
    directions = torch.randn(n_projections, d)
    directions = directions / directions.norm(dim=1, keepdim=True)

    # Project both point clouds onto each direction
    X_proj = X @ directions.T   # (n, n_projections)
    Y_proj = Y @ directions.T   # (m, n_projections)

    # Sort each projection independently
    X_sorted = torch.sort(X_proj, dim=0).values
    Y_sorted = torch.sort(Y_proj, dim=0).values

    # 1D Wasserstein = sum of absolute differences of sorted values
    return ((X_sorted - Y_sorted).pow(2).mean(dim=0)).mean().sqrt()

# Compare two 50-dimensional point clouds
n = 500
X = torch.randn(n, 50)
Y = torch.randn(n, 50) + 0.5   # slight shift in all dims

swd = sliced_wasserstein(X, Y)
print(f"Sliced Wasserstein distance: {swd.item():.4f}")

Sliced Wasserstein is the go-to choice when you need a Wasserstein-like loss in high dimensions. It shows up in generative modeling (comparing generated and real image distributions), style transfer, and point cloud reconstruction. Variants like Max-Sliced Wasserstein pick the single worst-case projection instead of averaging, giving a tighter (but noisier) bound.

Wasserstein Barycenters

A Wasserstein barycenter is the "average" of several distributions in the Wasserstein sense. Given distributions μ_1, …, μ_K with weights λ_1, …, λ_K, the barycenter minimizes the weighted sum of Wasserstein distances to all of them. It's the Fréchet mean in Wasserstein space.

Why not average the densities pointwise? Because pointwise averaging doesn't respect geometry. If you average two Gaussians at locations 0 and 10, the pointwise average is a bimodal distribution. The Wasserstein barycenter is a single Gaussian at location 5 — it interpolates through distribution space in a way that respects the underlying geometry. Peaks move rather than blend.

Applications include texture mixing (interpolate between texture distributions), shape interpolation (the barycenter of two 3D shapes is a smooth morph), and multi-source domain adaptation (find a common representation that's equidistant from multiple source domains).

Optimal Transport for Domain Adaptation (OTDA)

Domain adaptation is about training on data from one distribution (source) and deploying on another (target). The classic example: you have labeled medical images from Hospital A but need to deploy at Hospital B, where the scanners produce different image characteristics.

Optimal Transport Domain Adaptation (OTDA) finds a transport plan from source to target, then uses that plan to transform source samples so they look like target samples. Concretely: compute the optimal coupling between source and target feature distributions, then map each source point to a weighted combination of target points according to the coupling. The transformed source data now lives in the target domain, and you can train a classifier on it.

The beauty of OTDA is that it's geometry-aware. It doesn't assume the domain shift is a global translation or scaling. The transport plan can handle arbitrary reshaping of the distribution — stretching, rotating, compressing different regions differently. Libraries like POT (Python Optimal Transport) provide ready-to-use classes for this.

ℹ️ Key Insight

OTDA's advantage over simpler domain adaptation methods (like matching means and covariances) is that it respects the full geometry of the distributions. Two domains might have the same mean and variance but very different shapes. Optimal transport captures those shape differences and corrects for them.

Word Mover's Distance (NLP)

The Word Mover's Distance (WMD) applies optimal transport to natural language. Treat each document as a distribution over word embeddings: each word is a point in embedding space, weighted by its frequency in the document (after removing stop words). The WMD between two documents is the minimum cost of transporting one document's word distribution to the other's, where cost is the Euclidean distance between word embeddings.

This gives you a document similarity metric that understands synonyms and paraphrases without any training. "The president speaks to the media" and "The leader talks to the press" have high WMD similarity because "president" is close to "leader," "speaks" is close to "talks," and "media" is close to "press" in embedding space. The transport plan finds the cheapest word-to-word alignment.

WMD is expensive for long documents (it scales with vocabulary size), so people often use relaxed versions — the Relaxed WMD drops the constraint that target words must receive the right amount of mass, giving a fast lower bound that's useful for retrieval (filter candidates with RWMD, re-rank with exact WMD).

Connection to Attention Mechanisms

Here's the surprising part. The Sinkhorn algorithm — alternating row and column normalizations of a kernel matrix — looks a lot like what happens inside a transformer's attention mechanism. Both start with a pairwise similarity matrix (the cost matrix vs. the QK^T matrix). Both apply softmax-like normalization (the Gibbs kernel and row/column scaling vs. row-wise softmax). Both produce a coupling that says "how much does source position i attend to target position j?"

This isn't a coincidence. Standard attention applies row normalization only (each query attends to keys). But if you apply both row and column normalization — which is exactly Sinkhorn — you get a doubly stochastic attention matrix. This has been explored in work on Sinkhorn attention and sparse attention mechanisms, where the transport-plan interpretation enforces that each key is attended to by roughly the same total amount, preventing some tokens from being ignored entirely.

The connection goes deeper. The optimal transport plan between encoder and decoder representations can be interpreted as a form of cross-attention. Some recent architectures explicitly use Sinkhorn-based attention as a drop-in replacement for softmax attention, gaining better coverage (every token gets attended to) at the cost of slightly more computation per layer.

Connection to Maximum Entropy Principles

Entropic regularization isn't an arbitrary hack — it's deeply connected to the maximum entropy principle. When you add the entropy term εH(γ) to the transport cost, you're saying: "among all transport plans with similar cost, prefer the one that's least committed — the one that spreads mass as evenly as possible." This is exactly Jaynes' maximum entropy principle applied to transport plans.

The connection runs both ways. The Sinkhorn scaling vectors u and v are dual variables that correspond to entropic potentials. The regularized transport problem is equivalent to finding a joint distribution with prescribed marginals that maximizes entropy subject to a cost constraint. This connects optimal transport to exponential families, Boltzmann distributions, and the entire framework of information geometry.

The Sinkhorn-Softmax Connection

Softmax is a single-sided Sinkhorn iteration. Standard softmax normalizes rows of the attention matrix (ensuring each query's attention weights sum to 1). Sinkhorn normalizes both rows and columns (ensuring both queries and keys have balanced total attention). This makes Sinkhorn attention a natural extension of softmax attention, grounded in optimal transport theory.

WGAN and Optimal Transport

The Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) is where optimal transport made its biggest splash in deep learning. Standard GANs minimize the Jensen-Shannon divergence between real and generated distributions, which leads to the vanishing gradient problem when the discriminator gets too good. WGAN replaces this with the Wasserstein-1 distance.

The trick is the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality: W_1(μ, ν) = sup over 1-Lipschitz functions f of E[f(x)] − E[f(y)], where x comes from μ and y from ν. Instead of solving the transport problem directly, you train a neural network (called a "critic" instead of "discriminator") to approximate this supremum. The critic must be Lipschitz-continuous, which WGAN enforces through weight clipping.

WGAN-GP (gradient penalty) improved on this by replacing weight clipping with a penalty on the gradient norm of the critic. The penalty encourages the critic to have gradients of norm 1 everywhere, which is the necessary condition for a 1-Lipschitz function to achieve the supremum. Training becomes more stable, and the critic's loss directly estimates the Wasserstein distance — you can actually monitor convergence by watching the loss go down, unlike standard GANs where the discriminator loss is meaningless.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class WGANCritic(nn.Module):
    """Critic network for WGAN-GP (estimates Wasserstein distance)."""
    def __init__(self, input_dim=784):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(input_dim, 512),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2),
            nn.Linear(512, 256),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2),
            nn.Linear(256, 1)   # no sigmoid — outputs unbounded score
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        return self.net(x)

def gradient_penalty(critic, real, fake, device, lambda_gp=10.0):
    """
    Gradient penalty for WGAN-GP.
    Penalizes gradients that deviate from norm 1 along
    interpolations between real and fake samples.
    """
    batch_size = real.size(0)

    # Random interpolation between real and fake
    alpha = torch.rand(batch_size, 1, device=device)
    interpolated = (alpha * real + (1 - alpha) * fake).requires_grad_(True)

    # Critic score on interpolated points
    score = critic(interpolated)

    # Compute gradients w.r.t. interpolated inputs
    gradients = torch.autograd.grad(
        outputs=score,
        inputs=interpolated,
        grad_outputs=torch.ones_like(score),
        create_graph=True,
        retain_graph=True
    )[0]

    # Penalize deviation of gradient norm from 1
    grad_norm = gradients.norm(2, dim=1)
    penalty = lambda_gp * ((grad_norm - 1) ** 2).mean()
    return penalty

# In the training loop, the critic loss becomes:
# loss_critic = critic(fake).mean() - critic(real).mean() + gradient_penalty(...)
# loss_generator = -critic(fake).mean()
⚠️ Warning

WGAN requires training the critic multiple times per generator update (typically 5 critic steps per 1 generator step). This is because the Wasserstein estimate is only meaningful when the critic is close to optimal. If you update them equally, the critic never converges and the Wasserstein estimate is garbage. This is a common mistake when implementing WGAN from scratch.

Putting It All Together

Optimal transport is not one technique — it's a lens. Once you see distribution comparison through the transport lens, connections appear everywhere. The attention matrix in a transformer is a soft transport plan. Domain adaptation is a transport problem. Document similarity is a transport problem. GAN training is a transport problem. Even the simple act of matching predicted probabilities to ground-truth labels can be viewed as transporting mass from one distribution to another.

The practical toolkit has three tiers. For small-scale exact problems (up to a few thousand points), use the linear-programming solver in scipy.optimize.linear_sum_assignment or the POT library's ot.emd. For medium-scale differentiable problems (training losses, GPU batches), use Sinkhorn via geomloss or implement it yourself — it's 20 lines of code. For high-dimensional comparisons (images, embeddings), use Sliced Wasserstein — it's even shorter to implement and scales gracefully.

The field is moving fast. Unbalanced optimal transport relaxes the equal-mass constraint (useful when comparing distributions of different sizes). Partial transport lets you move only a fraction of the mass (useful for outlier-robust matching). Neural optimal transport learns the transport map with a neural network, avoiding the quadratic cost matrix entirely. But the fundamentals — Kantorovich's formulation, the Wasserstein metric, and Sinkhorn's algorithm — remain the bedrock. Learn those, and the rest is variations on a theme.
