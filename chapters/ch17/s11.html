Information-Theoretic Limits in ML

TL;DR

Information theory doesn't tell you how to build better models — it tells you when no model can do better. Fano's inequality, Le Cam's method, and Assouad's lemma give us hard floors on classification and estimation error. The data processing inequality explains why deep layers can't magically create information. Rate-distortion theory is the math behind autoencoders. And mutual information quietly powers everything from generalization bounds to the information gain in decision trees. These tools let you stop chasing impossible accuracy and focus your energy where it actually matters.

Why Impossibility Results Matter

Imagine you're building a classifier for a medical imaging task. You've got 500 labeled samples and 20 disease categories. You've tried ResNets, Vision Transformers, every trick in the book. Your accuracy plateaus at 72%. Your manager asks: "Can we hit 95%?" The honest answer might be: no algorithm in the universe can do that with this data.

That's what information-theoretic limits give you. Not a better model — a lower bound on how badly any model must perform given the amount of information available. Think of it as the speed of light for machine learning: a fundamental constraint that no amount of engineering cleverness can break.

Most ML courses focus on upper bounds — "this algorithm achieves error at most X." Information theory flips the question: "No matter what algorithm you use, the error is at least Y." When your upper bound meets your lower bound, you know you've found the optimal rate. When there's a gap, there's still room to improve — or the bounds aren't tight enough yet.

Fano's Inequality and Classification Error Lower Bounds

Let's start with the most practical tool. You're trying to classify an input into one of K classes. You observe some data Y that's statistically related to the true class X. Fano's inequality says your probability of making a mistake is bounded below by how little information Y carries about X.

Formally: P(error) ≥ (H(X|Y) − 1) / log(K). Here H(X|Y) is the conditional entropy — the remaining uncertainty about X after you've seen Y. If Y tells you almost everything about X, then H(X|Y) is close to zero and the bound is loose (good news — low error is possible). If Y is barely correlated with X, H(X|Y) stays close to H(X) = log(K) for uniform classes, and the bound says you must make lots of mistakes.

Here's the intuition with a concrete example. Say you have 10 disease classes (K = 10) with equal prevalence. Random guessing gives 90% error. If your imaging features only carry 1 bit of information about the disease out of log₂(10) ≈ 3.32 bits total, Fano's inequality tells you no classifier — not a neural net, not a human expert working from those same features — can get the error rate below a specific floor.

ℹ️ Key Insight

Fano's inequality is your reality check. Before spending months on model architecture, estimate the mutual information between your features and labels. If I(X;Y) is small relative to log(K), no architecture will save you — you need better features or more data.

Le Cam's Method for Minimax Lower Bounds

Fano's inequality works well for classification. But what about estimation problems — predicting a continuous value like a patient's blood pressure? Enter Le Cam's method, the simplest technique for proving minimax lower bounds.

The idea is elegant. You pick two specific parameter values, θ₀ and θ₁, that are far apart in the quantity you care about but whose data distributions look nearly identical. If the distributions P_θ₀ and P_θ₁ are hard to tell apart from N samples, then any estimator must have large error on at least one of them. You're essentially saying: "If you can't even solve this simple two-hypothesis problem, you certainly can't solve the harder general problem."

The bound involves the total variation distance between the two distributions. When you only have N samples from a distribution that could be either P_θ₀ or P_θ₁, and their total variation is small, you're stuck — like trying to decide if a coin has probability 0.50 or 0.51 of heads by flipping it 10 times. The data doesn't carry enough signal.

Assouad's Lemma

Le Cam's method uses two hypotheses. Assouad's lemma scales this up by using 2^d hypotheses, one for each corner of a d-dimensional hypercube. Each coordinate of the hypercube corresponds to a "local" parameter that can be flipped independently.

Think of it this way. Suppose you're estimating a function that's piecewise constant on d intervals. Each interval's value can be either "high" or "low." Assouad's lemma reduces the estimation problem to d independent binary testing problems. If each local test is hard (the distributions for high and low are close), then the overall estimation error is at least d times the local error.

This is incredibly powerful for high-dimensional problems. It's one of the main tools used to prove that nonparametric regression over a d-dimensional space with smoothness s has minimax rate n^(−2s/(2s+d)) — the famous curse of dimensionality, proven as an unavoidable lower bound, not an artifact of bad algorithms.

Minimax Rate of Estimation (Gaussian Mean)

Let's see these ideas in the cleanest possible setting. You observe N samples from a Gaussian with unknown mean θ and known variance σ². What's the best any estimator can do?

The minimax rate for estimating the mean is σ²/N. The sample mean achieves this. And Le Cam's method (or even direct calculation) proves no estimator can do better. The upper and lower bounds match — we say the problem is "solved" in the minimax sense.

This σ²/N rate is the benchmark everything else is measured against. When you see that a nonparametric problem has rate n^(−2s/(2s+d)), you can feel the curse of dimensionality — as d grows, you need exponentially more samples to achieve the same accuracy. That's not a failure of your algorithm. It's physics. 

# Demonstrating the minimax rate for Gaussian mean estimation
import numpy as np

np.random.seed(42)
true_mean = 3.0
sigma = 2.0
sample_sizes = [10, 50, 100, 500, 1000, 5000]

print("N samples | MSE (empirical) | Minimax rate (σ²/N)")
print("-" * 55)

for N in sample_sizes:
    # Run 10,000 trials, compute MSE of sample mean
    estimates = np.array([
        np.mean(np.random.normal(true_mean, sigma, size=N))
        for _ in range(10000)
    ])
    empirical_mse = np.mean((estimates - true_mean) ** 2)
    theoretical = sigma ** 2 / N
    print(f"  {N:>5}   |     {empirical_mse:.4f}       |     {theoretical:.4f}")

# Output confirms: sample mean MSE ≈ σ²/N — the minimax optimal rate

Data Processing Inequality for Representations

Here's one of the most important ideas in deep learning, even though most practitioners never see it stated formally. The data processing inequality (DPI) says: if X → Y → Z is a Markov chain (Z depends on X only through Y), then I(X; Z) ≤ I(X; Y). Processing data can only destroy information, never create it.

Now think about a neural network. Your input is X. Each layer transforms the representation: X → h₁ → h₂ → ... → h_L → ŷ. By the data processing inequality, each layer's representation carries at most as much information about the target as the previous layer. Information flows downhill.

This has a profound consequence: if your input features X contain only 2 bits of mutual information with the label Y, then no amount of depth, width, or architectural wizardry will give your final prediction more than 2 bits of information about Y. The bottleneck is at the input.

⚠️ Warning

The data processing inequality applies to information content, not to computational accessibility. A deep network might make existing information easier to use (linearly separable, for instance) without increasing the total information. That's the value of representation learning — not creating new information, but reorganizing what's already there into a form that a simple classifier can exploit. 

# Demonstrating data processing inequality with a simple chain
from scipy.stats import entropy
import numpy as np

def mutual_information_discrete(joint_prob):
    """Compute I(X;Y) from a joint probability table."""
    px = joint_prob.sum(axis=1)
    py = joint_prob.sum(axis=0)
    # Avoid log(0)
    mask = joint_prob > 0
    mi = np.sum(
        joint_prob[mask] * np.log2(
            joint_prob[mask] / (px[:, None] * py[None, :])[mask]
        )
    )
    return mi

# X -> Y -> Z chain
# Joint P(X, Y)
pxy = np.array([
    [0.30, 0.05],
    [0.10, 0.55]
])

# Deterministic-ish mapping Y -> Z (lossy)
# P(Z|Y) — a noisy channel
pz_given_y = np.array([
    [0.8, 0.2],
    [0.3, 0.7]
])

# Compute P(X, Z) by marginalizing over Y
py = pxy.sum(axis=0)  # P(Y)
pxy_expanded = pxy[:, :, None]  # (2, 2, 1)
pz_given_y_expanded = pz_given_y[None, :, :]  # (1, 2, 2)
pxyz = pxy_expanded * pz_given_y_expanded  # (2, 2, 2)
pxz = pxyz.sum(axis=1)  # marginalize over Y -> (2, 2)

i_xy = mutual_information_discrete(pxy)
i_xz = mutual_information_discrete(pxz)

print(f"I(X; Y) = {i_xy:.4f} bits")
print(f"I(X; Z) = {i_xz:.4f} bits")
print(f"I(X; Z) <= I(X; Y)? {i_xz <= i_xy + 1e-10}")
# Always True — processing Y into Z cannot increase info about X

Rate-Distortion Theory and Lossy Compression

Shannon's channel coding theorem tells you the limits of lossless communication. Rate-distortion theory answers the lossy question: if you're willing to tolerate some distortion D in your reconstruction, what's the minimum number of bits R(D) you need?

If you've ever trained an autoencoder, you've been doing rate-distortion optimization without calling it that. The bottleneck layer has a fixed capacity (the "rate"). The reconstruction loss is the "distortion." The rate-distortion function R(D) tells you the fundamental tradeoff — below R(D) bits, no encoder-decoder pair can achieve distortion D or less. You can't cheat the curve.

Variational autoencoders (VAEs) make this even more explicit. The ELBO loss has two terms: reconstruction loss (distortion) and KL divergence from the prior (rate). Training a VAE literally traces out points on an empirical rate-distortion curve. The information-theoretic R(D) function is the unreachable lower boundary of that curve.

For a Gaussian source with variance σ², the rate-distortion function is R(D) = ½ log(σ²/D) for D ≤ σ². Double the fidelity (halve D), pay one extra bit. That logarithmic scaling is why compression works so well in practice — you get enormous distortion reduction from the first few bits, with diminishing returns thereafter.

Mutual Information and Generalization Bounds

This is one of the hottest areas in modern learning theory. Traditional generalization bounds (VC dimension, Rademacher complexity) depend on the hypothesis class but not the algorithm or the data distribution. Mutual information generalization bounds fix this by measuring how much the algorithm's output depends on the specific training set.

The key result: if A(S) is the hypothesis chosen by algorithm A given training set S, then the expected generalization gap is bounded by √(2σ² · I(A(S); S) / N), where σ² relates to the loss function's range. The more your algorithm's output "remembers" about the specific training set (high mutual information), the worse the generalization bound.

This beautifully explains several empirical phenomena. Dropout and noise injection reduce I(A(S); S) by making the algorithm's output less dependent on any individual training example. Early stopping works because the algorithm hasn't had time to extract as many bits from the training set. Stochastic gradient descent generalizes better than full-batch gradient descent because the randomness limits information extraction per step.

The Information Bottleneck Hypothesis

Shwartz-Ziv and Tishby (2017) proposed that deep networks learn in two phases: first they increase I(h; Y) (fitting), then they decrease I(h; X) (compression) while maintaining I(h; Y). The compression phase supposedly explains generalization. This sparked a heated debate — the empirical evidence is mixed, especially for ReLU networks where the compression phase may not occur. But the framework of analyzing layers through the lens of I(h; X) and I(h; Y) remains influential.

Differential Entropy and Generative Model Quality

For continuous random variables, we use differential entropy h(X) = −∫ p(x) log p(x) dx instead of the discrete version. Unlike discrete entropy, differential entropy can be negative (a very peaked Gaussian has negative differential entropy). But differences in differential entropy are still meaningful.

When you evaluate a generative model, you're implicitly asking: how close is the model's distribution q to the true data distribution p? The KL divergence KL(p ‖ q) = h(p, q) − h(p) measures this gap, where h(p, q) is the cross-entropy. This is exactly the negative log-likelihood loss — when you train a generative model by maximizing log-likelihood, you're minimizing KL(p ‖ q), which is minimizing the excess bits needed to code data from p using the model q.

Metrics like FID (Fréchet Inception Distance) for GANs are approximations to distributional distance. But the information-theoretic gold standard remains: how many extra bits per sample does your model waste compared to the true distribution? For a perfect model, the answer is zero — it achieves the entropy rate of the data.

Information-Theoretic Sample Complexity Lower Bounds

How many samples do you need to learn a good model? Upper bounds come from specific algorithms. Lower bounds come from information theory, and they apply to every possible algorithm.

The classic argument goes like this. Suppose the parameter space has M well-separated hypotheses. To identify the right one, you need at least log₂(M) bits of information. Each sample provides at most some bounded amount of information (depending on the noise level). Therefore, you need at least log₂(M) / (info per sample) training examples.

For learning a d-dimensional linear model with Gaussian noise of variance σ², this gives a lower bound of Ω(d) samples — matching the upper bound from ordinary least squares. For nonparametric problems, the lower bounds grow much faster and explain why kernel methods need exponentially many samples in high dimensions.

ℹ️ Key Insight

When someone claims their method "breaks the curse of dimensionality," check their assumptions. They're likely exploiting special structure — sparsity, low intrinsic dimension, compositionality — that effectively reduces the size of the hypothesis space. The information-theoretic lower bounds for the unrestricted problem still hold. The win comes from knowing your problem isn't the worst case.

Connection to Entropy in Decision Tree Splitting

Here's where all of this circles back to something you probably use every week. When a decision tree algorithm like ID3 or C4.5 picks which feature to split on, it uses information gain: the reduction in entropy of the label distribution after the split. Information gain is literally mutual information between the split feature and the target.

Before a split, the label entropy is H(Y). After splitting on feature X_j, the conditional entropy is H(Y|X_j). The information gain is H(Y) − H(Y|X_j) = I(Y; X_j). The tree greedily picks the feature with the highest mutual information at each node.

This isn't a coincidence or a heuristic that happens to work. It's the information-theoretically optimal greedy strategy. Each split extracts the maximum number of bits about the label from the available features. The tree builds a lossy code for the labels using the features as the channel. 

# Information gain for decision tree splitting — from scratch
import numpy as np
from collections import Counter

def entropy(labels):
    """Compute H(Y) in bits."""
    counts = np.array(list(Counter(labels).values()), dtype=float)
    probs = counts / counts.sum()
    probs = probs[probs > 0]
    return -np.sum(probs * np.log2(probs))

def conditional_entropy(feature_vals, labels):
    """Compute H(Y|X) — entropy of labels conditioned on a feature."""
    n = len(labels)
    h_cond = 0.0
    for val in set(feature_vals):
        mask = (feature_vals == val)
        weight = mask.sum() / n
        h_cond += weight * entropy(labels[mask])
    return h_cond

def information_gain(feature_vals, labels):
    """I(Y; X) = H(Y) - H(Y|X)"""
    return entropy(labels) - conditional_entropy(feature_vals, labels)

# Example: choosing the best split feature
np.random.seed(42)
n = 200
labels = np.array(["spam"] * 100 + ["ham"] * 100)

# Feature A: strongly correlated with label
feature_a = np.where(
    labels == "spam",
    np.random.choice(["link", "no_link"], n, p=[0.85, 0.15]),
    np.random.choice(["link", "no_link"], n, p=[0.15, 0.85])
)

# Feature B: weakly correlated
feature_b = np.random.choice(["short", "long"], n, p=[0.5, 0.5])

# Feature C: moderately correlated
feature_c = np.where(
    labels == "spam",
    np.random.choice(["urgent", "normal"], n, p=[0.65, 0.35]),
    np.random.choice(["urgent", "normal"], n, p=[0.35, 0.65])
)

print(f"H(Y)                   = {entropy(labels):.4f} bits")
print(f"I(Y; feature_a 'link') = {information_gain(feature_a, labels):.4f} bits")
print(f"I(Y; feature_b 'len')  = {information_gain(feature_b, labels):.4f} bits")
print(f"I(Y; feature_c 'urg')  = {information_gain(feature_c, labels):.4f} bits")
print()
print("Decision tree picks: feature_a (highest mutual information)")
# The tree extracts the most bits about the label at each split

Pulling It All Together

Information theory gives ML practitioners something rare: certainty about uncertainty. Fano's inequality tells you when classification is fundamentally hard. Le Cam and Assouad tell you when estimation is fundamentally hard. The data processing inequality tells you information can only be lost, never gained, through processing. Rate-distortion theory tells you the price of compression. And mutual information generalization bounds tell you why regularization works.

These aren't abstract theorems for journal papers. They're engineering tools. When your model plateaus, ask: is this a modeling problem or a data problem? Estimate mutual information between features and labels. If it's low, no architecture change will help — you need richer inputs. If it's high but your model underperforms, the gap is computational, not informational, and better optimization or architecture might close it.

The next time someone claims their new method "breaks" some limit, check it against these bounds. Either they're exploiting structure that changes the problem (which is legitimate and valuable), or they're making a mistake somewhere. Information theory is the referee that never gets tired.

Practical Checklist

Before blaming your model: (1) Estimate I(X; Y) between your features and target — if it's low, get better features. (2) Check if your sample size exceeds the information-theoretic lower bound for your problem's complexity. (3) Compare your error to Fano's bound for your number of classes. (4) If you're compressing representations, check your bottleneck against the rate-distortion function. If you're hitting fundamental limits, no amount of hyperparameter tuning will help. Redirect your effort to data collection or problem reformulation.
