Matrix Factorization

Chapter 17: Learning Theory & Advanced ML 4 subtopics
TL;DR

Matrix factorization decomposes a big matrix into a product of smaller ones, revealing hidden structure. SVD gives you the optimal low-rank approximation — perfect for dimensionality reduction and noise removal. NMF adds non-negativity constraints and discovers parts-based representations (like topics in documents or components in images). Matrix completion fills in missing entries using the low-rank assumption — this is how Netflix recommends movies. These techniques are conceptually clean, computationally tractable, and show up everywhere from recommendation engines to text analysis.

Low-Rank Approximation

Why would you want to factor a matrix? Think about a user-movie ratings matrix. Netflix has millions of users and tens of thousands of movies, but the matrix isn't truly millions-by-thousands of independent numbers. Users fall into taste clusters. Movies fall into genre groups. The real "information content" of that matrix might be captured by 50–100 latent factors. A comedy-lover's ratings are predictable once you know they love comedies. That predictability means the matrix is approximately low-rank — it can be well-approximated by a product of two much smaller matrices.

SVD (Singular Value Decomposition)

The Singular Value Decomposition factors any m × n matrix X into three pieces:

X = UΣVᵀ

U is m × m (orthogonal), Σ is m × n (diagonal, non-negative, sorted descending), and V is n × n (orthogonal). The diagonal entries σ₁ ≥ σ₂ ≥ ... ≥ 0 are the singular values. The columns of U are the left singular vectors, the columns of V are the right singular vectors.

Each singular value tells you how much "energy" the corresponding component carries. The first component captures the dominant pattern in the data, the second captures the next most important pattern orthogonal to the first, and so on. If the singular values drop off quickly, most of the information lives in the first few components.

import numpy as np

# Create a rank-3 matrix buried in noise
np.random.seed(42)
true_rank = 3
A = np.random.randn(100, true_rank) @ np.random.randn(true_rank, 80)
X = A + 0.1 * np.random.randn(100, 80)  # add noise

U, sigma, Vt = np.linalg.svd(X, full_matrices=False)
print(f"Top 6 singular values: {sigma[:6].round(2)}")
# The first 3 will be large; the rest will be ~noise level

Truncated SVD

Truncated SVD keeps only the top k singular values and their corresponding vectors:

X̃ₖ = UₖΣₖVₖᵀ

This gives you the best rank-k approximation of X. "Best" here means it minimizes the Frobenius norm error ‖X - X̃ₖ‖_F over all rank-k matrices. It also minimizes the spectral norm error. That optimality is guaranteed by the Eckart-Young-Mirsky theorem.

Eckart-Young-Mirsky Theorem

The Eckart-Young-Mirsky theorem states: among all matrices of rank at most k, the truncated SVD X̃ₖ is the closest to X in both the Frobenius norm and the spectral (operator) norm. The approximation error is:

‖X - X̃ₖ‖_F = √(σ²ₖ₊₁ + σ²ₖ₊₂ + ... + σ²ᵣ)
‖X - X̃ₖ‖₂ = σₖ₊₁

where r is the rank of X. This theorem is the theoretical backbone of PCA (which is truncated SVD on the centered data matrix), latent semantic analysis, and many dimensionality reduction techniques. It says: if you must throw away information, SVD tells you the optimal way to do it.

Nuclear Norm Minimization

Just as the L1 norm promotes sparsity in vectors, the nuclear norm (sum of singular values) promotes low rank in matrices. When you have a noisy or incomplete matrix and want to find the simplest (lowest-rank) matrix that fits the data, you minimize:

minimize ‖M‖* subject to constraints

where ‖M‖* = Σᵢ σᵢ(M). This is a convex relaxation of the NP-hard rank minimization problem, exactly analogous to how L1 is a relaxation of L0. Nuclear norm minimization is the mathematical foundation of matrix completion, which we'll cover shortly.

Non-Negative Matrix Factorization (NMF)

SVD is mathematically optimal, but it has a problem for interpretability: the factors can be negative. A singular vector might have both positive and negative entries, and you combine them with both positive and negative coefficients. That's fine for reconstruction, but it makes the factors hard to interpret. What does a "negative amount" of a topic mean?

Non-Negativity Constraints

Non-Negative Matrix Factorization adds a natural constraint: both factor matrices must be non-negative. Given a non-negative matrix V (m × n), find non-negative matrices W (m × k) and H (k × n) such that:

V ≈ WH,   W ≥ 0,   H ≥ 0

Because everything is non-negative, the original data can only be built by adding the components — never subtracting. This forces a parts-based representation. When you apply NMF to face images, W contains parts of faces (eyes, noses, mouths), and H tells you how much of each part appears in each face. SVD would give you "eigenfaces" — ghostly whole-face patterns you add and subtract. NMF gives you parts. That's much more intuitive.

Parts-Based Representation

The parts-based property emerges naturally from the non-negativity constraint. Consider a document-term matrix: rows are documents, columns are words, entries are word counts (non-negative). NMF decomposes this into topics (columns of W are topic vectors over words) and topic mixtures (rows of H tell which topics appear in each document). Each topic has non-negative word weights — words either belong to a topic or they don't. Each document is a non-negative mixture of topics — documents can contain topics but can't "anti-contain" them.

This is topic modeling, similar to LDA (Latent Dirichlet Allocation), but arrived at through matrix factorization rather than probabilistic modeling. In practice, NMF topics are often just as interpretable as LDA topics and much faster to compute.

Multiplicative Update Rules

NMF can't be solved in closed form (the non-negativity constraint makes it non-convex). Lee and Seung (2001) proposed elegantly simple multiplicative update rules that guarantee non-negativity is maintained at every step:

import numpy as np

def nmf(V, k, n_iter=200):
    """Non-negative matrix factorization with multiplicative updates."""
    m, n = V.shape
    W = np.random.rand(m, k) + 0.1
    H = np.random.rand(k, n) + 0.1
    eps = 1e-10  # avoid division by zero
    
    for i in range(n_iter):
        # Update H
        H *= (W.T @ V) / (W.T @ W @ H + eps)
        # Update W
        W *= (V @ H.T) / (W @ H @ H.T + eps)
        
        if i % 50 == 0:
            err = np.linalg.norm(V - W @ H, 'fro')
            print(f"Iter {i}: reconstruction error = {err:.4f}")
    
    return W, H

# Demo: discover 3 topics in a 100-doc, 500-word vocabulary
V = np.random.rand(100, 500)  # pretend document-term matrix
W, H = nmf(V, k=3)

The update rule is: multiply each element by the ratio of a "good" gradient term (numerator) to a "bad" one (denominator). If the current value is too small, the ratio exceeds 1 and the value grows. If too large, the ratio is less than 1 and it shrinks. Since everything starts positive and we only multiply by positive ratios, the non-negativity constraint is automatically maintained. No projections or clipping needed.

Divergence Objectives

The multiplicative updates above minimize the Frobenius norm (squared error): ‖V - WH‖²_F. But squared error isn't always the right choice. For count data (like word frequencies), the KL divergence (generalized) is often better:

D_KL(V ‖ WH) = Σᵢⱼ [Vᵢⱼ log(Vᵢⱼ / (WH)ᵢⱼ) - Vᵢⱼ + (WH)ᵢⱼ]

KL-divergence NMF gives different update rules but the same multiplicative structure. It connects NMF to probabilistic latent semantic analysis (pLSA) — minimizing KL divergence in NMF is equivalent to maximum likelihood in pLSA. There's also the Itakura-Saito divergence, popular in audio source separation, which treats large and small values more equally than Frobenius or KL.

Matrix Completion

Now we get to the problem that won the Netflix Prize (or at least, was a big piece of it). You have a user-movie ratings matrix, but it's 99% empty — each user rates only a tiny fraction of all movies. Can you fill in the missing entries?

The Missing Data Problem

This isn't interpolation — you can't smooth between nearby entries because there's no natural notion of "nearby" in a user-movie matrix. And you certainly can't fill in the blanks with averages. The whole point is that different users have different tastes. The question is: what structure can you exploit?

The Low-Rank Assumption

The key insight: the complete ratings matrix is approximately low-rank. User preferences are driven by a small number of latent factors — action affinity, romance affinity, art-house tolerance, director loyalty, etc. If you knew these factors, you could predict any user's rating for any movie. Formally, you assume the true matrix M has rank r << min(m, n), and you've observed a subset of its entries. Can you recover M?

Candès and Recht (2009) proved: yes, with high probability, if you observe enough random entries and the matrix satisfies certain incoherence conditions (meaning the singular vectors aren't too "spiky" — concentrated on a few rows or columns). The number of observations needed scales as O(n r polylog(n)) for an n × n matrix of rank r.

Nuclear Norm Regularization

The convex approach to matrix completion minimizes the nuclear norm subject to matching the observed entries:

minimize ‖M‖*  subject to  Mᵢⱼ = Xᵢⱼ  for all observed (i,j)

This is a semidefinite program and can be solved exactly, but it's expensive for large matrices. In practice, people use approximate methods.

Alternating Least Squares (ALS)

Alternating Least Squares is the workhorse for large-scale matrix completion. Factor the matrix as M ≈ UVᵀ where U is m × k and V is n × k. The loss is:

minimize  Σ_{(i,j) observed} (Xᵢⱼ - uᵢᵀvⱼ)² + λ(‖U‖² + ‖V‖²)

This is non-convex jointly in U and V, but if you fix V and optimize over U, it's a simple ridge regression — one per row of U. Then fix U and optimize V — again ridge regression. Alternate until convergence.

import numpy as np

def als_matrix_completion(ratings, k=10, lam=0.1, n_iter=20):
    """
    ALS for matrix completion.
    ratings: list of (user, item, rating) tuples
    """
    users = max(u for u, _, _ in ratings) + 1
    items = max(i for _, i, _ in ratings) + 1
    
    U = np.random.randn(users, k) * 0.1
    V = np.random.randn(items, k) * 0.1
    
    # Group ratings by user and by item
    by_user = {}
    by_item = {}
    for u, i, r in ratings:
        by_user.setdefault(u, []).append((i, r))
        by_item.setdefault(i, []).append((u, r))
    
    for iteration in range(n_iter):
        # Fix V, update each row of U
        for u, items_rated in by_user.items():
            idx = [i for i, _ in items_rated]
            r = np.array([r for _, r in items_rated])
            V_sub = V[idx]  # (n_rated, k)
            U[u] = np.linalg.solve(
                V_sub.T @ V_sub + lam * np.eye(k),
                V_sub.T @ r
            )
        
        # Fix U, update each row of V
        for i, users_rated in by_item.items():
            idx = [u for u, _ in users_rated]
            r = np.array([r for _, r in users_rated])
            U_sub = U[idx]
            V[i] = np.linalg.solve(
                U_sub.T @ U_sub + lam * np.eye(k),
                U_sub.T @ r
            )
        
        # Compute training RMSE
        sse = sum((r - U[u] @ V[i])**2 for u, i, r in ratings)
        rmse = np.sqrt(sse / len(ratings))
        if iteration % 5 == 0:
            print(f"Iter {iteration}: RMSE = {rmse:.4f}")
    
    return U, V

ALS is embarrassingly parallel — each user's update is independent of other users, so you can distribute across machines. That's why it's the algorithm of choice at Netflix-scale. Spark's MLlib, for instance, uses ALS as its default recommendation algorithm.

SGD for Matrix Completion

An alternative to ALS is stochastic gradient descent. Pick a random observed entry (i, j, rᵢⱼ), compute the prediction error eᵢⱼ = rᵢⱼ - uᵢᵀvⱼ, and update only the relevant rows of U and V:

uᵢ ← uᵢ + η(eᵢⱼ vⱼ - λ uᵢ)
vⱼ ← vⱼ + η(eᵢⱼ uᵢ - λ vⱼ)

SGD is simpler to implement and can be faster for very sparse matrices (where most users have few ratings). It's what Simon Funk used in his famous Netflix Prize solution. ALS is better when you want to leverage parallelism across a cluster.

Incoherence Conditions

Not every low-rank matrix can be completed from partial observations. If a rank-1 matrix has all its energy in a single row, you must observe that row — missing it means losing everything. The incoherence conditions formalize this: they require that the singular vectors of M are "spread out" across all rows and columns, not concentrated on a few. Formally, the projections of the standard basis vectors onto the column and row spaces of M must be bounded. Random low-rank matrices are incoherent with high probability. Pathological matrices (like the single-row example) are coherent and can't be completed.

Applications

Collaborative Filtering

This is the canonical application. Users and items form a sparse ratings matrix. Matrix factorization discovers latent factors — maybe one factor captures "cerebral sci-fi vs mindless action," another captures "long vs short," another "old vs new." Each user is a vector of preferences over these factors. Each movie is a vector of attributes. The predicted rating is the dot product. This approach (with refinements like biases, temporal dynamics, and implicit feedback) powered Netflix's recommendation engine and remains the backbone of many recommendation systems.

Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA)

Latent Semantic Analysis applies truncated SVD to a term-document matrix (or its TF-IDF weighted version). The truncation maps documents and words into a shared low-dimensional space where semantically similar items end up close together. "Car" and "automobile" might be far apart in raw word space (different words!) but close in the SVD-reduced space because they co-occur with similar words. LSA was one of the earliest successes in computational semantics and paved the way for word embeddings like Word2Vec.

Image Compression

Apply truncated SVD to a grayscale image (a matrix). Keeping the top k singular values gives you an image that requires storing only k(m + n + 1) numbers instead of mn. For a 1000 × 1000 image with k = 50, that's 100,050 numbers instead of 1,000,000 — a 10x compression. The image looks slightly blurry but recognizable. This isn't competitive with JPEG for practical compression, but it beautifully illustrates what low-rank approximation does.

Topic Discovery

Both NMF and SVD can discover latent topics in text corpora. NMF has the interpretability advantage (non-negative topics), while SVD has the mathematical optimality advantage. In practice, people try both and pick whichever gives more interpretable results for their specific corpus. The number of topics k is a hyperparameter — set it too low and you get overly broad topics ("science"), too high and you get redundant or noisy topics.

Matrix Factorization ≠ Deep Learning, But...

Matrix factorization might seem old-fashioned compared to neural collaborative filtering or transformer-based recommenders. But it remains a remarkably strong baseline. In the 2020 RecSys conference, a well-tuned SVD++ model beat several neural approaches on standard benchmarks. When you have sparse, structured data and need interpretability, matrix factorization is often the right starting point — and sometimes the right ending point too.
