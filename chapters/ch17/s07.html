Nice to Know

Chapter 17: Learning Theory & Advanced ML 7 topics

These are terms and techniques you'll encounter in papers, job postings, and conference talks. You don't need to master any of them right now — but when they come up, you'll know what people are talking about and where to dig deeper.

The Topics

Meta-Learning

"Learning to learn." The idea is to train a model that can adapt to brand-new tasks with just a handful of examples. MAML (Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning) is the landmark algorithm — it finds weight initializations that are a few gradient steps away from good performance on any new task. If you need a model that generalizes from minimal data, see Chapter 16's meta-learning section for the full treatment.

Convergence Rates

How fast does your optimizer actually get to a good solution? Vanilla SGD converges at O(1/√T) for convex problems, and adding momentum improves that to O(1/T). Adam converges faster in practice, but its theoretical guarantees are weaker. The practitioner's take: your learning rate schedule matters far more than any convergence proof. Tune that first, worry about theory later.

Information-Theoretic Limits

There are fundamental bounds — rooted in mutual information and rate-distortion theory — on what any learning algorithm can extract from data. These results tell you when a problem is provably hard, not just hard for your particular model. Mostly relevant if you're doing PhD-level research or want to understand why certain problems have irreducible error floors.

Optimal Transport

The Wasserstein distance (a.k.a. Earth Mover's distance) measures how much "work" it takes to reshape one probability distribution into another. Unlike KL divergence, it's well-defined even when distributions don't overlap — which is why WGANs use it instead of the standard GAN loss. It also shows up in domain adaptation and any setting where you're comparing distributions. The Sinkhorn algorithm adds an entropy term that makes computation practical at scale.

Online Convex Optimization

The theoretical backbone of online learning. At each round, you pick a point, nature reveals a loss function, and you try to minimize total regret over time. Follow-the-Regularized-Leader (FTRL) is the key algorithm — it generalizes online gradient descent with a regularization term that controls stability. This framework powers real-time ad bidding systems at Google and Meta, where decisions must be made before outcomes are known.

Inverse Reinforcement Learning

Standard RL gives you a reward function and asks for a policy. Inverse RL flips it: given expert demonstrations (a human driving, a robot stacking blocks), learn what reward function the expert must be optimizing. Once you have the reward, you can train a policy with regular RL. Used heavily in robotics and autonomous driving, where hand-crafting reward functions is surprisingly hard.

Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning

What happens when multiple agents learn simultaneously in a shared environment? Game theory meets RL. Each agent's optimal policy depends on what the other agents are doing, so the environment is non-stationary from every agent's perspective. DeepMind's AlphaStar (StarCraft II) and OpenAI Five (Dota 2) are the famous examples. Also used in multi-robot coordination, traffic optimization, and auction design.

The Point of This Section

None of these topics require deep study right now. When you encounter them in a paper abstract, a system design doc, or an interview question, you'll have enough context to follow the conversation — and know exactly where to look when you need more.

What You Should Now Be Able To Do

  • Recognize meta-learning, MAML, and few-shot learning as "learning to learn" — and know it's covered in depth in Chapter 16
  • Understand that convergence rates describe optimizer speed, but learning rate tuning matters more in practice
  • Know that optimal transport (Wasserstein distance) measures distribution similarity and powers WGANs
  • Identify online convex optimization and FTRL as the theory behind real-time decision systems
  • Distinguish inverse RL (learn the reward from demos) from standard RL (learn the policy from a reward)
  • Recognize multi-agent RL as the intersection of game theory and reinforcement learning
