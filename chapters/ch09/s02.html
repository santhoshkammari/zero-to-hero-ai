Architecture Evolution

Chapter 9: CNNs & Computer Vision LeNet → AlexNet → VGG → ResNet → EfficientNet → ViT
TL;DR

Every major vision architecture solved a specific bottleneck its predecessors hit. LeNet proved CNNs work. AlexNet proved they scale with GPUs. VGG proved deeper is better. ResNet proved you can go arbitrarily deep with skip connections. EfficientNet proved you should scale all dimensions together. ViT proved you don't even need convolutions. Understanding why each jump happened matters far more than memorizing layer counts.

The story of vision architectures is the story of one question: "How do we go deeper without everything breaking?"

Every breakthrough on this timeline solved a specific limitation that the previous generation hit. LeNet ran out of compute. AlexNet ran out of depth. VGG ran out of clever ideas for going deeper. GoogLeNet complicated things. ResNet cracked the depth problem wide open. And then the Transformer people walked in and said, "What if we just don't use convolutions at all?"

Understanding this progression isn't trivia — it tells you why modern architectures look the way they do. When you see a skip connection in a diffusion model, or patch embeddings in a multimodal system, you'll know exactly where those ideas came from and why they work.

LeNet-5 (1998) — The Pioneer

Yann LeCun's LeNet-5 is where it all started. The architecture is almost comically simple by today's standards: two convolutional layers, two pooling layers (average pooling, not max), and three fully connected layers. It was designed for 32×32 grayscale images — handwritten digits from the MNIST dataset.

The pattern it established is the one every CNN still follows: Conv → Pool → Conv → Pool → Flatten → FC → Output. Convolutions extract spatial features at increasing levels of abstraction, pooling reduces spatial dimensions, and fully connected layers do the final classification.

LeNet had about 60,000 parameters. It could recognize handwritten checks at the post office. That was the benchmark for "useful AI" in 1998. But it couldn't scale. The hardware wasn't there, the datasets weren't there, and the training tricks (ReLU, dropout, batch norm) hadn't been invented yet. For fourteen years, CNNs largely gathered dust.

Why LeNet Matters

If you understand LeNet, you understand the template everything else builds on. Every subsequent architecture is essentially an answer to: "What if we made this deeper, wider, or fundamentally different?" The Conv-Pool-FC skeleton survived until the Transformer era.

AlexNet (2012) — The Big Bang

In 2012, Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, and Geoffrey Hinton entered a convolutional neural network into the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC). ImageNet had 1.2 million training images across 1,000 categories — dogs, cars, mushrooms, saxophones. The best systems at the time used hand-engineered features like SIFT and HOG, and the top-1 error rate was stuck around 26%.

AlexNet smashed it down to 16.4%. A ten-percentage-point improvement. In a competition where people fought over fractions of a percent. The ML world changed overnight.

What Made AlexNet Work

ReLU activation. This was arguably the most important ingredient. Previous networks used sigmoid or tanh activations, which saturate for large inputs and cause vanishing gradients. ReLU — max(0, x) — doesn't saturate for positive values. Training was about 6× faster compared to tanh. Without ReLU, you simply couldn't train networks this deep without the gradients dying.

GPU training. AlexNet was trained on two GTX 580 GPUs with 3GB of memory each. The network was literally split across both GPUs, with cross-GPU communication at specific layers. This was the first major demonstration that GPUs weren't just for gaming — they were the engine that deep learning needed.

Dropout. AlexNet used dropout with p=0.5 in the fully connected layers. At the time, this was a new technique, and it was critical for preventing overfitting on ImageNet. Without it, a network with 60 million parameters would memorize the training set.

Data augmentation. Random crops, horizontal flips, and PCA-based color jittering. These simple tricks artificially expanded the effective training set and improved generalization significantly.

The architecture itself — five conv layers, three FC layers, about 60M parameters — wasn't elegant. It used large 11×11 and 5×5 kernels. The split across two GPUs was an engineering hack, not a design insight. But none of that mattered. AlexNet proved that deep learning works on real-world vision, and the field pivoted overnight.

The Real Legacy

AlexNet's lasting contribution wasn't any specific architectural trick — it was proof of concept. It demonstrated that CNNs + GPUs + large datasets = superhuman-trajectory results. The subsequent gold rush in deep learning research traces directly back to this paper.

VGGNet (2014) — The Power of Simplicity

The Oxford Visual Geometry Group asked a simple question: what if we just stacked a bunch of 3×3 convolutions? No fancy tricks, no multi-branch architectures. Just 3×3 convolutions, 2×2 max pooling, and ReLU. Repeat.

The key insight was elegant: two 3×3 conv layers have the same effective receptive field as one 5×5 layer, but with fewer parameters and more non-linearity. Two stacked 3×3 layers use 2 × (3×3) = 18 parameters per channel pair, while a single 5×5 uses 25. You save parameters and get an extra ReLU in between, making the function more expressive. Three 3×3 layers match a 7×7 receptive field with even bigger savings.

VGG-16 (16 weight layers) and VGG-19 (19 layers) achieved excellent results on ImageNet. The architecture was beautifully simple — you could describe the entire thing in a few lines. But VGG was massive: 138 million parameters, most of them in the fully connected layers at the end. It was slow to train and memory-hungry. The lesson was clear: deeper is better, but you need smarter ways to go deep.

GoogLeNet / Inception (2014) — Let the Network Choose

While VGG went deep and simple, Google went wide and clever. The Inception module's philosophy: don't choose between 1×1, 3×3, and 5×5 convolutions — use all of them in parallel and let the network decide what works.

Each Inception module runs multiple convolution branches simultaneously: a 1×1 conv, a 3×3 conv, a 5×5 conv, and a 3×3 max pooling. The outputs are concatenated along the channel dimension. This lets the network capture features at multiple scales within the same layer.

The critical trick that made this computationally feasible was the 1×1 convolution as a bottleneck. Before the expensive 3×3 and 5×5 convolutions, a 1×1 conv reduces the channel dimension — say from 256 channels to 64. The subsequent 3×3 conv operates on 64 channels instead of 256, cutting computation by 4×. This bottleneck pattern shows up everywhere in modern architectures.

GoogLeNet (the Inception v1 network) had only 6.8 million parameters — 20× fewer than VGG — and won the 2014 ImageNet challenge. It also used auxiliary classifiers at intermediate layers to combat vanishing gradients during training, though later work showed these weren't strictly necessary.

ResNet (2015) — The Most Important Architecture Innovation

If you remember one thing from this entire section, make it ResNet. The ideas in this paper appear in virtually every modern architecture — in vision, NLP, speech, and beyond. Skip connections are arguably the single most impactful idea in deep learning architecture design.

The Degradation Problem

By 2015, people knew that deeper networks were more powerful in theory. But in practice, something strange happened when you tried to go beyond ~20 layers: the network got worse.

And this wasn't overfitting. When a network overfits, training accuracy is high but test accuracy is low. The degradation problem was different — training accuracy itself decreased with more layers. A 56-layer network had higher training error than a 20-layer network. More capacity was actively hurting learning.

Think about why this is weird. A 56-layer network should be at least as good as a 20-layer network. In the worst case, the extra 36 layers could just learn the identity function — pass the input straight through unchanged. The 56-layer network contains the 20-layer network as a subset. So why was it performing worse?

Because learning the identity function is hard. When you initialize a stack of conv layers with random weights and train them with SGD, pushing them toward the identity mapping requires very precise weight configurations. The optimization landscape makes it easier to learn something than to learn nothing. Paradoxically, the deeper network couldn't figure out that "do nothing" was the right answer for its extra layers.

The Residual Learning Framework

Kaiming He's insight was brilliantly simple. Instead of asking a block to learn the desired mapping H(x) directly, ask it to learn the residual F(x) = H(x) − x. Then the output is:

output = F(x) + x    # The skip connection

If the optimal transformation is close to the identity (which it often is in deep networks), then F(x) just needs to be close to zero. And pushing weights toward zero is easy — weight decay does it naturally, and small random initializations start near zero already. The skip connection provides a "highway" for the gradient to flow straight through without degradation.

This reframing changes the optimization problem fundamentally. Instead of learning transformations from scratch, each block learns modifications to an already-reasonable signal. The input passes through unchanged, and the block adds (or subtracts) small refinements. If a block has nothing useful to contribute, its weights go to zero, and the input passes through unmodified. No harm done.

Don't Confuse Residual Learning with "Just Adding Connections"

The skip connection isn't just an engineering hack for gradient flow (though it helps with that too). The conceptual shift matters: learning residuals (how to modify the input) is fundamentally easier than learning full mappings (how to transform the input from scratch). This is why ResNet works — it changes what the network is asked to learn, not just how gradients flow.

Building Blocks: Basic and Bottleneck

ResNet uses two types of residual blocks:

Basic Block (used in ResNet-18, ResNet-34): Two 3×3 conv layers with batch normalization and ReLU.

Bottleneck Block (used in ResNet-50, ResNet-101, ResNet-152): A 1×1 conv to reduce channels, a 3×3 conv at the reduced dimension, and a 1×1 conv to expand channels back. The 1×1 → 3×3 → 1×1 pattern reduces computation dramatically. For example, instead of a 3×3 conv on 256 channels (256 × 256 × 3 × 3 ≈ 590K params), you do 1×1 to 64 channels, 3×3 on 64 channels, and 1×1 back to 256 (total ≈ 70K params). That's an 8× reduction.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class BasicBlock(nn.Module):
    """ResNet Basic Block — used in ResNet-18/34."""
    expansion = 1

    def __init__(self, in_channels, out_channels, stride=1, downsample=None):
        super().__init__()
        self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(in_channels, out_channels, 3,
                               stride=stride, padding=1, bias=False)
        self.bn1 = nn.BatchNorm2d(out_channels)
        self.relu = nn.ReLU(inplace=True)
        self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(out_channels, out_channels, 3,
                               stride=1, padding=1, bias=False)
        self.bn2 = nn.BatchNorm2d(out_channels)
        self.downsample = downsample  # adjusts x when dimensions change

    def forward(self, x):
        identity = x

        out = self.relu(self.bn1(self.conv1(x)))
        out = self.bn2(self.conv2(out))

        if self.downsample is not None:
            identity = self.downsample(x)

        out += identity  # THE skip connection
        out = self.relu(out)
        return out


class Bottleneck(nn.Module):
    """ResNet Bottleneck Block — used in ResNet-50/101/152."""
    expansion = 4

    def __init__(self, in_channels, mid_channels, stride=1, downsample=None):
        super().__init__()
        out_channels = mid_channels * self.expansion
        # 1×1 reduce
        self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(in_channels, mid_channels, 1, bias=False)
        self.bn1 = nn.BatchNorm2d(mid_channels)
        # 3×3 spatial convolution
        self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(mid_channels, mid_channels, 3,
                               stride=stride, padding=1, bias=False)
        self.bn2 = nn.BatchNorm2d(mid_channels)
        # 1×1 expand
        self.conv3 = nn.Conv2d(mid_channels, out_channels, 1, bias=False)
        self.bn3 = nn.BatchNorm2d(out_channels)
        self.relu = nn.ReLU(inplace=True)
        self.downsample = downsample

    def forward(self, x):
        identity = x

        out = self.relu(self.bn1(self.conv1(x)))   # reduce
        out = self.relu(self.bn2(self.conv2(out)))  # 3×3
        out = self.bn3(self.conv3(out))             # expand (no ReLU yet)

        if self.downsample is not None:
            identity = self.downsample(x)

        out += identity  # residual addition
        out = self.relu(out)
        return out

When Dimensions Don't Match

The skip connection adds F(x) + x, which requires both tensors to have the same shape. But when you change spatial resolution (stride > 1) or channel count, the identity shortcut needs adjustment. ResNet handles this with a projection shortcut: a 1×1 convolution with the appropriate stride and output channels. This is the downsample parameter in the code above.

# Projection shortcut for dimension changes
downsample = nn.Sequential(
    nn.Conv2d(64, 128, kernel_size=1, stride=2, bias=False),
    nn.BatchNorm2d(128)
)
# Now a 64-channel, 56×56 input gets mapped to 128-channel, 28×28
# to match the block's output dimensions

ResNet Variants and Pre-Activation

The original ResNet (v1) uses the ordering: Conv → BN → ReLU, with the skip connection added before the final ReLU. He et al. later proposed Pre-Activation ResNet (v2), which reorders to: BN → ReLU → Conv. This means the skip connection is a true identity mapping — no activation sits on the shortcut path. The result: even better gradient flow and improved accuracy on deeper networks (1001 layers!).

class PreActBlock(nn.Module):
    """Pre-activation residual block (BN-ReLU-Conv ordering)."""
    def __init__(self, in_channels, out_channels, stride=1, downsample=None):
        super().__init__()
        self.bn1 = nn.BatchNorm2d(in_channels)
        self.relu = nn.ReLU(inplace=True)
        self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(in_channels, out_channels, 3,
                               stride=stride, padding=1, bias=False)
        self.bn2 = nn.BatchNorm2d(out_channels)
        self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(out_channels, out_channels, 3,
                               padding=1, bias=False)
        self.downsample = downsample

    def forward(self, x):
        identity = x
        out = self.relu(self.bn1(x))       # activation BEFORE conv
        if self.downsample is not None:
            identity = self.downsample(out)
        out = self.conv1(out)
        out = self.conv2(self.relu(self.bn2(out)))
        out += identity
        return out
ResNet's Reach

Skip connections didn't stay in CNNs. They became the backbone of U-Net (segmentation), DenseNet (feature reuse), Transformers (every sub-layer has a residual), diffusion models (noise prediction networks), and essentially every deep architecture designed after 2015. When someone says "residual connection" in any context, they're using He et al.'s idea.

DenseNet (2017) — Every Layer Connects to Every Other

DenseNet took the skip connection idea to its logical extreme: every layer receives input from all preceding layers and passes its features to all subsequent layers. Instead of adding features (as ResNet does), DenseNet concatenates them along the channel dimension.

If a DenseNet block has L layers, each producing k feature maps (k is called the "growth rate"), then layer l receives k₀ + k×(l−1) input channels, where k₀ is the initial channel count. With k=32 and 6 layers, the last layer in a block sees k₀ + 160 channels.

The advantages: extreme feature reuse (every feature is available to every subsequent layer), strong gradient flow (each layer has a direct connection to the loss), and surprising parameter efficiency. DenseNet-121 achieves comparable accuracy to ResNet with far fewer parameters. The disadvantage: memory. All those concatenated feature maps live in memory simultaneously, making DenseNet memory-hungry during training. Various memory-efficient implementations exist, but this remains a practical limitation.

MobileNet (2017) — Deep Learning in Your Pocket

All the architectures so far were designed for powerful GPUs. MobileNet asked: what if the model needs to run on a phone?

The key innovation is the depthwise separable convolution. A standard 3×3 conv with 256 input and 256 output channels does 256 × 256 × 3 × 3 ≈ 590K multiplications per spatial position. MobileNet splits this into two steps: a depthwise convolution (one 3×3 filter per input channel independently — 256 × 3 × 3 = 2.3K operations) followed by a pointwise convolution (a 1×1 conv that mixes channels — 256 × 256 × 1 × 1 = 65.5K operations). Total: about 68K vs. 590K. That's roughly an 8–9× reduction in computation for roughly the same representational power.

MobileNet-v1 stacks these depthwise separable convolutions to build a full network with about 4.2M parameters — 30× fewer than VGG — while achieving reasonable accuracy. MobileNet-v2 added inverted residual blocks (expand channels with 1×1, depthwise 3×3, then project back down with 1×1) and linear bottlenecks. These designs power real-time vision on every smartphone camera today.

EfficientNet (2019) — Scaling Done Right

Before EfficientNet, making a network better meant picking one dimension to scale: make it deeper (more layers), wider (more channels per layer), or use higher-resolution inputs. Practitioners would scale whichever dimension they felt like, often getting diminishing returns.

Mingxing Tan and Quoc Le asked: is there a principled way to scale all three dimensions simultaneously?

Compound Scaling

They discovered that scaling depth, width, and resolution together with a fixed ratio consistently outperforms scaling any single dimension. The compound scaling method uses a coefficient φ to uniformly scale all three:

# EfficientNet compound scaling
# depth:      d = α^φ
# width:      w = β^φ
# resolution: r = γ^φ
# subject to: α · β² · γ² ≈ 2  (roughly doubles FLOPS for φ += 1)

# Found via grid search on the baseline:
α = 1.2    # depth multiplier
β = 1.1    # width multiplier
γ = 1.15   # resolution multiplier

# EfficientNet-B0 (baseline): 224×224, 5.3M params
# EfficientNet-B7 (φ=6):     600×600, 66M params, 84.3% top-1

The intuition: if you make the network deeper, it can learn more complex features — but it needs wider layers to store those features, and higher-resolution inputs to provide the fine-grained detail that those features describe. Scale one without the others and you hit diminishing returns. Scale all three in harmony and each increment remains productive.

The Baseline Matters

EfficientNet didn't just scale well — it started from a strong baseline. EfficientNet-B0 was found via neural architecture search (NAS), using MobileNet-v2's inverted residual blocks as the building block, plus squeeze-and-excitation (SE) modules for channel attention. The resulting base network was already better than most hand-designed architectures at comparable FLOPS. Compound scaling then amplified that advantage.

EfficientNet-B7 achieved 84.3% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet — state-of-the-art at the time — with 8.4× fewer parameters than the best existing models. The message was clear: how you scale matters as much as what you scale.

When to Use EfficientNet

EfficientNet remains a strong default when you need a CNN backbone and have a fixed compute budget. Pick the EfficientNet variant (B0–B7) that fits your latency or memory constraint. For many practical tasks — especially with transfer learning — EfficientNet-B0 to B3 hit a sweet spot of accuracy vs. cost.

Vision Transformer — ViT (2020) — Throwing Out Convolutions Entirely

And then the Transformer people showed up.

The Vision Transformer paper (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) asked a radical question: what if we just use a standard Transformer — the same architecture powering BERT and GPT — directly on images? No convolutions at all?

The Core Idea: An Image Is a Sequence of Patches

That's it. That's the whole insight. Take a 224×224 image, split it into a grid of 16×16 patches (you get 14×14 = 196 patches), flatten each patch into a vector (16×16×3 = 768 dimensions for RGB), project it linearly to get a "patch embedding," add a learnable position embedding, and feed the resulting sequence into a standard Transformer encoder. Stick a classification token at the front, just like BERT's [CLS] token, and read the classification from there.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class PatchEmbedding(nn.Module):
    """Split image into patches and project to embedding dimension."""
    def __init__(self, img_size=224, patch_size=16, in_channels=3, embed_dim=768):
        super().__init__()
        self.num_patches = (img_size // patch_size) ** 2  # 196 for 224/16

        # Conv2d with kernel_size=stride=patch_size does the splitting + projection
        self.proj = nn.Conv2d(in_channels, embed_dim,
                              kernel_size=patch_size, stride=patch_size)

    def forward(self, x):
        # x: (B, 3, 224, 224) → (B, 768, 14, 14) → (B, 768, 196) → (B, 196, 768)
        return self.proj(x).flatten(2).transpose(1, 2)


class ViT(nn.Module):
    """Simplified Vision Transformer."""
    def __init__(self, img_size=224, patch_size=16, in_channels=3,
                 embed_dim=768, depth=12, num_heads=12, num_classes=1000):
        super().__init__()
        self.patch_embed = PatchEmbedding(img_size, patch_size,
                                          in_channels, embed_dim)
        num_patches = (img_size // patch_size) ** 2

        self.cls_token = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(1, 1, embed_dim))
        self.pos_embed = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(1, num_patches + 1, embed_dim))

        encoder_layer = nn.TransformerEncoderLayer(
            d_model=embed_dim, nhead=num_heads,
            dim_feedforward=embed_dim * 4, activation='gelu',
            batch_first=True, norm_first=True
        )
        self.encoder = nn.TransformerEncoder(encoder_layer, num_layers=depth)
        self.norm = nn.LayerNorm(embed_dim)
        self.head = nn.Linear(embed_dim, num_classes)

    def forward(self, x):
        B = x.shape[0]
        x = self.patch_embed(x)                          # (B, 196, 768)
        cls = self.cls_token.expand(B, -1, -1)           # (B, 1, 768)
        x = torch.cat([cls, x], dim=1)                   # (B, 197, 768)
        x = x + self.pos_embed                           # add position info
        x = self.norm(self.encoder(x))                   # transformer magic
        return self.head(x[:, 0])                        # classify from [CLS]

# ViT-Base/16: 86M params — comparable to ResNet-152
model = ViT(embed_dim=768, depth=12, num_heads=12)
print(f"Parameters: {sum(p.numel() for p in model.parameters()):,}")  # ~86M

Why Self-Attention Works for Vision

In a CNN, the receptive field starts small and grows as you stack layers. A neuron in layer 1 sees a 3×3 patch. A neuron in layer 5 might see a 35×35 region. You need many layers before any neuron can "see" the full image. This is by design — local processing first, global processing later.

Self-attention flips this. Every patch attends to every other patch from layer 1. A patch in the top-left corner can immediately relate to a patch in the bottom-right corner. The receptive field is global from the very first layer. This is incredibly powerful for tasks where long-range dependencies matter — recognizing that a steering wheel and tires belong to the same car, even though they're spatially distant in the image.

The cost? Self-attention is O(n²) in sequence length. For 196 patches, that's fine. For dense prediction tasks with thousands of patches, it gets expensive. This limitation spawned an entire sub-field of efficient attention mechanisms and hierarchical vision transformers.

The Data Hunger Problem

Here's the catch: ViT doesn't work well when trained on ImageNet alone (1.2M images). The original paper showed that ViT trained on ImageNet from scratch performed worse than ResNet. CNNs have strong inductive biases — local connectivity, translation equivariance — that act as built-in priors about how images work. These priors mean CNNs can learn from less data. Transformers have almost no built-in priors about spatial structure, so they need vastly more data to learn what CNNs get for free.

ViT only surpassed CNNs when pre-trained on JFT-300M (300 million images, Google's internal dataset) or ImageNet-21k (14 million images). With enough data, the lack of inductive bias becomes an advantage — the model isn't constrained by assumptions about locality and can learn whatever patterns actually exist in the data.

DeiT: Making ViT Data-Efficient

The data hunger problem seemed like a dealbreaker for most practitioners who don't have 300 million labeled images lying around. Then Facebook's DeiT (Data-efficient Image Transformers, 2021) showed that with the right training recipe — heavy augmentation (RandAugment, random erasing), regularization (stochastic depth, repeated augmentation), and a distillation token that learns from a CNN teacher — ViT could match or beat CNNs using only ImageNet. No JFT-300M required.

DeiT's insight: the problem wasn't the architecture, it was the training recipe. Transformers need different regularization than CNNs. Once you figure out the right bag of tricks, ViTs train efficiently on standard datasets.

Why ViT Changed Everything

The ultimate significance of ViT isn't just "Transformers work for vision." It's that the same architecture now works for vision AND language. This was the critical unlock for multimodal AI. CLIP, DALL-E, GPT-4V, LLaVA — all of these multimodal systems are possible because vision and language now share the same architectural backbone. A patch embedding goes in one end, a token embedding goes in the other, and the same Transformer processes both. That unification is perhaps the most consequential development in deep learning since ResNet.

ViT Model Naming

ViT-B/16 means ViT-Base with 16×16 patches. ViT-L/14 means ViT-Large with 14×14 patches. Smaller patches = more tokens = more compute but finer-grained features. The naming convention is: ViT-{Size}/{Patch Size} where Size ∈ {Ti, S, B, L, H} (Tiny, Small, Base, Large, Huge).

Swin Transformer (2021) — Hierarchical Vision Transformers

ViT processes all patches at the same resolution, which works for classification but is awkward for dense prediction tasks like object detection and segmentation that need multi-scale feature maps. Swin Transformer introduced shifted window attention: instead of global self-attention, each layer computes attention within local windows (e.g., 7×7 patches), and windows shift between layers to allow cross-window information flow.

This gives you the best of both worlds: CNN-like hierarchical feature maps (the spatial resolution is reduced in stages, just like a CNN) with Transformer-level modeling power. Swin creates feature maps at 4×, 8×, 16×, and 32× downsampling — exactly the multi-scale pyramid that detection heads like FPN expect. It quickly became the default backbone for vision tasks beyond classification, powering state-of-the-art results in object detection (COCO), segmentation (ADE20K), and more.

ConvNeXt (2022) — The CNN Strikes Back

ConvNeXt asked a provocative question: what if the Transformer's success wasn't about attention at all? What if it was about all the other modernizations — training recipes, larger kernels, layer norm, GELU activation, fewer activation functions, inverted bottleneck ratios?

Starting from a standard ResNet-50, the authors systematically applied Transformer-era design choices to the pure CNN: (1) adjusted stage ratios to match Swin (3:3:9:3 instead of 3:4:6:3), (2) replaced the ResNet stem with a 4×4 stride-4 "patchify" layer, (3) used depthwise convolutions, (4) inverted the bottleneck (wide depthwise → narrow pointwise), (5) increased kernel size to 7×7, (6) replaced ReLU with GELU, (7) replaced BatchNorm with LayerNorm, and (8) used fewer normalization and activation layers.

The result: a pure CNN that matches or beats Swin Transformer at every scale, from tiny to huge. ConvNeXt demonstrated that the ideas mattered more than whether you used attention or convolution. The field had attributed Transformer superiority to self-attention, but it turned out most of the gains came from better training practices and design modernizations that apply equally well to CNNs.

The Meta-Lesson

Don't get too attached to architecture tribes ("CNNs vs. Transformers"). The principles — residual connections, careful scaling, modern normalization, strong augmentation — transfer across architecture families. The best model for your task depends on your compute budget, data, and deployment constraints, not on ideology.
