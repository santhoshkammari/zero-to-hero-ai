Generative Vision Models

Chapter 9: CNNs & Computer Vision 4 subtopics
TL;DR

Generative vision models learn the distribution of images well enough to create new ones from scratch. GANs pit a generator against a discriminator in a minimax game — producing sharp images but suffering from training instability and mode collapse. VAEs encode images into a smooth latent distribution and decode back, giving principled uncertainty but blurrier outputs. Diffusion models — the current state of the art — gradually add noise to images and learn to reverse the process step by step, achieving the best quality-diversity tradeoff. NeRFs extend generation to 3D by learning volumetric scene representations from photos. Super-resolution networks upscale low-res images using perceptual losses that compare features, not pixels. Each family trades off quality, diversity, speed, and training stability differently.

So far in this chapter, we've used CNNs to understand images — classifying, detecting, segmenting. But what if a model could create images? Not retrieve them from a database, not stitch existing photos together, but synthesize entirely new, photorealistic images that never existed before. That's the promise of generative vision models, and it has gone from "interesting research curiosity" to "industry-transforming technology" in under a decade.

The applications are staggering. Data augmentation: need more training images of rare skin diseases? Generate them. Creative tools: artists use text-to-image models daily. Drug discovery: visualize candidate molecules and protein structures. Architecture: generate building designs from floor plans. Entertainment: games and films use neural rendering for photorealistic environments. The fundamental question behind all of this is deceptively simple: can a model learn the probability distribution of images well enough to sample new ones from it?

The answer is yes — but the "how" varies dramatically across model families. Let's walk through each one, from the adversarial cleverness of GANs to the elegant noise-removal of diffusion models.

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

The idea behind Generative Adversarial Networks is one of the most elegant in all of deep learning. Published by Ian Goodfellow in 2014, it frames image generation as a game between two neural networks. The Generator (G) tries to create fake images that look real. The Discriminator (D) tries to tell real images from fake ones. They train simultaneously, each getting better at its job, until the generator produces images so convincing that the discriminator can't distinguish them from real photographs.

Think of it as a counterfeiter versus a detective. The counterfeiter (generator) starts by producing terrible fakes — random noise. The detective (discriminator) easily spots them. But each round, the counterfeiter studies what the detective caught and improves. The detective, in turn, sharpens its eye. Over thousands of rounds, the counterfeiter becomes a master forger.

GAN Architecture
═══════════════

Random noise z ──→ ┌────────────┐      ┌───────────────┐
  (latent vector)  │  Generator │──→   │               │
                   │     (G)    │  Fake│ Discriminator  │──→ Real or Fake?
                   └────────────┘  img │     (D)        │      (0 to 1)
                                       │               │
          Real training images ──────→ │               │
                                       └───────────────┘

Training loop:
  1. D learns to output 1 for real, 0 for fake
  2. G learns to make D output 1 for its fakes
  3. Repeat until D can't tell the difference (outputs ≈ 0.5)

Mathematically, this is a minimax game. The discriminator maximizes the probability of correctly labeling real and fake images. The generator minimizes the same objective — it wants the discriminator to be wrong. The famous value function is: min_G max_D V(D, G) = E[log D(x)] + E[log(1 - D(G(z)))]. In practice, we alternate: train D for one step, then train G for one step.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

# Simplified GAN training loop
latent_dim = 100

generator = nn.Sequential(
    nn.Linear(latent_dim, 256),
    nn.ReLU(),
    nn.Linear(256, 512),
    nn.ReLU(),
    nn.Linear(512, 784),   # 28×28 image flattened
    nn.Tanh()               # output in [-1, 1]
)

discriminator = nn.Sequential(
    nn.Linear(784, 512),
    nn.LeakyReLU(0.2),
    nn.Linear(512, 256),
    nn.LeakyReLU(0.2),
    nn.Linear(256, 1),
    nn.Sigmoid()             # probability: real or fake
)

criterion = nn.BCELoss()
opt_G = torch.optim.Adam(generator.parameters(), lr=2e-4)
opt_D = torch.optim.Adam(discriminator.parameters(), lr=2e-4)

for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for real_images, _ in dataloader:
        batch_size = real_images.size(0)
        real_images = real_images.view(batch_size, -1)

        real_labels = torch.ones(batch_size, 1)
        fake_labels = torch.zeros(batch_size, 1)

        # === Train Discriminator ===
        z = torch.randn(batch_size, latent_dim)
        fake_images = generator(z).detach()

        loss_D = criterion(discriminator(real_images), real_labels) \
               + criterion(discriminator(fake_images), fake_labels)

        opt_D.zero_grad()
        loss_D.backward()
        opt_D.step()

        # === Train Generator ===
        z = torch.randn(batch_size, latent_dim)
        fake_images = generator(z)
        # G wants D to think fakes are real
        loss_G = criterion(discriminator(fake_images), real_labels)

        opt_G.zero_grad()
        loss_G.backward()
        opt_G.step()

    print(f"Epoch {epoch}: D_loss={loss_D.item():.4f}, G_loss={loss_G.item():.4f}")

The Evolution of GANs

DCGAN (Deep Convolutional GAN, 2016) replaced the fully-connected layers above with convolutional layers — transpose convolutions in the generator, strided convolutions in the discriminator. This was the first GAN architecture that reliably produced decent images. It established conventions still used today: batch normalization, LeakyReLU in the discriminator, no fully-connected hidden layers.

StyleGAN (2019, by NVIDIA) revolutionized face generation by introducing a style-based generator. Instead of feeding noise directly into the first layer, it maps it through a learned "mapping network" to a style vector, which then controls the generation at each resolution level independently. This lets you manipulate specific attributes — change the hairstyle without affecting the face shape, or age a person without altering their identity. The latent space became navigable: walk in one direction and faces smile, walk in another and they put on glasses.

Conditional GANs add a label to both the generator and discriminator, so you can say "generate a cat" or "generate a 7." Pix2Pix takes this further — given a paired dataset (e.g., sketches → photos), it learns to translate images from one domain to another. CycleGAN does the same thing without paired examples, using a clever cycle-consistency loss: translate a horse photo to zebra, then back to horse, and the result should match the original.

GAN Training Challenges

GANs are notoriously difficult to train. Two major problems plague them. Mode collapse happens when the generator finds a few images that fool the discriminator and just keeps producing those — it "collapses" to a small number of modes in the data distribution. Your GAN might generate the same face over and over, ignoring the vast diversity in the training set.

Training instability arises from the adversarial setup. If the discriminator gets too strong too fast, the generator receives useless gradients (everything looks equally fake). If the generator outpaces the discriminator, it exploits artifacts the discriminator hasn't learned to spot yet. Balancing the two is more art than science.

The Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) addressed instability by replacing the standard loss with the Earth Mover distance (Wasserstein distance), which provides smoother gradients even when the distributions don't overlap. Progressive growing (also from NVIDIA) trains the GAN starting at tiny 4×4 resolution and gradually adding layers, making high-resolution generation more stable. These innovations helped, but GANs never fully escaped their training fragility.

The GAN Training Balancing Act

If your discriminator loss drops to zero while the generator loss climbs, D is winning too hard — G gets no useful learning signal. If both losses oscillate wildly, training is unstable. The sweet spot is when both losses hover around similar values and the generated images steadily improve. In practice, many researchers monitor generated samples visually throughout training — metrics alone can be misleading.

Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) for Images

While GANs take the adversarial route, Variational Autoencoders approach generation through probability theory. A VAE has two parts: an encoder that compresses an image into a distribution in latent space (not a single point — a distribution), and a decoder that samples from that distribution and reconstructs an image.

VAE Architecture
════════════════

Input image x                          Reconstructed image x̂
     │                                        ▲
     ▼                                        │
┌──────────┐    ┌──────────────┐    ┌──────────────┐
│  Encoder │───→│ μ and σ      │───→│   Decoder    │
│  (CNN)   │    │ (mean, std   │  z │   (Deconv)   │
└──────────┘    │  of latent   │    └──────────────┘
                │  distribution)│
                └──────┬───────┘
                       │
                  z = μ + σ × ε     ← reparameterization trick
                  (ε ~ N(0, 1))       (makes sampling differentiable)

The key insight is the reparameterization trick. You can't backpropagate through a random sampling operation. But you can reparameterize: instead of sampling z ~ N(μ, σ²), compute z = μ + σ × ε where ε ~ N(0, 1). Now the randomness is in ε (which doesn't need gradients), and μ and σ are deterministic functions of the input that do receive gradients.

The VAE loss function, called the ELBO (Evidence Lower Bound), has two terms. The reconstruction loss measures how well the decoder rebuilds the original image (typically pixel-wise MSE or binary cross-entropy). The KL divergence term pushes the encoder's latent distributions toward a standard normal N(0, 1), ensuring the latent space is smooth and well-organized. If you dial up the KL weight, you get a smoother latent space but blurrier reconstructions. Dial it down, and you get sharp reconstructions but a fragmented latent space that's harder to sample from.

This tradeoff explains the classic VAE weakness: blurry outputs. The pixel-wise reconstruction loss averages over possible outputs at each pixel. If there are two plausible values for a pixel, the model learns to predict the average — which is blurry. GANs avoid this because the discriminator penalizes blurriness directly.

VQ-VAE (Vector Quantized VAE) tackles blurriness by using discrete latent codes instead of continuous ones. The encoder maps to a continuous vector, which is then snapped to the nearest entry in a learned codebook — like rounding continuous values to a finite alphabet. This discrete bottleneck forces the model to make sharp decisions instead of hedging with averages. VQ-VAE-2 combined this with a powerful autoregressive prior and produced images rivaling GANs in quality.

VAEs vs GANs: Different Strengths

VAEs give you a principled latent space you can smoothly interpolate through — morph one face into another by walking between latent codes. They also provide a proper likelihood estimate, useful for anomaly detection. GANs give sharper images but an entangled, discontinuous latent space. Modern architectures increasingly combine both ideas.

Diffusion Models

Diffusion models are the current king of image generation, powering Stable Diffusion, DALL·E, Midjourney, and Imagen. The core idea is surprisingly simple and strangely beautiful: take a real image, gradually add Gaussian noise to it over many steps until it becomes pure static, then train a neural network to reverse each step. Generation means starting from random noise and iteratively denoising.

Forward and Reverse Process

The forward process (also called the diffusion process) takes a clean image x₀ and adds a small amount of Gaussian noise at each timestep t = 1, 2, ..., T. After T steps (typically T = 1000), the image is indistinguishable from pure random noise. The amount of noise added at each step follows a noise schedule — usually starting small and increasing. A nice mathematical property: you can jump directly to any timestep t without computing all intermediate steps, using the formula x_t = √(ᾱ_t) × x₀ + √(1 - ᾱ_t) × ε where ε is standard Gaussian noise and ᾱ_t controls the signal-to-noise ratio.

Diffusion: Forward and Reverse Process
═══════════════════════════════════════

Forward process (destroy information):
  x₀ ──→ x₁ ──→ x₂ ──→ ... ──→ x_T
 [clean]  [tiny   [more          [pure
  image]   noise]  noise]         noise]
          +ε₁     +ε₂            +ε_T

  At each step: x_t = √(1-β_t) × x_{t-1} + √(β_t) × ε

Reverse process (learn to denoise):
  x_T ──→ x_{T-1} ──→ ... ──→ x₁ ──→ x₀
 [pure    [slightly              [almost  [clean
  noise]   cleaner]               clean]   image!]
          -ε̂_T                   -ε̂₁

  Neural network predicts the noise ε̂_t to subtract at each step
  Training: minimize ‖ε - ε̂_θ(x_t, t)‖²

The reverse process is where learning happens. A neural network (typically a U-Net with attention layers) takes in the noisy image x_t and the timestep t, and predicts the noise that was added. During training, you randomly sample a timestep, add the corresponding noise to a real image, and train the network to predict that noise. The loss is beautifully simple: just MSE between the true noise ε and the predicted noise ε̂. That's it. No adversarial games, no reconstruction-KL tradeoff.

This approach is called DDPM (Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models), published by Ho et al. in 2020. There's an equivalent interpretation through score matching: the model learns the gradient of the log-probability of the data (the "score function"), and generation follows that gradient uphill from noise to data.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn

# Simplified diffusion: one denoising step
# In practice, the model is a U-Net with time embeddings

class SimpleDenoiser(nn.Module):
    """Predicts noise given noisy image and timestep."""
    def __init__(self, img_dim=784, time_dim=128, hidden=512):
        super().__init__()
        self.time_embed = nn.Embedding(1000, time_dim)
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(img_dim + time_dim, hidden),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, hidden),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden, img_dim)  # predict noise
        )

    def forward(self, x_noisy, t):
        t_emb = self.time_embed(t)
        return self.net(torch.cat([x_noisy, t_emb], dim=-1))

model = SimpleDenoiser()
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-4)

# Training: sample random timestep, add noise, predict it
T = 1000
alphas_bar = torch.linspace(0.9999, 0.01, T)  # simplified schedule

for x_0, _ in dataloader:
    x_0 = x_0.view(x_0.size(0), -1)          # flatten
    t = torch.randint(0, T, (x_0.size(0),))   # random timestep per sample

    noise = torch.randn_like(x_0)
    a_bar = alphas_bar[t].unsqueeze(-1)
    x_t = torch.sqrt(a_bar) * x_0 + torch.sqrt(1 - a_bar) * noise

    noise_pred = model(x_t, t)
    loss = nn.MSELoss()(noise_pred, noise)     # simple MSE on noise

    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()

Latent Diffusion (Stable Diffusion)

Running diffusion directly on high-resolution images (say 512×512×3 = 786,432 dimensions) is computationally brutal. Latent diffusion models solve this by first encoding images into a much smaller latent space using a pretrained autoencoder (typically a VQ-VAE), then running the entire diffusion process in that compressed space. This is the architecture behind Stable Diffusion. The latent space might be 64×64×4 = 16,384 dimensions — nearly 50× smaller. After denoising in latent space, the decoder maps back to pixel space. The quality loss from compression is minimal because the autoencoder is trained to preserve perceptual quality.

Classifier-Free Guidance

Classifier-free guidance is the trick that makes text-to-image generation controllable. During training, you randomly drop the text conditioning some percentage of the time, training both a conditional and unconditional model simultaneously. At inference, you compute both the conditional and unconditional noise predictions, then amplify the difference: ε̂ = ε_uncond + w × (ε_cond - ε_uncond). The guidance scale w controls quality versus diversity. Higher w (7-15) gives images that strongly match the prompt but with less variation. Lower w (1-3) gives more diverse but potentially less prompt-faithful results.

Faster Sampling

DDPM requires 1000 denoising steps — painfully slow. Several methods accelerate this. DDIM (Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models) reformulates the reverse process as a deterministic ODE, enabling 50-step generation with minimal quality loss. DPM-Solver uses higher-order ODE solvers to achieve good quality in 10-20 steps. Distillation techniques like progressive distillation or consistency models push it down to 1-4 steps.

Why Diffusion Models Beat GANs

Diffusion models train with a simple, stable MSE loss — no adversarial balancing act. They don't suffer from mode collapse because they're trained on the entire data distribution equally. They naturally support conditioning (text, class labels, other images) without architectural surgery. And their iterative refinement process lets them correct mistakes at each step, producing more coherent high-frequency details. The tradeoff? They're slower at inference — but the sampling speed gap is closing fast.

Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF)

Everything we've discussed so far generates 2D images. Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF, 2020) take generative models into 3D. Given a handful of photographs of a scene taken from different angles, NeRF synthesizes photorealistic new viewpoints — what the scene would look like from angles you never photographed.

The idea: represent a 3D scene as a continuous function that maps a 3D coordinate (x, y, z) and viewing direction (θ, φ) to a color (r, g, b) and density (σ). This function is parameterized by a small MLP. To render an image from a new viewpoint, you cast rays through each pixel, sample points along each ray, query the network for color and density at each point, and composite them using volumetric rendering — the same physics equation used in medical CT scans and cloud rendering.

Positional encoding is critical. Raw (x, y, z) coordinates are too low-dimensional for an MLP to capture fine details. NeRF maps each coordinate through sinusoidal functions at multiple frequencies: γ(p) = [sin(2⁰πp), cos(2⁰πp), sin(2¹πp), cos(2¹πp), ..., sin(2⁹πp), cos(2⁹πp)]. This lets the network represent high-frequency texture and geometry.

The original NeRF was slow — hours to train one scene, seconds to render one frame. Instant-NGP (NVIDIA, 2022) replaced the positional encoding with a multi-resolution hash encoding, slashing training time from hours to seconds and rendering to real-time. This opened the door to practical applications: 3D scanning with your phone, VR environment creation, game asset generation, and cinematic virtual production.

Image Super-Resolution

Super-resolution is a focused generative task: given a low-resolution image, hallucinate plausible high-frequency details to produce a high-resolution version. It's an inherently ill-posed problem — many high-res images could correspond to the same low-res input. The model must make educated guesses about the missing information.

SRCNN (2014) was the first deep learning approach — just three convolutional layers that learn the mapping from low-res to high-res patches. Primitive by today's standards, but it beat all prior non-neural methods convincingly. The key lesson from SRCNN was that pixel-wise MSE loss produces smooth, blurry results. The model learns to predict the average of all plausible high-res details, which is a blur.

Perceptual loss fixed this by comparing images not at the pixel level but in the feature space of a pretrained VGG network. Two images that look perceptually similar will have similar VGG features, even if they differ pixel-by-pixel. This encourages the model to generate sharp textures that look realistic rather than mathematically averaging all possibilities.

ESRGAN (Enhanced Super-Resolution GAN) combined perceptual loss with adversarial training — a discriminator that judges whether the upscaled image looks like a real high-res photo. The pixel shuffle (sub-pixel convolution) layer efficiently upscales features: instead of using transpose convolutions (which cause checkerboard artifacts), it rearranges channels into spatial dimensions. A 1×1 region with 4 channels becomes a 2×2 region with 1 channel — clean, artifact-free upscaling.

Modern super-resolution models also handle real-world degradation. Real low-res images aren't just downsampled — they have compression artifacts, sensor noise, motion blur, and unknown downsampling kernels. Models like Real-ESRGAN train on randomly degraded images with complex pipelines that simulate these real-world imperfections, making them robust to messy inputs from actual cameras and compressed video.

The Generative Model Landscape

Each generative model family makes different tradeoffs. Here's how they compare across the dimensions that matter most in practice:

Property GANs VAEs Diffusion Models Flow Models
Image Quality High (sharp) Medium (blurry) Highest High
Diversity Medium (mode collapse risk) High High High
Training Stability Low (adversarial) High (simple loss) High (MSE loss) High
Inference Speed Fast (1 forward pass) Fast (1 forward pass) Slow (many steps) Moderate
Likelihood No Lower bound (ELBO) Yes (via connection to SDEs) Exact
Latent Space Entangled Smooth, structured N/A (iterative) Invertible
Conditioning Requires architecture changes Natural Natural (classifier-free guidance) Natural
Key Weakness Mode collapse, instability Blurry outputs Slow sampling Architecture constraints

Flow models (Normalizing Flows) deserve a brief mention. They learn an invertible mapping between the data distribution and a simple base distribution (Gaussian). Because the mapping is invertible, you get exact likelihood computation — no approximations. The constraint is that every layer must be invertible with a tractable Jacobian determinant, which limits architectural flexibility. Recent work like Flow Matching relaxes these constraints and bridges flow models with diffusion, blurring the boundaries between families.

The Trend Is Convergence

The boundaries between these families are dissolving. Stable Diffusion uses a VQ-VAE encoder with diffusion in latent space. StyleGAN-XL uses diffusion-like noise augmentation. Consistency models distill diffusion into single-step generators. The future isn't "which family wins" — it's hybrid architectures that cherry-pick the best ideas from each approach.

Ethical Reality Check

Generative vision models create images indistinguishable from photographs. This enables deepfakes, non-consensual imagery, misinformation, and copyright violations at unprecedented scale. Every capability discussed in this section has a dual-use concern. Watermarking (embedding invisible signatures in generated images), detection models, and regulatory frameworks are active areas of research and policy — and they're trailing behind the generative capabilities. If you build generative models, you carry responsibility for how they're used.
