Classic Architectures

Chapter 9: CNNs & Computer Vision 12 subtopics
TL;DR

CNN architectures evolved through a series of key insights: deeper networks (VGG), parallel multi-scale paths (Inception), skip connections to train very deep networks (ResNet), dense feature reuse (DenseNet), efficient mobile designs (MobileNet), principled scaling (EfficientNet), and finally replacing convolutions entirely with self-attention (ViT). Each architecture solved a specific problem its predecessor couldn't, and modern designs like ConvNeXt show that the best ideas from both CNNs and Transformers can be combined.

The history of CNN architectures reads like a detective story. Each design was a response to a specific frustration with the previous one. Understanding why each architecture exists — not just what it looks like — is the difference between memorizing a zoo of models and actually knowing how to design neural networks. Let's walk through this story chronologically.

LeNet-5 (1998): The Pioneer

It all started with Yann LeCun and handwritten digits. LeNet-5 was designed to read zip codes on mail — a 32×32 grayscale image in, one of ten digits out. The architecture is almost quaint by modern standards: two convolutional layers (5×5 kernels), two average pooling layers, and three fully-connected layers. About 60,000 parameters total.

But LeNet-5 established the template that every CNN follows: convolution → non-linearity → pooling → repeat → fully-connected classifier. It proved that learned convolutional features outperform hand-engineered features for visual recognition. The problem? It only worked for small, simple images. The compute and data needed to scale this approach to real photos didn't exist yet. LeNet-5 was right about everything — it was just 14 years too early.

AlexNet (2012): The ImageNet Earthquake

In 2012, Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, and Geoffrey Hinton submitted a CNN called AlexNet to the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC). It won by a staggering margin — 15.3% top-5 error versus 26.2% for the second-place system that used hand-crafted features. The gap was so large that it ended the debate about learned features versus engineered features overnight. Deep learning had arrived.

AlexNet's technical contributions were pragmatic rather than theoretical. It used ReLU activations instead of sigmoid/tanh, which trained 6× faster because ReLU doesn't saturate for positive inputs. It used dropout (randomly zeroing 50% of neurons during training) as a regularizer to combat overfitting on the relatively small-for-its-time ImageNet dataset. And critically, it trained on GPUs — two GTX 580s with 3GB of memory each — which made training a large CNN feasible in days instead of months.

The architecture itself: five conv layers (11×11, 5×5, then three 3×3), max pooling, and three FC layers with 4096 neurons each. About 60 million parameters, with most of them in the FC layers. By modern standards, it's poorly designed — the 11×11 first-layer kernel is wasteful, the FC layers are bloated, and the architecture isn't uniform. But it worked, and it changed the world.

AlexNet also used local response normalization (LRN) — a technique that normalized activations across neighboring channels at each spatial position. The idea was inspired by lateral inhibition in biological neurons. LRN has since been entirely replaced by batch normalization, which is more effective and more principled. But at the time, it gave AlexNet a small accuracy boost. The historical lesson: not every component of a successful architecture turns out to be essential. AlexNet's lasting contributions were ReLU, dropout, and the proof that GPUs + big data + deep CNNs could crush traditional computer vision.

VGGNet (2014): The Power of Simplicity

Karen Simonyan and Andrew Zisserman at Oxford asked a simple question: what happens if we make the network deeper while keeping everything else simple? VGGNet used exclusively 3×3 convolutions with stride 1 and 2×2 max pooling with stride 2. That's it. No clever tricks, just stack more layers.

The key insight was that two stacked 3×3 convolutions have the same receptive field as one 5×5 convolution, but with fewer parameters (18 vs 25) and two non-linearities instead of one. Three stacked 3×3 convolutions match a 7×7 receptive field with even better savings. So instead of using large kernels, just use many small ones.

VGG-16 has 16 weight layers (13 conv + 3 FC) and 138 million parameters. VGG-19 has 19. The architecture has a pleasing regularity: feature maps double in channels (64→128→256→512) each time spatial resolution halves. This established a design principle that persists today. However, VGG's massive FC layers (the first one alone has 102M parameters) made it impractical for deployment. It's also painfully slow to train — the 138M parameters all need gradient updates, and the uniform structure means no computational shortcuts.

VGG is rarely used in production today, but its influence is enormous. The "stack of 3×3 convolutions" philosophy became dogma for years. VGG feature extractors (particularly the intermediate layers) were the default backbone for style transfer, perceptual losses, and feature matching until ResNet came along. If you've ever seen neural style transfer generating art from photos, it was almost certainly using VGG features internally. The architecture also remains a popular teaching tool because of its simplicity — there's exactly one design decision (how many 3×3 layers to stack), making it easy to reason about.

GoogLeNet / Inception (2014): Going Wider

While VGG went deeper, Google went wider. GoogLeNet (a nod to LeNet) introduced the Inception module, which processes the same input through multiple parallel paths — 1×1 convolutions, 3×3 convolutions, 5×5 convolutions, and 3×3 max pooling — then concatenates the results. Different paths capture different spatial scales simultaneously.

The problem with naively running parallel convolutions is computational explosion. A 5×5 conv on 256 channels is expensive. The solution: 1×1 convolutions as bottlenecks. Before the 3×3 and 5×5 convolutions, a 1×1 conv reduces the channel count (say, from 256 to 64), making the subsequent larger convolution much cheaper. This "bottleneck" pattern — reduce channels, do expensive operation, expand channels — became a foundational design principle.

GoogLeNet also introduced auxiliary classifiers — small classification heads attached to intermediate layers during training. The idea was to combat vanishing gradients by injecting gradient signal deeper into the network. Later work showed these aren't strictly necessary (ResNet solved vanishing gradients more elegantly), but they provided a slight regularization benefit.

Inception v2/v3: Factorization

Subsequent Inception versions introduced convolution factorization. An n×n convolution can be decomposed into a 1×n followed by an n×1 convolution, reducing parameters from n² to 2n. A 7×7 convolution factorizes into 1×7 + 7×1 — cutting parameters by 72% while maintaining the same receptive field. Inception v3 also replaced 5×5 convolutions with two stacked 3×3 convolutions (the same VGG insight) and used batch normalization throughout. These incremental improvements pushed accuracy higher with better compute efficiency.

GoogLeNet had only 6.8 million parameters — 20× fewer than VGG — while achieving better accuracy. It proved that architecture design matters more than brute-force scaling, and that the fully-connected layers at the end were unnecessary (it used global average pooling). But Inception modules were complex to design and hard to modify. The field wanted something simpler.

ResNet (2015): The Skip Connection Revolution

Here's the puzzle that motivated ResNet: deeper networks should perform at least as well as shallower ones, because they could theoretically learn identity mappings for the extra layers. But in practice, networks deeper than about 20 layers performed worse — not from overfitting (training error was higher too), but from optimization difficulty. The gradients degraded as they propagated through dozens of layers, making it hard to learn useful features in the early layers.

Kaiming He's solution was almost insultingly simple: skip connections (also called shortcut connections or residual connections). Instead of learning the desired mapping H(x), each block learns the residual F(x) = H(x) - x, and the output is F(x) + x. The input is added directly to the output via a shortcut path that bypasses the conv layers.

Why does this work? If the optimal function for a block is close to identity (do nothing), learning F(x) ≈ 0 is much easier than learning H(x) ≈ x from scratch. The skip connection provides a gradient highway — during backpropagation, the gradient flows directly through the addition, bypassing the weight layers entirely. This means even in a 152-layer network, gradients can reach the first layer without being multiplied through 152 weight matrices.

Bottleneck Blocks

For deeper variants (ResNet-50 and above), the two-layer residual block (3×3, 3×3) was replaced with a three-layer bottleneck block: 1×1 (reduce channels), 3×3 (spatial processing on fewer channels), 1×1 (expand channels back). This is computationally cheaper and allows for deeper networks. ResNet-50 has about 25.5 million parameters — far fewer than VGG-16's 138 million — while being significantly more accurate.

import torch.nn as nn

# ResNet basic block (ResNet-18/34)
class BasicBlock(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, channels):
        super().__init__()
        self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(channels, channels, 3, padding=1, bias=False)
        self.bn1 = nn.BatchNorm2d(channels)
        self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(channels, channels, 3, padding=1, bias=False)
        self.bn2 = nn.BatchNorm2d(channels)
        self.relu = nn.ReLU(inplace=True)

    def forward(self, x):
        identity = x
        out = self.relu(self.bn1(self.conv1(x)))
        out = self.bn2(self.conv2(out))
        out += identity  # skip connection!
        return self.relu(out)

# ResNet bottleneck block (ResNet-50/101/152)
class Bottleneck(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, in_ch, mid_ch):
        super().__init__()
        out_ch = mid_ch * 4
        self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(in_ch, mid_ch, 1, bias=False)     # reduce
        self.bn1 = nn.BatchNorm2d(mid_ch)
        self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(mid_ch, mid_ch, 3, padding=1, bias=False)  # process
        self.bn2 = nn.BatchNorm2d(mid_ch)
        self.conv3 = nn.Conv2d(mid_ch, out_ch, 1, bias=False)    # expand
        self.bn3 = nn.BatchNorm2d(out_ch)
        self.relu = nn.ReLU(inplace=True)

    def forward(self, x):
        identity = x
        out = self.relu(self.bn1(self.conv1(x)))
        out = self.relu(self.bn2(self.conv2(out)))
        out = self.bn3(self.conv3(out))
        out += identity
        return self.relu(out)

ResNet changed everything. Skip connections became the default building block in virtually every architecture that followed. They enabled training networks with hundreds or even thousands of layers (though beyond ~152 layers, the returns diminish). The residual learning principle extends far beyond CNNs — Transformers use residual connections around every attention and FFN block. Pre-activation ResNet (He et al., 2016) later showed that moving BatchNorm and ReLU before the convolutions (BN→ReLU→Conv instead of Conv→BN→ReLU) further improves gradient flow and allows training even deeper networks.

DenseNet (2017): Feature Reuse

If skip connections are good, why not connect every layer to every other layer? DenseNet took this idea to its logical extreme. In a dense block, each layer receives the feature maps from all preceding layers (via concatenation, not addition) and passes its own feature maps to all subsequent layers. A layer in a dense block with 5 preceding layers receives inputs from all 5.

The growth rate k controls how many new feature maps each layer adds. With k=32, each layer concatenates 32 new channels to the running total. After 6 layers, you have 6×32 = 192 channels — a manageable number. Transition layers (1×1 conv + 2×2 avg pool) between dense blocks reduce the channel count and spatial size.

DenseNet's advantages: excellent feature reuse (every layer can access raw low-level features), strong gradient flow (each layer has a direct connection to the loss), and surprisingly few parameters (each layer only produces k new channels). DenseNet-121, for example, has only 8 million parameters. The drawback is memory: storing all those intermediate feature maps for concatenation is expensive during training. DenseNet is less common in production than ResNet, but its ideas influenced many subsequent designs.

MobileNet (2017-2019): Efficiency for Edge Devices

All the architectures above were designed for server-class GPUs with abundant memory and compute. MobileNet asked: what if you need to run on a phone?

MobileNetV1 replaced every standard convolution with a depthwise separable convolution. As we discussed in Section 1, this cuts computation by roughly 8-9×. A width multiplier α further controls the model size — setting α=0.5 halves all channel counts, cutting compute by 4× with modest accuracy loss. A resolution multiplier ρ allows using smaller input images (192 or 160 instead of 224).

MobileNetV2 introduced the inverted residual block. Unlike ResNet's bottleneck (wide→narrow→wide), MobileNetV2 goes narrow→wide→narrow. The "expansion" happens in the middle with a depthwise conv, and the residual connection is between the narrow (low-dimensional) representations. This is called "inverted" because the bottleneck is on the outside. It also uses linear bottlenecks — no ReLU after the final 1×1 conv — because ReLU destroys information in low-dimensional spaces. The reasoning is subtle but important: ReLU zeros out negative values, which effectively halves the capacity of a representation. In a high-dimensional space (the expanded middle layer), this loss is acceptable. In a low-dimensional space (the narrow bottleneck), it's devastating.

MobileNetV3 combined neural architecture search (NAS) with squeeze-and-excitation blocks, h-swish activation (a hardware-friendly approximation of swish), and platform-aware optimization to push the efficiency-accuracy Pareto frontier further. At this point, the architecture was no longer purely human-designed — it was a collaboration between human insight and automated search. MobileNetV3-Large achieved 75.2% ImageNet top-1 accuracy at 219M FLOPs — running in real-time on 2019-era smartphones.

ShuffleNet took a complementary approach to mobile efficiency. It used pointwise group convolutions (grouped 1×1 convolutions) to further reduce computation, then added a channel shuffle operation that randomly permutes channel order between groups. Without the shuffle, groups would be isolated — each group would only ever see its own subset of channels. The shuffle forces cross-group information flow, making grouped convolutions nearly as effective as standard ones at a fraction of the cost. ShuffleNet V2 later established practical design guidelines based on actual hardware latency rather than theoretical FLOPs, showing that memory access patterns and parallelism matter as much as raw operation count.

EfficientNet (2019): Principled Scaling

Before EfficientNet, scaling up a network was ad hoc. Need more accuracy? Make it deeper (more layers), or wider (more channels), or increase input resolution. But which dimension gives the best bang for the compute buck? EfficientNet answered this with compound scaling.

Mingxing Tan and Quoc Le showed that width, depth, and resolution should be scaled together in a balanced way. Their compound scaling rule uses a coefficient φ to scale all three simultaneously: depth scales as α^φ, width as β^φ, resolution as γ^φ, with α·β²·γ² ≈ 2 (so that doubling φ roughly doubles compute). The base network, EfficientNet-B0, was found via neural architecture search and uses MBConv blocks (mobile inverted bottleneck with SE attention).

Scaling from B0 to B7, EfficientNet achieved state-of-the-art ImageNet accuracy at every compute budget. EfficientNet-B7 matched or beat the best existing models while using 8.4× fewer parameters and 6.1× fewer FLOPs. The lesson: how you scale matters more than how big you scale.

EfficientNet also popularized the MBConv block — an inverted residual block (from MobileNetV2) with squeeze-and-excitation attention. This combination of efficient depthwise convolutions, channel attention, and skip connections became the default building block for efficient architectures. EfficientNetV2 later replaced some MBConv blocks with Fused-MBConv (standard convolution instead of depthwise separable) in early layers, because on modern hardware, the memory access overhead of depthwise convolutions can outweigh their theoretical FLOPs advantage at small spatial sizes.

Vision Transformer — ViT (2020): Attention Is All You Need (For Images Too)

In 2020, Google researchers asked an audacious question: what if we just use a Transformer — the architecture from NLP — directly on images, with no convolutions at all? Vision Transformer (ViT) splits an image into fixed-size patches (typically 16×16), flattens each patch into a vector, adds a learnable position embedding, prepends a learnable [CLS] token, and feeds the sequence into a standard Transformer encoder.

Each patch is treated like a "word" in NLP. Self-attention computes relationships between all pairs of patches — giving global receptive field from layer 1. No need for stacking convolutions to gradually expand the receptive field. The [CLS] token aggregates information from all patches and is used for classification.

The catch: ViT needs enormous amounts of training data. Trained on ImageNet alone (1.3M images), it underperforms ResNet. But pre-trained on JFT-300M (300 million images), it achieves state-of-the-art accuracy. Transformers lack the inductive biases of convolutions (local connectivity, translation equivariance), so they need more data to learn what CNNs get for free from their architecture. DeiT (Data-efficient Image Transformer) later showed that with careful training recipes — strong data augmentation, knowledge distillation from a CNN teacher — ViT can match CNNs even on ImageNet-scale data.

ViT comes in several sizes: ViT-B (Base, 86M params), ViT-L (Large, 307M params), and ViT-H (Huge, 632M params). The patch size also matters — ViT-B/16 uses 16×16 patches (196 tokens for a 224×224 image), while ViT-B/32 uses 32×32 patches (49 tokens). Smaller patches give higher resolution at the cost of quadratically more tokens and compute. The architectural simplicity is remarkable — no pooling, no convolutions, no special modules. Just patch embeddings, Transformer blocks, and a classification head on the [CLS] token.

Swin Transformer (2021): Hierarchical Vision Transformers

ViT processes all patches at a single scale with global attention — but self-attention is O(n²) in the number of patches, making it prohibitively expensive for high-resolution inputs. Swin Transformer (Shifted Window) solved this with two key ideas.

First, hierarchical feature maps: like a CNN, Swin progressively merges patches to create feature maps at 1/4, 1/8, 1/16, and 1/32 of the input resolution. This produces multi-scale features useful for dense prediction tasks (detection, segmentation) — something ViT couldn't do natively.

Second, shifted window attention: instead of global attention, Swin computes self-attention within local windows (e.g., 7×7 patches). To enable cross-window information flow, alternate layers shift the window partition by half the window size. This creates connections between windows at near-zero additional cost, achieving linear complexity O(n) instead of O(n²) while maintaining the ability to capture long-range dependencies.

Swin Transformer became the backbone of choice for many vision tasks, matching or beating CNNs on classification, detection, and segmentation simultaneously.

ConvNeXt (2022): CNNs Strike Back

After Swin Transformer's success, Meta researchers asked: is the Transformer architecture inherently better, or did it just benefit from modern training techniques? ConvNeXt started with a standard ResNet-50 and systematically applied every design choice from Swin Transformer — but using only convolutions.

The modifications: patchify stem (4×4 non-overlapping conv with stride 4, replacing ResNet's 7×7 conv + 3×3 max pool), larger kernels (7×7 depthwise convolutions, like Swin's 7×7 attention windows), inverted bottleneck (narrow→wide→narrow, like Transformers' FFN blocks), fewer normalization layers (one LayerNorm instead of batch norm after each block), and GELU activation instead of ReLU.

The result? ConvNeXt matched Swin Transformer across the board — same accuracy at same compute, on classification, detection, and segmentation. The conclusion was striking: pure convolutions can compete with Transformers when given the same training recipe and modern design choices. The advantage of Transformers wasn't self-attention per se — it was the ecosystem of training techniques (longer training, stronger augmentation, larger datasets) that had been developed for them.

ConvNeXt is particularly interesting because it reveals which Transformer innovations mattered and which didn't. Larger kernels (7×7 depthwise conv ≈ 7×7 attention window): mattered a lot. Layer normalization instead of batch normalization: mattered somewhat. GELU instead of ReLU: minor but consistent improvement. Fewer activation functions (only one per block instead of after every conv): surprisingly important. The patchify stem (replacing the gradual downsampling with an aggressive 4× stride at the start): mattered for throughput. ConvNeXt is a masterclass in principled architecture design — it shows that asking "which change actually helps?" for each component leads to better designs than blindly stacking innovations.

Architecture Comparison

Architecture Evolution at a Glance

LeNet (1998): Proved CNNs work — 60K params, MNIST-scale.
AlexNet (2012): GPU-trained, ReLU, dropout — 60M params, ImageNet champion.
VGGNet (2014): Small kernels, go deep — 138M params, beautifully simple but slow.
GoogLeNet (2014): Multi-scale Inception, 1×1 bottlenecks — 6.8M params, efficient.
ResNet (2015): Skip connections enable extreme depth — 25.5M params (ResNet-50), universal.
DenseNet (2017): Dense connections, maximal feature reuse — 8M params, memory-hungry.
MobileNet (2017-19): Depthwise separable, mobile-first — 3.4M params (V2), phone-ready.
EfficientNet (2019): Compound scaling, NAS-designed — 5.3M params (B0), Pareto-optimal.
ViT (2020): Pure attention on patches — 86M params (ViT-B), needs massive data.
Swin (2021): Hierarchical shifted-window attention — 88M params (Swin-B), versatile backbone.
ConvNeXt (2022): Modernized CNN matching Transformers — 89M params (ConvNeXt-B), proves CNNs aren't dead.

The trajectory tells a clear story. The early era (2012-2015) was about learning to go deep — solving gradient flow with ReLU, dropout, batch norm, and skip connections. The efficiency era (2017-2019) was about doing more with less — depthwise convolutions, neural architecture search, compound scaling. The attention era (2020-present) asked whether we need convolutions at all, and the answer was: not necessarily, but they're still competitive when modernized.

Which architecture should you use today? For most practical purposes: start with a pre-trained EfficientNet or ConvNeXt for classification tasks. Use Swin Transformer for detection and segmentation. Use MobileNet or EfficientNet-Lite for mobile deployment. The architecture matters less than the pre-training data and fine-tuning strategy — which brings us to the next section on transfer learning.

MLP-Mixer: The Wild Card

MLP-Mixer (2021) showed that neither convolutions nor attention are strictly necessary. It uses only MLPs: a "token-mixing" MLP operates across spatial locations (like attention but simpler), and a "channel-mixing" MLP operates across channels (like a 1×1 conv). On large datasets, it matches ViT. The lesson: the success of modern architectures comes more from scale, data, and training techniques than from the specific computational primitive (convolution, attention, or MLP).
