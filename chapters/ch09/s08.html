Semantic Segmentation

Chapter 9: CNNs & Computer Vision 12 subtopics
TL;DR

Semantic segmentation assigns a class label to every pixel in an image — not just "there's a cat," but "these exact pixels are cat." FCN replaced fully connected layers with 1×1 convolutions to produce dense predictions. U-Net added symmetric skip connections and dominates medical imaging. DeepLab introduced dilated convolutions and ASPP to capture multi-scale context without losing resolution. Instance segmentation (Mask R-CNN) gives each object its own mask. Panoptic segmentation unifies "stuff" and "things." SAM and Mask2Former bring foundation-model and transformer-based approaches. Training requires synchronized augmentation, specialized losses like Dice and Focal, and evaluation via mIoU — not pixel accuracy.

The Task: Why Pixel-Level Labels?

Image classification tells you "there's a cat in this image." Object detection goes further — it draws a bounding box and says "the cat is here." But bounding boxes are sloppy. They include background pixels. They can't tell you the cat's exact silhouette. Semantic segmentation solves this by assigning a class label to every single pixel in the image. The output isn't a label or a box — it's a full-resolution map where each pixel is colored by its class.

Why does this matter? Think about autonomous driving. A bounding box around a pedestrian doesn't tell the car's path planner which exact road surface is drivable. The system needs to know: these pixels are road, those are sidewalk, that cluster is a person, the strip above is sky. Pixel-level understanding is the difference between "there's stuff in front of the car" and "here is exactly where the car can and cannot go."

Medical imaging is another killer application. A radiologist doesn't just need to know a tumor exists — they need its precise boundary to measure volume, plan surgery, or track growth over time. A bounding box that captures the tumor plus surrounding healthy tissue is clinically useless for measurement. Satellite imagery is similar: you want to know exactly which pixels are forest, which are water, and which are urban development, not approximate rectangles.

Formally, if an input image has dimensions H × W, the segmentation model outputs an H × W tensor where each entry is a class index from 0 to C−1 (for C classes). During training, the model actually outputs a C × H × W tensor of logits (one score per class per pixel), and a softmax converts these to probabilities. The predicted class at each pixel is just the argmax across the C channels.

FCN: The Fully Convolutional Network

Before 2015, people tried segmentation by running classifiers on image patches — slide a window across the image, classify the center pixel. This was absurdly slow and threw away spatial context. The Fully Convolutional Network (FCN), published by Long, Shelhamer, and Darrell, changed everything with one elegant idea: take a classification CNN (like VGG-16), rip out the fully connected layers, and replace them with 1×1 convolutions.

Why does this work? A fully connected layer requires a fixed-size input (e.g., 7 × 7 × 512 flattened to 25,088). A 1×1 convolution operates on any spatial size — feed it a 14 × 14 × 512 feature map and you get a 14 × 14 output. This means the network can accept images of any size and produce a spatially dense output, not just a single class prediction.

The problem is resolution. After pooling layers in VGG, a 224 × 224 input is reduced to 7 × 7. The FCN's raw output is a coarse 7 × 7 prediction map. To get back to full resolution, FCN uses transposed convolutions (sometimes called "deconvolutions," though that's technically a misnomer). A transposed convolution is a learnable upsampling operation — it inserts zeros between pixels and then convolves, effectively learning how to upsample.

FCN came in three flavors that illustrate the power of skip connections for multi-scale fusion. FCN-32s upsamples the final layer directly by 32× — fast but very blobby predictions. FCN-16s combines predictions from the final layer and pool4 (stride 16), then upsamples by 16×. FCN-8s adds pool3 into the mix, fusing three scales before upsampling by 8×. Each refinement produced sharper boundaries because lower layers retain more spatial detail.

The Big Idea

FCN's insight was that classification networks already learn excellent features — you don't need a completely new architecture for segmentation. Just change the output head from "one vector" to "one vector per spatial location." This transfer-learning philosophy underlies almost every segmentation architecture that followed.

U-Net: The Medical Imaging Workhorse

U-Net was designed by Ronneberger et al. in 2015 specifically for biomedical image segmentation, where labeled data is scarce and precise boundaries matter enormously. Its architecture is elegantly symmetric: an encoder (contracting path) that captures context, a decoder (expanding path) that enables precise localization, and skip connections that bridge them.

The contracting path looks like any standard CNN — repeated blocks of two 3×3 convolutions followed by a 2×2 max pool, doubling the channels at each step. An input at 572 × 572 × 1 gets compressed down to 32 × 32 × 1024 through four downsampling steps. The expanding path mirrors this: each step applies a 2×2 transposed convolution (halving channels), concatenates the corresponding feature map from the contracting path, and applies two 3×3 convolutions.

The critical design choice: skip connections in U-Net concatenate, not add. When the decoder at the 256-channel level receives an upsampled feature map from below, it concatenates it with the 256-channel feature map from the encoder, producing a 512-channel input. This is different from ResNet-style skip connections that use element-wise addition. Concatenation preserves full feature richness from both paths, letting the network decide how to combine coarse semantic information (from the decoder) with fine spatial detail (from the encoder).

Why does U-Net dominate medical imaging? Three reasons. First, it works with very small datasets — the original paper used only 30 annotated images for training, using heavy augmentation (elastic deformations, rotations, flips) to prevent overfitting. Second, the skip connections preserve fine details critical for delineating organ boundaries. Third, the architecture is simple enough to train on a single GPU, which was important for hospital research labs in 2015 and remains practical today.

U-Net Variants

U-Net has spawned an entire family: Attention U-Net adds attention gates to skip connections so the decoder can focus on relevant encoder features. U-Net++ nests dense skip connections between encoder and decoder. 3D U-Net extends to volumetric data (CT/MRI scans). nnU-Net auto-configures preprocessing, architecture, and training for any new medical segmentation dataset and consistently wins competitions.

The DeepLab Family

Standard convolutions face a dilemma for segmentation: pooling and striding increase the receptive field (how much context each neuron sees) but destroy spatial resolution. You need both — wide context to know "this is a road" and high resolution to draw precise edges. The DeepLab family solves this with atrous convolutions (also called dilated convolutions).

An atrous convolution inserts "holes" (zeros) between filter weights, effectively spreading a 3×3 kernel over a larger spatial area without increasing the number of parameters. A 3×3 convolution with dilation rate 2 covers a 5×5 area. Rate 4 covers a 9×9 area. You get a larger receptive field without any pooling — the spatial resolution stays the same. It's like looking through a wider lens without losing pixel detail.

Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP) is the next key ingredient. Instead of picking one dilation rate, ASPP applies several atrous convolutions in parallel at different rates (e.g., 6, 12, 18) plus a 1×1 convolution and global average pooling. Each branch captures context at a different scale. The outputs are concatenated and fused with a 1×1 convolution. This multi-scale feature extraction is crucial for scenes where objects appear at wildly different sizes — a person standing near the camera vs. one far away.

The DeepLab family evolved over four major versions. DeepLabV1 introduced atrous convolutions and used a Conditional Random Field (CRF) as post-processing to sharpen boundaries. DeepLabV2 added ASPP for multi-scale processing. DeepLabV3 improved ASPP with batch normalization and global average pooling, dropping the CRF (the network itself became good enough). DeepLabV3+ combined the ASPP module with an encoder-decoder structure — the ASPP output is upsampled and concatenated with low-level features from the encoder, giving both multi-scale context and sharp edges.

Encoder-Decoder Architectures

U-Net and DeepLabV3+ are specific instances of a general encoder-decoder pattern that dominates segmentation. The encoder progressively reduces spatial resolution while increasing feature richness (the "what" path). The decoder progressively recovers spatial resolution (the "where" path). Skip connections bridge the two, preventing the decoder from having to reconstruct fine details from scratch.

SegNet, published around the same time as U-Net, uses a slightly different upsampling trick. Instead of transposed convolutions, it saves the max-pooling indices from the encoder and reuses them in the decoder for "unpooling" — placing each value back at its original max location. This is memory-efficient (you store only indices, not full feature maps) and produces sharper boundaries, though it loses some capacity to learn the upsampling.

PSPNet (Pyramid Scene Parsing Network) takes a different angle on the multi-scale problem. Instead of ASPP's parallel dilated convolutions, PSPNet uses a Pyramid Pooling Module: it applies global average pooling at four different grid sizes (1×1, 2×2, 3×3, 6×6), processes each with a 1×1 convolution, upsamples all to the same size, and concatenates. This captures context at scales from "the entire image" down to "small local regions." PSPNet was state-of-the-art on ADE20K in 2017.

The fundamental tradeoff in all encoder-decoder architectures is between resolution and context. Aggressive downsampling (deep encoders) gives each neuron awareness of a large image region but loses spatial precision. Shallow encoders preserve detail but each neuron only sees a tiny patch and can't distinguish "road" from "sidewalk" when both have similar texture. Skip connections are the engineering compromise that lets you have both — but they add memory cost and architectural complexity.

Instance Segmentation

Semantic segmentation has a blind spot: it labels every pixel with a class, but it doesn't distinguish between individual objects. If two dogs stand next to each other, semantic segmentation colors both with the same "dog" label — you can't tell where one ends and the other begins. Instance segmentation fixes this by giving each object its own unique mask.

Mask R-CNN, published by He et al. at Facebook AI in 2017, is the dominant approach. It extends Faster R-CNN (a two-stage object detector) with an additional branch that predicts a binary mask for each detected object. The pipeline works in three stages: first, a Region Proposal Network generates candidate bounding boxes. Second, for each proposal, the network predicts the class and refines the box coordinates. Third — and this is the new part — a small FCN head predicts a per-pixel binary mask inside each box.

A crucial technical detail makes Mask R-CNN work: RoI Align. The original Faster R-CNN used RoI Pooling, which quantizes box coordinates to integer grid positions. This introduces small misalignments — a pixel or two of error. For classification, that's fine. For mask prediction, a one-pixel shift is disastrous because you're trying to predict exact spatial boundaries. RoI Align replaces the harsh quantization with bilinear interpolation, sampling feature values at precise floating-point positions. This small change improved mask quality significantly.

The mask head itself is remarkably simple: a few 3×3 convolutional layers followed by a transposed convolution for upsampling, outputting a 28 × 28 binary mask per class per proposal. The mask predictions are decoupled from class predictions — the network predicts K masks (one per class) and uses the classification branch to select which mask to output. This decoupling lets the mask head focus purely on spatial precision without worrying about class distinctions.

Panoptic Segmentation

Real-world scenes contain two fundamentally different kinds of visual elements. "Stuff" is amorphous, uncountable regions — sky, road, grass, water. You don't count individual sky instances. "Things" are countable objects — cars, people, dogs. Semantic segmentation handles stuff well. Instance segmentation handles things well. Neither handles both.

Panoptic segmentation, proposed by Kirillov et al. in 2019, unifies the two. Every pixel gets both a semantic label and an instance ID. Stuff pixels share a single instance ID per class (there's only one "sky"). Thing pixels get unique instance IDs (car_1, car_2, person_1). The output is a single coherent map where nothing overlaps and nothing is left unlabeled.

This matters enormously for autonomous driving. A self-driving car needs to know that the road is drivable (stuff segmentation), that there are three separate pedestrians (instance segmentation), and that the sky/buildings are irrelevant to navigation (stuff classification). Panoptic segmentation delivers all of this in one unified prediction, which is both more efficient and more consistent than running separate models.

Panoptic FPN (Feature Pyramid Network) was an early architecture for this task. It takes Mask R-CNN's FPN backbone and adds a lightweight semantic segmentation branch alongside the instance branch. The stuff branch produces dense per-pixel labels for amorphous classes. The things branch produces instance masks. A merge step combines them, resolving overlaps (instance masks take priority). More recent approaches like Panoptic-DeepLab and MaskFormer have moved toward end-to-end architectures that handle both stuff and things with a single decoder.

Segment Anything Model (SAM)

In April 2023, Meta AI released SAM (Segment Anything Model), a foundation model for segmentation. The core idea: train a model on an enormous, diverse dataset so that it can segment any object in any image — even ones it has never seen — without task-specific fine-tuning. SAM is to segmentation what GPT is to text: a general-purpose engine that you prompt rather than retrain.

SAM is promptable. You can prompt it with a point (click on an object), a bounding box (draw a rough rectangle), a rough mask (scribble), or even text. The model returns one or more segmentation masks along with confidence scores. This makes SAM incredibly flexible — a radiologist can click on a suspicious region in a scan, and SAM produces the boundary without any medical-specific training.

The architecture has three components. The image encoder is a Vision Transformer (ViT-H, with 632M parameters) that processes the entire image once, producing a rich embedding. The prompt encoder converts the user's prompt (points, boxes, masks, or text) into tokens. The mask decoder is a lightweight transformer that takes image embeddings and prompt tokens and outputs segmentation masks. Because the image encoder runs only once, you can try many different prompts on the same image almost instantly.

The training dataset, SA-1B, contains over 1 billion masks on 11 million images. Meta built it using a data engine: an earlier version of SAM assisted human annotators, who corrected the model's outputs, and the corrected data was used to train a better model. This human-in-the-loop flywheel produced the largest segmentation dataset ever created.

SAM 2 (2024) extends the model to video. It propagates segmentation masks across frames using memory attention — you prompt a single frame, and SAM 2 tracks and segments the object through the entire video. This is a massive step toward interactive video understanding.

SAM's Limitation

SAM excels at segmenting objects you point to, but it doesn't inherently know class labels. It tells you "these pixels form one object" — not "these pixels are a cat." For tasks requiring class-aware segmentation, you still need to combine SAM with a classifier or use task-specific models. Fine-tuned variants like MedSAM adapt the model for domain-specific labeling.

MaskFormer & Mask2Former

Traditional segmentation architectures treat semantic, instance, and panoptic segmentation as fundamentally different tasks requiring different architectures. MaskFormer (Cheng et al., 2021) challenged this by reframing all three as a single mask classification problem: predict a set of binary masks, then classify each mask.

The architecture uses a transformer decoder that outputs N learnable "mask embeddings." Each embedding is compared against pixel-level features from the backbone to produce a binary mask (via dot product). A separate classification head assigns a class label to each mask. For semantic segmentation, masks of the same class are merged. For instance segmentation, each mask is a separate object. For panoptic segmentation, you get both. One architecture, three tasks — you just change the loss and post-processing.

Mask2Former (2022) improved on this with masked attention: instead of attending to the entire image, each query only attends to the region where its predicted mask is active. This dramatically improves efficiency and accuracy, especially for small objects. Mask2Former achieved state-of-the-art results on all three segmentation tasks simultaneously — semantic (ADE20K), instance (COCO), and panoptic (COCO) — which was unprecedented for a single architecture.

Lightweight & Real-Time Segmentation

The architectures above prioritize accuracy, often running at 1–5 frames per second on a GPU. That's fine for processing medical scans offline, but useless for a drone or self-driving car that needs segmentation at 30+ FPS. Lightweight models sacrifice some accuracy for dramatically faster inference.

ENet (Efficient Neural Network) was an early effort: it uses an asymmetric encoder-decoder where the decoder is much smaller than the encoder, aggressive early downsampling (8× in the first two blocks), and factorized convolutions (split a 5×5 conv into separate 5×1 and 1×5 operations). It runs at 135 FPS on a 640×360 input — fast enough for real-time driving — but the accuracy gap compared to DeepLab is significant.

BiSeNet (Bilateral Segmentation Network) takes a two-path approach: a shallow spatial path preserves resolution and captures fine detail, while a deep context path uses a full backbone network for semantic understanding. A Feature Fusion Module combines them. This avoids the expensive encoder-decoder pattern entirely and achieves 105 FPS on Cityscapes.

ICNet (Image Cascade Network) processes the image at three resolutions in parallel — full-res, half-res, and quarter-res. The low-res branch does the heavy semantic lifting with a deep network. The higher-res branches are lightweight and refine spatial details. Cascade fusion combines them progressively.

Loss Functions for Segmentation

Segmentation losses operate on every pixel independently, then aggregate. The simplest is pixel-wise cross-entropy: treat each pixel as an independent classification problem, compute cross-entropy loss for each, and average over the entire image. If you have C classes, the loss at pixel (i,j) is −log(p_c) where p_c is the predicted probability for the true class c.

The problem with plain cross-entropy is class imbalance. In a driving scene, 60% of pixels might be "road," 20% might be "sky," and only 0.5% might be "traffic light." The network quickly learns to predict "road" everywhere and gets 60% pixel accuracy for free. The tiny traffic light class is drowned out.

Dice loss directly optimizes the overlap between predicted and ground-truth masks, making it robust to class imbalance. The Dice coefficient measures how well two sets overlap: 2 × |A ∩ B| / (|A| + |B|). It ranges from 0 (no overlap) to 1 (perfect overlap). Dice loss = 1 − Dice coefficient. Because it measures proportional overlap rather than per-pixel accuracy, a small class that's well-segmented contributes just as much as a large class.

import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def dice_loss(pred_logits, target, smooth=1.0):
    """
    pred_logits: (B, C, H, W) raw model output
    target:      (B, H, W) integer class labels
    smooth:      prevents division by zero
    """
    num_classes = pred_logits.shape[1]
    probs = F.softmax(pred_logits, dim=1)             # (B, C, H, W)

    # One-hot encode target: (B, H, W) → (B, C, H, W)
    target_oh = F.one_hot(target, num_classes)         # (B, H, W, C)
    target_oh = target_oh.permute(0, 3, 1, 2).float() # (B, C, H, W)

    # Compute Dice per class, then average
    dims = (0, 2, 3)  # sum over batch, height, width
    intersection = (probs * target_oh).sum(dim=dims)
    cardinality  = probs.sum(dim=dims) + target_oh.sum(dim=dims)

    dice_per_class = (2.0 * intersection + smooth) / (cardinality + smooth)
    return 1.0 - dice_per_class.mean()

Focal loss, originally designed for object detection (RetinaNet), addresses class imbalance differently. It down-weights easy, well-classified pixels and focuses the loss on hard, misclassified ones. The formula adds a modulating factor (1 − p_t)^γ to the standard cross-entropy, where p_t is the predicted probability for the correct class and γ (typically 2) controls how aggressively easy examples are down-weighted. When a pixel is confidently correct (p_t ≈ 1), the loss contribution drops to near zero. When it's wrong (p_t ≈ 0), the loss is essentially unchanged.

Boundary loss is a newer idea that addresses a specific failure mode: models often produce masks that are roughly correct in the interior but sloppy at boundaries. Boundary loss penalizes distance from the predicted boundary to the ground-truth boundary, computed via distance transform maps. It's typically used as a secondary term added to Dice or cross-entropy loss, not alone.

In practice, most competitive segmentation systems use combined losses. A common recipe is cross-entropy + Dice, which combines the stable per-pixel gradients of cross-entropy with the overlap-aware properties of Dice. Weighted cross-entropy (where each class gets a weight inversely proportional to its frequency) is a simpler alternative that partially addresses class imbalance without introducing a second loss term.

Evaluation Metrics

Pixel accuracy is the most intuitive metric: what fraction of pixels did the model classify correctly? But it's deeply misleading when classes are imbalanced. If 80% of pixels in a driving scene are road/sky, a model that labels everything as road/sky achieves 80% pixel accuracy while being completely useless for detecting pedestrians, cars, or signs.

Mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) is the standard metric for semantic segmentation, and for good reason. For each class, compute the IoU: the number of pixels correctly predicted as that class (intersection) divided by the total number of pixels that are either predicted or actually that class (union). Then average IoU across all classes. A class that covers 0.1% of pixels counts equally with a class that covers 50%. This forces the model to care about every class.

import numpy as np

def compute_miou(pred, target, num_classes):
    """
    pred:   (H, W) integer array of predicted class labels
    target: (H, W) integer array of ground truth labels
    Returns: mIoU (float), per-class IoU (array)
    """
    ious = []
    for cls in range(num_classes):
        pred_mask   = (pred == cls)
        target_mask = (target == cls)

        intersection = np.logical_and(pred_mask, target_mask).sum()
        union        = np.logical_or(pred_mask, target_mask).sum()

        if union == 0:
            # Class not present in pred or target — skip
            continue
        ious.append(intersection / union)

    per_class_iou = np.array(ious)
    return per_class_iou.mean(), per_class_iou

The Dice coefficient (also called F1 score for binary segmentation) is closely related to IoU: Dice = 2 × IoU / (1 + IoU). It's preferred in medical imaging because it matches the Dice loss used during training — optimizing Dice loss directly improves the Dice metric. Numerically, Dice is always higher than IoU for the same prediction (e.g., IoU of 0.75 corresponds to Dice of 0.857), which can be confusing when comparing across papers that report different metrics.

Boundary F1 (BF1) measures how well the predicted segmentation boundaries match the ground-truth boundaries. It computes precision and recall of boundary pixels within a tolerance distance (typically a few pixels), then takes the F1 score. A model might achieve high mIoU with blobby predictions that roughly cover the right regions, but low BF1 because its boundaries are imprecise. BF1 is increasingly reported alongside mIoU, especially in applications like medical imaging where boundary precision directly impacts clinical measurements.

Metric Trap

Never report pixel accuracy alone for segmentation results. It's misleading enough that reviewers will reject your paper for it. Always report mIoU as the primary metric. If boundaries matter (medical, cartography), add Dice and BF1. If you're comparing models, make sure they report the same metric — Dice and IoU are different numbers for the same prediction.

Training Practices

Segmentation training has quirks that don't exist in classification. The biggest one is synchronized augmentation. In classification, you can flip, rotate, or crop an image independently — the label ("cat") stays the same. In segmentation, the mask must undergo exactly the same transformation as the image. If you rotate the image 15° clockwise, the mask must rotate 15° clockwise. If you apply a random crop to the image, the same crop coordinates must apply to the mask. Fail to synchronize and your model trains on misaligned image-mask pairs — it will never converge.

# Synchronized augmentation with albumentations
import albumentations as A

transform = A.Compose([
    A.RandomCrop(width=512, height=512),
    A.HorizontalFlip(p=0.5),
    A.RandomRotate90(p=0.5),
    A.RandomBrightnessContrast(p=0.3),    # color-only: applied to image
    A.GaussNoise(p=0.2),                  # color-only: applied to image
], additional_targets={'mask': 'mask'})

# Both image and mask get the SAME spatial transform
augmented = transform(image=image, mask=mask)
aug_image = augmented['image']    # rotated, cropped, color-jittered
aug_mask  = augmented['mask']     # rotated, cropped (no color changes)

Patch-based vs. full-image training is a key decision. High-resolution images (satellite imagery at 10,000 × 10,000, or whole-slide pathology at 100,000 × 100,000) don't fit in GPU memory as a single input. The solution is to train on random patches (e.g., 512 × 512 crops). This also acts as data augmentation — each epoch sees different random crops. The downside is that the model never sees the global context, which can hurt for objects whose identity depends on surroundings (is that blue patch a pool or a lake?).

At inference time, when you need the full-resolution prediction, you use sliding window inference. Slide a window across the image with some overlap (e.g., stride 256 with 512 × 512 windows), predict each patch, and average predictions in the overlapping regions. The overlap is important — it prevents artifacts at patch boundaries. Without overlap, you get visible seams where one patch's prediction abruptly transitions to the next.

Multi-scale inference (also called test-time augmentation for segmentation) further improves quality. Run the model on the same image at multiple resolutions (e.g., 0.5×, 1.0×, 1.5×, 2.0×), plus horizontally flipped versions, and average all predictions. This consistently adds 1–2 mIoU points at the cost of N× inference time (where N is the number of scales × flips). Most competition-winning submissions use this trick, but production systems often skip it for speed.

Practical Tip: Pre-trained Encoders

Almost nobody trains segmentation encoders from scratch. Use ImageNet pre-trained backbones (ResNet, EfficientNet, ConvNeXt, or even ViT) as the encoder, and only train the decoder from scratch. This converges faster, requires less data, and almost always performs better. Libraries like segmentation_models_pytorch make this trivial — pick a backbone, pick a decoder (U-Net, FPN, DeepLabV3+), and you're training in 10 lines of code.
