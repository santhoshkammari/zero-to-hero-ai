Nice to Know

Chapter 5: Supervised Learning GLMs · Survival · Specialized techniques

These topics are worth knowing exist. You probably won't need them daily, but when you do, you'll be glad you've heard of them. Each one solves a specific problem that standard classification and regression can't handle cleanly.

Generalized Linear Models (GLMs)

Linear regression assumes your target is normally distributed. GLMs extend this to any distribution from the exponential family — Poisson for count data, Gamma for positive continuous values, Binomial (which gives you logistic regression as a special case). A link function maps the linear predictor to the mean of the response distribution. You need this when your target isn't normally distributed: insurance claim counts (Poisson), claim amounts (Gamma), click-through rates (Binomial).

Survival Analysis

Models time-to-event data where some observations are censored — the event hasn't happened yet when you stop observing. Kaplan-Meier curves estimate survival probability over time. The Cox proportional hazards model finds which features accelerate or delay the event without assuming a specific time distribution. Used in healthcare (patient survival), SaaS (churn prediction where you know subscription start dates but not all customers have churned), and manufacturing (equipment failure prediction).

Quantile Regression

Standard regression predicts the mean. Quantile regression predicts any quantile — the median, the 90th percentile, whatever you need. This is invaluable when you care about prediction intervals or tail risk rather than just point estimates. "The median house price is $300k, but the 95th percentile is $800k" tells a completely different story than "the average is $400k." Use it for demand forecasting, financial risk, or any scenario where the spread of outcomes matters as much as the center.

Bayesian Linear Regression

Instead of getting single point estimates for your coefficients, you get full posterior distributions. Every prediction comes with a natural measure of uncertainty — not just "the price is $350k" but "the price is $350k ± $40k with 95% credibility." Computationally more expensive than ordinary least squares, but invaluable when you need calibrated confidence intervals on predictions, especially with small datasets or high-stakes decisions where knowing what you don't know matters.

Isotonic Regression

A non-parametric method that fits a non-decreasing (or non-increasing) step function to your data. Its killer use case: probability calibration. Many classifiers output scores that aren't well-calibrated probabilities (a model says "80% confident" but is only right 60% of the time). Isotonic regression maps those raw scores to true probabilities. Scikit-learn's CalibratedClassifierCV uses this under the hood. Reach for it when you need reliable probability estimates from a classifier whose raw outputs you don't trust.

Multi-label Classification

In standard multiclass classification, each sample belongs to exactly one class. Multi-label flips this: each sample can belong to multiple classes simultaneously. A movie can be both "comedy" AND "drama." A research paper can be tagged "NLP" AND "transformers" AND "efficiency." Three approaches: binary relevance (independent binary classifier per label — simple but ignores correlations), classifier chains (each classifier gets previous predictions as features), or specialized loss functions. You need this anytime your labels aren't mutually exclusive.

Ordinal Regression

When your classes have a natural order — star ratings (1–5), pain severity (low/medium/high), education level — but the gaps between classes aren't necessarily equal. Standard classification treats "predicting 1 when the truth is 5" the same as "predicting 4 when the truth is 5," which is absurd. Ordinal regression respects the ordering. Use it for survey responses, product ratings, or any ranked outcome where being close to the right answer is better than being far off.

Online Learning

Update the model incrementally as new data arrives, without retraining from scratch. Essential when data is too large to fit in memory, arrives as a stream, or the underlying distribution shifts over time. Scikit-learn's SGDClassifier and SGDRegressor support partial_fit() for exactly this. Also useful in production systems where you want the model to adapt to new patterns (fraud detection, recommendation engines) without the cost of full retraining.

What You Should Now Be Able To Do

  • Recognize when your target distribution calls for a GLM instead of plain linear regression
  • Know that survival analysis exists when you encounter censored time-to-event data
  • Reach for quantile regression when prediction intervals matter more than point estimates
  • Distinguish multi-label from multiclass problems and pick an appropriate strategy
  • Identify when online learning or ordinal regression would be the right tool for the job
