Linear Regression

Chapter 5: Supervised Learning 10 subtopics
TL;DR

Linear regression finds the best-fitting line (or hyperplane) through your data by minimizing squared errors. The normal equation gives an exact closed-form answer; gradient descent iterates toward it when the data is too large to invert a matrix. Ridge regression shrinks all coefficients to fight overfitting, Lasso drives some coefficients to zero for automatic feature selection, and ElasticNet blends both. The Gauss-Markov theorem guarantees that ordinary least squares is the best linear unbiased estimator — as long as a few assumptions hold. This section builds the full toolkit from a single straight line to production-grade regularized models.

Why a Straight Line Through Data Points?

Suppose you're predicting house prices. You have a spreadsheet with 500 houses: square footage in one column, sale price in the other. You plot them. There's a clear upward trend — bigger houses cost more — but no two points sit perfectly on a line. Some 1,500 sq ft houses sold for $200k, others for $260k. The relationship is real but noisy.

You want a formula: given the square footage of a new house, what price should you predict? The most natural first guess is a straight line: price = w₀ + w₁ × sqft. Here w₀ is the y-intercept (the predicted price of a zero-sqft house — a meaningless number, but mathematically necessary) and w₁ is the slope (how many extra dollars per additional square foot). That formula is linear regression.

Why start with a line? Two reasons. First, lines are interpretable. If w₁ = 150, you can tell your client: "every extra square foot adds about $150 to the predicted price." Try explaining that with a neural network. Second, lines are surprisingly effective. Many real-world relationships are approximately linear within the range of data you actually observe, even if the true relationship is curved across a wider range. A straight line is a solid default — you only reach for more complex models when it demonstrably fails.

Simple Linear Regression

Least Squares Estimation

You have n data points: (x₁, y₁), (x₂, y₂), ..., (xₙ, yₙ). You want to find the line ŷ = w₀ + w₁x that gets as close to the actual y values as possible. But what does "close" mean? You need a number that measures how wrong the line is. The standard choice is the sum of squared residuals: add up the squared differences between each actual y and the predicted ŷ.

Loss = Σᵢ (yᵢ - ŷᵢ)² = Σᵢ (yᵢ - w₀ - w₁xᵢ)²

Why squared? Because squaring does two useful things: it makes all errors positive (a prediction that's $10 too high is as bad as $10 too low), and it punishes large errors more than small ones. A prediction that's off by $100 contributes 10,000 to the loss, while ten predictions each off by $10 contribute only 1,000 total. The method of least squares finds the w₀ and w₁ that minimize this total.

Take the partial derivatives with respect to w₀ and w₁, set them to zero, and solve. You get closed-form formulas:

w₁ = Σᵢ (xᵢ - x̄)(yᵢ - ȳ) / Σᵢ (xᵢ - x̄)²
w₀ = ȳ - w₁ x̄

No iteration, no hyperparameters. Plug in the data, get the answer. This is one of the oldest results in statistics — Legendre published it in 1805, Gauss claimed he'd been using it since 1795.

Coefficient Interpretation

Every coefficient tells a story. In price = 50000 + 150 × sqft, the slope w₁ = 150 means: holding everything else constant, one additional square foot is associated with a $150 increase in predicted price. The intercept w₀ = 50,000 is the predicted price at zero square footage. That's physically meaningless — there's no zero-sqft house — but it positions the line correctly within the range of data you actually have.

A critical distinction: the coefficient tells you about association, not causation. Adding a room doesn't magically increase the price by exactly $150 per square foot. The coefficient captures whatever mix of causal and confounding factors exist in your data.

Residuals

The residual for data point i is eᵢ = yᵢ − ŷᵢ — the gap between what actually happened and what the model predicted. Residuals are your diagnostic tool. If the model is doing its job, they should look like random noise: no patterns, no trends, no funneling.

Plot residuals against predicted values. If you see a funnel shape (residuals get wider as ŷ increases), the variance of your errors isn't constant — that's heteroscedasticity, and it means your confidence intervals are wrong. If you see a curve, the relationship isn't linear and you need a polynomial term or a transformation. If residuals are ordered by time and show autocorrelation, successive observations aren't independent. Residual plots catch problems that R² hides.

R-squared and Adjusted R-squared

R² (the coefficient of determination) answers: "what fraction of the variance in y does the model explain?" It's computed as:

R² = 1 - (SS_res / SS_tot)

where SS_res = Σ(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)²    (residual sum of squares)
      SS_tot = Σ(yᵢ - ȳ)²     (total sum of squares)

If R² = 0.85, the model explains 85% of the variation in house prices. The remaining 15% is noise, missing features, or nonlinearity the model can't capture. R² = 1 means a perfect fit (suspicious — probably overfitting). R² near 0 means the model is no better than predicting the mean every time.

The trap: R² never decreases when you add more features. Throw in a random noise column, and R² will tick up slightly by chance. Adjusted R² penalizes for the number of features, giving a more honest assessment of whether that extra variable actually helped or if it's parasitic complexity.

Adjusted R² = 1 - ((1 - R²)(n - 1)) / (n - p - 1)

where n = number of observations, p = number of features

Assumptions: LINE

Ordinary least squares (OLS) rests on four assumptions, often remembered as LINE:

Linearity: the true relationship between x and y is linear. If the actual relationship is curved, a straight line will systematically miss in predictable ways. Check this with a residuals-vs-fitted plot.

Independence: the residuals are independent of each other. Observation 5's error doesn't influence observation 6's error. This fails with time series data or spatial data, where nearby observations tend to be correlated.

Normality: the residuals follow a normal distribution. This matters for confidence intervals and p-values. With large enough samples, the Central Limit Theorem makes this less critical for point estimates, but it still matters for inference.

Equal variance (Homoscedasticity): the spread of residuals is the same regardless of the predicted value. When it isn't — when expensive houses have more variable prices than cheap ones — the standard errors are wrong and your confidence intervals lie to you.

When these assumptions hold, OLS gives you the best possible estimates. When they break, you need corrections: transformations, robust standard errors, weighted least squares, or an entirely different model.

Confidence and Prediction Intervals

A confidence interval tells you how uncertain you are about the average response at a given x. For all 1,500 sqft houses in the city, the average price is between $220k and $240k with 95% confidence. A prediction interval tells you how uncertain you are about a single new observation. The next 1,500 sqft house to sell will go for between $180k and $280k with 95% confidence. Prediction intervals are always wider because they include the irreducible noise of individual outcomes on top of the uncertainty in the mean.

import numpy as np
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression

# Sample data: square footage → price
sqft = np.array([800, 1000, 1200, 1500, 1800, 2000, 2500]).reshape(-1, 1)
price = np.array([150, 185, 210, 260, 300, 340, 410])

model = LinearRegression()
model.fit(sqft, price)

print(f"Intercept: {model.intercept_:.2f}")
print(f"Slope: {model.coef_[0]:.2f} per sqft")
print(f"R²: {model.score(sqft, price):.4f}")

# Predict for a 1600 sqft house
new_sqft = np.array([[1600]])
print(f"Predicted price: ${model.predict(new_sqft)[0]:.0f}k")

Multiple Linear Regression

One feature is a starting point, not a destination. Real house prices depend on square footage, number of bedrooms, age of the house, distance to downtown, school district rating, and dozens more. Multiple linear regression extends the model to handle all of them at once: ŷ = w₀ + w₁x₁ + w₂x₂ + ... + wₚxₚ.

Matrix Formulation: The Normal Equation

Writing out the sum for every coefficient is tedious. Matrix notation compresses everything. Stack your data into a matrix X (n rows × p+1 columns, with a column of ones for the intercept), your targets into a vector y, and your coefficients into a vector w. The loss function becomes:

Loss = (y - Xw)ᵀ(y - Xw)

Take the derivative with respect to w, set it to zero, and you get the normal equation:

w = (XᵀX)⁻¹ Xᵀy

This is elegant and exact. No iterations, no learning rate. But there's a catch: you need to invert the matrix XᵀX, which is (p+1) × (p+1). When p is small (a few dozen features), this is instant. When p is in the millions — think text data with a bag-of-words representation — the matrix doesn't even fit in memory, let alone invert. That's when you switch to gradient descent.

Gradient Descent Alternative

Gradient descent is the iterative alternative. Instead of solving for the exact answer in one step, you start with random weights and repeatedly nudge them in the direction that reduces the loss. The update rule:

w ← w - α · ∇Loss(w)

where ∇Loss(w) = -2Xᵀ(y - Xw)  (the gradient)
      α = learning rate (step size)

If α is too large, you overshoot and the loss oscillates or explodes. Too small, and you crawl toward the answer over thousands of iterations. In practice, variants like stochastic gradient descent (SGD) compute the gradient on small random batches of data, making each step cheap and allowing the algorithm to scale to datasets with billions of rows.

import numpy as np
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression, SGDRegressor
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

# Multiple features: sqft, bedrooms, age
X = np.array([
    [800,  2, 30], [1000, 3, 25], [1200, 3, 20],
    [1500, 4, 15], [1800, 4, 10], [2000, 5,  5],
    [2500, 5,  2]
])
y = np.array([150, 185, 210, 260, 300, 340, 410])

# Approach 1: Normal equation (exact)
model_exact = LinearRegression()
model_exact.fit(X, y)
print("Normal equation coefficients:", model_exact.coef_)

# Approach 2: Gradient descent (iterative)
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X)

model_sgd = SGDRegressor(max_iter=1000, tol=1e-4, random_state=42)
model_sgd.fit(X_scaled, y)
print("SGD coefficients (scaled):", model_sgd.coef_)

Multicollinearity and VIF

What happens when two features are highly correlated? Say you include both "square footage" and "number of rooms." Big houses have more rooms. These two variables carry overlapping information, and the model can't tell how to split credit between them. The coefficients become unstable — add one more data point and the coefficient for sqft might flip sign while the one for rooms doubles. This is multicollinearity.

The Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) quantifies the damage. For each feature, regress it against all other features. If the R² from that regression is high, the feature is heavily predicted by the others and its VIF is large. Rule of thumb: VIF above 10 signals trouble. VIF above 5 deserves a careful look.

VIF(j) = 1 / (1 - R²_j)

Fixes: drop one of the correlated features, combine them (square footage per room), or use regularization, which handles collinearity gracefully.

Feature Selection

Not all features help. Some are noise, some are redundant. Three classical strategies exist. Forward selection starts with zero features and adds the one that improves the model the most, repeating until no addition helps. Backward elimination starts with all features and removes the least useful one at each step. Stepwise selection combines both, adding and removing features at each iteration. These methods are greedy — they don't guarantee the globally best subset — but they're practical when you have dozens of candidate features.

Interaction Terms and Categorical Encoding

Sometimes the effect of one feature depends on another. The price premium per square foot might be higher in good school districts than bad ones. An interaction term captures this: include sqft × school_rating as an additional feature. The model then has a coefficient for sqft, a coefficient for school_rating, and a coefficient for their product. The product term lets the slope for sqft change based on the value of school_rating.

What about non-numeric features like neighborhood? You can't multiply "Downtown" by a coefficient. Dummy variable encoding (one-hot encoding) converts a categorical variable with k categories into k−1 binary columns. If neighborhood has three values (Downtown, Suburbs, Rural), you create two columns: is_downtown and is_suburbs. Rural is represented when both are zero. You drop one category to avoid the dummy variable trap — perfect multicollinearity with the intercept.

Polynomial Regression

What if the relationship isn't a straight line? Fuel efficiency vs. engine displacement follows a curve. The marginal cost of additional server capacity is nonlinear. Polynomial regression handles this by adding powers of x as features: ŷ = w₀ + w₁x + w₂x² + w₃x³ + ...

This is still "linear" regression. That sounds contradictory, but "linear" refers to the model being linear in its parameters (the w's), not in x. Each wᵢ appears once, multiplied by a known function of x. You're fitting a curve through the data, but the math is the same least-squares machinery you already know.

Degree Selection and Overfitting

A degree-1 polynomial is a line. Degree-2 adds a parabola. Degree-10 can wiggle through almost any scatter plot. And that's the danger. A degree-20 polynomial can pass through every single training point with zero error, but its predictions between those points will be wildly wrong. It memorizes the training data rather than learning the underlying pattern. This is overfitting.

The antidote is cross-validation. Split your data into, say, 5 folds. For each candidate degree, train on 4 folds and evaluate on the held-out fold. Repeat, rotating the held-out fold. Average the errors. The degree with the lowest average validation error is the winner. Typically, that's degree 2 or 3. If cross-validation picks degree 15, something has gone wrong — you probably need more data or a different model class entirely.

Basis Function Expansion

Polynomial features are a special case of basis functions. The idea: transform the original features using any set of functions — polynomials, logarithms, sine waves, radial basis functions — and then run linear regression on the transformed features. The model is still linear in the transformed space, so all the theory still applies. This gives you enormous modeling flexibility while keeping the optimization straightforward.

from sklearn.preprocessing import PolynomialFeatures
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
from sklearn.pipeline import make_pipeline
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score
import numpy as np

np.random.seed(42)
X = np.sort(np.random.uniform(0, 10, 50)).reshape(-1, 1)
y = 3 + 2*X.ravel() - 0.3*X.ravel()**2 + np.random.normal(0, 2, 50)

# Compare polynomial degrees using cross-validation
for degree in [1, 2, 3, 5, 10]:
    pipe = make_pipeline(PolynomialFeatures(degree), LinearRegression())
    scores = cross_val_score(pipe, X, y, cv=5, scoring='neg_mean_squared_error')
    rmse = np.sqrt(-scores.mean())
    print(f"Degree {degree:2d}: CV RMSE = {rmse:.2f}")

Regularization: Keeping Coefficients Under Control

Here's a recurring problem. You add more features — interaction terms, polynomial terms, one-hot encoded categories — and the training error drops. But the test error rises. The model is fitting noise. The coefficients blow up to enormous values, positive and negative, canceling each other in elaborate ways that work on the training set and nowhere else.

Regularization fixes this by adding a penalty to the loss function that discourages large coefficients. You're telling the model: "fit the data, but keep the weights small." The tradeoff between fitting the data and keeping weights small is controlled by a hyperparameter, typically called λ (lambda) or α (alpha, depending on the library).

Ridge Regression (L2)

Ridge regression adds the sum of squared coefficients to the loss:

Loss_ridge = Σ(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)² + λ Σⱼ wⱼ²

The λΣwⱼ² term is the L2 penalty. When λ = 0, you get ordinary least squares. As λ grows, the model shrinks all coefficients toward zero — but never exactly to zero. Every feature stays in the model with a small, dampened coefficient. Ridge still has a closed-form solution:

w_ridge = (XᵀX + λI)⁻¹ Xᵀy

Notice the λI term. Adding it to XᵀX before inverting makes the matrix always invertible, even when features are perfectly collinear. This is why ridge regression handles multicollinearity so well — it's literally adding stability to the matrix inversion.

From a Bayesian perspective, ridge regression is equivalent to placing a Gaussian (normal) prior on the coefficients centered at zero. You're encoding the belief that coefficients are probably small, and the data has to provide evidence to pull them away from zero.

Lasso Regression (L1)

Lasso (Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator) uses the sum of absolute values instead:

Loss_lasso = Σ(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)² + λ Σⱼ |wⱼ|

This seemingly minor change — absolute values instead of squares — has a dramatic consequence: lasso drives some coefficients to exactly zero. Not close to zero. Exactly zero. It performs automatic feature selection. If you have 100 features and only 8 matter, lasso will zero out the other 92 if λ is large enough.

Why the difference? Geometrically, the L1 constraint region is a diamond (in 2D) with corners on the axes. The L2 constraint region is a circle. When the elliptical contours of the least-squares loss touch the diamond, they tend to hit a corner — where one coordinate is zero. When they touch the circle, they hit a smooth point where both coordinates are nonzero.

There's no closed-form solution for lasso. It's solved by coordinate descent: optimize one coefficient at a time while holding the others fixed, cycling through all coefficients repeatedly until convergence. The LARS (Least Angle Regression) algorithm computes the entire path of solutions for all values of λ efficiently.

ElasticNet: The Best of Both

ElasticNet combines L1 and L2 penalties:

Loss_elastic = Σ(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)² + λ₁ Σ|wⱼ| + λ₂ Σwⱼ²

Or equivalently, using a mixing parameter ρ (called l1_ratio in sklearn):

Loss_elastic = Σ(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)² + λ [ρ Σ|wⱼ| + (1-ρ) Σwⱼ²]

When ρ = 1, you get lasso. When ρ = 0, you get ridge. Values in between give you sparsity (some zeros) plus stability (correlated features share the coefficient rather than one randomly winning). ElasticNet is particularly useful when you have groups of correlated features — lasso arbitrarily picks one from the group and zeros the rest, while ElasticNet keeps the group together.

Concrete Comparison: Ridge vs Lasso vs ElasticNet

import numpy as np
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression, Ridge, Lasso, ElasticNet

np.random.seed(42)
n = 100

# 3 real features, 7 noise features
X_real = np.random.randn(n, 3)
noise_features = np.random.randn(n, 7)
X = np.hstack([X_real, noise_features])

# True relationship: y = 3*x1 + 1.5*x2 + 0.5*x3 + noise
y = 3*X[:, 0] + 1.5*X[:, 1] + 0.5*X[:, 2] + np.random.randn(n) * 0.5

true_coefs = [3.0, 1.5, 0.5, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0]

print("Feature:     ", [f"x{i}" for i in range(10)])
print(f"{'True:':<13}", [f"{c:.1f}" for c in true_coefs])

for name, model in [
    ("OLS",        LinearRegression()),
    ("Ridge",      Ridge(alpha=1.0)),
    ("Lasso",      Lasso(alpha=0.1)),
    ("ElasticNet", ElasticNet(alpha=0.1, l1_ratio=0.5)),
]:
    model.fit(X, y)
    coefs = model.coef_
    zeros = sum(1 for c in coefs if abs(c) < 0.01)
    print(f"{name:<13}", [f"{c:.1f}" for c in coefs], f"  ({zeros} zeros)")

Run this and you'll see: OLS assigns random-looking nonzero coefficients to the noise features. Ridge shrinks everything but zeros nothing. Lasso pushes 6–7 noise coefficients to exact zero. ElasticNet zeros most noise features while keeping the real ones slightly more stable than lasso. That's the tradeoff in action.

The Math Under the Hood

Linear Algebra You Need

The normal equation w = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy requires you to understand four things: matrix multiplication (XᵀX is a p×p matrix), matrix transpose (Xᵀ flips rows and columns), matrix inverse (the ⁻¹ — only exists if XᵀX has full rank, meaning no feature is a perfect linear combination of others), and positive definiteness (XᵀX is positive semi-definite, which guarantees the loss surface is a bowl with a single minimum). If any of these terms are new, Chapter 2 covers them in detail.

Calculus: Derivatives of the Loss

To derive the normal equation, you take the gradient (vector of partial derivatives) of the loss with respect to w:

∇_w Loss = ∇_w (y - Xw)ᵀ(y - Xw)
         = -2Xᵀ(y - Xw)
         = -2Xᵀy + 2XᵀXw

Set this to zero: 2XᵀXw = 2Xᵀy, so XᵀXw = Xᵀy, and if XᵀX is invertible, w = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy. This is the entire derivation. If you're comfortable with matrix calculus, it takes three lines. If you're not, just know that we differentiated a quadratic function and set the result to zero — the same thing you do in single-variable calculus to find the bottom of a parabola.

Optimization: Why Convexity Matters

The squared-error loss is a convex function of w. Geometrically, it's a bowl. There's exactly one bottom (the global minimum), and no matter where you start, gradient descent will roll downhill to that bottom. This is why linear regression is so well-behaved compared to neural networks, which have non-convex loss surfaces riddled with local minima. Convexity is the reason OLS has a closed-form solution and the reason gradient descent converges reliably for this problem.

Geometric Interpretation: Projection onto Column Space

There's a beautiful geometric way to understand what linear regression does. The columns of X define a subspace of ℝⁿ (the column space). Your target vector y probably doesn't live in that subspace — there's no perfect linear combination of features that reproduces y exactly. The best you can do is find the point in the column space that's closest to y. That closest point is the orthogonal projection of y onto the column space. The predicted values ŷ = Xw are that projection, and the residual vector e = y − ŷ is perpendicular to the column space.

The hat matrix H = X(XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀ is the projection matrix. It maps y to its projection: ŷ = Hy. The diagonal entries hᵢᵢ of H are the leverage of each data point — how much influence that point has on the fitted line. A point with high leverage is far from the center of the data in feature space and can pull the regression line toward it disproportionately.

Weighted and Generalized Least Squares

What if you know some observations are more reliable than others? Say you're fitting a model to sensor data, and some sensors have higher precision. Weighted Least Squares (WLS) assigns a weight wᵢ to each observation:

Loss_wls = Σ wᵢ(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)²

High-precision observations get larger weights and contribute more to the fit. In matrix form: w = (XᵀWX)⁻¹XᵀWy, where W is a diagonal matrix of weights. WLS is also the standard fix for heteroscedasticity: if the variance of eᵢ is proportional to some known function of x, you set the weights to the inverse of that variance.

Generalized Least Squares (GLS) extends this to the case where the errors are correlated with each other — not just different variances, but non-zero covariances. The error covariance matrix Ω replaces the diagonal W: w = (XᵀΩ⁻¹X)⁻¹XᵀΩ⁻¹y. GLS is common in time series and panel data, where errors at adjacent time points tend to be correlated.

Influence Diagnostics: Finding Problematic Points

Not all data points are created equal. Some sit quietly in the middle of the cloud. Others sit out on an edge, tugging the line toward them. You need tools to identify the influential ones.

Leverage (hᵢᵢ, the diagonal of the hat matrix) measures how unusual a point's x-values are. A point with extreme feature values has high leverage. It can influence the fit — but it might not if its y-value is consistent with the rest of the data.

Cook's Distance measures the actual influence: how much do all the predicted values change when you remove point i? It combines leverage and residual size into one number. A common threshold: Cook's D > 4/n flags a point as influential. Points with high Cook's distance deserve manual inspection. Sometimes they're data entry errors. Sometimes they're genuinely unusual cases that reveal important patterns. Don't delete them reflexively — investigate first.

# Quick influence check with statsmodels
import statsmodels.api as sm

The Gauss-Markov Theorem: Why OLS is Special

With all these estimation methods — OLS, WLS, ridge, lasso — why does ordinary least squares get special treatment? The answer is the Gauss-Markov theorem, and it says something specific and powerful.

BLUE stands for Best Linear Unbiased Estimator. Let's unpack each word:

Estimator: a formula that takes in data and produces a guess for the coefficients. OLS is one estimator. The sample mean is another. You could also estimate by throwing darts at a wall — that's an estimator too, just a terrible one.

Linear: the estimator is a linear function of the observed y values. OLS qualifies: w = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy, which is a matrix times y — a linear transformation. Ridge also qualifies. A neural network does not.

Unbiased: on average, across many possible datasets drawn from the same process, the estimator gives you the true coefficients. If the true slope is 3.0, an unbiased estimator doesn't systematically overestimate or underestimate it. OLS is unbiased. Ridge is biased — it deliberately shrinks coefficients toward zero, so it systematically underestimates the true values. That's the price of reduced variance.

Best: among all linear unbiased estimators, OLS has the smallest variance. No other linear unbiased estimator can do better. If you invent a clever new linear unbiased formula, the Gauss-Markov theorem guarantees its variance is at least as large as OLS.

The theorem holds under the LINE assumptions (linearity, independence, normality isn't required for Gauss-Markov — only equal variance and zero-mean errors). When those assumptions break, OLS loses its crown. Ridge or lasso might have higher bias but lower total error (bias² + variance), which is what you actually care about in prediction.

This is the fundamental tradeoff in regression: OLS is optimal in a narrow, theoretical sense. But in practice, a little bias often buys you a lot of variance reduction, and regularized models win on test data even though Gauss-Markov says OLS is "best." The theorem's conditions matter. When they don't hold, its guarantees vanish.

Putting It All Together

from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression, Ridge, Lasso, ElasticNet
from sklearn.preprocessing import PolynomialFeatures, StandardScaler
from sklearn.pipeline import make_pipeline
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score
from sklearn.datasets import make_regression
import numpy as np

# Generate realistic data: 200 samples, 20 features, only 5 informative
X, y = make_regression(
    n_samples=200, n_features=20, n_informative=5,
    noise=10, random_state=42
)

models = {
    "OLS":        LinearRegression(),
    "Ridge":      Ridge(alpha=1.0),
    "Lasso":      Lasso(alpha=0.5),
    "ElasticNet": ElasticNet(alpha=0.5, l1_ratio=0.5),
    "Poly2+Ridge": make_pipeline(
        PolynomialFeatures(degree=2, interaction_only=True),
        StandardScaler(),
        Ridge(alpha=10.0)
    ),
}

print(f"{'Model':<15} {'CV R² (mean ± std)'}")
print("-" * 40)
for name, model in models.items():
    scores = cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=5, scoring='r2')
    print(f"{name:<15} {scores.mean():.3f} ± {scores.std():.3f}")

This pipeline is what real projects look like. Generate or load data. Try multiple models. Compare with cross-validation. Pick the one that generalizes best. Linear regression isn't glamorous, but it's the foundation. Every concept here — loss functions, optimization, regularization, bias-variance tradeoff, cross-validation — reappears in every model you'll learn next. Understand these deeply, and the rest of supervised learning is variations on the theme.
