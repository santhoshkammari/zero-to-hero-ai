Time Series Forecasting

Chapter 5: Supervised Learning 5 subtopics
TL;DR

Time series forecasting predicts future values from past observations ordered in time. You decompose a series into trend, seasonality, and noise, then pick a model that captures those patterns. Classical ARIMA models work surprisingly well for stationary series with clear autocorrelation. Prophet handles messy business data with holidays and changepoints. Gradient-boosted trees with engineered lag features often win in practice. Neural approaches (LSTM, Transformers) shine when you have massive data and complex multi-step horizons. The key trap is data leakage — you must always validate with walk-forward splits that respect the arrow of time.

Time Series Fundamentals

Why Time Series Is Different

You need to predict next month's sales — what do you do? Your first instinct might be to grab the sales data, throw it into XGBoost, and call it a day. But time series data breaks rules that regular supervised learning takes for granted. The observations are not independent. January's sales depend on December's. Last summer's pattern will repeat this summer. There's an order to the data, and if you ignore that order, you'll build a model that looks brilliant on paper and fails catastrophically in production.

A time series is a sequence of data points indexed by time. Stock prices, website traffic, sensor readings, monthly revenue — anything measured repeatedly over time. What makes it special is that nearby points are correlated. Today's temperature tells you something about tomorrow's. That temporal dependence is both the challenge and the opportunity.

Components: Trend, Seasonality, Cyclicity, and Noise

Every time series can be mentally decomposed into a few building blocks. Trend is the long-term direction — are sales going up, down, or staying flat over years? Seasonality is a repeating pattern with a known, fixed period — ice cream sales peak every summer, retail spikes every December. Cyclicity is similar to seasonality but without a fixed period — business cycles expand and contract over years, but the length isn't predictable like calendar seasons. And noise (also called the residual or irregular component) is everything left over — random fluctuations that no model can predict.

How these components combine matters. In an additive decomposition, you assume the series equals trend + seasonality + noise. This works when the seasonal swings stay roughly the same size regardless of the level. In a multiplicative decomposition, the series equals trend × seasonality × noise. This is more appropriate when seasonal swings grow proportionally — like retail sales where a 20% December bump means bigger absolute numbers as the company grows.

Stationarity: The Foundation

Stationarity means the statistical properties of a series — its mean, variance, and autocorrelation — don't change over time. This sounds like a textbook abstraction, but it's deeply practical. Most classical forecasting models (ARIMA, for instance) assume stationarity. If you feed them a non-stationary series, they'll produce garbage.

Think of it this way: if a series is stationary, patterns you learned from the past are still valid in the future. If it's non-stationary — say, it has an upward trend — then the past mean is lower than the future mean, and a model trained on historical averages will systematically underpredict.

Two formal tests help you check. The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test has a null hypothesis that the series has a unit root (non-stationary). A small p-value (below 0.05) means you can reject that null and conclude stationarity. The KPSS test flips the logic — its null hypothesis is that the series is stationary. A small p-value means non-stationarity. Using both tests together gives you more confidence, because they can catch cases the other misses.

When a series isn't stationary, differencing is the standard fix. You replace each value with the difference from the previous value: y'(t) = y(t) − y(t−1). This removes a linear trend. If one round isn't enough (the differenced series is still non-stationary), you difference again — second-order differencing. For multiplicative trends, a log transform before differencing often helps. You can also detrend by fitting and subtracting a polynomial, but differencing is simpler and integrates naturally into ARIMA.

ACF, PACF, and Seasonal Decomposition

The Autocorrelation Function (ACF) measures how correlated a series is with its own lagged versions. ACF at lag 1 tells you the correlation between y(t) and y(t−1). ACF at lag 12 tells you the correlation with the value 12 steps back. If you see a big spike at lag 12 in monthly data, that's a strong seasonal signal.

The Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF) is like ACF but with the intermediate lags controlled for. PACF at lag 3 measures the correlation between y(t) and y(t−3) after removing the effects of lags 1 and 2. This distinction is crucial for choosing ARIMA parameters — we'll get to that shortly.

STL decomposition (Seasonal and Trend decomposition using Loess) is a robust method that separates a series into trend, seasonal, and residual components using locally weighted regression. Unlike simple moving-average decomposition, STL handles varying seasonal amplitude and is resistant to outliers. It's the go-to first step when you want to visually understand what's driving a series.

Classical Statistical Models

ARIMA: The Workhorse

ARIMA stands for AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average, and those three words describe exactly what it does. The AR (AutoRegressive) component says: the current value depends on the previous p values. Think of it as a linear regression where the "features" are past values of the series itself. The I (Integrated) component handles non-stationarity through differencing — the d parameter says how many times you difference. The MA (Moving Average) component says: the current value depends on the previous q forecast errors. It captures short-term shocks that linger.

An ARIMA(p, d, q) model combines all three. ARIMA(1, 1, 1) means: difference once, use one autoregressive lag, and one moving-average lag. The parameters p and q are chosen by looking at ACF and PACF plots of the differenced series. A sharp cutoff in PACF after lag p suggests an AR(p) model. A sharp cutoff in ACF after lag q suggests an MA(q) model. In practice, you also compare models using AIC (Akaike Information Criterion) or BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion) — lower is better. These penalize model complexity, helping you avoid overfitting.

After fitting, residual diagnostics tell you if the model captured the signal. The residuals should look like white noise — no autocorrelation, roughly normal distribution. The Ljung-Box test checks for remaining autocorrelation in the residuals. If the p-value is large (above 0.05), your residuals are clean and the model is adequate.

Here's the complete ARIMA workflow in Python:

import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller
from statsmodels.tsa.arima.model import ARIMA
from statsmodels.graphics.tsaplots import plot_acf, plot_pacf
from statsmodels.stats.diagnostic import acorr_ljungbox
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Load your time series data
# e.g., monthly airline passengers
data = pd.read_csv("airline_passengers.csv",
                    parse_dates=["Month"], index_col="Month")
y = data["Passengers"]

# Step 1: Check stationarity
result = adfuller(y)
print(f"ADF Statistic: {result[0]:.4f}")
print(f"p-value: {result[1]:.4f}")
# p-value > 0.05 → non-stationary, need differencing

# Step 2: Difference and re-test
y_diff = y.diff().dropna()
result2 = adfuller(y_diff)
print(f"After differencing — p-value: {result2[1]:.4f}")
# Now p-value < 0.05 → stationary, so d=1

# Step 3: Examine ACF/PACF to choose p, q
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 4))
plot_acf(y_diff, ax=axes[0], lags=20)
plot_pacf(y_diff, ax=axes[1], lags=20)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
# PACF cuts off after lag 2 → p=2
# ACF cuts off after lag 2 → q=2

# Step 4: Fit ARIMA(2,1,2)
model = ARIMA(y, order=(2, 1, 2))
fitted = model.fit()
print(fitted.summary())

# Step 5: Residual diagnostics
residuals = fitted.resid
lb_test = acorr_ljungbox(residuals, lags=[10, 20], return_df=True)
print(lb_test)
# p-values > 0.05 → residuals are white noise ✓

# Step 6: Forecast
forecast = fitted.get_forecast(steps=12)
pred = forecast.predicted_mean
ci = forecast.conf_int()
print(pred)

SARIMA: Adding Seasonal Structure

SARIMA extends ARIMA with seasonal parameters (P, D, Q, m), where m is the seasonal period (12 for monthly data with yearly seasonality, 7 for daily data with weekly seasonality, 4 for quarterly data). The seasonal AR, differencing, and MA operate at the seasonal lag — instead of looking at y(t−1), the seasonal AR looks at y(t−m). A SARIMA(1,1,1)(1,1,1,12) model handles both short-term autocorrelation and the yearly pattern simultaneously.

How do you know you need the seasonal component? Look at your ACF plot. If you see significant spikes at seasonal lags (12, 24, 36, ... for monthly data), SARIMA is telling you it wants seasonal terms. In statsmodels, the implementation is straightforward — you use SARIMAX with both the order and seasonal_order parameters:

from statsmodels.tsa.statespace.sarimax import SARIMAX

# SARIMA(1,1,1)(1,1,1,12) for monthly data with yearly seasonality
model = SARIMAX(y, order=(1, 1, 1), seasonal_order=(1, 1, 1, 12))
fitted = model.fit(disp=False)
print(fitted.summary())

# Forecast next 24 months
forecast = fitted.get_forecast(steps=24)
print(forecast.predicted_mean)

For automatic parameter selection, the pmdarima library provides auto_arima, which searches over (p, d, q) and (P, D, Q, m) combinations using AIC or BIC — essentially a grid search with some smart shortcuts. It's the closest thing to "just fit the best ARIMA for me" that exists in Python.

ARIMAX and SARIMAX: Adding External Variables

Sometimes the future depends on more than just the past values of the series itself. Maybe sales depend on advertising spend, or energy consumption depends on temperature. SARIMAX (the statsmodels implementation) lets you add exogenous variables — external covariates that help explain the series. The model is the same ARIMA/SARIMA core, but with extra regression terms. The catch: you need future values of those covariates at prediction time. If your exogenous variable is "temperature next month," you'd better have a weather forecast.

Exponential Smoothing: The Other Classical Family

While ARIMA models the autocorrelation structure, exponential smoothing takes a different philosophy: give more weight to recent observations and let the weight decay exponentially into the past. Simple Exponential Smoothing (SES) handles series with no trend and no seasonality — just a slowly drifting level. It has one parameter α (the smoothing factor) that controls how quickly old observations are forgotten.

Holt's method (double exponential smoothing) adds a trend component with its own smoothing parameter β. Holt-Winters (triple exponential smoothing) adds seasonality with parameter γ. You choose additive or multiplicative seasonality based on whether the seasonal swings are constant or proportional to the level.

The ETS framework (Error, Trend, Seasonality) unifies all exponential smoothing variants into a state-space model. Each component can be None, Additive, or Multiplicative, giving you 30 possible model specifications. The ETS framework also provides proper prediction intervals and model selection via AIC — something the original Holt-Winters formulation lacked. In many benchmarks, ETS is competitive with or better than ARIMA for purely univariate forecasting.

Modern Forecasting Methods

Prophet: Forecasting for Analysts

Prophet, developed at Meta (Facebook), is designed for business time series that have strong seasonality and several seasons of historical data. Under the hood, it's an additive regression model: y(t) = g(t) + s(t) + h(t) + ε(t), where g is a trend function, s is seasonality, h captures holiday effects, and ε is noise.

The trend can be piecewise linear or logistic (with a saturation cap). Changepoints are times when the trend slope changes — Prophet automatically detects these and places them in the first 80% of the data. You can adjust the changepoint_prior_scale parameter to make the trend more or less flexible. Seasonality is modeled using Fourier series — a sum of sine and cosine terms that approximate any periodic pattern. Yearly seasonality uses 10 Fourier terms by default; weekly uses 3. You can add custom seasonalities (e.g., monthly) with whatever order you like.

One killer feature is holiday and special event handling. You pass a dataframe of dates (Black Friday, Super Bowl, company-specific events) and Prophet adds indicator variables for each. This is exactly the kind of thing that trips up pure ARIMA models.

from prophet import Prophet
import pandas as pd

# Prophet wants columns named 'ds' (datestamp) and 'y' (value)
df = pd.read_csv("sales.csv")
df.columns = ["ds", "y"]

# Define holidays / special events
holidays = pd.DataFrame({
    "holiday": "black_friday",
    "ds": pd.to_datetime(["2021-11-26", "2022-11-25", "2023-11-24"]),
    "lower_window": -1,   # day before also affected
    "upper_window": 1     # day after also affected
})

# Fit the model
model = Prophet(
    yearly_seasonality=True,
    weekly_seasonality=True,
    holidays=holidays,
    changepoint_prior_scale=0.05  # less flexible trend
)
model.fit(df)

# Make a future dataframe and predict
future = model.make_future_dataframe(periods=90)  # 90 days ahead
forecast = model.predict(future)

# Visualize
model.plot(forecast)
model.plot_components(forecast)

Prophet's honest tradeoffs: it's excellent for daily/weekly business data with holidays. It struggles with high-frequency data (minute-level), very short series, or series where the dynamics change rapidly. It's also a univariate model — it can't natively incorporate correlated external regressors the way SARIMAX can (though you can add regressors manually).

ML for Time Series: Feature Engineering Is Everything

Here's the key insight: you can reframe any time series problem as a supervised learning problem. Instead of feeding raw timestamps into a model, you engineer features that capture temporal patterns, then use any tabular ML model — XGBoost, LightGBM, random forests.

The most important features are lag features. To predict sales at time t, use sales at t−1, t−2, ..., t−k as input features. Rolling statistics — the mean, standard deviation, min, and max over a rolling window — capture recent trends and volatility. Calendar features (day of week, month, quarter, is_holiday) capture seasonality without Fourier terms. You can also add external features like weather, marketing spend, or competitor pricing.

import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from xgboost import XGBRegressor
from sklearn.metrics import mean_absolute_error

df = pd.read_csv("daily_sales.csv", parse_dates=["date"])
df = df.sort_values("date").reset_index(drop=True)

# Feature engineering
for lag in [1, 7, 14, 28]:
    df[f"lag_{lag}"] = df["sales"].shift(lag)

for window in [7, 14, 28]:
    df[f"rolling_mean_{window}"] = (
        df["sales"].shift(1).rolling(window).mean()
    )
    df[f"rolling_std_{window}"] = (
        df["sales"].shift(1).rolling(window).std()
    )

# Calendar features
df["dayofweek"] = df["date"].dt.dayofweek
df["month"] = df["date"].dt.month
df["quarter"] = df["date"].dt.quarter
df["is_weekend"] = (df["dayofweek"] >= 5).astype(int)

# Drop rows with NaN from lagging
df = df.dropna()

# CRITICAL: time-based split, NOT random split
train = df[df["date"] < "2023-01-01"]
test = df[df["date"] >= "2023-01-01"]

feature_cols = [c for c in df.columns if c not in ["date", "sales"]]
X_train, y_train = train[feature_cols], train["sales"]
X_test, y_test = test[feature_cols], test["sales"]

model = XGBRegressor(
    n_estimators=500,
    max_depth=6,
    learning_rate=0.05,
    subsample=0.8,
    colsample_bytree=0.8,
    early_stopping_rounds=50
)
model.fit(X_train, y_train,
          eval_set=[(X_test, y_test)],
          verbose=50)

preds = model.predict(X_test)
print(f"MAE: {mean_absolute_error(y_test, preds):.2f}")

Data leakage is the single biggest risk in time-series ML. Every feature must use only information available before the prediction time. If your rolling mean at time t includes the value at time t (instead of t−1), you've leaked future information into your features. The model will look amazing during development and fail in production. Always use .shift(1) before any rolling computation. Never use random train/test splits — always split by time.

Neural Approaches

LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) and GRU (Gated Recurrent Unit) are recurrent neural networks designed to handle sequences. They maintain a hidden state that gets updated at each time step, allowing them to capture long-range dependencies. In practice, LSTMs work well when you have thousands of related time series (e.g., demand forecasting across 10,000 products) and can share learned patterns across series. For a single short series, they're usually overkill and underperform ARIMA.

Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN) use causal, dilated convolutions instead of recurrence. They process the entire sequence in parallel (faster training than LSTMs), handle very long histories through exponentially increasing dilation, and are easier to train. TCNs have become a strong baseline for sequence modeling.

Transformers for time series apply self-attention to capture dependencies at arbitrary lags without the vanishing gradient problem. Models like Informer and Autoformer introduce efficient attention mechanisms for long-horizon forecasting. However, the evidence is mixed — several benchmarks have shown that well-tuned linear models or simple MLPs can match or beat Transformer-based forecasters. The Transformer's strength is more evident in multivariate settings where cross-series attention adds value.

Foundation models for time series are the newest frontier. Models like TimesFM (Google), Chronos (Amazon), and Lag-Llama are pretrained on massive collections of time series and can produce zero-shot forecasts for new series without any task-specific training. They're the GPT moment for forecasting — impressive demos, but the practical value over a well-tuned XGBoost or ARIMA on your specific domain data is still being established. Watch this space closely.

Evaluation and Validation

Walk-Forward Validation: The Only Way

In regular ML, you do k-fold cross-validation with random splits. In time series, random splits are cheating — you'd be training on future data to predict the past. Walk-forward validation respects the arrow of time. You train on data up to time t, predict t+1 through t+h (your forecast horizon), then slide the training window forward and repeat.

Two flavors exist. Expanding window keeps all historical data in the training set — each fold trains on more data. Sliding window fixes the training window size and slides it forward — useful when old data becomes irrelevant (regime changes, concept drift). Expanding window is the default choice unless you have a reason to believe older data hurts.

Here's the concrete implementation. Say you have 5 years of monthly data and want a 3-month forecast horizon. In expanding-window cross-validation, fold 1 trains on months 1–24 and tests on months 25–27. Fold 2 trains on months 1–27 and tests on months 28–30. And so on. You average the test errors across all folds to get a robust estimate of future performance. In sliding-window CV with a 24-month training window, fold 1 trains on months 1–24 and tests on 25–27, but fold 2 trains on months 4–27 (dropping the oldest 3 months) and tests on 28–30. The choice between expanding and sliding depends on whether your data-generating process is stable or evolving.

Forecast Accuracy Metrics

MAE (Mean Absolute Error) gives you the average magnitude of errors in the same units as your data. Easy to interpret: "on average, we're off by 42 units." RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error) penalizes large errors more heavily because of the squaring. If occasional big misses are costly (e.g., inventory planning), RMSE is more appropriate.

MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error) expresses errors as percentages, making it scale-independent and easy to communicate to stakeholders: "we're off by 8% on average." But MAPE has a nasty flaw — it's undefined when actual values are zero and becomes asymmetric (over-predictions and under-predictions are penalized differently). SMAPE (Symmetric MAPE) fixes the asymmetry by dividing by the average of actual and predicted values, though it still struggles with near-zero values. No single metric is perfect. Report at least two — typically MAE or RMSE for model selection and MAPE or SMAPE for stakeholder communication.

Residual Diagnostics

Beyond aggregate metrics, always plot your residuals over time. Are there periods where the model consistently over- or under-predicts? That's a sign of unmodeled structure — maybe a missing seasonal component or a regime change. Check the residual ACF too. Significant autocorrelation in residuals means the model left predictable signal on the table.

Advanced Topics

Multivariate Time Series

Multivariate time series forecasting predicts multiple interrelated series simultaneously. Energy consumption across buildings, stock prices in a portfolio, sensor readings from a manufacturing line — these series influence each other. Vector Autoregression (VAR) extends AR to multiple series. Neural approaches (multi-output LSTMs, multivariate Transformers) can learn complex cross-series dependencies. The tradeoff: more modeling power, but also more parameters to estimate and more opportunities for overfitting.

Ensembling Time Series Models

Just as in classification and regression, combining multiple forecasters usually beats any single one. A simple average of ARIMA, Prophet, and XGBoost forecasts often outperforms the best individual model. Weighted ensembles (where weights are learned on a validation set) can do even better. The M-competition series — the Olympics of forecasting — has shown consistently that ensembles dominate the leaderboard.

Outliers and Missing Values

Outliers in time series can be point anomalies (a single weird value) or level shifts (a permanent change in the mean). STL decomposition helps detect them — large residuals flag potential outliers. Winsorizing (capping extremes) or replacing with interpolated values are common fixes, but always investigate why the outlier happened before removing it. It might be real signal — a COVID lockdown, a flash sale, a sensor failure.

Missing values are common in real-world time series. Simple options: forward-fill (carry the last known value), linear interpolation, or seasonal interpolation (use the value from the same season last year). More sophisticated: fit a model on the non-missing data and impute the gaps. Whatever you do, document it — downstream models can behave differently depending on your imputation strategy.

Transfer Learning for Time Series

Transfer learning in time series is less mature than in vision or NLP, but it's growing fast. The idea: pretrain a model on a large collection of diverse time series, then fine-tune on your specific domain. Foundation models (TimesFM, Chronos) are one approach. Another: pretrain an LSTM on thousands of demand series from a data marketplace, then fine-tune on your company's specific product line. The practical benefit is strongest when you have limited historical data for your specific series — the pretrained model brings general knowledge about temporal patterns, seasonality, and trend dynamics that would take years of your own data to learn.
