Simple look, big lesson. These two algorithms teach why machine learning works. Dig in.

What they are. k-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) predicts by finding training examples most similar to new point, then letting them vote. Naive Bayes predicts with probability theory, combining evidence from multiple features independently. Both old. Both fast. Both have crisp failure modes. Both save hours as baseline models on real problems. Both also teach distance, probability, independence, dimensionality — ideas everywhere in machine learning.

Before dive in. We build both algorithms from scratch with one running example: sorting fruit at farmers' market. Three fruit types — apples, oranges, lemons — each described by two measurements: weight in grams and color-redness score from 0 to 10. Same numbers stay with us across page. Math real. We work every calculation. By code section, nothing should feel mysterious.

Ask Your Neighbors

Picture booth at farmers' market. You sort fruit by hand — apples in one bin, oranges in another, lemons in third. Friend hands you unfamiliar fruit and asks what it is. You compare it to fruit already sorted. Find most similar ones. Check labels. If three most similar fruits are oranges, answer "orange." That is k-Nearest Neighbors.

Whole algorithm = neighborhood poll. To classify new point, find k training examples most similar to it, count labels, return majority class. For regression — predicting continuous number instead of category — average neighbors' values instead of voting. No traditional training phase. No weights. No optimization. Model memorizes full training set, works at prediction time. Computer scientists call this a lazy learner. Name fits.

Numbers now. Three fruits already sorted:

Fruit Weight (g) Redness (0–10) Label
Fruit A1828apple
Fruit B1306orange
Fruit C1152lemon

Mystery fruit arrives: weight 150 g, redness 7. Need label. With k=1, answer is whichever training fruit is closest. First need definition of "close."

What "Close" Means

Most natural closeness measure: straight-line distance from geometry — Euclidean distance. Between points (a₁, a₂) and (b₁, b₂), distance is hypotenuse length: sqrt((a₁ − b₁)² + (a₂ − b₂)²). Compute mystery-fruit distance to each known fruit.

Fruit A distance (apple): sqrt((182 − 150)² + (8 − 7)²) = sqrt(32² + 1²) = sqrt(1025) ≈ 32.0

Fruit B distance (orange): sqrt((130 − 150)² + (6 − 7)²) = sqrt(20² + 1²) = sqrt(401) ≈ 20.0

Fruit C distance (lemon): sqrt((115 − 150)² + (2 − 7)²) = sqrt(35² + 5²) = sqrt(1250) ≈ 35.4

Fruit B closest. With k=1, predict orange. Mystery fruit is most similar to orange in weight-redness space.

Another common option: Manhattan distance (L1 distance), named for city-grid travel where you cannot cut through buildings. Add absolute difference on each axis instead of taking hypotenuse: |a₁ − b₁| + |a₂ − b₂|. For mystery fruit, Manhattan distances are 33 (apple), 21 (orange), 40 (lemon) — same ranking as Euclidean. Both are Minkowski distance cases: Euclidean uses p=2, Manhattan uses p=1. Sklearn exposes this with p. Euclidean is usual default. Manhattan can be more robust to extreme outliers because squaring amplifies them, while Manhattan treats them linearly.

The Voronoi Map

With k=1, one geometric thing happens. Every training point "owns" region of feature space: every point in that region is closer to this training example than any other. On 2D plot, these territories form a Voronoi diagram. Decision boundary between two class regions is set of points equidistant from their training examples.

Voronoi diagram makes model visible. You can see prediction for every possible input in one picture. Drop mystery fruit into map. It lands in one territory. Territory label is prediction. Whole classifier, visible at once.

Catch: with k=1, boundaries are jagged. They snake around individual training points. If one apple was mislabeled "orange" during data collection, its Voronoi territory cuts small orange island inside apple region. Future points in that island get misclassified. Model memorized noise — high variance, high sensitivity to quirks of training set.

Turning the k Dial

Increase k. Boundaries smooth out. Each prediction averages over larger neighborhood, so one mislabeled point matters less. Boundaries stop chasing single training examples and start tracing broader, saner territories.

Can overdo this. When k equals total training examples, every prediction polls every point, so answer is always dataset majority class. No local structure left. Maximum bias. Zero variance. Useless except for predicting most common class.

Neighborhood-poll analogy helps here. With k=1, you knock on one door and trust answer. With k=100, you survey big neighborhood. One-person poll varies a lot — neighbor may be outlier. Hundred-person survey is stable, but may miss that you stand on boundary between very different neighborhoods. Both extremes fail. Need enough neighbors to smooth noise without erasing signal.

Useful refinement: weighted KNN. Closer neighbors get more influence, weighted by inverse distance. Neighbor at distance 2 gets weight 0.5. Neighbor at distance 0.1 gets weight 10. Class with highest total weight wins, not class with most votes. This softens hard cutoff from majority vote and often improves accuracy on real data. In sklearn, set weights='distance'. For k: try 3, 5, 7, 11, 15; choose with cross-validation; use odd values for binary classification to avoid ties.

Feature Scaling

Silent bug could wreck fruit classifier. Weight ranges about 100 to 250 grams. Redness ranges 0 to 10. Euclidean distance treats one-unit weight difference and one-unit redness difference as identical — but they are not. One gram is tiny. One point on ten-point redness scale is one-tenth of full range.

Actual numbers show it. Weight term contributed (32)² = 1024 to squared distance between mystery fruit and Fruit A. Redness term contributed (1)² = 1. Weight dominated by factor of thousand. Redness was functionally invisible. We did not build "nearest fruit" classifier. We built "nearest weight" classifier that accepts redness and ignores it.

Fix: standardization. Transform each feature to mean 0 and standard deviation 1 across training set. After scaling, one-unit shift in any feature means one standard deviation. Features contribute fairly to distance. Formula: (value − mean) / std, per feature. Not optional for distance-based methods. KNN, SVMs, PCA all need scaled features. Critical detail: fit scaler on training data only, then apply to test data. If you compute statistics on both sets, test information leaks into preprocessing. Sklearn's Pipeline handles this inside each cross-validation fold.

The Curse of Empty Rooms

Neighborhood poll works because nearby neighbors are similar. As feature count grows, that assumption breaks hard. Name: curse of dimensionality.

Picture nearest-neighbor search in studio apartment. Everyone close. Move to ten-room mansion, same number of people, scattered randomly. Still manageable. Now mansion has thousand rooms. Mostly empty. Finding nearest neighbor means searching most of house. Worst part: "nearest" neighbor may be almost as far as most distant one. "Near" stops meaning much.

Math makes it concrete. Suppose you want neighborhood large enough to contain 10% of training data. In 1D, cover 10% of range. In 2D, each axis must cover 0.10^(1/2) ≈ 32%. In 10D, each axis must cover 0.10^(1/10) ≈ 79%. By ten features, "local" neighborhood spans most of feature space. Locality assumption behind KNN mostly dissolves. In practice, KNN starts struggling around 20 features and is usually hopeless past 50 unless you apply dimensionality reduction — PCA, UMAP, feature selection — first.

Making It Fast

Every KNN prediction scans every training point to find k nearest ones. Cost: O(n × d) per prediction — n training examples, each with d features. Hundred training examples? Fine. Million? Every query needs million distance calculations.

Three speedup paths. Brute force: compute all distances, sort, return top k. Fine for small datasets or when d is large enough that spatial indexing gives little help. KD-trees: partition feature space recursively with axis-aligned splits — binary search for neighbors. Average query time drops to O(d log n), but degrades toward brute force in high dimensions because bounding boxes stop pruning enough branches above roughly 20 features. Ball trees: use hyperspheres instead of hyperrectangles, handle moderate dimensionality somewhat better, support non-Euclidean distance metrics. At very large scale, approximate nearest neighbor libraries like FAISS and HNSW trade small, tunable accuracy loss for huge speedups. Sklearn chooses among brute/KD/ball automatically with algorithm='auto'. For most learning-phase datasets, default is fine.

Code: KNN with Scaling

from sklearn.neighbors import KNeighborsClassifier
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline
import numpy as np

# Three fruits: [weight_g, redness_0_to_10]
X_train = np.array([[182, 8], [130, 6], [115, 2]])
y_train = ['apple', 'orange', 'lemon']

pipe = Pipeline([
    ('scaler', StandardScaler()),               # mandatory for KNN
    ('knn', KNeighborsClassifier(
        n_neighbors=1,
        weights='distance'))                    # closer neighbors count more
])

pipe.fit(X_train, y_train)

mystery = np.array([[150, 7]])
print(pipe.predict(mystery))                    # ['orange']

The Pipeline fits scaler on training data only. No peeking at whatever later goes into predict. On larger dataset, change n_neighbors and re-run to watch bias-variance tradeoff in accuracy. Compare weights='distance' and weights='uniform' on noisy data to see when distance weighting helps.

Counting Evidence Instead

Why KNN fails on text. One email might have 50,000 features — one per vocabulary word, with value equal to count. Nearest-neighbor search in 50,000-dimensional space fails. Curse of dimensionality eats it. Documents end up roughly equally distant. Neighborhood poll returns noise.

Need different idea. Stop asking "which training examples look most like this one?" Start asking "given what we observe here, which class is most probable?" That shift — similarity to probability — leads to Naive Bayes.

Bayes' Theorem from Scratch

Bayes' theorem tells you how to update belief from evidence. Version that matters for classification:

P(class | features) ∝ P(features | class) × P(class)

Read it aloud. Probability that example belongs to class, given observed features, is proportional to likelihood of those features if example really were that class, times how common that class is to begin with. Symbol ∝ means "proportional to." We drop denominator P(features) because it is identical for every class. It cannot change winner.

Walk through fruit example. Suppose market training data contains 40% apples, 35% oranges, 25% lemons. These are priors: P(apple) = 0.40, P(orange) = 0.35, P(lemon) = 0.25. Before any measurements, lean apple.

Now mystery fruit arrives: weight 150 g, redness 7. Need P(features | class) — likelihood of these measurements under each class. For continuous features, model with Gaussian bell curve. For each class, track mean and standard deviation of weight and redness across training examples. Suppose training apples average 180 g with standard deviation 15 g, and redness averages 8.0 with standard deviation 1.0. Weight 150 g sits two standard deviations below apple mean — low Gaussian density. Redness 7 sits one standard deviation below — moderate density. Multiply those per-feature likelihoods to get P(features | apple), then multiply by prior 0.40. Repeat for orange and lemon. Largest product wins. That is Gaussian Naive Bayes.

Full per-feature probability math for continuous features is tedious. Concept matters more. Multiply per-feature likelihoods by class prior. Pick winner. Code handles arithmetic.

The Naive Trick

Step where we multiplied per-feature likelihoods — P(weight | apple) × P(redness | apple) — is independence assumption. We treated weight and redness as if weight tells us nothing about redness once we know fruit is apple. That is "naive" part. Almost certainly false. Heavier apples may be redder. Features are correlated. Classifier often works anyway.

Reason is subtle. For classification, probability values do not need to be correct. Ranking needs to be correct. If true class gets higher score than others, we win — even if score is wildly overconfident. When each feature leans in right direction, multiplying those leans still points right way, even if magnitude is wrong.

Intuition still not perfect here. Best mental model: each feature is witness in courtroom. Witness A says weight suggests apple. Witness B says color suggests apple. We multiply testimony. Witnesses are not truly independent — they talked in hallway, stories share source. But if both lean apple, combined testimony still points apple. Verdict can be right even when stated confidence is inflated.

This also explains Naive Bayes' famously miscalibrated probabilities. Winning class often gets 0.9999 because model multiplied many moderate likelihoods, each slightly favoring that class. Do not treat these numbers as true confidence. Use them for ranking and top-class selection. High-level story explains part of why this works; exact reason it works so well on real data is still not fully settled.

Three Flavors

GaussianNB, MultinomialNB, and BernoulliNB differ in one thing: probability model for each feature. Match model to data type, results are good. Mismatch it, model runs but answers get weird.

Variant Assumes features are… Best for
GaussianNB Continuous, approximately normal Real-valued tabular data — temperatures, heights, scores
MultinomialNB Non-negative integer counts Text (word counts), document classification
BernoulliNB Binary presence/absence Short text, binary feature vectors
ComplementNB Non-negative counts (same as Multinomial) Imbalanced text datasets

GaussianNB estimates mean and variance per feature per class, then evaluates Gaussian density at observed value. No hyperparameters to tune. Often good enough even when features are not perfectly Gaussian. Fruit example above used this variant.

MultinomialNB models each class as distribution over feature counts. Classic text classifier: each feature is word, each value is count. Model learns which words appear often in which classes — "free," "offer," and "congratulations" cluster in spam; "meeting," "attached," and "quarterly" cluster in work email. Multiply enough word-frequency likelihoods, correct class wins. ComplementNB is variant that learns what is uncharacteristic of each class instead of characteristic. It often beats standard MultinomialNB on imbalanced datasets where one class dominates.

BernoulliNB cares only whether feature is present or absent, throwing away frequency. For text: "did this word appear at all?" not "how many times?" Key difference from MultinomialNB: Bernoulli explicitly penalizes absence of features common in class. If "money" appears in 80% of spam emails, non-spam email missing "money" gets likelihood boost under BernoulliNB. MultinomialNB ignores absent words. For short documents where presence matters more than frequency, BernoulliNB often wins.

The Zero-Probability Catastrophe

Failure mode can silently destroy Naive Bayes model. Suppose spam filter training data contains no legitimate (ham) emails with word "blockchain." So P("blockchain" | ham) = 0. Then legitimate email about cryptocurrency investment arrives and contains "blockchain." Ham-class product becomes zero — zero times anything is zero — no matter what every other word says. One unseen word poisons whole classification.

Fix: Laplace smoothing (add-one smoothing), controlled by alpha. Instead of raw counts, add alpha to every word count before computing probabilities:

P(word | class) = (count(word, class) + α) / (total_words_in_class + α × |vocabulary|)

With α = 1, every vocabulary word gets count at least 1 in every class, even if it never appeared in training. Denominator adjusts to keep probabilities normalized. Larger α smooths more aggressively, pushing word probabilities toward uniform and trusting training data less. Smaller α stays closer to raw counts. Default α = 1 works in almost every case. Sometimes slight cross-validation tuning helps on unusual datasets.

Surviving the Numbers

There's one more practical pitfall. When you multiply 50,000 probabilities together — one per word in the vocabulary — each might be something like 0.0002. Multiplying fifty thousand such numbers produces a result so close to zero that standard floating-point arithmetic rounds it to exactly 0.0. This is called numerical underflow, and it makes the classifier completely nonfunctional.

I'll be honest — the first time someone showed me the fix, I felt like I'd been doing math wrong my whole life. The solution is to work in log space. Instead of multiplying probabilities, take the logarithm of each and sum them:

log P(class | features) ∝ log P(class) + Σᵢ log P(xᵢ | class)

Since the logarithm is monotonically increasing, the class with the highest sum of log-probabilities is exactly the same class that would have had the highest product of probabilities. The ranking is preserved. But the numbers stay representable — log(0.0002) ≈ −8.5. Summing fifty thousand numbers around −8.5 gives you something around −425,000, a perfectly ordinary floating-point number. Sklearn handles this internally, so you don't need to write it yourself. But understanding it explains why predict_proba() returns sensible numbers even when the raw products would have underflowed to zero.

Text Classification: The Killer App

Combine MultinomialNB with TF-IDF vectorization and you have one of the most cost-effective classifiers ever built. TF-IDF — term frequency–inverse document frequency — transforms a document into a vector where each dimension is a word, weighted by how often it appears in this document relative to how often it appears across all documents. Common words like "the" and "is" get low weights. Distinctive, domain-specific words get high weights.

The courtroom analogy from the previous section comes back around here in a satisfying way. Each word is a witness. TF-IDF is the process of deciding how much credibility each witness deserves: a witness who shows up at every trial (common word) gets less weight than one who's distinctive to this specific case (rare word that strongly signals a class). Multiply the adjusted testimonies. Pick a verdict.

This combination is the classic spam filter. It's a strong baseline for sentiment analysis, topic labeling, language detection, and document routing. On small text datasets — hundreds to a few thousand documents — it frequently matches or outperforms neural networks and gradient-boosted trees. That's not because it's smarter; it's because with limited data, the simpler model generalizes better. The practical rule: always start a text classification project with TF-IDF plus MultinomialNB. If it gives you 88% accuracy in five minutes, you now have a baseline that tells you exactly how much improvement a more complex model needs to justify its training cost and deployment overhead.

from sklearn.naive_bayes import MultinomialNB
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer
from sklearn.pipeline import Pipeline
from sklearn.datasets import fetch_20newsgroups
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score

# Two categories to keep the example focused
cats = ['rec.sport.hockey', 'sci.space']
train = fetch_20newsgroups(subset='train', categories=cats)
test  = fetch_20newsgroups(subset='test',  categories=cats)

pipe = Pipeline([
    ('tfidf', TfidfVectorizer(stop_words='english', max_df=0.5)),
    ('nb',    MultinomialNB(alpha=1.0))     # alpha=1.0 is Laplace smoothing
])

pipe.fit(train.data, train.target)
preds = pipe.predict(test.data)
print(f"Accuracy: {accuracy_score(test.target, preds):.3f}")
# Accuracy: ~0.970 — two categories, under one second of training

Roughly 1,200 training documents, tens of thousands of features, classified in under a second. The model itself is a few kilobytes. Try matching that training time with a fine-tuned transformer.

When Simple Wins

Both KNN and Naive Bayes beat more complex models in specific, predictable conditions. Knowing when is part of being a good practitioner.

KNN wins when your dataset is small and your feature space is low-dimensional — say, under a few thousand examples and under twenty features. It wins when you need locally interpretable predictions: "this loan application is similar to these five applications that all defaulted" is a compelling story that a gradient-boosted tree can't easily tell. It wins as a quick baseline that requires almost no distributional assumptions, and it wins when decision boundaries are genuinely irregular in ways that no parametric model would assume.

Naive Bayes wins on text — reliably, repeatedly, cheaply. It wins on any high-dimensional sparse data. It wins on tiny datasets where a complex model would overfit badly, because estimating a mean and variance per feature per class requires very few data points per class. It wins when training time is constrained — a single pass through the data is all it needs. It wins on streaming data where you need to update the model one example at a time; Naive Bayes updates are trivially incremental. And it wins when you need a production model that's a few kilobytes rather than a few gigabytes.

Both algorithms win as baselines. Before you train any complex model on a new problem, train KNN and Naive Bayes first. They set the floor — any model that doesn't substantially beat them is not worth the added complexity, training time, or deployment cost. I've seen teams skip this step and spend weeks tuning a neural network to achieve accuracy that a properly set-up Naive Bayes matched in twenty seconds. Don't be those teams.
