Support Vector Machines (SVMs)

Chapter 5: Supervised Learning 7 subtopics ~20 min read
TL;DR

An SVM finds the widest possible road between two groups of data points. The points closest to that road — the support vectors — are the only ones that matter. When data isn't linearly separable, the kernel trick implicitly maps it to a higher-dimensional space where a straight separator exists, without ever computing the coordinates in that space. SVMs dominated ML from the mid-1990s to the early 2010s, but have since been overtaken by gradient boosting for tabular data and neural networks for images, text, and audio.

Linear SVM — The Widest Road Between Two Groups

Imagine you're standing in a parking lot. On your left, red cars. On your right, blue cars. You need to paint a dividing line on the asphalt so that every red car is on one side and every blue car is on the other. There are infinitely many lines you could paint. Which one is the best?

Intuition says: the best line is the one that leaves the most breathing room on both sides. If a new car pulls in slightly off-center, you want the line to still classify it correctly. You want the widest road between the two groups.

That's exactly what a Support Vector Machine does. It finds the maximum margin hyperplane — the decision boundary that maximizes the distance between itself and the nearest data points from each class. In two dimensions, this boundary is a line. In three dimensions, it's a plane. In higher dimensions, it's a hyperplane — but the idea is always the same: find the widest gap.

Margin and Support Vectors

The margin is the total width of that road — the distance from the decision boundary to the nearest data point on either side, doubled. A larger margin generally means better generalization to new data, because there's more room for noise before a point gets misclassified.

The support vectors are the data points that sit right on the edge of the margin. They're the critical ones. If you removed any other data point, the decision boundary wouldn't change at all. But move a support vector, and the whole boundary shifts. The SVM is literally "supported" by these vectors — hence the name.

Here's what this looks like mathematically. For a binary classification problem with labels y ∈ {-1, +1} and input vectors x, the decision boundary is defined by a weight vector w and a bias b:

Decision boundary:  w · x + b = 0

Positive margin:    w · x + b = +1    (support vectors from class +1)
Negative margin:    w · x + b = -1    (support vectors from class -1)

Margin width:       2 / ||w||

Goal: maximize 2/||w||  ⟹  minimize ||w||²/2

Primal and Dual Formulations

The primal formulation is the straightforward optimization: minimize ½||w||² subject to the constraint that every training point is on the correct side of the margin. In math, that's: y_i(w · x_i + b) ≥ 1 for all i.

This is a convex optimization problem — specifically, a quadratic objective with linear constraints. Convex means there's exactly one global minimum, no tricky local minima to get trapped in. That's a huge advantage over neural networks.

The dual formulation rewrites the same problem using Lagrange multipliers α_i — one per training example. Why bother? Two reasons. First, the dual depends on the data only through dot products x_i · x_j, which is the gateway to the kernel trick (more on that soon). Second, the dual is often easier to solve when the number of features is larger than the number of samples.

Dual problem:
  maximize    Σ α_i  -  ½ Σ_i Σ_j α_i α_j y_i y_j (x_i · x_j)
  subject to  0 ≤ α_i    and    Σ α_i y_i = 0

After solving:
  w = Σ α_i y_i x_i        (only nonzero for support vectors)
  b = y_s - w · x_s         (for any support vector x_s)

The KKT (Karush-Kuhn-Tucker) conditions are the optimality conditions for this constrained optimization. The key insight: α_i > 0 only when y_i(w · x_i + b) = 1, which means the point lies exactly on the margin boundary. These are your support vectors. Every other point has α_i = 0 and is irrelevant to the solution. That's why SVMs are memory-efficient at prediction time — you only need to store the support vectors, not the entire training set.

Soft Margin — Real Data is Messy

The hard-margin SVM assumes every point can be perfectly separated. Real data laughs at this assumption. You'll almost always have some overlap — a few red cars parked on the blue side. If you insist on perfect separation, either no solution exists or the margin collapses to nearly zero, overfitting wildly to noise.

The soft margin SVM introduces slack variables ξ_i (xi) that allow individual points to violate the margin or even be misclassified. Each ξ_i measures how far point i is on the wrong side. A point correctly classified and outside the margin has ξ_i = 0. A point inside the margin but on the correct side has 0 < ξ_i < 1. A misclassified point has ξ_i > 1.

Soft margin objective:
  minimize   ½||w||²  +  C · Σ ξ_i
  subject to y_i(w · x_i + b) ≥ 1 - ξ_i    and    ξ_i ≥ 0

The C parameter controls the tradeoff between a wide margin and few misclassifications. Large C says "I hate misclassifications — make the margin as tight as needed to classify everything correctly." Small C says "I'd rather have a wide, smooth margin, even if some points end up on the wrong side." Think of C as a dial between underfitting (small C, very wide margin, many mistakes) and overfitting (large C, razor-thin margin, memorizing noise).

The penalty term C · Σ ξ_i is equivalent to the hinge loss: L(y, f(x)) = max(0, 1 - y·f(x)). Hinge loss is zero when a point is classified correctly with at least margin 1, and grows linearly as the point drifts into the wrong zone. It's a convex upper bound on the 0-1 misclassification loss, which makes optimization tractable.

Practical Tip

Start with C = 1.0, then search over [0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100] using cross-validation. If your best C is at the boundary of your search range, expand it.

The Kernel Trick — The Key Insight

This is the idea that made SVMs famous. Pay attention — it's beautiful.

Some datasets can't be separated by any line or hyperplane in their original space. Classic example: points arranged in two concentric circles. The inner circle is class A, the outer ring is class B. No straight line in 2D can separate them.

But what if you added a third dimension? Suppose you mapped each point (x₁, x₂) to (x₁, x₂, x₁² + x₂²). That third coordinate is just the distance from the origin, squared. In this 3D space, the inner circle lifts up lower than the outer ring. Now a flat plane can slice between them cleanly. You've made a nonlinear problem linearly separable by going to a higher dimension.

# The concrete 2D → 3D example
# Original 2D data: concentric circles
import numpy as np

# Inner circle (class -1): points near radius 1
# Outer ring  (class +1): points near radius 3
theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 20)
inner = np.column_stack([np.cos(theta), np.sin(theta)])          # r ≈ 1
outer = np.column_stack([3*np.cos(theta), 3*np.sin(theta)])      # r ≈ 3

# In 2D: no linear separator exists.

# Map to 3D: φ(x₁, x₂) = (x₁, x₂, x₁² + x₂²)
inner_3d = np.column_stack([inner, (inner**2).sum(axis=1)])  # z ≈ 1
outer_3d = np.column_stack([outer, (outer**2).sum(axis=1)])  # z ≈ 9

# In 3D: a plane at z = 5 separates them perfectly!

That mapping φ (phi) is called the feature map. It takes data from a low-dimensional input space to a high-dimensional (or even infinite-dimensional) feature space. The problem: computing φ(x) explicitly can be insanely expensive. If your kernel implies a feature space with millions of dimensions, you can't store or compute those coordinates.

The kernel trick sidesteps this entirely. Remember that the dual formulation only uses dot products x_i · x_j. In the transformed space, that becomes φ(x_i) · φ(x_j). A kernel function K(x_i, x_j) computes this dot product directly — without ever computing φ(x_i) or φ(x_j). You get the answer to the high-dimensional dot product using only the original low-dimensional coordinates.

# The trick in one line:
# Instead of:  φ(x_i) · φ(x_j)    (expensive, high-dimensional)
# Compute:     K(x_i, x_j)          (cheap, in original space)

# Example: polynomial kernel of degree 2
# K(a, b) = (a · b + 1)²
# This implicitly computes the dot product in a space with
# all terms up to degree 2: (1, x₁, x₂, x₁², x₂², x₁x₂, ...)

For a kernel function to be valid, it must satisfy Mercer's condition: the Gram matrix K_ij = K(x_i, x_j) — computed for any set of points — must be positive semi-definite. This guarantees that K actually corresponds to a dot product in some (possibly infinite-dimensional) feature space. If Mercer's condition holds, the kernel is legitimate, and the SVM math works.

Common Kernels

KernelFormulaWhen to Use
LinearK(a, b) = a · bHigh-dimensional data (text, genomics). Already linearly separable or close to it.
PolynomialK(a, b) = (γ·a·b + r)dWhen interaction terms between features matter. Degree d controls complexity.
RBF (Gaussian)K(a, b) = exp(-γ||a - b||²)The default choice. Works well for most problems. Implied feature space is infinite-dimensional.
SigmoidK(a, b) = tanh(γ·a·b + r)Mimics a neural network. Rarely used — doesn't always satisfy Mercer's condition.

The RBF kernel has one key hyperparameter: gamma (γ). Large gamma means the kernel considers only very close neighbors, creating tight, complex boundaries (risk of overfitting). Small gamma means the kernel smooths over large distances, creating simple boundaries (risk of underfitting). Gamma and C interact — you nearly always need to grid-search both together.

Kernel Hyperparameter Selection

For the polynomial kernel, you also pick the degree d. Degree 2 captures quadratic interactions. Degree 3 and above gets expensive and prone to overfitting. For RBF, a common starting point for gamma is 1 / (n_features × variance_of_X). Scikit-learn's gamma='scale' does exactly this.

from sklearn.svm import SVC
from sklearn.model_selection import GridSearchCV
from sklearn.datasets import make_moons
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

X, y = make_moons(n_samples=300, noise=0.2, random_state=42)
X = StandardScaler().fit_transform(X)

# Grid search over C and gamma
param_grid = {
    'C': [0.1, 1, 10, 100],
    'gamma': [1, 0.1, 0.01, 0.001]
}
grid = GridSearchCV(SVC(kernel='rbf'), param_grid, cv=5, scoring='accuracy')
grid.fit(X, y)

print(f"Best params: {grid.best_params_}")
print(f"Best CV accuracy: {grid.best_score_:.3f}")
⚠ Always Scale Your Features

SVMs compute distances between data points. If one feature ranges from 0 to 1 and another from 0 to 1,000,000, the second feature will dominate. Always standardize (zero mean, unit variance) or min-max scale before fitting an SVM.

Multi-Class SVM

SVMs are inherently binary classifiers — they find a boundary between two classes. But most real problems have more than two classes. There are several strategies to extend SVMs to K classes.

One-vs-Rest (OvR), also called one-vs-all, trains K separate binary classifiers. Each one asks: "Is this class k, or everything else?" At prediction time, you pick the class whose classifier gives the highest decision value. This requires K models. It's simple and usually works well, but the classifiers are trained on imbalanced datasets (one small class vs. all others combined).

One-vs-One (OvO) trains a binary classifier for every pair of classes — K(K-1)/2 models total. At prediction time, each model votes, and the class with the most votes wins. More models, but each one trains on a smaller subset of data, so total training time can actually be faster. This is what scikit-learn's SVC uses by default.

DAGSVM (Directed Acyclic Graph SVM) uses the same K(K-1)/2 models as OvO, but organizes prediction into a decision graph. It starts at the root and at each node, eliminates one class, following a path down the DAG until one class remains. This reduces prediction to K-1 binary evaluations instead of the full K(K-1)/2.

The Crammer-Singer formulation solves all classes simultaneously in a single optimization problem. It's more theoretically elegant but slower to train and rarely used in practice. Scikit-learn offers it via LinearSVC(multi_class='crammer_singer').

SVM for Regression (SVR)

SVMs can do regression too. The idea flips: instead of maximizing the margin between classes, you define a tube of width ε (epsilon) around the predicted function and only penalize points that fall outside it.

The epsilon-insensitive loss is zero for any prediction error smaller than ε. Errors larger than ε are penalized linearly, just like hinge loss. This means the model doesn't care about small errors — it only tries to keep predictions within the epsilon tube. Points inside the tube contribute nothing to the loss; points outside it become support vectors.

# ε-insensitive loss:
# L(y, f(x)) = max(0, |y - f(x)| - ε)
#
# If prediction is within ε of the true value → zero loss
# If prediction is farther than ε → linear penalty

Nu-SVR is a variant that replaces the ε parameter with ν (nu), which controls the fraction of data points allowed to be outside the tube. It's more intuitive: ν ∈ (0, 1] directly sets the upper bound on the fraction of margin errors and lower bound on the fraction of support vectors.

from sklearn.svm import SVR
import numpy as np

# Generate noisy sine wave
np.random.seed(42)
X = np.sort(5 * np.random.rand(200, 1), axis=0)
y = np.sin(X).ravel() + np.random.normal(0, 0.1, X.shape[0])

# Fit SVR with RBF kernel
svr = SVR(kernel='rbf', C=100, gamma=0.1, epsilon=0.1)
svr.fit(X, y)

print(f"Number of support vectors: {len(svr.support_)}")
print(f"R² score: {svr.score(X, y):.3f}")

Optimization — How SVMs Are Actually Solved

The SVM dual is a quadratic programming (QP) problem — minimize a quadratic objective with linear constraints. Standard QP solvers work, but they're too slow for large datasets because they need the full Gram matrix in memory (n × n, where n is the number of samples).

Sequential Minimal Optimization (SMO), invented by John Platt in 1998, changed everything. Instead of solving the full QP at once, SMO breaks it into the smallest possible sub-problems: at each step, it picks just two Lagrange multipliers and optimizes them analytically (closed-form solution). It iterates until convergence. SMO is the algorithm behind LibSVM, the library that scikit-learn wraps when you call SVC().

LibSVM handles nonlinear kernels efficiently using SMO. LibLinear is optimized specifically for the linear kernel case — it uses coordinate descent and is dramatically faster for linear SVMs on large datasets. In scikit-learn, SVC(kernel='linear') uses LibSVM, but LinearSVC() uses LibLinear. For large datasets with a linear kernel, always prefer LinearSVC or SGDClassifier(loss='hinge').

from sklearn.svm import SVC, LinearSVC
from sklearn.linear_model import SGDClassifier
from sklearn.datasets import make_classification
import time

X, y = make_classification(n_samples=50000, n_features=100, random_state=42)

# LinearSVC (LibLinear) — fast for linear kernels
start = time.time()
LinearSVC(max_iter=5000).fit(X, y)
print(f"LinearSVC:      {time.time() - start:.2f}s")

# SGDClassifier with hinge loss — scales to millions of samples
start = time.time()
SGDClassifier(loss='hinge', max_iter=1000).fit(X, y)
print(f"SGDClassifier:  {time.time() - start:.2f}s")

# SVC with linear kernel (LibSVM) — much slower at this scale
# start = time.time()
# SVC(kernel='linear').fit(X, y)

Math Prerequisites — The Foundations Under the Hood

You don't need to derive every equation to use SVMs, but understanding the mathematical scaffolding helps you debug strange behavior and pick hyperparameters with intention rather than guesswork.

Convex Optimization

Convex optimization is optimization where the objective function is convex (shaped like a bowl) and the constraints define a convex set (no holes or dents). The critical property: any local minimum is also the global minimum. No saddle points, no local traps. This is why SVMs are so well-behaved compared to neural networks — the solution you find is guaranteed to be the best one.

Lagrangian Duality

Lagrangian duality is the technique that converts the primal constrained optimization into the dual unconstrained form. You take each constraint, multiply it by a Lagrange multiplier (α_i ≥ 0), and subtract it from the objective to form the Lagrangian L(w, b, α). Then you minimize L over the primal variables (w, b) and maximize over the dual variables (α). Under the Slater condition — which holds for SVMs — strong duality applies, meaning the primal and dual have the same optimal value.

Kernel Spaces (RKHS)

The mathematical foundation for kernels is the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). A Hilbert space is an infinite-dimensional generalization of Euclidean space — it has inner products, distances, and completeness. The "reproducing" part means there exists a kernel function that reproduces the inner product in that space: ⟨φ(x), φ(y)⟩ = K(x, y). The RKHS is the feature space that the kernel implicitly defines. For the RBF kernel, this space is infinite-dimensional — but the kernel trick means you never need to work in it explicitly.

Inner Products and Projections

The dot product (inner product) x · y measures how similar two vectors are. It's the foundation of the SVM decision function: f(x) = w · x + b. When w is a unit vector, w · x is the signed projection of x onto w — it tells you how far x lies in the direction of w. The SVM finds the direction w that best separates the classes, and the bias b shifts the decision boundary along that direction.

Putting It All Together — Practical Sklearn Code

from sklearn.svm import SVC, LinearSVC, SVR
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.pipeline import make_pipeline
from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score
from sklearn.datasets import load_iris, make_moons

# === 1. Linear SVM on Iris (multi-class) ===
iris = load_iris()
linear_svm = make_pipeline(
    StandardScaler(),
    LinearSVC(C=1.0, max_iter=10000)
)
scores = cross_val_score(linear_svm, iris.data, iris.target, cv=5)
print(f"Linear SVM on Iris: {scores.mean():.3f} ± {scores.std():.3f}")

# === 2. RBF SVM on make_moons (nonlinear) ===
X, y = make_moons(n_samples=500, noise=0.3, random_state=42)
rbf_svm = make_pipeline(
    StandardScaler(),
    SVC(kernel='rbf', C=10, gamma=0.5)
)
scores = cross_val_score(rbf_svm, X, y, cv=5)
print(f"RBF SVM on Moons:   {scores.mean():.3f} ± {scores.std():.3f}")

# === 3. SVR for regression ===
import numpy as np
np.random.seed(42)
X_reg = np.sort(5 * np.random.rand(300, 1), axis=0)
y_reg = np.sin(X_reg).ravel() + 0.1 * np.random.randn(300)

svr_pipe = make_pipeline(
    StandardScaler(),
    SVR(kernel='rbf', C=100, gamma=0.1, epsilon=0.1)
)
scores = cross_val_score(svr_pipe, X_reg, y_reg, cv=5, scoring='r2')
print(f"SVR on sine wave:   {scores.mean():.3f} ± {scores.std():.3f}")

Honest Assessment — Where SVMs Stand Today

Let's be real about SVMs in 2024.

For tabular data, gradient boosting (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost) almost always wins. It handles mixed feature types, missing values, and categorical features natively. SVMs need careful preprocessing, scaling, and kernel selection — and still usually lose on accuracy. The Kaggle leaderboard tells the story: gradient boosting dominates tabular competitions.

For images, text, and audio, deep neural networks (CNNs, Transformers) demolished SVMs years ago. The gap is enormous and growing.

For small datasets (hundreds to a few thousand samples), SVMs remain competitive. Their convex optimization guarantees a global solution, the margin gives a natural form of regularization, and kernel flexibility handles nonlinearities without needing huge amounts of data.

For high-dimensional sparse data — text classification with TF-IDF features, genomics — linear SVMs are fast, effective, and hard to beat. LinearSVC and SGDClassifier(loss='hinge') scale to millions of samples.

SVMs also don't naturally output probabilities. You can force it with Platt scaling (SVC(probability=True)), which fits a sigmoid on top of the decision values, but it's a post-hoc hack, adds training time via cross-validation, and the resulting probabilities are often poorly calibrated.

⚠ Scaling Limitations

Training time for nonlinear SVMs scales between O(n²) and O(n³) in the number of samples. Beyond ~50,000 samples, consider LinearSVC, SGDClassifier, or switching to gradient boosting entirely. The kernel Gram matrix alone requires O(n²) memory.

Bottom line: SVMs are a beautiful piece of mathematical engineering. Understanding them makes you a better ML engineer — the ideas about margins, duality, and kernels appear throughout modern ML. But for most practical problems today, they're not your first choice. Learn them for the ideas. Use gradient boosting or neural nets for the results.
