Home/Ch 2/Statistics
TL;DR

Statistics is the engine that turns raw data into decisions. Descriptive stats summarize what you have. Hypothesis testing tells you whether a result is real or noise. Estimation theory formalizes how to extract parameters from data. Bayesian statistics lets you update beliefs as evidence arrives. Resampling methods give you confidence intervals without strong assumptions. Causal inference asks the hardest question: not just what correlates, but what causes what. Every model you train, every A/B test you run, every metric you report — statistics is doing the heavy lifting underneath.

Descriptive Statistics

Before you build any model, you need to look at your data. Not glance at it. Actually look at it. Descriptive statistics give you the vocabulary to talk about what you see — where values cluster, how spread out they are, and what the shape of the distribution looks like. Skip this step and you'll spend days debugging a model when the real problem was a skewed feature column all along.

Let's start with measures of central tendency — fancy words for "where's the middle?" The mean (arithmetic average) is the one you already know. Add up all values, divide by how many there are. It's the workhorse, but it has a fatal weakness: sensitivity to outliers. One billionaire walks into a bar and the average income of every patron goes through the roof. The median is the value sitting right in the middle when you sort everything. It doesn't care about outliers. If you have income data, housing prices, or anything with a long right tail, the median is almost always what you actually want. The mode is the most frequently occurring value. It's the only measure of central tendency that makes sense for categorical data — you can't compute the mean of {red, blue, blue, green}, but you can say blue is the mode.

Central tendency alone is dangerous. Knowing the average temperature is 22°C tells you nothing about whether it swings between 20 and 24 or between -10 and 54. You need measures of spread. The variance computes the average of squared deviations from the mean. Squaring does two things: it makes all deviations positive, and it punishes big deviations more than small ones. The standard deviation is just the square root of variance, bringing you back to the original units. An income standard deviation of $15,000 is more interpretable than a variance of 225,000,000 dollars-squared. The interquartile range (IQR) is the distance between the 25th percentile and the 75th percentile — the range that contains the middle half of your data. Like the median, it's robust to outliers, which makes it excellent for detecting them.

Two more shape descriptors that earn their keep. Skewness measures asymmetry. A distribution with a long right tail (like income) has positive skew. A long left tail gives negative skew. Zero skew means symmetric, like a Gaussian. When your features are heavily skewed, many models struggle — that's why log transforms are so common. Kurtosis measures how heavy the tails are. A normal distribution has a kurtosis of 3 (or 0 in "excess kurtosis" form). Higher kurtosis means fatter tails — more extreme values than a Gaussian would predict. If your residuals have high kurtosis, your model is being surprised by outliers more than it should be.

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

data = np.array([2, 3, 3, 4, 5, 5, 5, 6, 7, 8, 42])

print(f"Mean:     {np.mean(data):.2f}")       # 8.18 — pulled up by 42
print(f"Median:   {np.median(data):.2f}")      # 5.00 — unaffected
print(f"Mode:     {stats.mode(data, keepdims=False).mode}")  # 5

print(f"Variance: {np.var(data, ddof=1):.2f}") # 128.96 — ddof=1 for sample variance
print(f"Std Dev:  {np.std(data, ddof=1):.2f}") # 11.36
q75, q25 = np.percentile(data, [75, 25])
print(f"IQR:      {q75 - q25:.2f}")            # 3.00

print(f"Skewness: {stats.skew(data):.2f}")     # 2.29 — heavy right tail
print(f"Kurtosis: {stats.kurtosis(data):.2f}") # 5.32 — fat tails (excess kurtosis)
Key Insight

Always compute the median alongside the mean. If they disagree significantly, your distribution is skewed, and the mean is probably misleading. This one habit will save you from an embarrassing number of data bugs.

Hypothesis Testing

Here's the scenario. You've trained two models — your current production model and a shiny new one. The new model gets 91.2% accuracy on a held-out test set versus 90.5% for the old one. Is the new model actually better? Or did you just get lucky with the test split?

This is the question hypothesis testing was built to answer. You start by assuming the boring explanation is true: the null hypothesis (H₀) says there's no real difference — both models are equally good, and any observed gap is just random noise. The alternative hypothesis (H₁) says no, there is a real difference. The entire machinery of hypothesis testing is about deciding whether the evidence is strong enough to reject the null hypothesis.

The tool for that decision is the p-value. It answers one specific question: if the null hypothesis were true, what's the probability of observing a result at least as extreme as what we actually got? A small p-value (conventionally below 0.05) means the data would be very surprising under the null hypothesis, so we reject it. A large p-value means the data is perfectly consistent with the null. Here's what a p-value is not: it is not the probability that the null hypothesis is true. It is not the probability that your result is a fluke. It is not a measure of effect size. A tiny p-value with a trivially small effect size is statistically significant and completely useless. Always pair significance with practical significance.

Watch Out

A p-value of 0.03 does not mean there's a 3% chance the null hypothesis is true. It means there's a 3% chance of seeing data this extreme if the null hypothesis is true. This is a conditional probability — confusing it is one of the most common errors in applied statistics.

Two ways to be wrong. A Type I error (false positive) happens when you reject the null hypothesis but it's actually true — you deploy the new model thinking it's better, but it's not. The probability of this is your significance level α, typically 0.05. A Type II error (false negative) happens when you fail to reject the null but it's actually false — you stick with the old model even though the new one really is better. The probability of this is β. Statistical power (1 - β) is the probability of correctly detecting a real effect. In ML, we often have large test sets, which gives us high power, but we also run many comparisons, which inflates Type I errors. Both errors have real costs: a false positive wastes engineering resources deploying a useless model, while a false negative means leaving genuine improvements on the table.

The workhorse tests. A one-sample t-test checks if a sample mean differs from a known value. A two-sample t-test (also called an independent t-test) compares the means of two independent groups. A paired t-test compares matched observations — this is exactly what you want when comparing two models on the same test set, because the predictions are paired by example. The pairing removes variance from the test items themselves, making the test more sensitive to the actual model difference.

from scipy import stats
import numpy as np

# Paired t-test: comparing two models on the same test examples
np.random.seed(42)
model_a_scores = np.random.normal(0.90, 0.02, size=100)  # per-example scores
model_b_scores = np.random.normal(0.91, 0.02, size=100)

# Paired test — correct, because each pair shares the same test example
t_stat, p_value = stats.ttest_rel(model_a_scores, model_b_scores)
print(f"Paired t-test: t={t_stat:.3f}, p={p_value:.4f}")

# Independent test — wrong here, but shown for comparison
t_stat2, p_value2 = stats.ttest_ind(model_a_scores, model_b_scores)
print(f"Independent t-test: t={t_stat2:.3f}, p={p_value2:.4f}")
# The paired test is more powerful because it removes per-example variance

The chi-squared test is what you reach for when your data is categorical. Suppose you're checking whether a model's errors are uniformly distributed across demographic groups or whether certain groups get more errors. You construct a contingency table of observed counts and compute how far those counts deviate from what you'd expect if the variables were independent. Large deviations yield a large chi-squared statistic and a small p-value.

When you have more than two groups — say, comparing three different model architectures — don't run three pairwise t-tests. That inflates your false positive rate. Instead, use ANOVA (Analysis of Variance), which tests whether any of the group means differ. If ANOVA is significant, then follow up with pairwise tests to find out which groups differ.

And this brings us to a critical issue: multiple testing correction. Every time you run a hypothesis test at α = 0.05, you have a 5% chance of a false positive. Run 20 tests and you expect one false positive even if nothing is real. This is the multiple comparisons problem, and it's rampant in ML research where you might compare dozens of hyperparameter settings. The Bonferroni correction is the simplest fix: divide your significance level by the number of tests. Running 20 tests? Each one needs p < 0.0025 instead of p < 0.05. It's conservative but bulletproof. The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure is smarter — it controls the false discovery rate (the proportion of rejected nulls that are false positives) rather than the overall error rate, giving you more statistical power when you're testing many hypotheses simultaneously.

Key Insight

If you run a hyperparameter search over 50 configurations and pick the best one, you've implicitly run 50 hypothesis tests. The winning configuration's apparent advantage is inflated by selection bias. Always evaluate your final model on a truly held-out test set that wasn't involved in any selection decisions.

Estimation Theory

Hypothesis testing asks yes-or-no questions. Estimation theory asks "what's the best value?" Given some data, what's the most reasonable estimate for a parameter, and how certain should we be about that estimate?

A point estimate gives you a single best-guess value — "the mean is 42." An interval estimate (a confidence interval) gives you a range — "the mean is between 39 and 45 with 95% confidence." The confidence level doesn't mean there's a 95% probability the true parameter is in the interval. It means if you repeated the experiment many times, 95% of the intervals you'd construct would contain the true value. Subtle but important: the interval is random, not the parameter.

Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) is the most important estimation method you'll encounter. The idea is disarmingly simple: pick the parameter values that make the observed data most probable. You flip a coin 100 times and get 73 heads. What's the probability of heads? MLE says: find the value of p that maximizes the probability of seeing exactly 73 heads in 100 flips. That's the binomial likelihood, and calculus will tell you the answer is p = 0.73. That's it. MLE just asks "which parameter values would have been most likely to generate the data I actually saw?"

In practice, you work with the log-likelihood instead of the likelihood itself — it turns products into sums (numerically stable) and doesn't change where the maximum occurs. For Gaussian data, the MLE for the mean is the sample mean, and the MLE for the variance is the sample variance (with n in the denominator, not n-1 — MLE for variance is slightly biased). When you train a neural network by minimizing cross-entropy loss, you are doing maximum likelihood estimation. The connection is exact: minimizing negative log-likelihood equals maximizing likelihood.

Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation extends MLE by incorporating a prior belief about the parameter. Instead of maximizing the likelihood P(data|θ) alone, MAP maximizes the posterior P(θ|data) ∝ P(data|θ) × P(θ). The prior P(θ) encodes what you believed about the parameter before seeing data. If the prior is flat (uniform), MAP reduces to MLE. A Gaussian prior on weights corresponds to L2 regularization. A Laplacian prior corresponds to L1 regularization. This is one of the most beautiful connections in ML: regularization is just Bayesian estimation with specific priors.

Key Insight

L2 regularization = MAP estimation with a Gaussian prior on weights. L1 regularization = MAP estimation with a Laplace prior. Every regularizer can be interpreted as a Bayesian prior. This isn't just an analogy — the math is identical.

The Method of Moments is the blue-collar alternative to MLE. You set the theoretical moments (mean, variance, etc.) of the distribution equal to the sample moments and solve for the parameters. It's often easier to compute than MLE and gives you a reasonable starting point, though it's generally less efficient (higher variance in estimates). For simple distributions, it gives the same answer as MLE. For complex ones, MLE usually wins.

How good can an estimator get? A sufficient statistic captures everything the data has to say about a parameter — once you have it, the raw data gives you no additional information. For a Gaussian with known variance, the sample mean is sufficient for the population mean. Fisher Information quantifies how much information a sample carries about a parameter. More Fisher Information means you can estimate the parameter more precisely. The Cramér-Rao Lower Bound says that no unbiased estimator can have variance smaller than 1/I(θ), where I(θ) is the Fisher Information. It sets the floor on how well you can possibly estimate something. If your estimator achieves this bound, it's as good as mathematically possible — it's called efficient.

Bayesian Statistics

Frequentist statistics treats parameters as fixed but unknown constants. Bayesian statistics treats them as random variables with probability distributions. This isn't just a philosophical difference — it changes what questions you can answer and how you answer them.

The full Bayesian workflow starts with a prior distribution P(θ) — your belief about the parameter before seeing any data. Maybe you think a coin is probably fair, so your prior on the probability of heads is concentrated around 0.5. Then you observe data and compute the posterior distribution P(θ|data) using Bayes' theorem: posterior ∝ likelihood × prior. The posterior combines what you believed before with what the data tells you. Finally, the posterior predictive distribution P(new data|observed data) tells you what to expect from future observations by averaging the likelihood over all possible parameter values, weighted by the posterior. This naturally incorporates your uncertainty about the parameter.

The math gets messy fast because the posterior involves an integral in the denominator (the evidence or marginal likelihood) that's usually intractable. Conjugate priors are the elegant escape hatch. A conjugate prior is one where the posterior has the same functional form as the prior. The Beta-Binomial pair is the classic example: if your prior on a coin's bias is Beta(α, β) and you observe h heads in n flips, the posterior is Beta(α + h, β + n - h). No integrals needed — you just update two numbers. The Normal-Normal pair works similarly: a Gaussian prior on the mean of Gaussian data gives a Gaussian posterior. Conjugate priors are computationally wonderful, but they constrain your modeling choices. Real-world problems often need more flexible priors.

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
import matplotlib
# For non-interactive environments
matplotlib.use('Agg')

# Beta-Binomial conjugate update
# Prior: Beta(2, 2) — slightly informistic, favoring fair coin
alpha_prior, beta_prior = 2, 2

# Data: 7 heads out of 10 flips
heads, tails = 7, 3

# Posterior: Beta(alpha + heads, beta + tails)
alpha_post = alpha_prior + heads
beta_post = beta_prior + tails

x = np.linspace(0, 1, 200)
prior = stats.beta.pdf(x, alpha_prior, beta_prior)
likelihood = stats.beta.pdf(x, heads + 1, tails + 1)
posterior = stats.beta.pdf(x, alpha_post, beta_post)

print(f"Prior mean:     {alpha_prior / (alpha_prior + beta_prior):.3f}")
print(f"MLE:            {heads / (heads + tails):.3f}")
print(f"Posterior mean: {alpha_post / (alpha_post + beta_post):.3f}")
# Posterior mean (0.643) is pulled toward prior mean (0.500) from MLE (0.700)
# This shrinkage toward the prior IS regularization

When conjugate priors aren't available — which is most of the time in serious models — you need numerical methods. Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) generates samples from the posterior distribution by constructing a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the posterior. The Metropolis-Hastings algorithm proposes random moves and accepts or rejects them based on the posterior density ratio. Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) uses gradient information to make smarter proposals. MCMC gives you asymptotically exact samples, but it can be slow. You need to run chains long enough to converge, check diagnostics, and handle autocorrelation between samples. For a small model with a few parameters, MCMC is fine. For a neural network with millions of parameters, it's computationally infeasible.

Variational Inference (VI) trades exactness for speed. Instead of sampling from the true posterior, you pick a tractable family of distributions (often factored Gaussians) and find the member closest to the true posterior by minimizing the KL divergence. This turns inference into optimization, which we know how to do at scale. The approximation can be poor if the true posterior has complex correlations that the variational family can't capture, but for large-scale models, VI is often the only practical option. Variational autoencoders (VAEs) use this idea as a core architectural component.

When does the Bayesian vs. frequentist choice actually matter? For large datasets, the likelihood dominates the prior and both approaches converge to the same answer. Bayesian methods shine when data is scarce, when you have genuine prior knowledge worth encoding, when you need uncertainty estimates (not just point predictions), or when your model has many parameters relative to the data (the prior acts as regularization). Frequentist methods are simpler, faster, and often sufficient for well-specified problems with abundant data.

Resampling Methods

Classical statistics relies heavily on assumptions about distributions — normality, independence, equal variances. Resampling methods say: forget the assumptions, let the data speak for itself. The core idea is to simulate what would happen if you could repeat your experiment many times, using only the data you already have.

The bootstrap is the star of the show. Given a sample of n observations, you draw n observations with replacement from your sample, compute your statistic (mean, median, model accuracy, whatever), and repeat thousands of times. The distribution of those bootstrapped statistics approximates the sampling distribution of your estimator. From that, you can construct confidence intervals without assuming anything about the underlying distribution. Need a 95% confidence interval? Take the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of your bootstrap distribution. Done.

import numpy as np

# Bootstrap confidence interval for the median
np.random.seed(42)
data = np.array([2.1, 3.5, 4.2, 3.8, 5.1, 6.7, 4.9, 3.3, 5.5, 8.2])

n_bootstrap = 10000
bootstrap_medians = np.array([
    np.median(np.random.choice(data, size=len(data), replace=True))
    for _ in range(n_bootstrap)
])

ci_lower = np.percentile(bootstrap_medians, 2.5)
ci_upper = np.percentile(bootstrap_medians, 97.5)
print(f"Sample median: {np.median(data):.2f}")
print(f"95% Bootstrap CI: [{ci_lower:.2f}, {ci_upper:.2f}]")
print(f"Bootstrap std error: {bootstrap_medians.std():.3f}")

# Compare with parametric approach (assumes normality — which this data may not follow)
se_parametric = 1.253 * np.std(data, ddof=1) / np.sqrt(len(data))
print(f"Parametric std error (assuming normal): {se_parametric:.3f}")

Permutation tests take a different approach to hypothesis testing. Want to know if two groups have different means? Under the null hypothesis, the group labels don't matter. So you shuffle the labels randomly, compute the difference in means, and repeat thousands of times. The p-value is the fraction of permutations that produce a difference as extreme as the observed one. No distributional assumptions required. This is especially useful when your test statistic doesn't have a known theoretical distribution — which happens a lot with complex ML metrics.

The jackknife is the bootstrap's older, more conservative cousin. Instead of resampling with replacement, you leave out one observation at a time, compute your statistic on the remaining n-1 observations, and look at how the estimate changes. It's primarily used for estimating the bias and variance of an estimator. The jackknife is less flexible than the bootstrap (it doesn't work well for statistics like the median that aren't smooth functions of the data), but it's deterministic — you always get the same answer, unlike the bootstrap which depends on random resamples.

Watch Out

The bootstrap assumes your sample is representative of the population. If your sample is biased (e.g., training data that doesn't match production data), bootstrapping won't fix that — it'll just give you a very precise estimate of the wrong thing. Garbage in, confident garbage out.

Causal Inference Basics

Here's a trap that catches smart people constantly. You train a model and find that feature X has a large positive coefficient. You conclude: "increasing X will increase Y." Maybe. Maybe not. That coefficient tells you about correlation — what tends to happen together. Causation — what happens when you intervene — is a fundamentally different question.

The classic example: ice cream sales and drowning deaths are positively correlated. Banning ice cream won't save lives. The confounding variable is temperature — hot weather causes both more ice cream consumption and more swimming. Simpson's paradox makes this even more treacherous: a trend that appears in every subgroup can reverse when the groups are combined. A treatment might look better overall but worse within every age group, because the groups have different sizes and baseline rates. If you've ever been surprised that aggregate statistics told a different story than disaggregated ones, you've met Simpson's paradox.

Randomized controlled experiments (A/B tests) are the gold standard for establishing causation. By randomly assigning subjects to treatment and control, you break the link between the treatment and any confounders. If the groups differ in outcome, the treatment caused it — because randomization ensured nothing else was systematically different. But randomized experiments aren't always possible. You can't randomly assign smoking to study lung cancer. You often can't randomly assign users to radically different product experiences. When you're stuck with observational data, you need tools from causal inference to untangle causation from correlation.

The formal language of modern causal inference uses Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) to represent causal relationships. Nodes are variables, directed edges represent direct causal effects. Judea Pearl's do-calculus provides rules for computing the effect of interventions — P(Y | do(X)) rather than P(Y | X). The "do" operator means you physically set X to a value, rather than passively observing it. The distinction matters enormously: P(lung cancer | carrying a lighter) is high (smokers carry lighters), but P(lung cancer | do(carry a lighter)) is not — forcing someone to carry a lighter doesn't give them cancer.

Key Insight

When someone shows you a feature importance plot and says "this feature drives the outcome," ask: "drives it, or is correlated with it?" In ML, we usually have correlation. Causal claims require either randomized experiments or careful application of causal inference methods. Confusing the two leads to interventions that don't work — or worse, cause harm.

For ML practitioners, the causal mindset matters in at least three places. First, feature selection: including a variable that's a consequence of the target (rather than a cause) creates data leakage. Second, fairness: adjusting for the "wrong" variables when assessing algorithmic bias can mask or manufacture discrimination (Simpson's paradox again). Third, deployment: a model trained on correlations may break when the world changes and those correlations shift, while a model that captures causal structure is more robust. You don't need to become a causal inference expert, but you do need to develop the reflex of asking "is this causal, or just correlational?" every time someone makes an intervention recommendation based on model outputs.
