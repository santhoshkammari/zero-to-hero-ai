Calculus

Chapter 2: Mathematical Foundations 9 subtopics
TL;DR

Gradient descent is the engine behind every neural network, and calculus is the fuel. Derivatives tell you which direction to nudge each parameter to reduce loss. The chain rule lets you propagate that signal backward through an entire network — that is backpropagation. Partial derivatives generalize to gradient vectors that point uphill on a loss surface; we walk downhill. Integrals compute probabilities from density functions and expectations from distributions. Taylor expansions justify why gradient descent works at all. Automatic differentiation (reverse-mode AD) makes all of this computationally tractable, letting frameworks like PyTorch differentiate through millions of parameters in a single backward pass.

Derivatives

Gradient descent is calculus's killer app in ML. To understand it, we need to start with the humble derivative. A derivative measures how fast a function's output changes when you wiggle its input. If you have a function f(x) and you increase x by a tiny amount h, the derivative is the ratio of the change in output to the change in input, in the limit as h shrinks to zero: f'(x) = lim(h→0) [f(x+h) - f(x)] / h. Geometrically, it is the slope of the tangent line at point x.

Why does ML care? Because training a model means minimizing a loss function. The loss is a function of the model's parameters — weights and biases. The derivative of the loss with respect to a single weight tells you: "if I increase this weight by a tiny bit, does the loss go up or down, and by how much?" That is exactly the information you need to decide which direction to adjust that weight. A positive derivative means the loss increases when the weight increases, so you should decrease the weight. A negative derivative means the opposite. The magnitude tells you how sensitive the loss is to that weight.

Differentiation Rules

You do not need to go back to the limit definition every time you need a derivative. A handful of rules cover almost every function you will encounter. The power rule says that if f(x) = x^n, then f'(x) = n * x^(n-1). So the derivative of x^3 is 3x^2. The derivative of x (which is x^1) is 1. The derivative of a constant is zero — constants do not change when you wiggle the input.

The product rule handles multiplication of two functions: if f(x) = g(x) * h(x), then f'(x) = g'(x)*h(x) + g(x)*h'(x). You differentiate one factor at a time and add the results. The quotient rule handles division: if f(x) = g(x)/h(x), then f'(x) = [g'(x)*h(x) - g(x)*h'(x)] / h(x)^2. In practice, you can often rewrite a quotient as a product with a negative exponent and use the product rule instead.

The Chain Rule

The chain rule is the single most important calculus concept for machine learning. It tells you how to differentiate a composition of functions. If y = f(g(x)), then dy/dx = f'(g(x)) * g'(x). You differentiate the outer function evaluated at the inner function, then multiply by the derivative of the inner function. In Leibniz notation: dy/dx = (dy/du) * (du/dx), where u = g(x). The chain rule lets you peel apart a complicated function layer by layer.

Neural networks are literally compositions of functions. Layer 1 takes the input and produces an output. Layer 2 takes that output and produces another. Layer 3, another. The loss function sits at the end. To compute the derivative of the loss with respect to a weight in layer 1, you must differentiate through every subsequent layer. The chain rule makes this possible: you multiply the local derivatives at each layer together. This is backpropagation — nothing more, nothing less than the chain rule applied systematically from the output back to each parameter.

Consider a two-layer example. You have z = w*x, then a = sigmoid(z), then L = (a - y)^2. To find dL/dw, apply the chain rule: dL/dw = dL/da * da/dz * dz/dw. Each factor is a local derivative that is easy to compute. The chain rule assembles them into the global derivative you actually need.

Partial Derivatives and Gradients

Real ML models have not one parameter but millions. The loss is a function of all of them: L(w1, w2, ..., wn). A partial derivative is the derivative of L with respect to one parameter, treating all others as constants. The notation is ∂L/∂w1. You compute it exactly like an ordinary derivative, just pretending the other variables are fixed numbers.

Stack all the partial derivatives into a vector and you get the gradient: ∇L = [∂L/∂w1, ∂L/∂w2, ..., ∂L/∂wn]. The gradient is a vector that points in the direction of steepest ascent of the loss function. Its magnitude tells you how steep the slope is. This is the key concept in optimization: to minimize the loss, you walk in the opposite direction of the gradient. That is gradient descent in one sentence.

A directional derivative tells you the rate of change of the function along any direction, not just along coordinate axes. It equals the dot product of the gradient with the unit direction vector. The gradient direction itself gives the maximum directional derivative — it is the steepest way uphill.

Jacobian and Hessian Matrices

When the output is a vector instead of a scalar, you need the Jacobian matrix. If f maps R^n to R^m, the Jacobian is an m-by-n matrix where entry (i,j) is ∂f_i/∂x_j. Each row is the gradient of one output component. Jacobians appear when you differentiate a layer that produces a vector output — the softmax layer, for instance. Backpropagation through such a layer involves multiplying by the Jacobian (or its transpose).

The Hessian matrix contains all second-order partial derivatives: entry (i,j) is ∂²L/(∂w_i ∂w_j). It captures the curvature of the loss surface. Where the gradient tells you the slope, the Hessian tells you how the slope itself is changing. A positive-definite Hessian means you are sitting in a bowl — a local minimum. Eigenvalues of the Hessian reveal how steep the curvature is in different directions. In practice, the full Hessian is too expensive to compute for large networks (it is n-by-n, where n is the number of parameters), but approximations to it drive optimizers like L-BFGS and inform learning rate schedules.

Higher-order derivatives beyond the second are rarely used in ML. The third derivative (the "jerk" of the loss surface) is occasionally relevant in theoretical analysis but never in practical training. The key insight is that first-order information (gradients) is cheap and gives you the direction; second-order information (Hessians) is expensive but gives you curvature. Modern deep learning almost exclusively uses first-order methods because the gradient is all you can afford to compute at scale.

Key ML Functions and Their Derivatives

Sigmoid

The sigmoid function is sigma(z) = 1 / (1 + exp(-z)). It squashes any real number into the range (0, 1), making it useful as an output activation for binary classification — you can interpret the output as a probability. Its derivative has a famously elegant form. Start with sigma(z) = (1 + exp(-z))^(-1). Using the chain rule: sigma'(z) = exp(-z) / (1 + exp(-z))^2. Now notice that exp(-z) / (1 + exp(-z)) equals 1 - sigma(z). So sigma'(z) = sigma(z) * (1 - sigma(z)). The derivative is expressed entirely in terms of the function itself. During backpropagation, you already have sigma(z) from the forward pass, so computing the gradient is nearly free.

The sigmoid derivative peaks at 0.25 when z = 0 and falls toward zero for large positive or negative z. This means gradients through many stacked sigmoid layers shrink exponentially — the vanishing gradient problem. This is why sigmoid activations fell out of favor for hidden layers in deep networks.

ReLU

The ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) function is f(z) = max(0, z). Its derivative is 1 for z > 0 and 0 for z < 0. At z = 0, the derivative is technically undefined, but implementations pick either 0 or 1 (it does not matter in practice since z = 0 exactly has probability zero on continuous distributions). ReLU solved the vanishing gradient problem because its derivative is either 0 or 1 — gradients that flow through active ReLU units are not attenuated at all. The tradeoff: if a neuron's input is always negative, its gradient is always zero and it stops learning entirely. This is the "dying ReLU" problem. Variants like Leaky ReLU (which uses a small positive slope like 0.01 for z < 0) address this.

Softmax

The softmax function converts a vector of raw scores (logits) into a probability distribution. For a vector z of length K, softmax(z)_i = exp(z_i) / sum_j(exp(z_j)). Every output is positive and they sum to one. The Jacobian of softmax is a K-by-K matrix: ∂softmax_i/∂z_j = softmax_i * (delta_ij - softmax_j), where delta_ij is the Kronecker delta (1 if i = j, else 0). This Jacobian is dense, but in practice you rarely compute it explicitly because softmax is almost always paired with cross-entropy loss, and their combined gradient simplifies dramatically.

Tanh

The tanh function is tanh(z) = (exp(z) - exp(-z)) / (exp(z) + exp(-z)). It maps inputs to the range (-1, 1), centered at zero. Its derivative is tanh'(z) = 1 - tanh(z)^2. Like sigmoid, the derivative is expressible in terms of the function value. Tanh was preferred over sigmoid for hidden layers because its output is zero-centered, which helps gradient flow. But it still saturates at extreme values, so it suffers from vanishing gradients in deep networks, though less severely than sigmoid.

Log-Sum-Exp Trick

Computing softmax naively involves exponentiating large numbers, which causes numerical overflow. The log-sum-exp trick subtracts the maximum value before exponentiating: log(sum_i(exp(z_i))) = max(z) + log(sum_i(exp(z_i - max(z)))). After subtraction, the largest exponent is zero, so no term overflows. This trick is not a calculus concept per se, but it is essential whenever you compute log-probabilities from logits, which happens in nearly every classification model.

Integrals

If derivatives tell you about rates of change, integrals tell you about accumulation. The definite integral of f(x) from a to b computes the area under the curve f(x) between those two points. Geometrically, you are adding up infinitely many infinitely thin rectangles. The notation is ∫[a,b] f(x) dx.

ML uses integrals constantly, though you may not always see them written explicitly. Every probability distribution has a probability density function (PDF), and the probability that a random variable falls in some interval is the integral of the PDF over that interval. The expected value of a function g(X) under distribution p(x) is E[g(X)] = ∫ g(x) p(x) dx. Loss functions in probabilistic models are expectations. Marginal distributions require integrating out variables. Bayesian inference requires integrating over the entire parameter space to compute the evidence (the normalizing constant in Bayes' theorem). Many of these integrals are intractable, which is why ML relies so heavily on Monte Carlo approximation — replace the integral with an average over samples.

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus ties derivatives and integrals together: differentiation and integration are inverse operations. If F(x) = ∫[a,x] f(t) dt, then F'(x) = f(x). Conversely, ∫[a,b] f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a), where F is any antiderivative of f. This theorem tells you that to compute a definite integral, find a function whose derivative is the integrand, then evaluate it at the endpoints. When an antiderivative exists in closed form, integration is straightforward. When it does not — which is often the case in ML — you resort to numerical methods.

Integration Techniques

Substitution is the integration counterpart of the chain rule. If you can rewrite the integrand as f(g(x)) * g'(x), you substitute u = g(x) and get ∫ f(u) du, which is often simpler. Integration by parts is the counterpart of the product rule: ∫ u dv = uv - ∫ v du. You use it when the integrand is a product of two functions and differentiating one makes it simpler. These techniques are useful for deriving closed-form results in statistics, such as the moments of common distributions.

Multiple Integrals

Multiple integrals extend integration to functions of several variables. A double integral ∫∫ f(x,y) dx dy computes a volume under a surface. In ML, you encounter multiple integrals when marginalizing over variables. If you have a joint distribution p(x, y) and want the marginal p(x), you integrate out y: p(x) = ∫ p(x, y) dy. In Bayesian inference, computing the posterior requires integrating the likelihood times the prior over the entire parameter space. When parameters live in high-dimensional spaces, these integrals become astronomically expensive — the curse of dimensionality. This is the core challenge that drives variational inference and Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods.

Optimization

Critical Points and Derivative Tests

A critical point of a function is a point where the derivative is zero (or undefined). At a critical point, the function is momentarily flat — it could be a local minimum, a local maximum, or a saddle point. The first derivative test checks the sign of the derivative on either side: if the derivative goes from negative to positive, the critical point is a local minimum. If it goes from positive to negative, it is a local maximum.

The second derivative test uses curvature: at a critical point, if f''(x) > 0, the function curves upward like a bowl, so it is a local minimum. If f''(x) < 0, it curves downward and you have a local maximum. If f''(x) = 0, the test is inconclusive. In multiple dimensions, you replace the second derivative with the Hessian matrix and check its eigenvalues: all positive means a local minimum, all negative means a local maximum, and a mix of signs means a saddle point. In high-dimensional neural networks, saddle points vastly outnumber local minima, which is one reason why training rarely gets stuck at bad local optima.

Convex Functions

A function is convex if a line segment connecting any two points on the graph lies above the graph. Equivalently, its second derivative (or Hessian) is non-negative everywhere. Convex functions have a beautiful property: every local minimum is a global minimum. There is only one bowl, and gradient descent will find the bottom of it. Linear regression's squared loss is convex. Logistic regression's loss is convex. This is why these classical models are reliably trainable. Neural network losses are emphatically not convex — they have many saddle points and local minima — but empirically, gradient descent still finds good solutions. Understanding convexity helps you appreciate why some problems are easy and others are hard.

Gradient Descent from Taylor Expansion

Here is where calculus directly produces an algorithm. Take the first-order Taylor expansion of the loss function around the current parameter vector w: L(w + delta) ≈ L(w) + ∇L(w) · delta. You want to choose delta so that L(w + delta) is as small as possible. The term ∇L(w) · delta is minimized when delta points in the opposite direction of the gradient. So you set delta = -alpha * ∇L(w), where alpha is a small positive number called the learning rate. The update rule becomes: w_new = w - alpha * ∇L(w). That is gradient descent, derived directly from a linear approximation of the loss function.

The learning rate alpha controls the step size. Too large and you overshoot the minimum, bouncing around or diverging entirely. Too small and convergence is painfully slow, taking thousands of extra steps to reach a good solution. In practice, you often start with a moderate learning rate and decay it over time, or use adaptive methods like Adam that maintain a per-parameter learning rate. The fundamental tradeoff is between speed and stability.

import numpy as np

def gradient_descent(f, grad_f, x0, lr=0.01, steps=100):
    """Vanilla gradient descent."""
    x = x0.copy()
    history = [x.copy()]
    for _ in range(steps):
        g = grad_f(x)
        x = x - lr * g  # THE update rule
        history.append(x.copy())
    return x, history

# Minimize f(x,y) = (x-3)^2 + (y-1)^2
def f(x): return (x[0]-3)**2 + (x[1]-1)**2
def grad_f(x): return np.array([2*(x[0]-3), 2*(x[1]-1)])

x_min, path = gradient_descent(f, grad_f, np.array([0.0, 0.0]), lr=0.1, steps=50)
print(f"Found minimum at: {x_min}")  # Close to [3, 1]

Constrained Optimization

Lagrange multipliers handle optimization with equality constraints. Suppose you want to minimize f(x) subject to g(x) = 0. The method introduces a new variable lambda (the multiplier) and finds points where ∇f = lambda * ∇g. Geometrically, at the optimum, the gradient of the objective must be parallel to the gradient of the constraint — otherwise you could move along the constraint surface and still improve the objective. In ML, Lagrange multipliers appear in the derivation of support vector machines (the dual formulation) and in maximum entropy models.

When you have inequality constraints (g(x) ≤ 0), the framework extends to the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions. These add complementary slackness: either the constraint is active (g(x) = 0) and the multiplier is positive, or the constraint is inactive (g(x) < 0) and the multiplier is zero. KKT conditions are necessary for optimality in constrained problems and form the theoretical backbone of SVMs and many optimization algorithms used in ML.

Taylor Series

A Taylor series approximates a function as an infinite sum of polynomial terms built from its derivatives at a single point: f(x) ≈ f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + f''(a)(x-a)^2/2! + f'''(a)(x-a)^3/3! + ... The more terms you include, the better the approximation near point a.

The first-order approximation drops everything after the linear term: f(x) ≈ f(a) + f'(a)(x-a). This is a tangent line, and it directly gives you gradient descent. When you update parameters by subtracting alpha times the gradient, you are trusting this linear approximation to tell you which direction reduces the loss. The approximation is only accurate close to the current point, which is why you take small steps.

The second-order approximation includes the quadratic term: f(x) ≈ f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + f''(a)(x-a)^2/2. In multiple dimensions, this uses the Hessian. Setting the derivative of this quadratic to zero and solving gives Newton's method: delta = -H^(-1) * ∇L, where H is the Hessian. Newton's method accounts for curvature and can converge much faster than gradient descent — quadratically near the optimum, compared to linearly. But it requires computing and inverting the Hessian, which is O(n^2) storage and O(n^3) computation for n parameters. For a network with 100 million parameters, that is out of the question. This is why deep learning stops at first-order methods: the gradient is O(n) and that is all you can afford. Quasi-Newton methods like L-BFGS approximate the Hessian cheaply and work well for smaller models.

Backpropagation

Chain Rule Through Layers

Backpropagation is the algorithm for computing the gradient of the loss with respect to every parameter in a neural network. Despite the mystique, it is just the chain rule applied systematically. Consider a network with L layers. The forward pass computes: z1 = W1*x + b1, a1 = activation(z1), z2 = W2*a1 + b2, a2 = activation(z2), ..., up to the loss. Each layer is a function whose output feeds into the next.

The backward pass starts from the loss and works backward. At each layer, you compute the local gradient (derivative of the layer's output with respect to its input and its parameters), then multiply by the incoming gradient from the layer above. You pass the gradient with respect to the input backward to the previous layer, and you store the gradient with respect to the parameters for the update step. The total cost is roughly twice the cost of a forward pass — once forward, once backward — making backpropagation remarkably efficient.

Computational Graphs

Modern frameworks represent the forward pass as a computational graph: a directed acyclic graph where each node is an operation (add, multiply, activate) and edges carry tensors. During the forward pass, the framework records which operations were performed and in what order. During the backward pass, it traverses this graph in reverse, applying the chain rule at each node. Static graphs (like older TensorFlow) build the graph once and reuse it. Dynamic graphs (like PyTorch) rebuild the graph on every forward pass, which is more flexible — you can use Python control flow like if statements and for loops, and the graph adapts accordingly. Dynamic graphs made debugging and prototyping so much easier that they became the default.

Vanishing and Exploding Gradients

When a network has many layers, the gradient is a product of many local derivatives. If each local derivative is less than 1, the product shrinks exponentially toward zero — this is the vanishing gradient problem. Layers near the input receive negligible gradient signal and learn extremely slowly. Sigmoid activations are especially prone to this because their maximum derivative is 0.25. Multiply 0.25 by itself 20 times and you get approximately 10^(-12). The fix: use ReLU activations (derivative is 1 for positive inputs), residual connections (which add a shortcut that lets gradients bypass layers), and careful initialization schemes like He or Xavier initialization.

The opposite problem is exploding gradients: if local derivatives exceed 1, the product grows exponentially, weights update by huge amounts, and training collapses. The standard fix is gradient clipping: if the gradient's norm exceeds a threshold, scale it down before applying the update. This is especially important in recurrent neural networks.

Backpropagation Through Time

Backpropagation through time (BPTT) is backpropagation applied to recurrent neural networks (RNNs). An RNN processes a sequence by applying the same weights at every time step: h_t = f(W_h * h_{t-1} + W_x * x_t). To unroll this for backpropagation, you treat each time step as a layer. A sequence of length T produces a graph T layers deep, with shared weights. Gradients flow backward through all T steps, which makes vanishing and exploding gradients particularly severe. Long sequences of 100 or more time steps mean the gradient is a product of 100+ matrices. This is the fundamental reason LSTMs and GRUs were invented — their gating mechanisms control gradient flow to prevent it from vanishing over long sequences.

Automatic Differentiation

Three Approaches to Differentiation

There are three ways a computer can compute derivatives. Symbolic differentiation manipulates mathematical expressions using rules (power rule, chain rule, etc.) to produce a new expression for the derivative. Tools like SymPy and Mathematica do this. The result is exact, but expressions can explode in size — the derivative of a complicated function may be far more complex than the function itself. This approach does not scale to functions defined by programs with loops and conditionals.

Numerical differentiation approximates the derivative using finite differences: f'(x) ≈ [f(x+h) - f(x-h)] / (2h). This central difference formula is O(h^2) accurate. It is simple, works for any function you can evaluate, and requires no knowledge of the function's internals. But it has two problems. First, you must choose h: too large and the approximation is inaccurate; too small and floating-point cancellation destroys the result. Second, for n parameters, you need 2n function evaluations. For a network with a million parameters, that is two million forward passes to get one gradient. Unacceptable for training, but excellent for testing — you can use numerical gradients to verify that your analytical gradients are correct.

import numpy as np

def numerical_gradient(f, x, h=1e-5):
    """Compute gradient numerically using central differences."""
    grad = np.zeros_like(x)
    for i in range(len(x)):
        x_plus = x.copy(); x_plus[i] += h
        x_minus = x.copy(); x_minus[i] -= h
        grad[i] = (f(x_plus) - f(x_minus)) / (2 * h)
    return grad

# Example: gradient of f(x,y) = x^2 + 3xy + y^2
def f(x):
    return x[0]**2 + 3*x[0]*x[1] + x[1]**2

point = np.array([1.0, 2.0])
grad = numerical_gradient(f, point)
print(f"Numerical gradient at (1,2): {grad}")
# Analytical: df/dx = 2x+3y = 8, df/dy = 3x+2y = 7
print(f"Analytical gradient at (1,2): [8, 7]")

Forward-Mode vs Reverse-Mode AD

Automatic differentiation (AD) is the third approach and the one that actually powers deep learning. It does not manipulate expressions symbolically and does not use finite differences. Instead, it decomposes the function into a sequence of elementary operations (add, multiply, exp, log, etc.), each of which has a known derivative, and applies the chain rule through this sequence. The result is exact (up to floating-point precision) and efficient.

Forward-mode AD propagates derivatives forward alongside the computation. For each input, you track both the value and its derivative through every operation. If you have n inputs and one output, you need n forward passes (one per input) to build the full gradient. This is efficient when you have few inputs and many outputs.

Reverse-mode AD propagates derivatives backward from the output. One backward pass gives you the derivative of the output with respect to every input. If you have many inputs and one output — which is exactly the situation in ML, where you have millions of parameters and a single scalar loss — reverse mode is spectacularly efficient. One forward pass plus one backward pass gives the complete gradient. This is why PyTorch and TensorFlow use reverse-mode AD. It is why backpropagation works. The computational cost of the backward pass is roughly two to four times the cost of the forward pass, regardless of how many parameters the network has.

import torch

# Define parameters with gradient tracking
x = torch.tensor([1.0, 2.0], requires_grad=True)

# Forward pass: compute loss
loss = x[0]**2 + 3*x[0]*x[1] + x[1]**2

# Backward pass: compute gradients
loss.backward()

print(f"PyTorch autograd gradient: {x.grad}")
# tensor([8., 7.]) — matches our analytical result
import torch
import torch.nn as nn

# Tiny network: 2 inputs -> 3 hidden -> 1 output
model = nn.Sequential(
    nn.Linear(2, 3),
    nn.ReLU(),
    nn.Linear(3, 1)
)

x = torch.randn(4, 2)   # batch of 4 samples
y = torch.randn(4, 1)   # targets

# Forward pass
pred = model(x)
loss = ((pred - y)**2).mean()

# Backward pass — PyTorch builds the graph and backprops
loss.backward()

# Every parameter now has a .grad
for name, param in model.named_parameters():
    print(f"{name}: shape={param.shape}, grad_norm={param.grad.norm():.4f}")

Softmax + Cross-Entropy Gradient

This derivation is so central to classification that it deserves its own section. You have a network that outputs raw logits z. You apply softmax to get predicted probabilities: y_hat_i = exp(z_i) / sum_j(exp(z_j)). The true label is a one-hot vector y (all zeros except a 1 at the correct class). The cross-entropy loss is L = -sum_i(y_i * log(y_hat_i)). Since y is one-hot, this simplifies to L = -log(y_hat_k), where k is the correct class.

Now compute the gradient ∂L/∂z_i. You need to differentiate through the softmax and the log. For i = k (the correct class): ∂L/∂z_k = y_hat_k - 1. For i ≠ k: ∂L/∂z_i = y_hat_i. Combining both cases using the one-hot vector: ∇_z L = y_hat - y. The gradient of cross-entropy loss with respect to the logits is simply the predicted probability minus the true label. That is it. No messy Jacobians, no complicated expressions. Just y_hat minus y.

This simplification is not just elegant — it is numerically essential. Computing log(softmax(z)) naively is unstable because softmax can produce values extremely close to zero, and log of a near-zero number is negative infinity. Frameworks provide a combined log_softmax function (or equivalently, cross_entropy_loss that takes raw logits) that uses the log-sum-exp trick internally for stability. Always pass raw logits to the loss function rather than computing softmax yourself. The gradient y_hat - y is computed directly from the stable combined implementation.

Advanced Topics

Differential Equations in ML

Residual networks compute h_{t+1} = h_t + f(h_t), which looks like an Euler step for the ODE dh/dt = f(h). Neural ODEs take this literally: they parameterize a continuous transformation using a neural ODE and solve it with a numerical ODE solver. The backward pass uses the adjoint method, solving another ODE backward in time to compute gradients with constant memory cost. Neural ODEs enable continuous-depth networks, continuous normalizing flows, and time-series models with irregular sampling.

Variational Calculus

Variational calculus extends optimization from finding optimal points to finding optimal functions. In ML, this appears in variational inference: you approximate an intractable posterior p(z|x) with a simpler distribution q(z) by minimizing KL divergence, equivalently maximizing the evidence lower bound (ELBO). Variational autoencoders (VAEs) use this framework, optimizing both encoder and decoder jointly using the reparameterization trick to backpropagate through random sampling.

Information Geometry

Information geometry treats families of probability distributions as points on a manifold, with the Fisher information matrix as its metric. Natural gradient descent uses the Fisher information to precondition the gradient, taking steps that respect the curvature of distribution space rather than Euclidean space. This makes optimization invariant to reparameterization of the model.

L2 Regularization as Weight Decay

L2 regularization adds a penalty term (lambda/2) * ||w||^2 to the loss function. Taking the derivative of the regularized loss with respect to w: ∂/∂w [L(w) + (lambda/2)*||w||^2] = ∇L(w) + lambda*w. The gradient descent update becomes: w_new = w - alpha*(∇L(w) + lambda*w) = (1 - alpha*lambda)*w - alpha*∇L(w). The factor (1 - alpha*lambda) shrinks the weights slightly before each update — hence the name weight decay. This discourages any single weight from growing too large, acting as a soft constraint that improves generalization. For standard SGD, L2 regularization and weight decay are mathematically identical. For adaptive optimizers like Adam, they differ subtly, which is why AdamW (which decouples weight decay from the gradient) was introduced.

OLS Closed-Form Solution

The ordinary least squares problem is: minimize ||Xw - y||^2. Expand: L(w) = w^T X^T X w - 2 w^T X^T y + y^T y. Take the gradient: ∇L = 2 X^T X w - 2 X^T y. Set to zero: X^T X w = X^T y. If X^T X is invertible, the closed-form solution is w = (X^T X)^(-1) X^T y. This is the normal equation. It gives the exact answer in one shot. However, inverting X^T X costs O(d^3), which becomes prohibitive when d is large. In that regime, gradient descent with its O(nd) per-step cost is more practical — closed-form solutions are elegant but iterative methods scale.
