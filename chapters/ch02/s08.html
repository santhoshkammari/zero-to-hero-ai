Nice to Know

Chapter 2: Mathematical Foundations 27 topics
TL;DR

These are topics you'll encounter in papers, advanced codebases, or niche subfields. You don't need to master any of them right now — but when one shows up in the wild, you'll know what you're looking at and where to dig deeper. Think of this as a field guide: enough to recognize the animal, not enough to perform surgery on it.

Linear Algebra

Tensor Decomposition (CP, Tucker)

Matrices are 2D. But data is often 3D or higher — think user × item × time, or spatial × spectral × temporal. Tensor decompositions (CP decomposes into rank-one components, Tucker gives a core tensor with factor matrices) are the higher-dimensional analog of SVD. They compress multi-way data while preserving structure.

You'll need this when: working with multi-dimensional data that doesn't flatten into a matrix without losing structure — recommendation systems, signal processing, or compressing neural network weight tensors.

Jordan Normal Form

Not every matrix is diagonalizable. The Jordan normal form is the "next best thing" — a block-diagonal matrix with ones on the super-diagonal of each block. It's the canonical form for understanding how repeated eigenvalues behave. Purely theoretical in most ML contexts, but it shows up in stability analysis of dynamical systems and recurrent network theory.

You'll need this when: reading papers on RNN stability or linear dynamical systems where eigenvalue multiplicity matters.

Kronecker Product

The Kronecker product A ⊗ B creates a block matrix where each entry aᵢⱼ is replaced by aᵢⱼB. It's the natural way to represent operations on pairs of spaces simultaneously. Sounds exotic, but it shows up surprisingly often — multi-task learning, graph neural networks (adjacency ⊗ feature transforms), and vectorizing matrix equations.

You'll need this when: you see papers on multi-relational learning, graph convolutions over product spaces, or structured linear layers.

Matrix Square Root

A matrix square root S satisfies S² = A. The most famous ML application is neural style transfer, where matching the Gram matrix square root of feature covariances transfers artistic style between images. It also appears in the natural gradient, where you need the inverse square root of the Fisher information matrix.

You'll need this when: implementing style transfer, whitening transforms, or natural gradient methods like K-FAC.

Pseudoinverse (Moore-Penrose)

The Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse A⁺ generalizes the matrix inverse to non-square and rank-deficient matrices. It gives the minimum-norm least-squares solution to Ax = b, no matter what shape A is. Under the hood, it's computed via SVD: invert the non-zero singular values, leave the zeros alone.

You'll need this when: solving underdetermined or overdetermined systems — least-squares regression, minimum-norm solutions, or understanding what happens when you have more features than samples.

Calculus

Neural ODEs

What if instead of stacking discrete layers, your network was a continuous transformation? Neural ODEs define the hidden state as the solution to an ODE: dh/dt = f(h, t, θ). You use a black-box ODE solver for the forward pass, and the adjoint method for memory-efficient backpropagation — no need to store intermediate activations.

You'll need this when: modeling continuous-time dynamics, irregularly-sampled time series, or when you want adaptive computation depth.

Variational Calculus

Regular calculus optimizes over numbers. Variational calculus optimizes over functions — finding the function that minimizes a functional (a function of functions). The Euler-Lagrange equation is the analog of "set the derivative to zero." In ML, this is the mathematical backbone of variational inference, where you search for the best approximating distribution.

You'll need this when: reading the derivation of variational autoencoders, understanding ELBO, or working with functional optimization in physics-informed neural networks.

Stochastic Calculus / SDEs

Stochastic differential equations are ODEs with a noise term: dX = f(X)dt + g(X)dW, where dW is a Wiener process (Brownian motion). The rules of calculus change — Itô's lemma replaces the chain rule. This isn't abstract math anymore: diffusion models (Stable Diffusion, DALL-E) are literally defined as SDEs, and the score-matching framework is stochastic calculus through and through.

You'll need this when: understanding diffusion model theory, score-based generative models, or stochastic optimization with continuous-time analysis.

Implicit Differentiation

Sometimes the output of your computation is defined implicitly — not as y = f(x), but as the solution to g(x, y) = 0. Implicit differentiation lets you compute dy/dx without explicitly solving for y. In ML, this powers deep equilibrium models (where the forward pass finds a fixed point) and meta-learning algorithms like iMAML that differentiate through an inner optimization loop.

You'll need this when: working with bilevel optimization, equilibrium networks, or any model where the forward pass involves solving an equation to convergence.

Probability & Statistics

Copulas

Correlation only captures linear relationships, and it assumes Gaussian-like marginals. Copulas separate the dependency structure between variables from their individual distributions. You model each marginal however you want, then glue them together with a copula that captures how they co-move — including tail dependencies that correlation completely misses.

You'll need this when: modeling financial risk, any setting where extreme events are correlated (joint tail risk), or when your variables have wildly different marginal distributions.

Sufficient Statistics

A sufficient statistic is a summary of your data that captures everything relevant about a parameter — no information is lost. For example, the sample mean and variance are sufficient for a Gaussian. The concept is beautiful because it tells you exactly how much you can compress your data without losing inferential power.

You'll need this when: understanding why certain estimators are optimal, working with exponential family distributions, or designing efficient data summaries for streaming/online learning.

Exponential Family Distributions

Gaussian, Bernoulli, Poisson, Gamma, Beta — they're all special cases of the exponential family: p(x|θ) = h(x) exp(η(θ)·T(x) - A(θ)). This unifying framework gives you natural parameters, sufficient statistics, and conjugate priors for free. Once you learn the pattern, every new distribution in this family becomes a fill-in-the-blanks exercise.

You'll need this when: working with generalized linear models, variational inference, or Bayesian conjugate analysis — basically anytime distributions need to play nicely together.

Nonparametric Statistics

Nonparametric methods make minimal assumptions about the underlying distribution. Kernel density estimation smooths your data into a continuous distribution without assuming a parametric form. Rank tests (Mann-Whitney, Wilcoxon) compare groups using only the ordering of values, not their magnitudes — making them robust to outliers and non-normality.

You'll need this when: your data doesn't fit standard distributional assumptions, you need robust hypothesis tests, or you want to estimate densities without committing to a family.

Survival Analysis

Survival analysis models time-to-event data — how long until a customer churns, a machine fails, or a patient relapses. The key complication is censoring: some events haven't happened yet when you collect data. Kaplan-Meier curves, Cox proportional hazards, and hazard functions all handle this gracefully.

You'll need this when: predicting churn, equipment failure, or any duration-based outcome where you can't wait for every event to occur.

Causal Discovery Algorithms (PC, FCI)

Most ML finds correlations. Causal discovery algorithms try to learn actual causal structure from observational data. The PC algorithm uses conditional independence tests to build a causal graph. FCI extends this to handle unmeasured confounders. They won't give you certainty, but they'll narrow down which causal stories are consistent with your data.

You'll need this when: you want to go beyond prediction to understanding why — policy decisions, interventions, or building models that generalize under distribution shift.

Optimization

Mirror Descent

Mirror descent generalizes gradient descent by replacing the Euclidean distance with a Bregman divergence. Instead of "move in the direction of steepest descent in L2 space," it says "move in the direction that's steepest relative to the geometry of your problem." When your parameters live on a simplex (probability distributions), mirror descent with KL divergence is the natural choice — and it's equivalent to exponentiated gradient.

You'll need this when: optimizing over probability distributions, reading online learning theory, or working with non-Euclidean parameter spaces.

Proximal Operators

Gradient descent assumes your objective is smooth. But L1 regularization (|w|) isn't differentiable at zero, which is exactly where you want parameters to land for sparsity. Proximal operators handle non-smooth terms elegantly: take a gradient step on the smooth part, then apply the prox operator for the non-smooth part. For L1, the proximal operator is soft thresholding — it's what makes ISTA and FISTA work.

You'll need this when: implementing sparse models, LASSO at scale, or any optimization with non-differentiable regularizers.

Frank-Wolfe Algorithm

Most constrained optimization methods require projecting back onto the constraint set after each step, and projection can be expensive. The Frank-Wolfe algorithm (a.k.a. conditional gradient) avoids projection entirely — it instead solves a linear minimization over the constraint set, which is often much cheaper. The iterates are convex combinations of extreme points, so they stay feasible automatically.

You'll need this when: your constraint set is complex (matrix norm balls, polytopes) but linear optimization over it is easy — common in structured prediction and nuclear norm minimization.

Alternating Minimization

When your objective depends on two sets of variables and optimizing both jointly is hard, just alternate: fix one, optimize the other, repeat. This is the engine behind NMF (non-negative matrix factorization), the EM algorithm, matrix completion, and k-means clustering. It's not guaranteed to find global optima, but it's simple, often fast, and frequently good enough.

You'll need this when: factorizing matrices, fitting mixture models, or any problem that decomposes into two easier sub-problems.

Information Theory

Rate-Distortion Theory

How much can you compress data if you're willing to tolerate some loss? Rate-distortion theory gives the fundamental lower bound: for any distortion level D, there's a minimum number of bits R(D) you must use. It's the theoretical floor for lossy compression — JPEG, video codecs, and neural compression all operate in this framework.

You'll need this when: working on learned compression, understanding information bottleneck theory, or analyzing representation quality vs. compression tradeoffs.

Channel Capacity

Shannon's noisy channel theorem says there's a maximum rate at which you can communicate reliably over a noisy channel — the channel capacity. Below this rate, error-free communication is possible. Above it, errors are unavoidable. It's one of the most profound results in all of science, and the conceptual ancestor of every error-correcting code and communication system in existence.

You'll need this when: studying information-theoretic bounds on learning, understanding why certain noise levels are insurmountable, or working with communication-efficient distributed learning.

Rényi Entropy

Rényi entropy is a one-parameter family that generalizes Shannon entropy. At order α → 1 you get Shannon entropy; at α = 2 you get collision entropy; at α → ∞ you get min-entropy (worst-case uncertainty). Different orders emphasize different parts of the distribution's tail. Min-entropy, in particular, is the foundation of differential privacy guarantees.

You'll need this when: working with privacy-preserving ML, analyzing divergence bounds in PAC-Bayes theory, or when Shannon entropy doesn't capture the right notion of uncertainty for your problem.

f-Divergences

KL divergence is just one way to measure how different two distributions are. f-divergences are a whole family, parameterized by a convex function f: D_f(P||Q) = E_Q[f(dP/dQ)]. KL, reverse KL, χ² divergence, total variation, Hellinger distance — all f-divergences. The choice of f determines what kind of distribution mismatch you penalize most.

You'll need this when: understanding GAN theory (different GAN variants minimize different f-divergences), variational inference, or when KL divergence doesn't behave the way you need.

Numerical Computing

Automatic Mixed Precision Beyond fp16

fp16 and bfloat16 are just the beginning. INT8 quantization packs weights into 8-bit integers for inference, halving memory again with minimal accuracy loss. fp4 and NF4 (4-bit NormalFloat) push even further — QLoRA uses NF4 to fine-tune 65B-parameter models on a single GPU. The trend is clear: training will stay in higher precision, but inference is racing toward the lowest bit-width that doesn't break things.

You'll need this when: deploying large models on constrained hardware, running inference at scale, or fine-tuning models that shouldn't fit in memory but somehow do.

Kahan Summation

Adding a million small numbers to a running total? Floating-point rounding errors accumulate with every addition. Kahan summation keeps a separate compensation variable that tracks the rounding error from each step and corrects for it in the next. It's a beautifully simple algorithm that gives you nearly full-precision sums at almost no extra cost.

You'll need this when: you're writing custom gradient accumulators, computing running statistics over long sequences, or anytime naive summation is silently eating your precision.

Matrix-Free Methods

Sometimes your matrix is so large you can't store it, but you can compute matrix-vector products on the fly. Matrix-free methods — conjugate gradient, Lanczos, randomized SVD — only need this multiply operation, never the explicit matrix. Hessian-vector products in deep learning are computed this way (via a second backward pass), enabling second-order optimization without ever forming the full Hessian.

You'll need this when: working with Hessian-based optimization, large-scale kernel methods, or any setting where the matrix has structure you can exploit without materializing it.

Structured Matrices (Toeplitz, Circulant)

A Toeplitz matrix has constant diagonals. A circulant matrix is a Toeplitz matrix that wraps around. These structures appear in convolution operations — and because circulant matrices are diagonalized by the FFT, you can multiply them in O(n log n) instead of O(n²). This is why convolution in the frequency domain is fast, and it's the mathematical trick behind efficient long-sequence models.

You'll need this when: understanding why FFT-based convolution is fast, working with state space models, or implementing any algorithm that exploits Toeplitz structure for speed.
