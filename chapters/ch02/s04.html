Probability

Chapter 2: Mathematical Foundations Section 4 of 8
TL;DR

Probability is the language ML models speak. Every prediction is a distribution over outcomes, every loss function is a measure of distributional mismatch, and every training run is an attempt to recover the true data-generating distribution from finite samples. This section covers probability axioms, Bayes' theorem, the distributions you will meet repeatedly (Gaussian, Bernoulli, Beta, Dirichlet, and friends), expectation and variance, information theory (entropy, cross-entropy, KL divergence), concentration inequalities for generalization bounds, and MLE/MAP estimation. If you only learn one math chapter deeply, make it this one.

Probability Fundamentals

Every ML model is making probabilistic bets about data. When a classifier says "this email is 93% spam," it is assigning a probability to an event. When a language model picks the next word, it is sampling from a distribution over the entire vocabulary. To build or debug these systems, you need the underlying machinery. Let us start from the ground up.

Sample Space and Events

The sample space, usually written as the Greek letter Omega, is the set of all possible outcomes of an experiment. If you flip a coin, the sample space is {heads, tails}. If you roll a six-sided die, it is {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}. In ML, the "experiment" is often something like "observe a data point," and the sample space might be every possible image of size 224 by 224 pixels, which is staggeringly large but still finite in principle. An event is any subset of the sample space. "The die shows an even number" is the event {2, 4, 6}. Events are the things we assign probabilities to.

Probability Axioms

Andrey Kolmogorov formalized probability with three axioms that every probability function P must satisfy. First, non-negativity: for any event A, P(A) is greater than or equal to zero. Probabilities are never negative. Second, normalization: P(Omega) = 1, meaning the probability that something from the sample space happens is exactly one. Third, additivity: if two events A and B have no outcomes in common (they are mutually exclusive), then P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B). These three rules sound obvious, but they are powerful. Everything else in probability theory follows from them.

Frequentist vs Bayesian Interpretation

There are two major camps in how to interpret what a probability "means." The frequentist interpretation says probability is the long-run frequency of an event. If you flip a fair coin a million times, roughly half will be heads, so P(heads) = 0.5. This view works well for repeatable experiments but struggles with one-off events. What is the "probability" that a particular startup succeeds? You cannot repeat that experiment.

The Bayesian interpretation says probability is a degree of belief. P(heads) = 0.5 means you are equally uncertain about heads and tails. Under this view, you can assign probabilities to anything, including one-off events, hypotheses about the world, or model parameters. Bayesian reasoning updates beliefs when new evidence arrives, using Bayes' theorem. Most modern ML has a Bayesian flavor, even when the algorithms themselves use frequentist shortcuts. Understanding both perspectives is essential.

Conditional Probability

Conditional probability is the probability of an event given that another event has occurred, written P(A|B). The formula is P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B), provided P(B) is not zero. This is arguably the single most important concept in probability for ML. Every time a model conditions on input features to predict an output, it is computing a conditional probability. A spam classifier estimates P(spam | email text). A language model estimates P(next word | previous words). Almost everything in ML is conditional.

The multiplication rule follows directly: P(A and B) = P(A|B) times P(B). This lets you decompose joint probabilities into conditionals and marginals. It works in both directions: P(A and B) = P(B|A) times P(A) as well, and setting these two expansions equal is exactly how we derive Bayes' theorem.

Independence

Two events A and B are independent if knowing one tells you nothing about the other. Formally, P(A|B) = P(A), or equivalently P(A and B) = P(A) times P(B). Independence is a strong assumption. In the real world, features are almost never truly independent, but we sometimes assume they are for computational convenience. Naive Bayes classifiers make exactly this assumption, treating all features as independent given the class label, and they work surprisingly well in practice despite the assumption being wildly wrong. Independence simplifies computation enormously, which is why it appears as an assumption throughout ML.

Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem connects conditional probabilities in two directions. Start with the multiplication rule written two ways: P(A and B) = P(A|B) times P(B) and P(A and B) = P(B|A) times P(A). Setting these equal and dividing by P(B) gives us: P(A|B) = P(B|A) times P(A) / P(B).

In ML language, A is often a hypothesis (like the model parameters) and B is the observed data. The prior P(A) captures what we believe before seeing data. The likelihood P(B|A) tells us how probable the data is under a particular hypothesis. The posterior P(A|B) is what we believe after seeing the data. The denominator P(B) is the evidence or marginal likelihood, which acts as a normalizing constant.

Here is a concrete example. Suppose a medical test for a rare disease has a 99% true positive rate and a 1% false positive rate, and the disease affects 0.1% of the population. You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease? The prior is P(disease) = 0.001. The likelihood is P(positive | disease) = 0.99. The false positive rate gives P(positive | no disease) = 0.01. Using the law of total probability, P(positive) = 0.99 times 0.001 + 0.01 times 0.999 = 0.01098. So P(disease | positive) = 0.99 times 0.001 / 0.01098, which is about 0.09 or 9%. Even with an excellent test, the low base rate means most positive results are false positives. This kind of reasoning is critical when you are evaluating ML models on imbalanced datasets.

Law of Total Probability

The law of total probability lets you compute the probability of an event by breaking it down over a partition of the sample space. If events B1, B2, ..., Bn are mutually exclusive and exhaustive (they cover the whole sample space), then P(A) = P(A|B1) times P(B1) + P(A|B2) times P(B2) + ... + P(A|Bn) times P(Bn). We used this implicitly in the medical test example above to compute P(positive). In ML, this appears whenever you marginalize over a latent variable, summing over all possible hidden states weighted by their probabilities.

Chain Rule of Probability

The chain rule of probability extends the multiplication rule to multiple variables. For three variables: P(A, B, C) = P(A) times P(B|A) times P(C|A, B). For n variables: P(X1, X2, ..., Xn) = P(X1) times P(X2|X1) times P(X3|X1, X2) times ... times P(Xn|X1, ..., Xn-1). This decomposition is exact and always valid. It is the foundation of autoregressive models like GPT. When a language model generates text, it factors the joint probability of a sentence into a product of conditional probabilities: P(word1) times P(word2|word1) times P(word3|word1, word2), and so on. The chain rule is not an approximation here. It is a mathematical identity.

Random Variables

A random variable is a function that maps outcomes in the sample space to numbers. Instead of talking about "the event that the die shows 3," we define a random variable X that takes the value of the die roll. This lets us use all the machinery of algebra and calculus on probabilistic outcomes. Random variables come in two flavors.

A discrete random variable takes on a countable number of values: the number of heads in 10 flips, the class label of an image, the count of words in a document. A continuous random variable takes on values in a continuous range: a person's height, the output of a neural network before a final activation, the value of a pixel in a normalized image.

PMF, PDF, and CDF

For discrete random variables, the probability mass function (PMF) gives the probability that X equals a specific value: P(X = x). For a fair die, P(X = 3) = 1/6. All the PMF values must be non-negative and sum to one.

For continuous random variables, we cannot assign non-zero probability to a single point (there are uncountably many). Instead, the probability density function (PDF) f(x) gives the density of probability at each point. The probability that X falls in an interval [a, b] is the integral of f(x) from a to b. The PDF can exceed one at a point, but the total area under the curve must equal one.

The cumulative distribution function (CDF) works for both types. F(x) = P(X <= x). It starts at zero, ends at one, and is non-decreasing. The CDF of a continuous random variable is the integral of the PDF from negative infinity to x. For discrete variables, it is a step function. The CDF is useful for computing probabilities of intervals and for generating random samples via inverse transform sampling.

Joint, Marginal, and Conditional Distributions

Joint distributions describe the probability of two or more random variables simultaneously. P(X = x, Y = y) for discrete variables, or f(x, y) for continuous ones. In ML, your data often has many features, and the joint distribution over all features is what your model is trying to learn or approximate.

Marginal distributions are what you get when you "integrate out" or "sum out" variables you do not care about. If you have a joint distribution P(X, Y) and only want P(X), you sum over all possible values of Y: P(X = x) = sum over y of P(X = x, Y = y). For continuous variables, replace the sum with an integral. Marginalization is how a generative model can answer questions about subsets of variables.

Conditional distributions describe one variable given another. P(Y | X) = P(X, Y) / P(X). In supervised learning, you model the conditional distribution of the output Y given the input X. The distinction between modeling the joint P(X, Y) (generative modeling) versus the conditional P(Y|X) (discriminative modeling) is a foundational design choice.

Distributions — The Main Event

Distributions are the vocabulary of probability. Each one models a different type of randomness, and knowing which distribution to reach for in a given situation is a superpower in ML. Let us go through the important ones, starting with discrete distributions and then moving to continuous ones.

Bernoulli Distribution

The Bernoulli distribution models a single binary outcome: success with probability p, failure with probability 1-p. Its PMF is P(X = 1) = p and P(X = 0) = 1-p. The parameter p is a single number between zero and one. In ML, Bernoulli shows up everywhere. Binary classification outputs follow a Bernoulli distribution. Dropout randomly sets neurons to zero with some probability, which is a Bernoulli trial per neuron per forward pass. Any time you have a yes/no outcome, you are in Bernoulli territory.

Binomial Distribution

The Binomial distribution counts the number of successes in n independent Bernoulli trials, each with success probability p. If you classify 100 test images and each classification is correct with probability 0.9, the number of correct predictions follows a Binomial(100, 0.9) distribution. The mean is np and the variance is np(1-p). This distribution is useful for reasoning about accuracy, error rates, and sample sizes in ML experiments.

Categorical Distribution

The Categorical distribution (sometimes called a Multinoulli) generalizes Bernoulli from two outcomes to K outcomes. A single draw produces one of K classes, with probabilities p1, p2, ..., pK that sum to one. The output of a softmax layer in a neural network defines a Categorical distribution over classes. When your model says "80% cat, 15% dog, 5% rabbit," it is parameterizing a Categorical distribution over three classes.

Multinomial Distribution

The Multinomial distribution extends Categorical the same way Binomial extends Bernoulli: it counts the number of times each of K outcomes appears in n independent trials. In text modeling, if you treat a document as a bag of words drawn independently from a vocabulary, the word counts follow a Multinomial distribution. Naive Bayes text classifiers, topic models, and many NLP pipelines use the Multinomial as a building block.

Poisson Distribution

The Poisson distribution models the count of events in a fixed interval when events occur independently at a constant average rate lambda. P(X = k) = (lambda^k times e^{-lambda}) / k!. If a website averages 5 visits per minute, the number of visits in a given minute is approximately Poisson(5). In ML, Poisson regression is used for count data: predicting the number of clicks, failures, or arrivals. The Poisson is also the limiting case of a Binomial with large n and small p.

Geometric Distribution

The Geometric distribution counts the number of Bernoulli trials needed to get the first success. With probability p of success on each trial, P(X = k) = (1-p)^{k-1} times p. It is the discrete analog of the exponential distribution. In ML, you might encounter it when modeling the number of attempts until a certain event, or in analyses of training convergence where you ask "how many epochs until the loss first drops below a threshold."

Uniform Distribution

The Uniform distribution assigns equal probability to all values in an interval [a, b]. Its PDF is 1/(b-a) for x in [a, b] and zero elsewhere. The mean is (a+b)/2 and the variance is (b-a)^2/12. In ML, uniform distributions appear in random initialization of parameters, random search over hyperparameters, and as a baseline "maximum ignorance" prior. Uniform random numbers are also the starting point for generating samples from any other distribution via inverse transform sampling.

Normal (Gaussian) Distribution

The Normal distribution, also called the Gaussian, is the most important distribution in ML. Its PDF is the familiar bell curve, parameterized by mean mu and variance sigma-squared: f(x) = (1 / sqrt(2 pi sigma^2)) times exp(-(x - mu)^2 / (2 sigma^2)). The mean controls the center, the variance controls the spread. About 68% of the probability mass lies within one standard deviation of the mean, 95% within two, and 99.7% within three.

Why is the Gaussian everywhere? The Central Limit Theorem says that the sum of many independent random variables tends toward a Gaussian regardless of the individual distributions. Since many real-world quantities are the result of many small additive effects, Gaussians arise naturally. In ML, weight initialization schemes (Xavier, He) sample from Gaussians. Gaussian noise is added during data augmentation and in diffusion models. Gaussian processes use multivariate Gaussians for non-parametric regression. The noise in stochastic gradient descent is approximately Gaussian. You will see this distribution more than any other.

Standard Normal Distribution

The Standard Normal is a Gaussian with mu = 0 and sigma = 1. Any Gaussian random variable X with mean mu and standard deviation sigma can be converted to a standard normal Z via Z = (X - mu) / sigma. These are Z-scores. Batch normalization and layer normalization in neural networks essentially standardize activations to have mean zero and unit variance, exploiting this transformation. The standard normal serves as the reference distribution for hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, and normalization throughout statistics and ML.

Multivariate Gaussian

The Multivariate Gaussian generalizes the Gaussian to multiple dimensions. It is parameterized by a mean vector mu and a covariance matrix Sigma. The covariance matrix captures not just the spread of each variable but also the correlations between variables. If Sigma is diagonal, the dimensions are independent. Off-diagonal entries encode linear dependencies. In ML, multivariate Gaussians are the backbone of Gaussian Mixture Models for clustering, Gaussian processes for regression, and the latent spaces of Variational Autoencoders. Linear Discriminant Analysis assumes class-conditional Gaussian distributions. Understanding the covariance matrix is essential for working with any of these methods.

Exponential Distribution

The Exponential distribution models the time between events in a Poisson process. Its PDF is lambda times exp(-lambda x) for x >= 0. It has a unique "memoryless" property: the probability of waiting another t time units is the same regardless of how long you have already waited. In ML, exponential distributions appear in survival analysis, where you model the time until an event like customer churn or equipment failure. The exponential is also a special case of the Gamma distribution.

Beta Distribution

The Beta distribution is defined on the interval [0, 1], making it perfect for modeling probabilities. It has two parameters, alpha and beta, that control the shape. When alpha = beta = 1, it is uniform. When alpha and beta are both large and equal, it is approximately Gaussian and centered at 0.5. When alpha > beta, it skews right toward 1. In Bayesian ML, the Beta is the conjugate prior for the Bernoulli parameter p. If you have a prior Beta(alpha, beta) and observe s successes and f failures, the posterior is Beta(alpha + s, beta + f). This makes Bayesian updating trivial. The Beta distribution shows up in Thompson sampling for multi-armed bandits, Bayesian A/B testing, and anywhere you need uncertainty estimates over a probability.

Gamma Distribution

The Gamma distribution is defined on positive reals and parameterized by shape k and rate (or scale). It generalizes the exponential distribution (Gamma with shape 1 is exponential). In Bayesian statistics, the Gamma is the conjugate prior for the Poisson rate parameter and for the precision (inverse variance) of a Gaussian. It also appears in modeling waiting times and as a prior over positive quantities like learning rates or regularization strengths.

Dirichlet Distribution

The Dirichlet distribution generalizes the Beta from a single probability to a probability vector. If you have K categories, a draw from a Dirichlet produces a vector of K non-negative numbers that sum to one, which is a valid probability distribution. The parameter is a vector of K positive concentration values. In ML, the Dirichlet is the conjugate prior for the Categorical distribution. It is central to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) for topic modeling, where each document's topic distribution is drawn from a Dirichlet. It also appears in Bayesian neural networks and any setting where you need a prior over discrete distributions.

Student's t-Distribution

The Student's t-distribution looks like a Gaussian but has heavier tails, controlled by a degrees-of-freedom parameter nu. As nu approaches infinity, the t-distribution converges to a Gaussian. With small nu, extreme values are more likely. In classical statistics, the t-distribution arises when estimating the mean of a normally distributed population from a small sample. In ML, t-distributions are used in robust regression (less sensitive to outliers than Gaussian assumptions), in t-SNE for visualization (the "t" stands for Student's t), and as a predictive distribution in Bayesian linear regression with unknown variance.

Chi-Squared Distribution

The Chi-squared distribution with k degrees of freedom is the distribution of the sum of k independent squared standard normal random variables. It is a special case of the Gamma distribution. In statistics, it appears in goodness-of-fit tests (does the observed data match the expected distribution?), in tests of independence for categorical data, and in confidence intervals for variance. In ML contexts, chi-squared tests can help with feature selection by testing whether a feature is independent of the target variable.

Expectation, Variance, and Covariance

Expected Value

The expected value (or mean) of a random variable is the weighted average of its possible values, weighted by their probabilities. For a discrete variable, E[X] = sum of x times P(X = x) over all x. For a continuous variable, E[X] = integral of x times f(x) dx. Intuitively, the expected value is what you would get "on average" if you repeated the experiment many times. In ML, the loss function you minimize during training is typically an expected value: the average loss over the data distribution.

Expectation has a crucial property: linearity. E[aX + bY] = a E[X] + b E[Y], always, even if X and Y are dependent. This makes expected values easy to work with algebraically. Linearity of expectation is the workhorse behind many proofs in ML theory and behind simple facts like "the expected total loss is the sum of expected per-sample losses."

Variance and Standard Deviation

Variance measures how spread out a random variable is around its mean: Var(X) = E[(X - E[X])^2] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2. A variance of zero means the variable is constant. Large variance means it takes values far from the mean frequently. The standard deviation is the square root of variance, and it has the same units as X, making it more interpretable.

Variance is not linear. Var(aX + b) = a^2 Var(X). If X and Y are independent, Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y). But if they are dependent, you also need the covariance. In ML, variance appears in the bias-variance tradeoff: a model with high variance overfits, changing drastically with different training sets. Understanding variance is also essential for initializing neural network weights (you want to preserve variance across layers) and for normalizing features.

Covariance and Correlation

Covariance measures how two random variables move together: Cov(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])] = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]. Positive covariance means they tend to increase together, negative means one increases while the other decreases, and zero covariance means no linear relationship (but not necessarily independence). If X and Y are independent, their covariance is zero, but the converse is not true.

The correlation coefficient normalizes covariance to the range [-1, 1]: Corr(X, Y) = Cov(X, Y) / (std(X) times std(Y)). Correlation of 1 or -1 means a perfect linear relationship. This is useful because covariance depends on the scale of the variables, while correlation does not.

For a vector of random variables, the covariance matrix Sigma is a matrix where entry (i, j) is Cov(Xi, Xj). The diagonal entries are the variances. The covariance matrix is symmetric and positive semi-definite. It appears in PCA (principal component analysis), where you eigendecompose the covariance matrix to find the directions of maximum variance. It is the second parameter of the multivariate Gaussian. It is how you decide which features are redundant in feature selection. If you understand the covariance matrix, you understand a large chunk of classical ML.

Key Theorems

Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) says that the sample average converges to the expected value as the sample size grows. If you flip a fair coin 10 times, you might get 7 heads. If you flip it 10,000 times, the fraction of heads will be very close to 0.5. This is why training on more data helps: your empirical loss (the average loss over training samples) becomes a better approximation of the true expected loss. The LLN also justifies using sample means, sample variances, and other sample statistics as estimates of their population counterparts. Monte Carlo methods, which approximate expectations by averaging over random samples, rely directly on the LLN.

Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is one of the most remarkable results in all of mathematics. It says that the average of n independent random variables, regardless of their original distribution, approaches a Gaussian as n grows. Even if the individual variables are exponential, uniform, or Bernoulli, their average will look Gaussian for large enough n. This is why the Gaussian is everywhere: any quantity that results from averaging or summing many small independent effects will be approximately normally distributed.

In ML, the CLT explains why stochastic gradient descent works well with mini-batches: the average gradient over a mini-batch is approximately Gaussian even if individual gradients are not. It also justifies many statistical tests that assume normality and helps explain why Gaussian weight initialization is a reasonable default.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# CLT: average of non-Gaussian samples → Gaussian
np.random.seed(42)
sample_sizes = [1, 2, 5, 30]

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 4, figsize=(16, 3))
for ax, n in zip(axes, sample_sizes):
    means = [np.random.exponential(2, n).mean() for _ in range(10000)]
    ax.hist(means, bins=50, density=True, alpha=0.7)
    ax.set_title(f"Mean of {n} exponentials")
    ax.set_xlim(0, 8)

plt.suptitle("Central Limit Theorem in Action")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Information Theory

Information theory, founded by Claude Shannon, gives us rigorous tools for measuring uncertainty and the "distance" between distributions. These concepts are not abstract curiosities. They are the mathematical foundations of the loss functions you use every day in ML.

Entropy

Entropy measures the average amount of surprise or uncertainty in a random variable. For a discrete distribution with probabilities p1, p2, ..., pK, the entropy is H = -sum of pi times log(pi). When we use the natural logarithm, entropy is measured in nats; with log base 2, in bits. A fair coin has maximum entropy (1 bit). A loaded coin that always lands heads has entropy zero because there is no uncertainty. A uniform distribution over K outcomes has maximum entropy log(K).

In ML, entropy measures how "spread out" or "uncertain" a probability distribution is. A model that assigns equal probability to all classes has high entropy (it is confused). A model that assigns probability 0.99 to one class has low entropy (it is confident). Entropy is also the theoretical lower bound on the average number of bits needed to encode samples from a distribution, which connects it to data compression.

Cross-Entropy

Cross-entropy measures the average surprise when using distribution q to encode samples from the true distribution p: H(p, q) = -sum of pi times log(qi). If q matches p perfectly, cross-entropy equals entropy. Otherwise, cross-entropy is always larger. The excess is exactly the KL divergence.

Here is the critical connection to ML: the standard classification loss function is cross-entropy loss. When your model outputs a distribution q over classes and the true label defines a distribution p (a one-hot vector), the cross-entropy reduces to -log(q_true_class). Minimizing cross-entropy loss is equivalent to maximizing the log-likelihood of the true labels under the model's predicted distribution. This is not a coincidence. It is the same mathematical objective viewed from two angles.

KL Divergence

KL divergence (Kullback-Leibler divergence) measures how much one distribution q differs from a reference distribution p: KL(p || q) = sum of pi times log(pi / qi). It is always non-negative (Gibbs' inequality) and equals zero only when p and q are identical. Critically, KL divergence is asymmetric: KL(p || q) is not equal to KL(q || p). This asymmetry matters in practice. KL(p || q) penalizes q for placing low probability where p has high probability (it is "zero-avoiding"). KL(q || p) penalizes q for placing high probability where p has low probability (it is "zero-forcing").

In ML, KL divergence is the regularization term in Variational Autoencoders, where it forces the approximate posterior to stay close to the prior. It appears in knowledge distillation, where a student model is trained to match the teacher's output distribution. And here is the key connection: minimizing cross-entropy H(p, q) over q is equivalent to minimizing KL(p || q), because H(p, q) = H(p) + KL(p || q) and H(p) is a constant with respect to q. So when you train a classifier with cross-entropy loss, you are implicitly minimizing the KL divergence from the true distribution to your model.

Mutual Information

Mutual information I(X; Y) measures how much knowing X tells you about Y (and vice versa). Formally, I(X; Y) = KL(P(X,Y) || P(X)P(Y)). If X and Y are independent, P(X,Y) = P(X)P(Y) and mutual information is zero. Mutual information is symmetric and always non-negative. In ML, it is used for feature selection (select features with high mutual information with the target), for evaluating clustering quality, and in InfoGAN where it encourages the generator to use the latent codes meaningfully.

Maximum Entropy Principle

The maximum entropy principle says that given a set of constraints (like known moments), the least biased distribution to assume is the one with maximum entropy. If you know nothing, assume uniform. If you know the mean and variance, the maximum entropy distribution is Gaussian. If you know the mean and the variable is positive, it is exponential. This principle provides a principled way to choose distributions and is the theoretical justification for many modeling choices in ML.

import numpy as np
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

# Manual cross-entropy
def cross_entropy(logits, target):
    """Cross-entropy from raw logits (numerically stable)."""
    # Log-softmax for stability
    log_probs = logits - np.log(np.exp(logits).sum())
    return -log_probs[target]

logits = np.array([2.0, 1.0, 0.1])
target = 0  # true class

manual_loss = cross_entropy(logits, target)
torch_loss = F.cross_entropy(
    torch.tensor(logits).unsqueeze(0),
    torch.tensor([target])
)

print(f"Manual cross-entropy: {manual_loss:.4f}")
print(f"PyTorch cross-entropy: {torch_loss.item():.4f}")
# They match!

Conjugate Priors

In Bayesian inference, you combine a prior distribution with observed data to get a posterior distribution. In general, this requires computing an integral that may not have a closed-form solution. Conjugate priors are a special class of priors where the posterior has the same distributional family as the prior. This gives you closed-form Bayesian updates, making inference fast and exact.

Bernoulli-Beta Conjugate Pair

If your data comes from a Bernoulli distribution with unknown probability p, and your prior on p is Beta(alpha, beta), then after observing s successes and f failures, the posterior is Beta(alpha + s, beta + f). The prior "pseudo-counts" alpha and beta act as if you had already seen alpha - 1 successes and beta - 1 failures before collecting data. As you observe more data, the posterior concentrates around the true value of p. This is the simplest and most intuitive example of Bayesian updating.

Gaussian-Gaussian Conjugate Pair

If your data comes from a Gaussian with unknown mean mu and known variance sigma-squared, and your prior on mu is Gaussian(mu_0, sigma_0^2), then the posterior on mu is also Gaussian. The posterior mean is a weighted average of the prior mean and the sample mean, with weights determined by the relative precisions (inverse variances). More data means the sample mean dominates; less data means the prior matters more. This is how Bayesian linear regression works under the hood.

Poisson-Gamma Conjugate Pair

If your data comes from a Poisson with unknown rate lambda, and your prior on lambda is Gamma(alpha, beta), then after observing n data points with total count T, the posterior is Gamma(alpha + T, beta + n). The Gamma prior encodes your belief about the event rate before seeing data, and the posterior smoothly incorporates the evidence.

Conjugate priors are computationally convenient, but they do constrain the form of your prior beliefs. In practice, if a conjugate prior does not match your actual beliefs, you should use a more appropriate prior and resort to approximate inference methods like MCMC or variational inference.

Gaussian Mixture Models

A Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) models data as coming from a mixture of K Gaussian distributions, each with its own mean and covariance. The probability of a data point x is: P(x) = sum over k of pi_k times N(x | mu_k, Sigma_k), where pi_k are the mixing coefficients (they sum to one) and N denotes the Gaussian PDF. Each data point is assumed to have been generated by first choosing a component k with probability pi_k and then sampling from that component's Gaussian.

GMMs are a fundamental clustering method. Unlike K-means, which assigns each point to exactly one cluster, GMMs give soft assignments: a probability of belonging to each cluster. This is more appropriate when clusters overlap. The parameters of a GMM are typically estimated using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which alternates between computing the responsibilities (how much each component "claims" each data point) and updating the component parameters to maximize the likelihood. EM is guaranteed to increase the likelihood at each step but may converge to a local maximum. GMMs are used in speech recognition, anomaly detection, and as a building block in more complex generative models.

Concentration Inequalities

Concentration inequalities tell you how close a random variable is likely to be to its expected value. They are the mathematical tools behind generalization bounds in learning theory, answering questions like "if the model performs well on training data, how confident can we be that it performs well on unseen data?"

Markov's and Chebyshev's Inequalities

Markov's inequality is the simplest: for a non-negative random variable X, P(X >= a) <= E[X] / a. It requires only the mean and gives a loose bound. Chebyshev's inequality is tighter: P(|X - E[X]| >= k sigma) <= 1/k^2. It uses both the mean and variance. For example, no matter what the distribution looks like, at least 75% of the probability mass lies within two standard deviations of the mean. These are crude bounds but they apply to any distribution.

Hoeffding's Inequality

Hoeffding's inequality is much tighter for bounded random variables. If X1, ..., Xn are independent random variables bounded in [a_i, b_i], the probability that the sample mean deviates from the expected value by more than t decays exponentially in n and t^2. This is directly relevant to ML: it tells you that the empirical risk (average loss on training data) converges to the true risk (expected loss on the data distribution) exponentially fast as the training set grows. Hoeffding's inequality is a key ingredient in PAC learning (Probably Approximately Correct), which provides formal guarantees about when and how well ML algorithms can learn.

Chernoff Bound, McDiarmid's Inequality, and Sub-Gaussians

The Chernoff bound uses moment-generating functions to get exponentially tight tail bounds. It is often the sharpest bound for sums of independent random variables. McDiarmid's inequality generalizes Hoeffding to functions of independent variables where changing any single variable does not change the function value by more than a bounded amount. This is useful for analyzing algorithms where the output depends on many data points but is not too sensitive to any single one.

A random variable is sub-Gaussian if its tails decay at least as fast as a Gaussian's. Sub-Gaussian concentration is the backbone of high-dimensional statistics and modern learning theory. Many quantities in ML, like the average of bounded random variables, are sub-Gaussian, which gives us the exponential tail bounds we need for generalization guarantees. If you ever read a paper on learning theory or statistical learning, concentration inequalities will be the primary technical tool.

MLE and MAP Estimation

Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) finds the parameter values that make the observed data most probable. Given data x1, ..., xn and a model with parameter theta, the likelihood is L(theta) = product of P(xi | theta). In practice, we maximize the log-likelihood instead: log L(theta) = sum of log P(xi | theta). The log turns products into sums, which is numerically stable and easier to differentiate. Setting the derivative of the log-likelihood to zero gives the MLE estimate.

For a Gaussian with unknown mean and variance, the MLE estimates are just the sample mean and sample variance (using N in the denominator, not N-1). For a Bernoulli, the MLE of p is the fraction of successes. MLE is the default estimation method in most of ML. When you train a neural network by minimizing cross-entropy loss, you are performing MLE on the parameters of the model.

MAP Estimation

Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation adds a prior to MLE. Instead of maximizing L(theta), you maximize P(theta | data), which by Bayes' theorem is proportional to P(data | theta) times P(theta). Taking the log, you maximize log P(data | theta) + log P(theta). The first term is the log-likelihood (same as MLE), and the second is the log-prior, which acts as a regularization term.

This gives a beautiful interpretation of regularization. If your prior on each parameter is a Gaussian centered at zero (P(theta_i) proportional to exp(-theta_i^2 / (2 tau^2))), the log-prior becomes a sum of squared parameters, which is L2 regularization (weight decay). If your prior is Laplacian (P(theta_i) proportional to exp(-|theta_i| / b)), the log-prior becomes a sum of absolute values, which is L1 regularization (encouraging sparsity). So L2 and L1 regularization are not ad-hoc tricks. They are MAP estimation with specific priors. This connection between Bayesian inference and regularization is one of the deepest insights in ML.

import numpy as np

# Generate data from a Gaussian
true_mu, true_sigma = 5.0, 2.0
data = np.random.normal(true_mu, true_sigma, 1000)

# MLE estimates (derived by setting derivative of log-likelihood to zero)
mu_mle = data.mean()
sigma_mle = data.std()  # uses N, not N-1

print(f"True: μ={true_mu}, σ={true_sigma}")
print(f"MLE:  μ={mu_mle:.3f}, σ={sigma_mle:.3f}")
