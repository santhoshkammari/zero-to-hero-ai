Probability & Statistics

Chapter 2: Mathematical Foundations Section 4 of 8
TL;DR

Probability is the language ML models think in. Every prediction is a probability distribution. Every loss function is a negative log-likelihood in disguise. Bayes' theorem tells you how to update beliefs when new data arrives — and its mechanics underpin everything from spam filters to large language models. Distributions are your toolkit: Gaussians dominate because of the Central Limit Theorem, Beta and Dirichlet distributions are your go-to priors, and understanding when to reach for each one separates fluent practitioners from people who just call sklearn.fit(). MLE gives you point estimates by maximizing likelihood; MAP adds a prior and gives you regularization for free (Gaussian prior = L2, Laplace prior = L1). Hypothesis testing tells you whether your model improvement is real or noise. This section is the probabilistic backbone that holds together everything else in this book.

Probability Fundamentals

The sample space Ω is the set of all possible outcomes of an experiment. An event is a subset of the sample space. Probability assigns a number between 0 and 1 to each event, following three axioms: (1) P(A) >= 0 for any event A, (2) P(Ω) = 1, and (3) for mutually exclusive events, P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B). Everything else in probability theory is built on these three axioms. If you have ever wondered why probabilities must add to one — it is axiom 2. If you have wondered why P(not A) = 1 - P(A) — it follows from axioms 2 and 3.

Frequentist vs Bayesian

Two camps interpret probability differently. Frequentists say probability is the long-run frequency of an event: flip a coin a million times, the fraction of heads converges to the probability of heads. Bayesians say probability is a degree of belief: before flipping the coin at all, you can assign a probability to heads based on your prior knowledge about the coin. In ML, this distinction matters. MLE is frequentist — it finds the parameters that best explain the observed data. MAP and full Bayesian inference are Bayesian — they incorporate prior beliefs and produce distributions over parameters, not point estimates. Most modern ML is pragmatic: use whichever interpretation gives you a better model.

Conditional Probability

This is where probability gets interesting and where most mistakes happen. The conditional probability P(A|B) is the probability of event A given that event B has occurred. The formula is:

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)

Read it as: out of all the worlds where B happened, what fraction also had A? That denominator P(B) is doing critical work — it restricts the universe to only the outcomes where B is true. This is exactly what happens when you condition on data in ML. You have a model with parameters θ, you observe data D, and you want P(θ|D) — what should I believe about my parameters given what I have seen?

Here is the trap that catches even experienced practitioners. P(A|B) is not the same as P(B|A). The probability that a person has a fever given they have the flu is high. The probability that a person has the flu given they have a fever is much lower — fevers have many causes. Confusing the two is called the prosecutor's fallacy, and it has literally sent innocent people to prison. In ML terms: the probability of observing your data given a particular model (the likelihood) is not the same as the probability of the model given the data (the posterior). Bayes' theorem is what converts between them.

The Conditioning Trap: Whenever you see P(A|B), ask yourself: "Am I sure I'm not accidentally thinking about P(B|A)?" This single confusion causes more errors in probabilistic reasoning than any other mistake.

Independence

Two events A and B are independent if knowing that one occurred gives you no information about the other: P(A|B) = P(A), or equivalently, P(A ∩ B) = P(A) * P(B). Flipping two separate fair coins gives independent events. Independence is a strong assumption, and ML uses it constantly. Naive Bayes assumes all features are conditionally independent given the class label — obviously false for most real data, yet it works surprisingly well because the classification boundary is robust even when the probability estimates are poor.

Conditional independence is more nuanced: A and B are conditionally independent given C if P(A ∩ B | C) = P(A|C) * P(B|C). Two symptoms might be correlated in the general population but conditionally independent given the underlying disease. Graphical models (Bayesian networks) are built entirely on conditional independence assumptions — they factor a joint distribution into smaller, manageable pieces.

Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem is arguably the single most important equation in machine learning. It combines conditional probability with the law of total probability to reverse the direction of conditioning:

P(θ|D) = P(D|θ) * P(θ) / P(D)

Each piece has a name and a role:

TermNameWhat It Represents
P(θ|D)PosteriorWhat you believe about θ after seeing data D
P(D|θ)LikelihoodHow probable the data is under a specific θ
P(θ)PriorWhat you believed about θ before seeing any data
P(D)EvidenceTotal probability of data across all possible θ (normalizing constant)

The posterior is proportional to the likelihood times the prior. The evidence P(D) is just a normalizing constant that ensures the posterior integrates to one. In many applications, you never compute it explicitly — you work with the unnormalized posterior and normalize at the end (or avoid normalization entirely, as in MAP estimation).

Here is the deep intuition. The prior encodes what you knew before the experiment. The likelihood encodes what the data tells you. The posterior is their compromise. With little data, the prior dominates — your beliefs barely budge. With lots of data, the likelihood dominates — the data overwhelms your prior. This is exactly the behavior you want: be cautious with small samples, be data-driven with large ones.

Let us make this concrete with the classic medical test example, which illustrates the base rate fallacy — one of the most counterintuitive results in all of probability.

Scenario: a disease affects 1 in 1,000 people. A test for the disease has 99% sensitivity (P(positive|disease) = 0.99) and 95% specificity (P(negative|no disease) = 0.95). You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease?

Most people guess 99% or 95%. The real answer is about 2%. Let us see why.

import numpy as np

# --- Bayes' theorem: medical test example ---
prevalence = 0.001           # P(disease) — the prior (base rate)
sensitivity = 0.99           # P(positive | disease) — true positive rate
specificity = 0.95           # P(negative | no disease) — true negative rate
false_positive_rate = 1 - specificity  # P(positive | no disease) = 0.05

# P(positive) — total probability of testing positive (the evidence)
p_positive = (sensitivity * prevalence) + (false_positive_rate * (1 - prevalence))
# = 0.99 * 0.001 + 0.05 * 0.999
# = 0.00099 + 0.04995
# = 0.05094

# Bayes' theorem: P(disease | positive)
p_disease_given_positive = (sensitivity * prevalence) / p_positive

print(f"P(positive)        = {p_positive:.5f}")
print(f"P(disease|positive) = {p_disease_given_positive:.4f}")
# P(disease|positive) ≈ 0.0194 — less than 2%!

# Why? Out of 100,000 people:
n = 100_000
sick = int(n * prevalence)          # 100 actually have the disease
healthy = n - sick                   # 99,900 are healthy
true_positives = int(sick * sensitivity)        # 99 correctly flagged
false_positives = int(healthy * false_positive_rate)  # 4,995 false alarms

print(f"\nOut of {n:,} people:")
print(f"  True positives:  {true_positives}")
print(f"  False positives: {false_positives}")
print(f"  Total positives: {true_positives + false_positives}")
print(f"  Precision:       {true_positives / (true_positives + false_positives):.4f}")

The key insight: when the base rate (prior) is very low, even a highly accurate test produces far more false positives than true positives. Out of ~5,094 positive tests, only ~99 actually have the disease. The false alarms swamp the real cases. This is exactly why you should not train a fraud detection model on data where fraud is 0.01% of transactions and then celebrate 99% accuracy — the model could be predicting "not fraud" for everything.

In ML terms: Base rate fallacy is why class imbalance destroys naive accuracy metrics. A spam filter with 0.1% spam prevalence can hit 99.9% accuracy by never predicting spam. Precision and recall exist precisely to combat this — they are Bayesian reasoning applied to classifier evaluation.

Law of Total Probability

If you have a set of mutually exclusive, exhaustive events B1, B2, ..., Bn (they partition the sample space), then for any event A:

P(A) = P(A|B1)*P(B1) + P(A|B2)*P(B2) + ... + P(A|Bn)*P(Bn)

This is what we used in the denominator of Bayes' theorem above — P(positive) was computed by summing over the two cases: have the disease and do not have the disease. The law of total probability lets you break a complicated probability into simpler conditional pieces. In ML, you use it when marginalizing: P(y) = Σ_x P(y|x)*P(x) for discrete variables, or the integral for continuous ones. Every time you sum out a latent variable, you are applying this law.

Chain Rule of Probability

The chain rule (not to be confused with the calculus chain rule) lets you decompose any joint probability into a product of conditionals:

P(A, B, C) = P(A) * P(B|A) * P(C|A, B)

For n variables: P(x1, x2, ..., xn) = P(x1) * P(x2|x1) * P(x3|x1,x2) * ... * P(xn|x1,...,xn-1). This is not an assumption — it is a mathematical identity that always holds. The magic happens when you choose a smart ordering.

This is exactly how autoregressive models work. GPT generates text by factoring the probability of a sequence as: P(token1) * P(token2|token1) * P(token3|token1,token2) * ... Each token is predicted conditioned on all previous tokens. The chain rule guarantees this factorization is exact — no information is lost. The model just needs to be good at estimating each conditional. WaveNet does the same for audio. PixelCNN does it for images, predicting each pixel conditioned on all previously generated pixels.

Random Variables

A random variable is a function that maps outcomes in the sample space to numbers. It is the bridge between abstract probability (events like "the coin lands heads") and the numbers we actually compute with. Discrete random variables take on countable values (number of heads in 10 flips). Continuous random variables take on uncountable values in an interval (the exact weight of a randomly chosen person).

PMF, PDF, CDF

A discrete random variable is described by its probability mass function (PMF): P(X = x) for each possible value x. The values are non-negative and sum to one. A continuous random variable is described by its probability density function (PDF): f(x). The PDF is not a probability — it can be greater than 1. Probabilities come from integrating the PDF over an interval: P(a < X < b) = ∫[a,b] f(x) dx. The probability of any single exact value is zero for continuous variables.

The cumulative distribution function (CDF) works for both discrete and continuous: F(x) = P(X ≤ x). It starts at 0, ends at 1, and is non-decreasing. The CDF of a continuous variable is the integral of its PDF. The CDF is underrated in practice — it tells you directly what fraction of your data falls below a threshold, which is useful for percentile calculations, hypothesis testing, and generating random samples via the inverse CDF method.

Joint, Marginal, and Conditional Distributions

When you have two random variables X and Y, their joint distribution P(X, Y) describes the probability of every (x, y) pair. The marginal distribution P(X) is obtained by summing (or integrating) out Y: P(X = x) = Σ_y P(X = x, Y = y). The conditional distribution P(Y|X) tells you the distribution of Y given a specific value of X.

This connects directly to the fundamental split in ML models. Discriminative models learn P(Y|X) directly — given features X, what is the distribution over labels Y? Logistic regression, neural networks, SVMs (with probabilistic outputs). Generative models learn the joint P(X, Y) = P(X|Y) * P(Y), which means they model how the data was generated. Naive Bayes, GANs, VAEs, diffusion models. The generative approach lets you sample new data, detect outliers, and handle missing features — but it requires modeling the much harder distribution P(X), the distribution over the entire input space.

The Gaussian (Normal) Distribution

The Gaussian distribution is parameterized by mean μ and variance σ²: f(x) = (1 / sqrt(2πσ²)) * exp(-(x-μ)² / (2σ²)). It shows up everywhere in ML, and this is not a coincidence — the Central Limit Theorem guarantees that sums of many independent random variables converge to a Gaussian regardless of the original distribution.

Why does ML care so much about Gaussians?

  • Weight initialization: We initialize neural network weights from N(0, σ²) with carefully chosen σ (He initialization uses σ² = 2/n_in).
  • Noise models: Assuming Gaussian noise in regression is equivalent to minimizing mean squared error — the MSE loss is the negative log-likelihood of a Gaussian.
  • Latent spaces: VAEs force the latent space to be Gaussian, which enables smooth interpolation and sampling.
  • Batch normalization: Normalizes activations to approximately N(0, 1) within each mini-batch.
  • Gaussian processes: A fully Bayesian approach to regression where the prior over functions is a Gaussian.

The 68-95-99.7 rule: approximately 68% of values fall within 1σ of the mean, 95% within 2σ, and 99.7% within 3σ. This gives you quick mental math for outlier detection — anything beyond 3σ is suspicious.

The Multivariate Gaussian

The multivariate Gaussian generalizes to d dimensions. It is parameterized by a mean vector μ (d-dimensional) and a covariance matrix Σ (d × d, symmetric positive definite). The PDF is: f(x) = (1 / sqrt((2π)^d * det(Σ))) * exp(-0.5 * (x-μ)^T * Σ^(-1) * (x-μ)).

The covariance matrix Σ controls the shape and orientation of the probability cloud. Diagonal Σ means the dimensions are uncorrelated — the cloud is axis-aligned. Off-diagonal entries introduce correlations — the cloud tilts. The eigenvectors of Σ give the principal axes of the ellipsoid, and the eigenvalues give the variance along each axis. This is literally what PCA does: it finds the eigenvectors of the sample covariance matrix, then projects data onto the directions of highest variance.

The Mahalanobis distance (x-μ)^T * Σ^(-1) * (x-μ) is the natural distance metric for multivariate Gaussians. It accounts for correlations and different scales across dimensions. Euclidean distance treats all dimensions equally — Mahalanobis distance does not, which is why it is better for outlier detection in correlated data.

The Beta Distribution

The Beta distribution Beta(α, β) lives on [0, 1] — perfect for modeling probabilities. Its shape is controlled by two parameters α and β. When α = β = 1, it is uniform. When α > 1 and β > 1, it is unimodal (bell-shaped). When α < 1 and β < 1, it is U-shaped (bimodal at the boundaries). Think of α as "pseudo-counts of successes" and β as "pseudo-counts of failures" from imaginary prior observations.

The Beta distribution is the conjugate prior for the Bernoulli and Binomial likelihoods. This means that if your prior is Beta(α, β) and you observe k successes in n trials, the posterior is Beta(α + k, β + n - k). No integrals, no MCMC — the update is just adding counts. This is one of the few cases where Bayesian inference is analytically tractable, and it is beautiful: your prior belief (α, β) gets updated by simply accumulating evidence (k successes, n-k failures).

The Dirichlet Distribution

The Dirichlet distribution Dir(α1, α2, ..., αK) is the multivariate generalization of the Beta — it produces vectors that live on the probability simplex (non-negative entries that sum to one). It is the conjugate prior for the Categorical and Multinomial distributions. In Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), the Dirichlet prior is placed over topic-word distributions and document-topic distributions, which is where the model gets its name.

When all αk are equal and greater than 1, the Dirichlet concentrates mass on uniform-ish distributions. When all αk are less than 1, it concentrates mass on sparse distributions (most of the probability on a few categories). When αk vary, the distribution favors specific categories over others. This makes it a flexible tool for encoding your prior beliefs about how categorical probabilities should look.

Bernoulli, Binomial, Categorical, Poisson

The Bernoulli distribution is the simplest: a single coin flip with P(X=1) = p. Every binary classifier's output is a Bernoulli. The negative log-likelihood of a Bernoulli is the binary cross-entropy loss: -[y*log(p) + (1-y)*log(1-p)]. This is not a coincidence — the standard classification loss was derived from assuming Bernoulli outputs and applying MLE.

The Binomial counts successes in n independent Bernoulli trials. The Categorical generalizes Bernoulli to K classes — a single draw from a discrete distribution. The softmax output of a neural network defines a Categorical distribution, and the negative log-likelihood of a Categorical is the categorical cross-entropy loss. Again, the loss function you use every day is just MLE under a specific distributional assumption.

The Poisson distribution models counts of events that happen at a constant average rate. P(X=k) = λ^k * exp(-λ) / k!. It appears in NLP for modeling word counts and in any setting where you are counting occurrences in a fixed window of time or space. The Poisson is the limit of the Binomial when n is large and p is small (rare events).

Exponential, Gamma, Chi-squared, Uniform, Student-t, Multinomial, Geometric

The Exponential distribution models time between events in a Poisson process — memoryless, with rate parameter λ. The Gamma distribution generalizes it (the sum of k Exponentials is Gamma) and serves as a conjugate prior for the Poisson rate. Chi-squared is a special case of Gamma — the sum of squared standard normals — and drives goodness-of-fit and independence tests.

The Uniform assigns equal probability across an interval — the maximum-entropy distribution when you only know the range. The Student-t looks like a Gaussian but with heavier tails; it approaches the Gaussian as degrees of freedom increase and is used for robust regression and small-sample hypothesis testing. The Multinomial counts across K categories in n trials (Binomial generalized to K categories). The Geometric counts trials until the first success.

Expectation, Variance, Covariance

Expected Value

The expected value E[X] is the weighted average of all possible values, weighted by their probabilities. For discrete: E[X] = Σ x * P(X=x). For continuous: E[X] = ∫ x * f(x) dx. It is the "center of mass" of the distribution.

Linearity of expectation is one of the most powerful properties in probability: E[aX + bY] = a*E[X] + b*E[Y]. This holds always, even when X and Y are dependent. This is why the expected loss over a mini-batch equals the average of the expected losses per sample — no independence assumption needed.

Variance and Standard Deviation

Variance measures spread: Var(X) = E[(X - E[X])²] = E[X²] - (E[X])². The second form is often easier to compute. Standard deviation σ = sqrt(Var(X)) is in the same units as X. Key properties: Var(aX + b) = a² * Var(X) — constants get squared, shifts disappear. For independent variables: Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y). This is why averaging n independent samples reduces variance by a factor of n — the variance of the sample mean is σ²/n.

Covariance, Correlation, and the Covariance Matrix

Covariance measures how two variables move together: Cov(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])] = E[XY] - E[X]*E[Y]. Positive covariance means they tend to increase together. Negative means one increases as the other decreases. Zero covariance means no linear relationship — but they could still be nonlinearly dependent.

Correlation normalizes covariance to [-1, 1]: ρ(X, Y) = Cov(X, Y) / (σ_X * σ_Y). It measures the strength of the linear relationship, making it scale-invariant.

For a random vector X = [X1, ..., Xd], the covariance matrix Σ has entry (i,j) = Cov(Xi, Xj). It is symmetric and positive semi-definite. The diagonal entries are the variances. PCA decomposes this matrix: the eigenvectors give the principal components (directions of maximum variance) and the eigenvalues give the variance explained along each direction. When you "whiten" data, you multiply by Σ^(-1/2) to make the covariance the identity matrix — each dimension becomes uncorrelated with unit variance.

Key Theorems

Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) says that the sample mean converges to the true mean as the number of samples grows. Take n i.i.d. samples from any distribution with mean μ. As n → ∞, the sample mean (1/n) * Σ Xi → μ with probability 1 (the strong law). This is the mathematical justification for using empirical averages as estimators. It is also why more training data helps — the empirical risk (average loss over training data) converges to the true risk (expected loss over the data distribution) as n grows.

Monte Carlo estimation relies on LLN: if you cannot compute an integral analytically, draw samples and average. The more samples, the better the approximation. This is the backbone of MCMC, importance sampling, and every simulation-based method in ML.

Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is one of the deepest results in probability. Take n i.i.d. samples from any distribution with mean μ and finite variance σ². The distribution of the sample mean is approximately Gaussian with mean μ and variance σ²/n, regardless of the original distribution. As n grows, the approximation improves.

The CLT explains why Gaussians are everywhere. Heights are the sum of many genetic and environmental factors — Gaussian. Measurement errors are the sum of many small independent sources — Gaussian. The gradients in mini-batch SGD are averages over batch_size samples, so by CLT, the stochastic gradient is approximately Gaussian around the true gradient. This has a profound implication: the noise in SGD is approximately Gaussian, and the variance decreases as 1/batch_size. Larger batches give more accurate gradient estimates but with diminishing returns — doubling the batch size only reduces gradient variance by half.

import numpy as np

# --- CLT in action: averages of non-Gaussian data become Gaussian ---
np.random.seed(42)
n_experiments = 10_000

# Source: heavily skewed Exponential distribution (definitely not Gaussian)
lam = 2.0
true_mean = 1 / lam  # = 0.5
true_var = 1 / lam**2  # = 0.25

for batch_size in [1, 5, 30, 100]:
    # Each experiment: draw batch_size samples, compute mean
    means = [np.mean(np.random.exponential(1/lam, batch_size))
             for _ in range(n_experiments)]
    means = np.array(means)
    print(f"batch_size={batch_size:3d}  "
          f"sample_mean={np.mean(means):.4f} (true={true_mean:.4f})  "
          f"sample_std={np.std(means):.4f} (CLT predicts={np.sqrt(true_var/batch_size):.4f})")

MLE, MAP, and Estimation Theory

Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

MLE finds the parameter values that make the observed data most probable. Given data D = {x1, ..., xn} assumed i.i.d. from a distribution with parameter θ, the likelihood is: L(θ) = P(D|θ) = Π_i P(xi|θ). MLE finds θ* = argmax_θ L(θ).

In practice, you always work with the log-likelihood because products become sums: log L(θ) = Σ_i log P(xi|θ). Maximizing the log-likelihood is equivalent to maximizing the likelihood (log is monotonic), but sums are numerically stable and have nicer gradients.

Here is the punchline that connects MLE to every loss function you have ever used:

Model AssumptionNegative Log-LikelihoodResulting Loss Function
Gaussian noise on yΣ (yi - ŷi)² / (2σ²) + constMean Squared Error
Bernoulli output-Σ [yi*log(pi) + (1-yi)*log(1-pi)]Binary Cross-Entropy
Categorical output-Σ log(p_yi)Categorical Cross-Entropy

When you minimize cross-entropy loss, you are doing MLE under a Bernoulli or Categorical assumption. When you minimize MSE, you are doing MLE under a Gaussian noise assumption. Understanding this connection means you can derive the right loss function for any distributional assumption, rather than memorizing loss functions as arbitrary formulas.

Code: MLE Estimation

import numpy as np

np.random.seed(42)

# --- MLE for Gaussian parameters: estimate μ and σ² from data ---
true_mu, true_sigma = 5.0, 2.0

# Watch estimates converge as sample size grows
for n in [10, 50, 200, 1000, 10000]:
    data = np.random.normal(true_mu, true_sigma, n)

    # MLE estimates for Gaussian:
    # μ_MLE = sample mean, σ²_MLE = sample variance (biased)
    mu_mle = np.mean(data)
    sigma2_mle = np.mean((data - mu_mle)**2)  # MLE uses 1/n, not 1/(n-1)

    print(f"n={n:5d}  μ_MLE={mu_mle:.4f} (true={true_mu})  "
          f"σ²_MLE={sigma2_mle:.4f} (true={true_sigma**2:.1f})")

# --- Log-likelihood as a function of μ (with σ fixed) ---
data = np.random.normal(true_mu, true_sigma, 100)

def log_likelihood(mu, data, sigma=true_sigma):
    """Gaussian log-likelihood as a function of mu."""
    n = len(data)
    return -n/2 * np.log(2 * np.pi * sigma**2) - np.sum((data - mu)**2) / (2 * sigma**2)

# Evaluate over a grid — the peak should be at the sample mean
mu_grid = np.linspace(2, 8, 200)
ll_values = [log_likelihood(mu, data) for mu in mu_grid]
best_mu = mu_grid[np.argmax(ll_values)]
print(f"\nGrid search peak: μ={best_mu:.3f}, sample mean: {np.mean(data):.3f}")

Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) Estimation

MAP adds a prior to MLE. Instead of maximizing just the likelihood, MAP maximizes the posterior: θ_MAP = argmax_θ P(θ|D) = argmax_θ P(D|θ) * P(θ). Taking the log: θ_MAP = argmax_θ [log P(D|θ) + log P(θ)]. The first term is the log-likelihood (the data fit). The second term is the log-prior (your regularization).

This is where the famous regularization connections come from:

Prior on WeightsLog-PriorRegularization Effect
Gaussian: P(w) ∝ exp(-w²/(2σ²))-Σ wi² / (2σ²)L2 regularization (weight decay)
Laplace: P(w) ∝ exp(-|w|/b)-Σ |wi| / bL1 regularization (sparsity)

A Gaussian prior says "I believe weights are probably small, centered at zero" — it penalizes large weights quadratically. A Laplace prior says the same but penalizes linearly, which drives many weights exactly to zero (sparsity). Every time you add weight decay to a neural network, you are implicitly making a Bayesian assumption: the weights have a Gaussian prior. The regularization strength λ is inversely proportional to the prior variance σ² — a narrow prior (strong belief that weights are small) means heavy regularization.

MLE vs MAP vs Full Bayesian: MLE gives you a single best-fit parameter. MAP gives you a single best-fit parameter with regularization. Full Bayesian inference gives you the entire posterior distribution over parameters — no single answer, but a distribution of answers weighted by plausibility. In practice, most deep learning uses MLE or MAP (via regularization). Full Bayesian is used in Gaussian processes, Bayesian neural networks, and small-data regimes where uncertainty quantification matters.

Confidence Intervals

A 95% confidence interval is a range computed from the data such that, across many repetitions of the experiment, 95% of such intervals would contain the true parameter. It is not "95% probability that the true value is in this interval" — the true value is fixed, the interval is random. The distinction matters for interpretation, though in practice most people use the Bayesian credible interval interpretation (which is a probability statement about the parameter) and that is fine as long as you are consistent about it.

Fisher Information and Cramér-Rao Bound

The Fisher information I(θ) measures how much information the data carries about the parameter θ. Formally: I(θ) = E[(d/dθ log P(X|θ))²] = -E[d²/dθ² log P(X|θ)]. High Fisher information means the log-likelihood has sharp curvature near the true parameter — the data is very informative about θ.

The Cramér-Rao bound says that no unbiased estimator can have variance less than 1/I(θ). It sets a fundamental limit on estimation accuracy. An estimator that achieves this bound is called efficient. MLE is asymptotically efficient — for large samples, MLE achieves the Cramér-Rao bound. This is one reason MLE is so widely used: it is not just convenient, it is provably optimal (in the large-sample limit).

Method of Moments

An alternative to MLE: set the population moments equal to sample moments and solve for parameters. For a Gaussian, the first moment (mean) gives μ and the second central moment (variance) gives σ². It is simpler than MLE but often less efficient. In practice, MLE is almost always preferred, but method of moments can be useful as a quick initial estimate or when the likelihood is hard to compute.

Bayesian Inference

Prior → Likelihood → Posterior

The Bayesian workflow is always the same: (1) specify a prior P(θ) encoding your beliefs before seeing data, (2) specify a likelihood P(D|θ) encoding how data is generated given parameters, (3) apply Bayes' theorem to get the posterior P(θ|D). The posterior becomes your new belief about θ, incorporating both your prior knowledge and the evidence from data. If new data arrives, the current posterior becomes the prior for the next update. This sequential updating is one of the great strengths of the Bayesian framework — it is naturally incremental.

Conjugate Priors

A prior is conjugate to a likelihood if the posterior has the same functional form as the prior. Conjugate priors make Bayesian updating analytically tractable — no integrals to approximate. Here are the ones you should know:

LikelihoodConjugate PriorPosteriorUpdate Rule
Bernoulli / BinomialBeta(α, β)Beta(α + k, β + n - k)Add successes to α, failures to β
Gaussian (known σ²)Gaussian(μ₀, σ₀²)Gaussian(μ_n, σ_n²)Precision-weighted average of prior and data
PoissonGamma(α, β)Gamma(α + Σxi, β + n)Add total count to α, sample size to β
Categorical / MultinomialDirichlet(α₁,...,αK)Dirichlet(α₁+c₁,...,αK+cK)Add counts per category

Posterior Predictive Distribution

Once you have the posterior P(θ|D), you can predict new data by integrating out the parameter: P(x_new|D) = ∫ P(x_new|θ) * P(θ|D) dθ. This is the posterior predictive distribution. Unlike plug-in predictions (where you use a single point estimate of θ), the posterior predictive accounts for parameter uncertainty — if you are unsure about θ, your predictions will be wider. This is why Bayesian models naturally give you calibrated uncertainty estimates.

Code: Bayesian Updating (Beta-Binomial)

import numpy as np

# --- Bayesian updating: Beta-Binomial model ---
# Estimating the probability of a coin being heads.
# True probability (unknown to us): 0.7
true_p = 0.7

# Prior: Beta(2, 2) — mild belief that p is near 0.5
alpha, beta_param = 2.0, 2.0

print(f"Prior: Beta({alpha:.0f}, {beta_param:.0f})")
print(f"  Prior mean: {alpha / (alpha + beta_param):.3f}")
print(f"  Prior is mildly informative — we think p ≈ 0.5\n")

# Simulate data arriving in batches
np.random.seed(42)
batch_sizes = [5, 10, 20, 50, 100]

for batch in batch_sizes:
    flips = np.random.binomial(1, true_p, batch)
    heads = np.sum(flips)
    tails = batch - heads

    # Bayesian update: just add counts!
    alpha += heads
    beta_param += tails

    posterior_mean = alpha / (alpha + beta_param)
    # 95% credible interval from Beta distribution
    from scipy.stats import beta as beta_dist
    ci_low, ci_high = beta_dist.ppf([0.025, 0.975], alpha, beta_param)

    print(f"After {batch:3d} flips ({heads} heads): "
          f"Beta({alpha:.0f}, {beta_param:.0f})  "
          f"mean={posterior_mean:.3f}  "
          f"95% CI=[{ci_low:.3f}, {ci_high:.3f}]")

print(f"\nTrue p = {true_p}  |  Final estimate = {alpha/(alpha+beta_param):.3f}")
print("Notice: the credible interval shrinks as data accumulates.")

Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)

When conjugate priors are not available (which is most of the time in complex models), you cannot compute the posterior analytically. MCMC is the workhorse solution: construct a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the posterior, run it long enough, and the samples you collect approximate the posterior distribution.

The Metropolis-Hastings algorithm is the simplest MCMC method. At each step: (1) propose a new parameter value from a proposal distribution, (2) compute the acceptance ratio (ratio of posterior probabilities), (3) accept or reject the proposal with probability equal to the ratio. If the proposal has higher posterior probability, always accept. If lower, accept with some probability — this prevents the chain from getting stuck. The proposals you accept form your approximate posterior samples.

Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) is a more sophisticated variant that uses gradient information to make intelligent proposals, traveling along the posterior surface rather than randomly stumbling around. It explores high-dimensional posteriors much more efficiently than Metropolis-Hastings. PyMC and Stan use HMC variants (specifically NUTS — the No-U-Turn Sampler) under the hood.

Variational Inference

Variational Inference (VI) takes a completely different approach: instead of sampling from the posterior, approximate it with a simpler distribution and optimize the approximation. Choose a family of distributions q(θ; φ) (e.g., a factorized Gaussian), then find the parameters φ that make q as close to the true posterior as possible.

"Close" is measured by KL divergence: KL(q || p) = ∫ q(θ) * log(q(θ) / P(θ|D)) dθ. Minimizing this directly requires knowing P(θ|D), which is what we are trying to find. The trick: instead, maximize the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO): ELBO = E_q[log P(D|θ)] - KL(q || P(θ)). The first term says "the approximate posterior should explain the data." The second says "the approximate posterior should not stray too far from the prior." Maximizing ELBO is equivalent to minimizing KL divergence.

VI is the engine behind Variational Autoencoders (VAEs). The encoder maps data to an approximate posterior q(z|x) over latent variables. The decoder maps latent variables back to data via P(x|z). The VAE loss is exactly the negative ELBO: reconstruction loss (the likelihood term) plus KL divergence between the encoder output and the prior (typically a standard Gaussian). Now you know where the VAE loss function comes from — it is not ad hoc, it is variational inference.

Hypothesis Testing & A/B Testing

Null and Alternative Hypotheses

A hypothesis test formalizes the question: "Is this effect real, or could it be explained by random chance?" The null hypothesis H₀ is the boring explanation — no effect, no difference, the model improvement is just noise. The alternative hypothesis H₁ is what you are trying to show — there is a real effect. You never "prove" H₁; you either reject H₀ (strong evidence against the boring explanation) or fail to reject it (insufficient evidence).

p-values

The p-value is the probability of observing data at least as extreme as what you got, assuming the null hypothesis is true. A small p-value means "this data would be very unlikely if there were really no effect," which is evidence against H₀.

What p-values are NOT:
  • NOT the probability that H₀ is true. A p-value of 0.03 does not mean "3% chance the null is true."
  • NOT the probability that your result happened by chance.
  • NOT a measure of effect size. A tiny, meaningless effect can have a tiny p-value with enough data.
  • NOT comparable across different tests or sample sizes.
The p-value is a statement about the data under the null, not a statement about the null under the data. Confusing these is the conditional probability swap again — P(data|H₀) vs P(H₀|data).

Type I/II Errors and Power

H₀ True (no effect)H₀ False (real effect)
Reject H₀Type I Error (false positive, rate = α)Correct! (Power = 1 - β)
Fail to reject H₀Correct!Type II Error (false negative, rate = β)

Statistical power (1 - β) is the probability of detecting a real effect when it exists. Power depends on: (1) effect size — larger effects are easier to detect, (2) sample size — more data means more power, (3) significance level α — higher α gives more power but more false positives, (4) variance — lower variance makes effects stand out more. Before running an experiment, always do a power analysis to determine how many samples you need. Running underpowered experiments wastes resources and produces unreliable conclusions.

t-Tests for Model Comparison

When comparing two models on the same test set, use a paired t-test. For each test sample, compute the difference in performance (e.g., model A got it right and model B got it wrong, or the difference in losses). The paired test accounts for the fact that some samples are inherently harder than others — it tests whether the differences are systematically nonzero, not whether the raw scores differ.

Chi-squared and ANOVA

The Chi-squared test checks if observed frequencies differ from expected frequencies — useful for testing independence between categorical variables or goodness-of-fit. ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) tests whether the means of three or more groups differ; it generalizes the two-sample t-test. In ML, ANOVA occasionally appears in feature selection (testing whether a feature's mean differs across classes).

Multiple Testing Correction

If you run 20 hypothesis tests at α = 0.05, you expect one false positive even if every null is true. When testing many hypotheses simultaneously (e.g., which of 1,000 features are relevant), you must correct for multiple testing. Bonferroni correction divides α by the number of tests — simple but conservative. Benjamini-Hochberg controls the false discovery rate (FDR) instead of the family-wise error rate — it is less conservative and more appropriate when you expect some true positives. In practice, Benjamini-Hochberg is the standard in genomics, feature selection, and any setting with many parallel tests.

Code: Paired t-Test for Model Comparison

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

np.random.seed(42)

# --- Compare two models on the same 200-sample test set ---
n_test = 200

# Model A: 82% accuracy, Model B: 78% accuracy (but is the difference significant?)
# Simulate per-sample correctness (1 = correct, 0 = wrong)
# Models share some "easy" samples and some "hard" samples
difficulty = np.random.uniform(0, 1, n_test)  # lower = easier
model_a_correct = (difficulty + np.random.normal(0, 0.15, n_test) < 0.82).astype(int)
model_b_correct = (difficulty + np.random.normal(0, 0.15, n_test) < 0.78).astype(int)

acc_a = model_a_correct.mean()
acc_b = model_b_correct.mean()
print(f"Model A accuracy: {acc_a:.3f}")
print(f"Model B accuracy: {acc_b:.3f}")
print(f"Raw difference:   {acc_a - acc_b:.3f}\n")

# Paired t-test: test if per-sample differences are systematically nonzero
differences = model_a_correct - model_b_correct
t_stat, p_value = stats.ttest_rel(model_a_correct, model_b_correct)

print(f"Paired t-test:")
print(f"  Mean difference: {differences.mean():.4f}")
print(f"  t-statistic:     {t_stat:.3f}")
print(f"  p-value:         {p_value:.4f}")

if p_value < 0.05:
    print("  → Reject H₀: The difference is statistically significant at α=0.05")
else:
    print("  → Fail to reject H₀: No significant difference detected")

# Effect size: Cohen's d for paired samples
cohens_d = differences.mean() / differences.std()
print(f"  Cohen's d:       {cohens_d:.3f} ({'small' if abs(cohens_d)<0.5 else 'medium' if abs(cohens_d)<0.8 else 'large'})")

Descriptive Statistics & Resampling

You know these. Mean (average), median (middle value — robust to outliers), mode (most frequent value). Variance and standard deviation measure spread. Skewness measures asymmetry (income distributions are right-skewed). Kurtosis measures tail heaviness (high kurtosis = more outliers than a Gaussian). In practice, always look at the median alongside the mean — if they differ substantially, your distribution is skewed and the mean is misleading.

Bootstrap Confidence Intervals

The bootstrap is beautifully simple: to estimate the uncertainty of any statistic, resample your data with replacement many times, compute the statistic on each resample, and look at the distribution of results. The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the bootstrap distribution give you a 95% confidence interval. No distributional assumptions, no formulas — just resampling. It works for means, medians, model accuracies, AUC scores, anything.

import numpy as np

np.random.seed(42)

# --- Bootstrap confidence interval for model accuracy ---
# Suppose model predictions on a test set of 500 samples
n_test = 500
# Simulated: model gets ~85% accuracy
correct = np.random.binomial(1, 0.85, n_test)
observed_accuracy = correct.mean()

# Bootstrap: resample with replacement, compute accuracy each time
n_bootstrap = 10_000
boot_accuracies = np.array([
    np.random.choice(correct, size=n_test, replace=True).mean()
    for _ in range(n_bootstrap)
])

ci_low = np.percentile(boot_accuracies, 2.5)
ci_high = np.percentile(boot_accuracies, 97.5)

print(f"Observed accuracy:      {observed_accuracy:.4f}")
print(f"Bootstrap mean:         {boot_accuracies.mean():.4f}")
print(f"Bootstrap std:          {boot_accuracies.std():.4f}")
print(f"95% Bootstrap CI:       [{ci_low:.4f}, {ci_high:.4f}]")
print(f"\nInterpretation: we are 95% confident the true accuracy")
print(f"lies between {ci_low:.1%} and {ci_high:.1%}.")

Permutation Tests

Permutation tests assess significance by randomly shuffling labels and seeing how extreme your observed statistic is compared to the shuffled distribution. Want to know if two models differ? Randomly swap which predictions belong to model A vs B many times. If the observed difference is in the top 5% of the shuffled distribution, reject the null at α=0.05. Like bootstrap, permutation tests are nonparametric — no distributional assumptions. They are especially useful when the data does not meet the assumptions of parametric tests.

Concentration Inequalities

Concentration inequalities bound how far a random variable can deviate from its expected value. They are the theoretical backbone of generalization bounds in learning theory.

Markov's inequality: For a non-negative random variable, P(X >= a) <= E[X] / a. Very loose, but requires almost no assumptions. Chebyshev's inequality: P(|X - μ| >= kσ) <= 1/k². Uses variance information to give a tighter bound. Still distribution-free.

Hoeffding's inequality is the workhorse in learning theory. For n independent bounded random variables in [a, b] with sample mean X̄ and true mean μ: P(|X̄ - μ| >= t) <= 2 * exp(-2nt² / (b-a)²). The probability of the sample mean deviating from the true mean decreases exponentially with n. This directly gives you PAC (Probably Approximately Correct) learning bounds: with high probability (the "probably" part), the empirical risk is close to the true risk (the "approximately correct" part). The bound tells you how many samples you need for a given level of confidence.

Sub-Gaussian random variables satisfy Hoeffding-like tail bounds even when unbounded. Gaussians are sub-Gaussian (obviously), and so are all bounded random variables. Many concentration results in modern ML theory use sub-Gaussian assumptions because they give exponential tail bounds without requiring bounded support.

Gaussian Mixture Models

A Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) assumes data is generated from a mixture of K Gaussian distributions. Each data point comes from one of the K components, but you do not know which one. The generative process: (1) pick a component k with probability πk (the mixing weight), (2) sample x from N(μk, Σk). The overall density is: P(x) = Σ_k πk * N(x | μk, Σk).

GMMs are fit using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which iterates between two steps:

  • E-step: For each data point, compute the posterior probability (responsibility) that it belongs to each component: r_ik = πk * N(xi | μk, Σk) / Σ_j πj * N(xi | μj, Σj). This is just Bayes' theorem.
  • M-step: Update each component's parameters using the responsibilities as soft assignments: μk = (Σ_i r_ik * xi) / (Σ_i r_ik), and similarly for Σk and πk.

EM alternates until convergence. Each iteration is guaranteed to increase (or maintain) the log-likelihood — it never gets worse. However, EM can converge to local optima, so you should run it multiple times with different initializations.

GMMs perform soft clustering: each point has a probability of belonging to each cluster, rather than a hard assignment. K-means is actually a special case of GMM where all covariances are isotropic and equal, and you take hard assignments (argmax instead of soft responsibilities). When you need cluster shapes that are not spherical, or when you need uncertainty in cluster assignments, GMMs are the right tool.

Causal Inference

Correlation does not imply causation. You have heard this a thousand times, but it still trips up practitioners. Ice cream sales correlate with drowning deaths — not because ice cream causes drowning, but because both increase in hot weather. The hot weather is a confounder: a variable that influences both the treatment and the outcome, creating a spurious association.

Simpson's paradox is the most dramatic example: a trend that appears in several groups reverses when the groups are combined. A treatment can appear better overall but worse in every subgroup (or vice versa), because the groups have different sizes and baseline rates. This is not a paradox in the logical sense — it is confounding in action. The solution is to condition on the confounder, not to blindly aggregate.

Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) formalize causal relationships. Nodes are variables, directed edges represent causal influence. Judea Pearl's do-calculus lets you determine whether a causal effect is identifiable from observational data alone, and how to estimate it. The key operation is the "do" operator: P(Y | do(X=x)) is different from P(Y | X=x) because "doing" removes the influence of confounders on X.

Why should ML practitioners care? Because models trained on observational data learn correlations, not causal effects. A model might learn that carrying a lighter predicts lung cancer — not because lighters cause cancer, but because smokers carry lighters. If you use such a model to make interventions (recommending people discard their lighters to prevent cancer), you will fail. Causal reasoning tells you which features are actually actionable and which are merely associated. As ML moves into healthcare, policy, and other high-stakes domains, causal thinking becomes essential.
