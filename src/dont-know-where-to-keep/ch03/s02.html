Data Labeling and Annotation

Chapter 3: Data Fundamentals 5 subtopics
TL;DR

Models learn from examples, but someone has to create those examples. Data labeling is the process of attaching meaning — categories, boundaries, transcriptions — to raw data so a model can learn the pattern. It is often the most expensive and time-consuming part of any ML project. This section covers how to label well, how to measure whether your labels are consistent, how to catch mistakes, and how to cheat smartly with weak supervision and active learning so you label less data while still training strong models.

Annotation Strategies and Tools

What Annotation Is and Why It Matters

Before a model can learn to distinguish cats from dogs, someone has to look at a thousand photos and write "cat" or "dog" next to each one. That act — attaching a label to a data point — is called annotation (also called labeling). Without it, supervised learning simply cannot start. The quality of your labels sets the ceiling for your model's performance. Feed it sloppy labels and it learns sloppy patterns. No architecture trick can fix that.

Different Data, Different Annotation

The shape of your data determines how you annotate it. Structured data — think spreadsheets, SQL tables, CSV files with tidy columns — is the easiest. Labels are typically a single column: "churned" or "stayed," "$42,500 price." You rarely need a specialized tool because the label is just another field in the row.

Unstructured data is where annotation gets interesting and expensive. For images, you might draw bounding boxes around objects (object detection), paint pixel-level segmentation masks (semantic segmentation), or simply assign one label to the whole image (classification). For text, you highlight spans to create named entity annotations ("New York" → LOCATION) or assign sentiment labels to full documents. For audio, you mark timestamps where words start and stop, or label speaker identity. For video, you do all of the above — per frame — which is why video annotation budgets can be enormous.

Semi-structured data like JSON and XML sits in between. It has some schema, some free-form fields. You might label specific nested values or flag entire documents. Time series data — sensor readings, stock prices, heart-rate monitors — requires labeling windows of time: "anomaly from t=120 to t=135." Graph and network data needs labels on nodes ("fraudulent account"), edges ("knows"), or whole subgraphs. Geospatial data might need polygon annotations on satellite imagery. And multi-modal data — say, a medical record with text notes, X-ray images, and lab values — demands that annotators understand all modalities to produce a coherent label.

Common Tools

You do not need to build an annotation platform from scratch. Label Studio is open-source, supports images, text, audio, video, and time series, and runs locally or in the cloud. Prodigy, built by the spaCy team, is optimized for NLP and integrates active learning out of the box. CVAT (Computer Vision Annotation Tool) excels at bounding boxes and video tracking. Amazon SageMaker Ground Truth provides a managed workforce and built-in quality controls if you want to outsource. Pick based on your data type, your budget, and whether you need a built-in workforce or will use your own annotators.

Designing Annotation Guidelines

The most underrated step in any labeling project is writing clear guidelines. An annotation guideline is a document that tells annotators exactly what to label, how to handle edge cases, and what to do when they are unsure. Without it, ten annotators will make ten different decisions on the same ambiguous example. Good guidelines include positive and negative examples for every class, explicit rules for boundary cases ("if the object is less than 20% visible, skip it"), and a process for escalating disagreements. Spend a day on guidelines before you spend a month on labeling. It pays for itself.

Inter-Annotator Agreement

Why Multiple Annotators Matter

If only one person labels your data, you have no way to know whether those labels reflect the task or that person's quirks. Having at least two annotators label the same examples lets you measure consistency. When annotators agree, you can be more confident the labels are reliable. When they disagree, you have found the examples that need clearer guidelines or are genuinely ambiguous.

Cohen's Kappa

Cohen's Kappa (κ) measures agreement between two annotators while correcting for chance. Two people flipping coins would agree about 50% of the time on a binary task — Kappa accounts for that. The formula is κ = (p_o − p_e) / (1 − p_e), where p_o is the observed agreement and p_e is the agreement expected by chance. A Kappa of 1 means perfect agreement. A Kappa of 0 means you are doing no better than random. Below 0 means you are doing worse than random, which usually signals a misunderstanding in the guidelines.

from sklearn.metrics import cohen_kappa_score

# Two annotators labeled 10 examples as 'pos' or 'neg'
annotator_1 = ['pos','pos','neg','pos','neg','neg','pos','pos','neg','pos']
annotator_2 = ['pos','pos','neg','neg','neg','neg','pos','pos','neg','pos']

kappa = cohen_kappa_score(annotator_1, annotator_2)
print(f"Cohen's Kappa: {kappa:.3f}")
# Output: Cohen's Kappa: 0.800
# Interpretation: substantial agreement

As a rough guide: κ below 0.20 is poor, 0.21–0.40 is fair, 0.41–0.60 is moderate, 0.61–0.80 is substantial, and above 0.80 is near-perfect. For most ML projects, aim for at least 0.60 before trusting the labels. If you cannot reach that, your task definition probably needs work.

Fleiss' Kappa for More Than Two Annotators

When three or more annotators label the same items, Cohen's Kappa does not directly apply because it only handles pairs. Fleiss' Kappa generalizes the idea to any number of raters. The intuition is the same — compare observed agreement to chance — but the math accounts for all raters simultaneously. Use it when you run larger annotation campaigns with rotating teams of labelers.

Labeling Quality Control

Gold Standard Examples and Honeypots

Even well-intentioned annotators make mistakes, especially after hours of repetitive work. A gold standard example (sometimes called a honeypot) is an item where you already know the correct answer. You slip these into the annotation queue without telling annotators which ones they are. If someone gets the gold examples wrong, you know their recent batch needs review. This is the simplest and most effective quality check: cheap to set up, hard to game, and immediately actionable.

Adjudication Workflows

When two annotators disagree on an example, someone has to break the tie. An adjudication workflow routes disagreements to a senior annotator or domain expert who makes the final call. A common pattern: have two annotators label everything, automatically accept items where they agree, and send disagreements to a third reviewer. This keeps costs down because most items will agree, and only the hard cases need the expensive expert time.

Improving Quality Over Time

Quality control is not a one-time event. Track annotator accuracy against gold standards over weeks. Look for drift — an annotator who was great in week one might get sloppy by week three. Retrain annotators when their scores drop. Update your guidelines when you discover new edge cases. Treat labeling as a living process, not a one-shot task.

Weak Supervision and Programmatic Labeling

The Core Idea

Manual annotation is accurate but slow. What if you could write programs that label data for you? That is the idea behind weak supervision. Instead of hand-labeling each example, you write labeling functions — small Python functions that use heuristics, patterns, or external knowledge bases to guess labels. Each function is noisy and incomplete. It might only fire on 10% of the data and be wrong 20% of the time. But combine many noisy labeling functions together and a model can learn which functions to trust.

Snorkel is the framework that popularized this approach. You write labeling functions, Snorkel learns a noise-aware generative model that estimates each function's accuracy and correlations, and then it produces probabilistic labels for your whole dataset. Here is what a labeling function looks like:

from snorkel.labeling import labeling_function

SPAM = 1
HAM = 0
ABSTAIN = -1

@labeling_function()
def lf_contains_free(x):
    """If the email contains 'free money', guess spam."""
    return SPAM if "free money" in x.text.lower() else ABSTAIN

@labeling_function()
def lf_short_email(x):
    """Very short emails are usually not spam."""
    return HAM if len(x.text.split()) < 5 else ABSTAIN

@labeling_function()
def lf_known_sender(x):
    """Emails from contacts are ham."""
    return HAM if x.sender in known_contacts else ABSTAIN

Notice the ABSTAIN option. A labeling function does not have to vote on every example — it can stay silent when it is not confident. This is key. You are not writing one perfect rule; you are writing many imperfect ones.

When Weak Supervision Wins

Weak supervision shines when you have lots of unlabeled data, some domain knowledge you can encode as rules, and not enough budget for manual annotation. It is not a silver bullet. If your task is highly subjective (like sarcasm detection) and you cannot write reliable heuristics, weak supervision will produce noisy garbage. The honest tradeoff: you trade label quality for label quantity. For many practical problems — spam detection, information extraction, medical coding — that tradeoff is worth it.

Active Learning for Efficient Labeling

The Labeling Bottleneck

Imagine you have a million unlabeled images. You can afford to label ten thousand. Which ten thousand should you pick? If you choose randomly, many will be "easy" examples the model already handles well. You waste budget on labels that teach the model nothing new. Active learning flips the script: the model tells you which examples it is most confused about, and you label those first.

Uncertainty Sampling

The simplest active learning strategy is uncertainty sampling. Train your model on whatever labeled data you have. Run it on the unlabeled pool. Find the examples where the model's predicted probability is closest to 0.5 (for binary tasks) — those are the ones it is least sure about. Send those to annotators. Retrain. Repeat. Each round, the model improves faster because it is learning from the examples that challenge it most.

import numpy as np

def uncertainty_sample(model, X_unlabeled, n=100):
    """Select the n most uncertain examples for labeling."""
    probs = model.predict_proba(X_unlabeled)
    # For binary: uncertainty is highest when prob is near 0.5
    uncertainty = 1 - np.max(probs, axis=1)
    top_indices = np.argsort(uncertainty)[-n:]
    return top_indices

Query-by-Committee

Query-by-committee takes a different angle. Instead of one model, train several models (a "committee") on the same labeled data — maybe a random forest, a logistic regression, and a small neural network. Run them all on the unlabeled pool. The examples where the committee members disagree most are the most informative. If all three models say "cat," that example is easy. If two say "cat" and one says "dog," that example is worth labeling.

The Practical Payoff

Active learning does not give you free labels. It gives you smarter labeling priorities. In practice, teams using active learning often reach the same model performance with 30–50% fewer labeled examples compared to random sampling. The tradeoff is engineering complexity: you need an infrastructure loop that trains models, scores unlabeled data, routes examples to annotators, collects labels, and retrains. For large projects, that loop pays for itself quickly. For a one-off dataset of a few hundred examples, random sampling is fine — the overhead of active learning is not worth it.

The theme across all of these techniques — careful annotation, agreement measurement, quality control, weak supervision, active learning — is the same: labels are precious. Every shortcut that produces bad labels will cost you later in debugging, retraining, and lost trust. Every smart strategy that produces good labels faster will compound into better models, sooner.
