Privacy and Compliance

Chapter 3: Data Fundamentals 6 subtopics
TL;DR

Privacy in machine learning is not just a legal checkbox — it shapes trust, fairness, and the long-term viability of your models. Regulations like GDPR, CCPA, and HIPAA impose real constraints on how you collect, store, and use data, including the thorny problem of "unlearning" data from trained models. Anonymization techniques (k-anonymity, l-diversity, t-closeness) offer some protection but fail more often than you'd expect. Differential privacy gives you a mathematically rigorous guarantee by adding calibrated noise, at the cost of model utility. Federated learning keeps raw data on-device, reducing exposure. Synthetic data generation can sidestep privacy concerns entirely — if you can verify the generator didn't memorize the originals. Tying it all together requires a data governance framework: clear ownership, access controls, audit trails, and retention policies that your team actually follows.

Why Privacy Matters in ML

Let's start with the uncomfortable truth. Most machine learning systems are giant functions that memorize patterns from data — and some of that data belongs to real people. If your training set contains medical records, browsing histories, or financial transactions, your model has, in some compressed form, "seen" all of it. That alone should make you take privacy seriously, even before a lawyer gets involved.

But there are three reasons beyond legality. First, trust. Users share data because they expect it to be handled responsibly. One breach or one headline about your model leaking personal information, and that trust evaporates. Rebuilding it is far more expensive than getting privacy right from the start.

Second, fairness. Privacy failures don't hit everyone equally. Minority groups and vulnerable populations are disproportionately harmed by data leaks and re-identification attacks. A model trained on poorly anonymized health data doesn't just violate someone's privacy — it can reinforce existing inequalities if the exposure leads to discrimination.

Third, model risk. If you have to delete someone's data after the model is trained (and regulations increasingly say you do), what happens to the model? Can you prove it no longer "remembers" that data? If you can't, your deployed model becomes a legal liability. Privacy isn't a feature you bolt on at the end. It's a constraint that shapes your entire pipeline.

GDPR, CCPA, and HIPAA Implications

Three regulations come up constantly in ML work, and they each care about different things.

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) applies to anyone processing data of EU residents. Its core principles are lawful basis for processing, data minimization (collect only what you need), purpose limitation (use it only for what you said you would), and — the one that keeps ML engineers up at night — the right to erasure. Article 17 says individuals can request deletion of their personal data. If that data was used to train a model, simply deleting it from your database isn't enough. The model's weights may still encode information about that individual. This has spawned an entire subfield called machine unlearning, which we'll touch on shortly.

The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) gives California residents the right to know what data is collected about them, the right to delete it, and the right to opt out of its sale. For ML teams, "sale" has a broad definition — sharing data with a third-party model provider could qualify. CCPA is less prescriptive than GDPR about technical measures, but the penalties for non-compliance are real.

HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) governs protected health information (PHI) in the United States. If you're building ML models on patient data, HIPAA requires a "minimum necessary" standard — you access only the PHI you need — and mandates specific safeguards for storage, transmission, and access. De-identification under HIPAA follows either the "Safe Harbor" method (removing 18 specific identifiers) or the "Expert Determination" method (a statistician certifies re-identification risk is very small).

The practical impact on ML pipelines is significant. Data minimization means you should be asking "do I actually need this feature?" before including it in your training set. Collecting everything "just in case" is not just sloppy engineering — it's a compliance violation. Purpose limitation means a model trained for fraud detection can't casually be repurposed for marketing targeting without new consent. And the right to erasure means you need to track data lineage: which records went into which training runs, and what models were produced.

Anonymization Techniques

The intuition behind anonymization is simple: strip out identifying information so you can't trace a record back to a person. The reality is much harder than it sounds.

Pseudonymization replaces direct identifiers (names, emails) with tokens or codes. A lookup table maps tokens back to identities. This is not anonymization — it's reversible. GDPR treats pseudonymized data as still personal data. It reduces risk, but it doesn't eliminate the compliance burden.

Anonymization, in contrast, is irreversible. Once done properly, the data is no longer personal data under GDPR. The challenge is doing it properly.

k-anonymity requires that every record in your dataset is indistinguishable from at least k−1 other records with respect to certain quasi-identifiers (like age, zip code, gender). If k=5, any combination of quasi-identifier values must appear in at least 5 rows.

# Before k-anonymity (k=2): each row is unique on (Age, Zip)
#   Age  Zip    Disease
#   29   10001  Flu
#   31   10001  Diabetes
#   29   10002  HIV
#   32   10002  Flu

# After generalization for k=2:
#   Age    Zip    Disease
#   29-31  1000*  Flu
#   29-31  1000*  Diabetes
#   29-32  1000*  HIV
#   29-32  1000*  Flu
# Now each quasi-identifier group has at least 2 records.

The problem? k-anonymity doesn't protect the sensitive attribute. If all 5 people in a group have the same disease, knowing someone is in that group tells you their diagnosis. This is called a homogeneity attack.

l-diversity fixes this by requiring that each equivalence class has at least l "well-represented" values for the sensitive attribute. If l=3, each group of quasi-identifier-matching records must contain at least 3 distinct diseases. This prevents the homogeneity attack but is still vulnerable if the distribution of sensitive values in a group differs drastically from the overall population.

t-closeness addresses this by requiring that the distribution of the sensitive attribute in any equivalence class is within distance t of the attribute's overall distribution. It's the strongest of the three, but also the hardest to achieve without destroying data utility.

Here's the honest tradeoff: real-world anonymization failures are common. In 2006, AOL released "anonymized" search logs; researchers re-identified users within days. Netflix's "anonymized" movie ratings were cross-referenced with public IMDb reviews to identify individuals. The lesson is that quasi-identifiers are everywhere, and the more auxiliary data an attacker has, the easier re-identification becomes. Anonymization is a risk-reduction measure, not a guarantee.

Differential Privacy

If anonymization gives you a best-effort promise, differential privacy (DP) gives you a mathematical one. The core idea: the output of an analysis should be roughly the same whether or not any single individual's data is included.

Formally, a randomized mechanism M satisfies ε-differential privacy if for any two datasets D and D′ differing in exactly one record, and for any set of possible outputs S:

P[M(D) ∈ S] ≤ e^ε × P[M(D′) ∈ S]

The parameter ε (epsilon) controls the privacy budget. Smaller ε means stronger privacy but noisier results. In practice, ε values between 1 and 10 are common; ε below 1 is considered strong privacy; ε above 10 offers weak guarantees.

How do you achieve this? By adding calibrated noise. The Laplace mechanism adds noise drawn from a Laplace distribution scaled to the query's sensitivity (how much one person's data can change the output). The Gaussian mechanism uses Gaussian noise and provides (ε, δ)-differential privacy, a slightly relaxed variant where the guarantee holds with probability 1−δ.

For ML specifically, the key technique is DP-SGD (Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent). The idea: during training, you clip each per-example gradient to bound its influence, then add Gaussian noise to the aggregated gradient before updating weights.

# DP-SGD pseudocode
for each minibatch B:
    for each example x_i in B:
        g_i = compute_gradient(model, x_i)
        # Clip individual gradient to bound sensitivity
        g_i = g_i * min(1, C / ||g_i||)

    # Aggregate and add noise
    g_noisy = (1/|B|) * (sum(g_i) + N(0, σ²C²I))

    # Update model
    model.weights -= lr * g_noisy

# Privacy accounting: track cumulative ε across all steps
# Tools like Opacus (PyTorch) or TF Privacy handle this.

The tradeoff is real and unavoidable. More privacy (smaller ε) means more noise, which means lower model accuracy. In practice, DP-SGD models often lose 2–10% accuracy compared to non-private training, depending on the task and dataset size. Larger datasets help because the noise is amortized across more examples. Small datasets suffer disproportionately.

A common pitfall: people apply differential privacy to the training step but forget about hyperparameter tuning. Every time you evaluate on a validation set and adjust, you're consuming privacy budget. Proper privacy accounting must cover the entire pipeline, not just the final training run.

Federated Learning (Data Aspects)

Federated learning flips the usual ML paradigm. Instead of bringing data to the model, you bring the model to the data. Each participant (a phone, a hospital, a bank branch) trains a local copy of the model on its own data and sends only model updates — gradients or weight deltas — to a central server, which aggregates them.

The privacy benefit is obvious: raw data never leaves the device. The central server never sees an individual patient's records or a user's typing history. Google uses this for next-word prediction on Android keyboards; hospitals use it for collaborative diagnostic models without sharing patient data across institutions.

The standard aggregation protocol is Federated Averaging (FedAvg): each client runs several steps of SGD locally, then the server averages the resulting weight updates, weighted by each client's dataset size. More sophisticated protocols exist — secure aggregation uses cryptographic techniques so the server can compute the sum of updates without seeing any individual update.

But federated learning alone is not a privacy guarantee. Model updates can leak information. Gradient inversion attacks have shown that it's possible to reconstruct training images from shared gradients, especially in early training rounds with small batch sizes. This is why production federated systems combine FL with differential privacy (adding noise to local updates before sharing) and secure aggregation.

Communication efficiency is a practical concern. Sending full model updates over mobile networks is expensive. Techniques like gradient compression (sending only the top-k largest gradient values), quantization (reducing precision from 32-bit to 8-bit or lower), and federated distillation (sharing predictions instead of gradients) help reduce bandwidth. Each comes with its own privacy and accuracy implications — compression, for instance, can inadvertently leak more information about outlier data points.

Synthetic Data for Privacy

Here's an appealing idea: instead of anonymizing real data, generate fake data that has the same statistical properties. Train your model on the synthetic version. No real person's data is in the training set, so no privacy violation. Right?

Mostly right — with important caveats.

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and diffusion models can produce synthetic tabular data, images, or time series that closely match the distribution of real data. For tabular data, models like CTGAN learn the joint distribution of features and generate new rows that preserve correlations, marginal distributions, and even complex multi-column dependencies.

# Using CTGAN (sdv library) to generate synthetic tabular data
from sdv.single_table import CTGANSynthesizer
from sdv.metadata import Metadata

metadata = Metadata.detect_from_dataframe(real_data)
synthesizer = CTGANSynthesizer(metadata, epochs=500)
synthesizer.fit(real_data)
synthetic_data = synthesizer.sample(num_rows=10000)

# Evaluate quality
from sdmetrics.reports.single_table import QualityReport
report = QualityReport()
report.generate(real_data, synthetic_data, metadata)
print(report.get_score())  # 0.0 to 1.0

Evaluating synthetic data quality matters. You need to check that the synthetic data preserves the statistical properties you care about (column distributions, pairwise correlations, downstream ML model performance) while not preserving individual-level details. Common metrics include column-wise distribution similarity, pairwise correlation comparison, and "train on synthetic, test on real" (TSTR) accuracy.

The big risk is memorization. If your generative model overfits to the training data, the "synthetic" samples may be near-copies of real records. This defeats the entire purpose. You can test for this by checking nearest-neighbor distances between synthetic and real records — if any synthetic record is suspiciously close to a real one, you have a memorization problem. Combining synthetic generation with differential privacy guarantees (DP-GANs) is the gold standard, but it further reduces data quality.

Data Governance Frameworks

All the techniques above are useless if your organization doesn't have the operational structure to enforce them. Data governance is the boring, essential work of defining who owns data, who can access it, and what happens to it over time.

Data ownership means every dataset has a named owner — not "the data team" but a specific person or role responsible for its quality, access, and lifecycle. When an incident occurs, ownership determines who gets paged. When a deletion request arrives, ownership determines who acts on it.

Access controls should follow the principle of least privilege. An ML engineer building a recommendation model doesn't need access to raw PII — they need access to feature-engineered, de-identified datasets. Role-based access control (RBAC) is the minimum; attribute-based access control (ABAC) is better for complex environments where access depends on context (time of day, project, data sensitivity level).

Audit trails record every access, transformation, and model training run. When a regulator asks "who accessed this patient's data and what did they do with it?", you need an answer within hours, not weeks. Immutable audit logs — append-only, tamper-evident — are essential. Tools like Apache Atlas, Amundsen, or cloud-native solutions (AWS Lake Formation, GCP Data Catalog) can help, but the tool matters less than the discipline of using it consistently.

Data retention policies define how long data is kept and when it's deleted. GDPR's storage limitation principle says you shouldn't keep personal data longer than necessary for its purpose. In ML terms, this means you need to decide: how long do you keep training data? What about the model trained on it? What about cached predictions? A retention policy should cover the entire data lifecycle from ingestion through model deployment through retirement.

Practical implementation often starts with a data classification system: tag every dataset with a sensitivity level (public, internal, confidential, restricted). Tie access controls and retention policies to these levels. Automate enforcement where possible — don't rely on people remembering to delete data after 90 days. Schedule it. Log it. Verify it.

The hardest part isn't the technology. It's getting engineers, product managers, and legal teams to agree on policies and actually follow them. A governance framework that exists only in a wiki page is worse than having no framework at all — it creates a false sense of security. Make governance part of your CI/CD pipeline. Automated checks that flag PII in training data, alert on unusual access patterns, and enforce retention schedules will do more than any policy document.

Privacy and compliance in ML is not a solved problem. The regulations are evolving. The attacks are getting more sophisticated. The techniques for protection are improving but always involve tradeoffs. What you can do today is build your pipeline with privacy as a first-class design constraint — not an afterthought. Track your data lineage. Minimize what you collect. Add formal privacy guarantees where you can. And build the governance structures to keep everything accountable over time. Your future self — and your legal team — will thank you.
